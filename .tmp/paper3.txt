Problems to be studied in Part II

It is seen from Propositions 1.2, 1.3, and 1.4 that for any given u0 satisfying (1.8), the classical
solution (u(t, x; u0 ), v(t, x; u0 )) of (1.1) exists globally and stays bounded on (0, ‚ąě) provided that

the nonlinear cross diffusion is weak in certain sense. The following question arises naturally: How
does a globally defined bounded positive solution of (1.1) change as time changes?

including uniform persistence and stabilization of positive constant solutions. To be more precise,
we study the following problems:

Contributions of Part II

To the best of our knowledge, the problems stated above are studied here for the first time when
ő≤ > 0. From the modeling viewpoint, the factor (1 + v)‚ąíő≤ may be viewed as a signal-dependent
desensitization mechanism (or receptor saturation/adaptation): when v is large, the effective drift
toward the signal is reduced (when Ōá0 > 0). Negative sensitivity (Ōá0 < 0) corresponds to chemorepulsion, for which the drift acts against concentration of the signal. It is then expected that large
ő≤ or negative sensitivity would weaken the possibility of aggregation and pattern initiation, or
strengthen the possibility of stabilization at constant solutions. However, the signal-dependent
sensitivity complicates the analysis of the asymptotic dynamics of (1.1). Many existing approaches
for the study of stability of positive stationary solutions in chemotaxis models are difficult to apply
directly to (1.1). The current paper makes several contributions to the study of the asymptotic
dynamics of (1.1), including
‚ÄĘ (Establishment of stability regimes) This paper establishes explicit linear stability and instability
criteria for positive constant solutions, together with computable critical sensitivity thresholds and
explicit parameter dependence (see Theorem 2.2), which clarifies the impact of each parameter
on the stability of positive constant solutions. It also establishes several sets of explicit sufficient
conditions for global stability of the unique positive equilibrium in the non-minimal model (see
Theorems 2.3 and 2.4) and for global stability of the family of positive constant solutions in the
minimal model (see Theorem 2.5). These conditions provide various parameter regimes where
chemical signal does not induce aggregation or nontrivial pattern formation, which is of great
biological interest.
‚ÄĘ (Discovery of interesting biological scenarios) The results established in this paper reveal the following interesting biological scenarios when m ‚Č• 1: the chemical signal does not enforce asymptotic
