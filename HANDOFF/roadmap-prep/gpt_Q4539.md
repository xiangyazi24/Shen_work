# Decisive verdict

No substantially shorter route is currently available for the theorem

[

#E_0(L)le 21,

qquad

L=mathbf Q(a), a^3=3a+1.

]

The proposed fixed-instance three-isogeny descent, followed by one specialized 3-adic separatedness lemma, is the shortest mathematically honest route at the present Mathlib revision.

The important qualification is that this does not require building a general elliptic-isogeny or Selmer library. The economical formalization is a curve-specific certificate:

1. two explicit point maps phi and phihat, with their denominator-cleared identities;

1. two explicit Kummer functions and the finite 3 + 81 = 84 candidate table;

1. one local lemma saying that an infinitely 3-divisible point in the formal kernel at the prime over 3 is zero.

Every proposed finite-field, division-polynomial, or two-prime shortcut stops at the torsion subgroup. None detects or excludes a free Mordell–Weil summand.

# 1. The structural obstruction to a finite-reduction proof

Suppose reduction maps at finitely many good primes were available:

[

operatorname{red}_v:E_0(L)longrightarrow widetilde E_0(k_v).

]

Let

[

M=operatorname{lcm}_{vin S}#widetilde E_0(k_v).

]

For every P : E0(L),

[

operatorname{red}_v(MP)=Moperatorname{red}_v(P)=0

]

at every v in S. Hence

[

M E_0(L)subseteqbigcap_{vin S}ker(operatorname{red}_v).

]

If P has infinite order, then MP is a nonzero element of this common kernel. Therefore the theorem one would need,

```javascript
Function.Injective
  (red_p.prod red_q : E0L →+ EredP × EredQ)
```

on all L-rational points is false. It is true only after restricting to torsion and using the prime-primary description of the two kernels.

This is the decisive reason why two-prime reduction proves

```javascript
Finite (AddCommGroup.torsion E0L)
```

but cannot prove

```javascript
Finite E0L
```

or Nat.card E0L ≤ 21.

The same objection applies to any fixed finite collection of congruence conditions. A positive-rank subgroup always has nonzero multiples lying in all selected reduction kernels.

# 2. What the pinned Mathlib revision actually supplies

The useful existing pieces are:

- Mathlib.AlgebraicGeometry.EllipticCurve.Affine.Point: the elliptic-curve point group and maps induced by field homomorphisms;

- Mathlib.AlgebraicGeometry.EllipticCurve.Reduction: integral and minimal models, WeierstrassCurve.reduction, and HasGoodReduction;

- Mathlib.AlgebraicGeometry.EllipticCurve.DivisionPolynomial.Basic: division polynomials and fixed multiplication-polynomial identities;

- finite fields, number fields, ideals, quotient rings, valuations, and finite enumeration;

- Mathlib.RingTheory.FormalGroup.Basic: abstract one-dimensional formal group laws.

The missing pieces are exactly the ones relevant here:

- no point-reduction homomorphism from local elliptic points to the reduced curve;

- no identification of the reduction kernel with the Weierstrass formal group;

- no theorem that the good-reduction kernel has only residue-characteristic torsion;

- no elliptic-isogeny object or Vélu API;

- no elliptic Selmer package;

- no elliptic canonical height or Mordell–Weil finite-generation theorem.

Affine.Point.map does not solve reduction: it requires a field/algebra homomorphism, while there is no field homomorphism from a fraction field to its residue field.

The abstract FormalGroup file is also not enough by itself. It does not construct the formal group of a WeierstrassCurve or identify its points with the kernel of reduction.

# 3. Route-by-route feasibility audit

## 3.1 Two good primes

### Exact theorem that would make it work

For the whole group one would need

```javascript
Function.Injective (red_p.prod red_q : E0L →+ EredP × EredQ)
```

or at least a finite common kernel.

### Status

The statement is false for a positive-rank elliptic curve, by the M E(L) argument above.

The torsion-only version is mathematically correct:

```javascript
Function.Injective
  ((red_p.comp torsion.subtype).prod
   (red_q.comp torsion.subtype))
```

when the two kernel torsion groups are respectively p-primary and q-primary. The pure group-theoretic part is short; the point-reduction and formal-kernel theorem are not in Mathlib.

### Verdict

Useful for proving the already-known torsion subgroup is exactly Z/21, but irrelevant to excluding non-torsion points.

---

## 3.2 The fact that the reduction over F3 has exactly seven points

The seven known prime-to-3 torsion points can account for every residue class. This proves that every P : E0L is congruent modulo the prime over 3 to a known torsion point.

It does not prove equality with that torsion point. Each smooth residue point has an entire 3-adic disk of lifts. If the global rank were positive, suitable multiples of a non-torsion point would lie in the disk above the identity.

### Exact theorem that would make it work

One would need:

```javascript
∀ P Q : E0L, red3 P = red3 Q → P = Q
```

or “each residue disk contains at most one L-rational point.”

### Status

This is false in general and is essentially a rank-zero/Chabauty conclusion, not a reduction theorem.

### Verdict

The point count 7 is extremely useful after weak three-descent, but cannot replace that descent.

---

## 3.3 Division polynomials

Division polynomials can prove, for a fixed n,

```javascript
n • P = 0 ↔ divisionPolynomial n vanishes at P
```

and therefore enumerate E0(L)[n] by fixed polynomial calculations.

### Exact theorem that would make it work

To conclude that the complete point group has at most 21 elements, one must first prove

```javascript
∀ P : E0L, 21 • P = 0
```

or at least that every point has finite order dividing a fixed integer.

### Status

That is precisely the desired rank-zero theorem. Division polynomials detect torsion; they do not prove that an arbitrary point is torsion.

### Verdict

Excellent for verifying the cyclic table and exact order of G; no help with the free part.

---

## 3.4 Heights or a denominator bound

A height proof would be valid in principle. One could combine:

```javascript
canonicalHeight P = 0 ↔ IsOfFinAddOrder P
```

with an explicit positive lower bound for non-torsion points and an upper bound below that threshold.

### Exact theorem that would make it work

For example:

```javascript
∃ c > 0,
  (∀ P, ¬ IsOfFinAddOrder P → c ≤ canonicalHeight P) ∧
  (∀ P : E0L, canonicalHeight P < c)
```

Alternatively, one needs a certified finite height search together with a theorem that every L-point lies below the search bound.

### Status

Mathlib has no elliptic canonical-height package at the pinned revision. More importantly, finite reduction supplies no global height upper bound. Producing such a bound requires local heights, Northcott, descent/saturation, elliptic logarithms, or comparable machinery.

A generalized Lutz–Nagell theorem would still classify only torsion points. Non-torsion integral points can exist, and arbitrary points need not be integral.

### Verdict

Mathematically sound but substantially larger than the fixed three-isogeny descent.

---

## 3.5 Galois trace and the cyclic cubic extension

Because L/Q is cyclic cubic and E0 is defined over Q, one can decompose

[

E_0(L)otimesmathbf Q

]

into the invariant part and a two-dimensional rational representation of Gal(L/Q).

### Exact theorem that would make it work

One needs the zero-trace subgroup to be finite:

```javascript
Finite {P : E0L // P + σ P + σ^2 P = 0}
```

or equivalently

```javascript
rank E0(L) = rank E0(Q).
```

### Status

This is exactly the rank-growth problem. Under restriction of scalars it becomes the rank-zero problem for the complementary abelian surface. It does not disappear.

The curve has

[

j(E_0)=-140625/8,

]

which is not an algebraic integer, so E0 is not a CM elliptic curve. There is no CM-specific algebraic shortcut.

### Verdict

A reformulation of the same problem, not a proof.

---

## 3.6 Root numbers, parity, and analytic rank zero

The base-change L-function can be decomposed into the rational form and two cubic-character twists. Exact modular-symbol computations can certify analytic nonvanishing.

### Exact theorem that would make it work

One would need a formal theorem of the form

```javascript
analyticRank E0 L = 0 → Finite E0L
```

through Kato, Kolyvagin, Gross–Zagier, or BSD in this setting.

Parity alone would give only the parity of the rank. Even rank does not distinguish 0 from 2,4,....

### Status

None of the required arithmetic-rank implications is present in Mathlib, and formalizing them would be vastly deeper than the fixed descent.

### Verdict

Valid on paper but wholly unsuitable as a shortcut.

---

## 3.7 Mordell–Weil finite generation plus saturation

Mordell–Weil finite generation by itself is insufficient. A finitely generated group can contain the known Z/21 subgroup and also have a free summand.

### Exact theorem that would make it work

One still needs either

```javascript
E0L / 3 • E0L ≃ ZMod 3
```

or a height-based proof that the known subgroup has finite index and is saturated at every prime.

### Status

The first statement is the three-descent output; the second is height/descent machinery. Mordell–Weil finite generation is itself absent.

### Verdict

Strictly longer than the current MW-FG-free proof.

---

## 3.8 A direct use of the rational-fiber coefficient filter

There is one possible application-level bypass, but it does not prove #E0(L) ≤ 21.

Write

[

X=x_0+x_1a+x_2a^2,qquad

Y=y_0+y_1a+y_2a^2

]

and expand the elliptic equation and the rational-fiber filter over Q. If the resulting saturated polynomial ideal in the six rational coefficients is zero-dimensional, then a Gröbner/resultant certificate could enumerate only the filtered points and avoid computing all of E0(L).

### Exact theorem that would make it work

A concrete certificate of the form

```javascript
filteredPoint P → P ∈ knownFiniteList
```

proved from a zero-dimensional elimination ideal.

### Status

This must be established from the actual filter equations. It is not implied by the current finite-field data, and it would not yield the requested group-cardinality theorem. If the ideal has positive dimension, the shortcut disappears.

Elliptic Chabauty is another way to exploit the rationality filter, but it requires a rank bound and substantial p-adic integration infrastructure that Mathlib does not have.

### Verdict

Worth one Gröbner-dimension diagnostic if the end application can be weakened, but not an alternative proof of #E0(L) ≤ 21.

The rational 3-torsion makes the current descent much cheaper than a generic descent:

- the forward cohomology side has only 3 global candidates;

- the dual side is L(S,3) ≅ F3^4, hence only 81 candidates;

- the local conditions occur only at the primes over 2 and 3;

# 5. The minimal final proof contract

Let G be the already-verified point of order 21. Define

```javascript
T  := 7 • G   -- order 3
H7 := AddSubgroup.zmultiples (3 • G)  -- order 7
H21 := AddSubgroup.zmultiples G
```

The shortest proof needs only the following three substantive statements.

## 5.1 Weak three-descent

```javascript
theorem weak_three_descent :
  ∀ P : E0L,
    ∃ i : ZMod 3, ∃ Q : E0L,
      P = i.val • T + 3 • Q
```

This is exactly what the explicit isogeny/Kummer certificate proves.

Iterating it gives, for each P, a t ∈ <T> such that

```javascript
∀ n : ℕ, ∃ Qn : E0L,
  P - t = (3 ^ n) • Qn
```

because 3 • T = 0.

## 5.2 The known order-seven subgroup accounts for reduction

Using the already-verified seven-point reduction table:

```javascript
theorem red3_on_H7_bijective :
  Function.Bijective
    (red3.comp H7.subtype : H7 →+ Ered3.Point)
```

The cardinality calculation is 7 = 7; injectivity can be checked on the explicit table.

Thus one can subtract a known h7 : H7 having the same reduction as P - t.

Since multiplication by 3 is an automorphism of H7, h7 is itself infinitely 3-divisible. Therefore

```javascript
R := P - t - h7
```

both reduces to zero and is infinitely 3-divisible.

## 5.3 Specialized formal-kernel separatedness

```javascript
theorem formalKernel_three_separated
    (R : E0L)
    (hred : red3 R = 0)
    (hdiv : ∀ n : ℕ, ∃ Q : E0L,
      (3 ^ n) • Q = R) :
    R = 0
```

This theorem should be proved only for the fixed good integral model at the prime pi = a - 1.

No general formal-group equivalence is needed. Use the local parameter at infinity, for example z = -x/y, and the fixed triplication formula. For points in the formal kernel, the parameter has positive integral valuation. The formal expansion of [3] has zero constant term, linear term 3z, and integral higher coefficients. Since v_pi(3)=3, multiplication by 3 increases the parameter valuation by at least one. If

[

R=3^nQ_n

]

for every n, then v_pi(z(R)) is at least n for every n, forcing R=O.

The fact that each Q_n is also in the formal kernel follows from #Ered3(F3)=7: multiplication by 3^n is injective on a group of order 7.

This is a small fixed valuation lemma, not a general Weierstrass formal-group library.

## 5.4 Final conclusion

The preceding lemmas give

```javascript

  -- subtract matching h7 using red3_on_H7_bijective

```

Hence every point is one of the already-listed 21 points. Together with their verified distinctness:

# 6. Recommended implementation boundary

Do not build:

- a scheme-level category of elliptic isogenies;

- a general Selmer group;

- a general elliptic formal-group/reduction-kernel equivalence;

- canonical heights or Mordell–Weil finite generation.

Build only:

```javascript
structure N18IsogenyCertificate where
  phi      : E0L →+ EprimeL
  phihat   : EprimeL →+ E0L
  phihat_phi : ∀ P, phihat (phi P) = 3 • P
  phi_phihat : ∀ Q, phi (phihat Q) = 3 • Q
  -- fixed affine formulas and kernel clauses

structure N18KummerCertificate where
  weak_three_descent :
    ∀ P : E0L, ∃ i : ZMod 3, ∃ Q, P = i.val • T + 3 • Q
  -- the 84-candidate local table and its soundness
```

and the one fixed local theorem formalKernel_three_separated.

The ring identities and finite candidate tables are already the easy, decidable part. The remaining generic adapters are real mathematics, but every alternative route replaces them with something larger or with a false statement.

# Bottom line

[

boxed{text{Keep the explicit }3text{-isogeny/Kummer descent and the fixed }3text{-adic separatedness lemma.}}

]

Two-prime reduction can close torsion finiteness and exact torsion, but it cannot see rank. Division polynomials can certify the 21-point table, but they cannot show arbitrary points are torsion. Heights, analytic rank, parity, restriction of scalars, and elliptic Chabauty all require infrastructure at least as deep—and usually much deeper—than the current fixed descent.

The only diagnostic worth trying before finishing Block 4 is a zero-dimensional Gröbner test on the rational-fiber filter, and that would be a bypass for the final N18 application rather than a proof that E0(L) itself has 21 points.
