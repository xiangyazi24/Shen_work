# 2. Why the proposed gonality argument fails

Suppose one only knows that a function g realizing the difference has pole degree at most 4.

That cannot force g to be constant:

- x has pole divisor

- y-V(x), for a cubic polynomial V, typically has pole degree 3 at one infinity after cancellation at the other;

- products and quotients of such functions easily have pole degree 4.

Even the statement “pole degree at most 2” does not by itself imply constancy: every fractional-linear function of x has pole degree 2.

What is true is the sharper classification:

A nonconstant function of pole degree at most 2 is a fractional-linear function of x; therefore its zero and pole divisors are full fibers of the hyperelliptic map.

The reducedness condition then excludes those fibers.

# 8. Minimal Riemann–Roch fallback

If the direct valuation proof is not used, the exact minimal geometric theorem required is not full Riemann–Roch. It is the degree-two linear-system classification:

```javascript
theorem degree_two_linear_system
    (A : EffectiveDivisor C)
    (hdeg : A.degree ≤ 2) :
    1 < l A ↔
      A.degree = 2 ∧ IsHyperellipticFiber A
```

Equivalently:

```javascript
theorem low_degree_function_is_hyperelliptic
    {g : C.FunctionField}
    (hnconst : ¬ IsConstant g)
    (hdeg : (poleDivisor g).degree ≤ 2) :
    IsHyperellipticFiber (poleDivisor g)
```

This is the genus-two case of uniqueness of the hyperelliptic g¹₂, and follows from Clifford/Riemann–Roch. But the explicit (a+b y)/c proof above establishes it directly, so importing this theorem is optional.

# 9. Answers to the four questions

## (1) Is the proposed argument correct?

No.

- Pole degree at most 4 does not force constancy.

- Gonality 2 does not classify degree-two functions.

- The norm degree alone loses cancellation information at the two infinities.

- A pair (u,v) omits the infinity-orientation coordinate.

After correcting these points, the elementary proof works: balanced representatives give pole degree at most 2, and the explicit degree argument proves that the only nonconstant functions at this degree are Möbius transforms of x.
