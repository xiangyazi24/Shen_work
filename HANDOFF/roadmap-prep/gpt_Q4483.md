# Executive verdict

There is no quotient of (X_1(25)) over (mathbf Q) of genus (1) or (2) that can carry the order-(25) problem. The complete diamond-quotient lattice has genera

[

12longrightarrow 4longrightarrow 0,

qquad

12longrightarrow 0longrightarrow 0,

]

and a rational modular-symbol computation gives

[

J_1(25)sim_{mathbf Q}A_4	imes A_8,

]

where (A_4) and (A_8) are (mathbf Q)-simple of dimensions (4) and (8). Consequently any nonconstant (mathbf Q)-map from (X_1(25)) to a genus-(1) or genus-(2) curve would put an abelian subvariety of dimension (1) or (2) inside (J_1(25)), which is impossible.

The best genuine quotient is the degree-(2) diamond quotient

[

C_4:=X_{{pm1,pm7}}(25),qquad g(C_4)=4.

]

Its Jacobian is the simple factor (A_4). Thus the narrow order-(25) statement does not literally require the whole (12)-dimensional group (J_1(25)(mathbf Q)), but it does require arithmetic control of at least this (4)-dimensional simple factor. There is no elliptic or genus-(2) rank-zero substitute hiding underneath it.

The honest bottom line is therefore:

## The genus-(4) factor is not secretly CM or split

The factor (A_4=J_{{pm1,pm7}}(25)) is attached to one newform orbit with coefficient field generated by a root of

[

The nebentypus has order (5), so the coefficient field is a copy of (mathbf Q(zeta_5)), but the newform itself is not CM. In particular, the (mathbf Q(zeta_5))-multiplication is GL2-type multiplication, not an idempotent splitting into elliptic curves.

This is exactly analogous to the warning encountered for N18: a coefficient/endomorphism field is not automatically a splitting field.

# 4. Why the genus-zero maps do not turn the problem into a finite check

## 4.1 The map to (X_0(25))

The curve (X_0(25)) is rational and has infinitely many rational points. It parametrizes infinitely many rational cyclic (25)-isogenies.

The fiber of

[

X_1(25)longrightarrow X_0(25)

]

has degree (10). Over a rational point of (X_0(25)), asking for a rational lift is asking whether the isogeny character on a generator is trivial. That is a nontrivial (C_{10})-torsor condition.

For one fixed base point the fiber is finite, but the base has infinitely many rational points. Determining which fibers split is exactly the rational-point problem on (X_1(25)); it does not produce a finite table.

## 4.2 The map to (X_1(5))

The map

[

(E,P_{25})longmapsto(E,5P_{25})

]

has degree (25). Over a rational (5)-torsion point (Q), its fiber is the (5)-division torsor

[

[5]^{-1}(Q).

]

Again (X_1(5)simeqmathbf P^1) has infinitely many rational points. The bad primes of the corresponding torsors vary with the parameter, so there is no fixed (S)-unit space and no fixed finite list of global fifth-power classes.

## 4.3 The genus-zero diamond quotient

The degree-(5) map from (X_1(25)) to the other genus-zero intermediate curve has the same defect. Writing its function field as a fifth-root extension does not make the base parameter finite.

A statement such as

[

w^5=R(t)

]

has a finite fiber for each (t), but (tinmathbf Q) ranges over an infinite set and the prime support of (R(t)) is unbounded. Proving that the cover has only cuspidal rational points is the original global Diophantine problem in another coordinate system.

## 4.4 Reduction alone is insufficient

At a small prime such as (2), a rational order-(25) modular point must reduce to a cusp: a smooth elliptic curve over (mathbf F_2) cannot contain a point of order (25). But this only places the rational point in a cusp residue disk.

To conclude equality with the cusp one still needs either:

- a formal-immersion argument into an abelian quotient with finite rational points, or

- a direct local uniqueness theorem for the covering equation.

Separatedness by itself does not prove rank zero; it only finishes after a descent has shown repeated divisibility.

# 5. The cyclic quintic cover and the only plausible elementary descent

The map

[

C_4longrightarrow X_0(25)simeqmathbf P^1

]

is a cyclic Galois cover of degree (5). Riemann–Hurwitz gives

[

2g(C_4)-2=6=5(-2)+16.

]

Every ramified point in a cyclic degree-(5) cover contributes (4), so the cover has four fully ramified branch points.

After base change to

[

F=mathbf Q(zeta_5),

]

it can be put in Kummer form

[

oxed{w^5=R(t)}

]

with four branch places. This gives an order-(5) automorphism and an action of

[

mathbf Z[zeta_5]

]

on (A_4=J(C_4)).

Let

[

lambda=1-zeta_5.

]

The (lambda)-isogeny has kernel of order (25); equivalently, (A_4[lambda]) is two-dimensional over (mathbf F_5). Therefore a Schaefer-style descent can in principle be built from two branch-divisor generators and two Kummer functions.

This is the one credible algebraic route avoiding Kato:

1. derive an explicit Kummer equation for (C_4/F);

1. choose two generators of (A_4[lambda]);

1. construct the two evaluation/Kummer functions;

1. enumerate the finite (S)-unit fifth-power classes in (F);

1. compute local images at the primes above (5) and the branch discriminant;

1. prove the surviving (lambda)-Selmer classes are exactly the known cuspidal torsion classes;

1. use (5)-adic separatedness to remove an infinitely (lambda)-divisible remainder;

1. enumerate the finite images of rational cusps and exclude noncuspidal points.

This can reuse the shape of the N18 ((1-zeta_3))-descent:

```plain text
cyclic-cover branch divisors
→ explicit Kummer functions
→ finite S-unit candidates
→ local image tables
→ separatedness.
```

But it does not reuse N18 as a black box. New ingredients include a genus-(4) Picard/Jacobian implementation, fifth-power local arithmetic over (mathbf Q(zeta_5)), and a (5)-adic formal-group filtration. This is not a small Layer-1 computation.

## Why this is not simply FLT exponent (5)

The quotient is a four-branch cyclic quintic cover, not the Fermat quintic. Its Jacobian factor (A_4) is non-CM, whereas Fermat-curve Jacobians decompose into CM factors. No birational reduction to

[

a^5+b^5=c^5

]

or another classical Fermat equation emerges from the quotient structure.

An equation (w^5=R(t)) may eventually yield finitely many quintic Thue or (S)-unit equations, but obtaining and solving those equations is the (lambda)-descent; it is not an already-known one-line FLT consequence.

# 7. The shortest Lean route in practice

## Route A: formal immersion through the fourfold (A_4)

This is the smallest route if one is willing to supply a rank-zero/finite-group certificate for (A_4), whether analytically or by descent.

The proof shape is:

```plain text
Tate order-25 witness
  → rational point P on X1(25)
  → reduction of P mod 2 is a cusp c
  → Abel–Jacobi class [P-c]
  → projection to A4(Q)
  → reduction is zero
  → torsion reduction injective at 2
  → image of [P-c] is zero
  → formal immersion at c
  → P=c, contradiction.
```

Only the dimension-(4) quotient is needed; the dimension-(8) factor and the full order (227555) need not appear.

# 8. Direct answers

## (1) Is there a genus-(0), (1), or (2) quotient that solves the problem?

- There are genus-(0) quotients.

- There is no genus-(1) or genus-(2) quotient over (mathbf Q).

- The only positive-genus proper diamond quotient is the genus-(4) curve (X_{{pm1,pm7}}(25)).

- The (4+8) simple Jacobian decomposition rules out every other (mathbf Q)-defined genus-(1)/(2) quotient, including one arising from an Atkin–Lehner lift.

## (2) Do (X_1(5)) or (X_0(25)) give a finite fiber calculation?

No. Their rational points form infinite one-parameter families. The lifting condition is a degree-(25) or degree-(10) torsor whose arithmetic varies with the parameter. A fixed fiber is finite; the union over all rational base points is not.

## (3) Is there a cover model reducing the problem to lower genus plus local checks?

The best model is the cyclic degree-(5) cover

[

C_4	o X_0(25)simeqmathbf P^1,

]

which becomes (w^5=R(t)) over (mathbf Q(zeta_5)). It leads to an explicit ((1-zeta_5))-descent, but the relevant Jacobian is still a simple fourfold. The local checks form part of a real Selmer computation, not a fixed finite decide table derived solely from the genus-zero base.

## (4) What is the shortest Lean-formalizable route?

The best compromise is:

[

oxed{

X_1(25)longrightarrow C_4,quad

J(C_4)=A_4,quad

}

]

This avoids the full (12)-dimensional Jacobian and the dimension-(8) factor. But without either an imported rank-zero theorem for (A_4) or a new explicit ((1-zeta_5))-Selmer computation, it does not close.

Thus N25 is not secretly an N14/N20-style ring computation. It is the remaining genuinely high-genus seam. The reduction from genus (12) to genus (4) is real and worthwhile, but there is no elliptic/genus-(2) shortcut comparable to N49 or N18 Route C.
