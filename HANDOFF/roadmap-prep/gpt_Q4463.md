# Executive verdict

There are two decisive corrections.

1. A rational point of order p is naturally a noncuspidal rational point of X_1(p), not merely of X_0(p). Its image on X_0(p) is a rational cyclic p-isogeny. For p=11,13,17,19, X_0(p) actually has noncuspidal rational points, so the statement

```plain text
X_0(p)(Q) consists only of cusps
```

is false in exactly the range where one might hope to use it.

1. To prove the bound |E(Q)_tors| ≤ 16 from scratch, it is not enough to exclude prime orders 11 and 13. A cyclic group of order 17, 19, 23, or any larger prime is itself a counterexample to the desired cardinality bound. Restricting to p≤13 assumes the conclusion one is trying to prove.

The honest shortest decomposition is therefore:

```plain text
fixed explicit theorem for p = 11
fixed explicit theorem for p = 13
one uniform hard tail theorem excluding every prime p ≥ 17
```

The first two pieces are fixed-curve arithmetic. The third piece is still Mazur-level content. It may be stated more weakly than the full prime-torsion theorem, but it cannot be replaced by a finite check unless some independent uniform prime bound is already available.

# 1. The relevant modular curves

For prime p>3, a rational point of exact order p on an elliptic curve over Q gives a point of Y_1(p)(Q). The forgetful map

forgets the generator and remembers only the cyclic subgroup. On coarse curves its generic degree is

Thus a rational point of X_0(p) is only a rational cyclic subgroup. It need not possess a rational generator, even after identifying P with -P.

The genus-one models are the familiar conductor-p curves:

```plain text
X₀(11) : 11a1, rank 0, torsion Z/5
X₀(17) : 17a1, rank 0, torsion Z/4
X₀(19) : 19a1, rank 0, torsion Z/3
```

Choosing a cusp as the origin identifies all rational points with the displayed finite torsion groups. The cuspidal difference already generates those groups. Hence the extra group elements are genuine noncuspidal rational isogeny points.

For p=23, J₀(23) is a rank-zero abelian surface. Rank zero alone is not the final rational-point theorem: one must still enumerate which torsion classes lie in the Abel–Jacobi image of X₀(23). The result is that only the two cusps occur.

# 2. What is elementary for p = 11, 17, 19?

## 2.1 The rank-zero machinery does apply

The shared-filtration architecture already used in the composite-order work is reusable:

```plain text
explicit isogeny descent
  -> G = H + nG
  -> local n-adic separatedness of the formal kernel
  -> a uniform annihilator for G
  -> finite point enumeration
```

No Mordell–Weil finite-generation API is needed if the descent output is the genuine weak-descent equality and the local formal-kernel separation is proved.

The most natural descent primes differ:

```plain text
X₀(11) or X₁(11): a 5-isogeny descent is natural;
X₀(17):             a 2-isogeny descent is natural;
X₀(19):             a 3-isogeny descent is natural.
```

The abstract group-theoretic Block-5 argument is identical after replacing the multiplier by 5, 2, or 3. Only the explicit Kummer maps and local calculations change.

## 2.2 But X₀ rank zero does not by itself exclude p-torsion

For each noncuspidal R ∈ X₀(p)(Q), the corresponding cyclic subgroup C has a Galois character

A rational point above R on the coarse X₁(p) exists exactly when the character is trivial after quotienting by ±1; equivalently, after a possible quadratic twist, the subgroup has a rational generator. In concrete coordinates, this becomes a rational-root test for the polynomial whose roots are the x-coordinates of the nonzero kernel points. Its degree is (p-1)/2.

Therefore the X₀ route has two required outputs:

```javascript
/-- Exhaust all rational points on the rank-zero X₀(p). -/
theorem x0_p_rational_points :
  ∀ R : X0p.QPoint, R ∈ explicitFiniteList

/-- No noncuspidal listed isogeny has a rational generator, even after
    the ± identification relevant to the coarse X₁ fiber. -/
theorem x1_fiber_empty_over_non_cusps :
  ∀ R ∈ explicitNonCuspidalList,
    ¬ HasRationalX1Lift R
```

For the three primes, the finite fiber degrees are:

```plain text
p = 11: degree 5 over each of 3 noncuspidal X₀-points;
p = 17: degree 8 over each of 2 noncuspidal X₀-points;
p = 19: degree 9 over the single noncuspidal X₀-point.
```

These are finite exact polynomial checks. They are not consequences of rank zero.

## 2.3 Cheapest choice for p = 11

For p=11, direct use of X₁(11) is probably shorter than going through X₀(11) and three degree-five fibers:

```plain text
X₁(11) is genus one;
its Mordell–Weil rank is zero;
its rational points are the rational cusps.
```

# 3. The fixed primes 13 and 23 do not intrinsically require Eisenstein theory

## 3.1 p = 13

X₀(13) has genus zero and infinitely many rational points, because rational cyclic 13-isogenies occur in a one-parameter family. Consequently X₀(13) is useless for a finite rational-point enumeration.

The relevant curve is

For this single prime, one can avoid a general Eisenstein-ideal development:

```plain text
explicit genus-two model of X₁(13)
  -> explicit 2-descent on its Jacobian
  -> weak descent plus local separatedness, or an exact finite Jacobian description
  -> finite Abel–Jacobi intersection
  -> all rational points are cusps.
```

This is still serious arithmetic, but it is a fixed-curve problem of the same species as the N18 work, and substantially smaller than a variable-level Hecke-algebra construction.

A suitable endpoint is:

```javascript
theorem x1_13_rational_points_are_cusps :
  ∀ P : X1_13.QPoint, IsCusp P

theorem no_rational_point_order_13 :
  ¬ HasRationalPointOfOrder E 13
```

Thus the statement “p=13 needs the Eisenstein ideal” is too strong. It needs hard arithmetic, but not necessarily the general Mazur machine.

## 3.2 p = 23

Likewise, the fixed prime 23 can be handled without a uniform Eisenstein theorem:

```plain text
X₀(23) is genus two;
J₀(23)(Q) has rank zero;
a finite Jacobian/Abel–Jacobi check leaves only the two cusps.
```

Since a rational 23-torsion point would give a noncuspidal point of X₀(23)(Q), this already excludes order 23.

Again, this is a fixed genus-two descent, not a uniform proof for all large primes.

# 4. Why “only p = 11,13” is circular

Suppose the only prime-order exclusions proved are 11 and 13. Consider the abstract finite group

```javascript
ZMod 17
```

It is cyclic, has no element of any forbidden composite order, satisfies all usual rank-two structure restrictions, and has cardinality 17. Thus it survives Layers 1 and 3 and violates the desired bound.

Formally, the missing implication is obvious:

```plain text
point of order p
  -> p divides |E(Q)_tors|
  -> |E(Q)_tors| ≥ p.
```

For p≥17, this is precisely a counterexample to |T|≤16; it is not a case that can be ignored after assuming the target inequality.

The reduction to only 11 and 13 becomes valid only after proving an independent theorem such as

```javascript
theorem prime_order_lt_17
    {p : ℕ} (hp : p.Prime)
    (hP : HasRationalPointOfOrder E p) :
    p < 17
```

or equivalently

```javascript
theorem no_prime_order_ge_17
    {p : ℕ} (hp : p.Prime) (hp17 : 17 ≤ p) :
    ¬ HasRationalPointOfOrder E p
```

But this uniform tail is itself the hard content. It is weaker than Mazur’s final prime bound p≤7, so it is the minimal hard theorem actually required for the cardinality-16 project, but it is not a finite arithmetic check.

## Recommended hybrid

```javascript
theorem no_prime_order_ge_17
    (E : WeierstrassCurve ℚ) [E.IsElliptic]
    {p : ℕ} (hp : p.Prime) (hp17 : 17 ≤ p) :
    ¬ HasRationalPointOfOrder E p := ...
```

Then the advertised prime layer is elementary to assemble:

```javascript
theorem mazur_prime_torsion_bound
    (E : WeierstrassCurve ℚ) [E.IsElliptic]
    {p : ℕ} (hp : p.Prime) (hp11 : 11 ≤ p) :
    ¬ HasRationalPointOfOrder E p := by
  by_cases hp17 : 17 ≤ p
  · exact no_prime_order_ge_17 E hp hp17
  have hp_le_16 : p ≤ 16 := by omega
  have : p = 11 ∨ p = 13 := by
    interval_cases p <;> simp_all [Nat.prime_def]
  rcases this with rfl | rfl
  · exact no_order_11 E
  · exact no_order_13 E
```

The fixed pieces are reusable, explicit, and testable. The hard uniform theorem has the narrowest possible statement.

# 6. What the uniform tail really requires

There is no current Mathlib API that turns the tail into a short proof. A genuine Mazur-style implementation needs, in some form, the following objects and theorems.

## 6.1 Modular interpretation and reduction

```javascript
primeTorsionToX1 :
  HasRationalPointOfOrder E p → NoncuspidalQPoint (X1 p)

forgetGenerator : X1 p ⟶ X0 p
```

One must control extension to integral models and specialization at a small residue prime.

## 6.2 Jacobian quotient with finite rational group

Construct a suitable winding/Eisenstein quotient A_p of J₀(p) or the corresponding quotient used in the chosen proof, together with

```javascript
quotientMap : J0 p →+ A p
finite_rational_points : AddMonoid.IsTorsion (A p).QPoint
```

For a finite-generation-free project, “rank zero” must again be replaced by an actual bounded-exponent or separatedness theorem.

## 6.3 Hecke and Eisenstein input

The proof needs enough Hecke algebra to define the Eisenstein ideal and enough modular-symbol or q-expansion arithmetic to prove the required quotient is nonzero and has finite rational points. This is not a single native_decide check: the level p is variable.

## 6.4 Formal immersion

There must be a theorem of the shape

```javascript
formalImmersionAtCusp :
  FormalImmersion (modularAbelJacobi p) cusp residuePrime
```

plus the specialization argument saying that a rational modular point reducing to the cusp and mapping to the same finite Jacobian-quotient point must itself be the cusp.

This is the geometric heart that converts finite Jacobian information into absence of noncuspidal rational points.

## 6.5 Exceptional local cases

The proof must separately discharge the possibilities for reduction of the original elliptic curve at the residue prime and at p. These are Néron-model/Tate-curve statements, not mere genus computations.

# 7. Alternatives and why they are not shorter

## Full rational-isogeny theorem

One could first formalize Mazur’s classification of rational prime-degree isogenies. Then a rational prime torsion point can occur only for

```plain text
11, 13, 17, 19, 37, 43, 67, 163
```

beyond the already allowed primes, and each remaining X₁→X₀ fiber can be checked explicitly.

This turns the final step into a finite list, but proving the rational-isogeny theorem is at least as difficult as the uniform torsion tail and introduces additional exceptional isogeny cases. It is not the shortest route unless that theorem is already available for another layer.

## Merel/Parent/Oesterlé bound

A general uniform boundedness theorem specialized to degree one would also reduce the problem to finitely many primes. Formalizing such a theorem is not shorter: it uses modular curves, formal immersion, and arithmetic comparable to or beyond the narrow Mazur tail.

## Enumerating many small modular curves

Without a proved uniform upper bound, checking p=17,19,23,29,... one by one never terminates. A finite database cutoff is not a theorem about all primes.

# 8. Recommended project boundary

The most economical honest architecture is:

```plain text
Layer 2A: fixed order 11
  X₁(11), genus one, explicit descent and cusp enumeration

Layer 2B: fixed order 13
  X₁(13), genus two, explicit Jacobian descent and cusp enumeration

Layer 2C: uniform tail p ≥ 17
  one separately scoped Mazur/Parent formal-immersion theorem

Layer 2D: three-line arithmetic dispatcher
  prime p ≥ 11 is 11, 13, or ≥17
```

Optional fixed developments for 17, 19, and 23 are useful as validation targets for the uniform tail and may be considerably easier than the general theorem, but they do not replace it.

# Bottom line

- X₀(11), X₀(17), and X₀(19) are rank-zero elliptic curves, but not cusp-only curves.

- Their rank-zero calculations can be reused, provided one also performs the finite X₁→X₀ fiber exclusions.

- p=11 is cheapest directly on the genus-one curve X₁(11).

- p=13 is a fixed genus-two problem and can be handled without general Eisenstein theory.

- p=23 is also a fixed genus-two problem if desired.

- For the theorem |E(Q)_tors|≤16, excluding only 11 and 13 is circular. The minimal remaining hard statement is the uniform exclusion of every prime p≥17.

- Therefore the mountain shrinks from “full Mazur prime torsion for all p≥11” to “two fixed curves plus one uniform tail,” but it does not collapse to only two primes unless an independent uniform bound has already been proved.
