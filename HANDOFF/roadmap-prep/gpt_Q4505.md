# The single analytic-rank-to-Mordell–Weil input

## Executive verdict

There is a clean **single citable arithmetic theorem** that covers both requested uses:

> Let `A/ℚ` be an abelian variety whose simple factors are the modular abelian varieties attached to Galois orbits of normalized weight-two newforms on `Γ₁(N)` (allowing nebentypus). If the Hasse–Weil value `L(A,1)` is nonzero, then `A(ℚ)` is finite.

For a simple newform quotient `A_f`, the hypothesis can equivalently be stated as

```text
L(f^σ,1) ≠ 0 for every embedding σ of the coefficient field,
```

because, up to the standard normalization,

```text
L(A_f,s) = ∏_σ L(f^σ,s).
```

This is the rank-zero corollary of Kato's Euler-system theorem for weight-two modular forms, together with the ordinary Mordell–Weil theorem. For modular elliptic curves it is also within the classical Kolyvagin–Logachev rank-zero package.

It applies to:

1. the winding quotient `A_p` of `J₀(p)`, once one has proved that its newform factors are precisely factors with nonvanishing central value;
2. the four-dimensional factor `A₄` of `J₁(25)`, once one has proved that it is the modular abelian variety attached to the stated newform orbit and that `L(A₄,1) ≠ 0`.

However, this theorem is **not by itself the entire prime-tail proof**. It supplies only the finiteness of the target Mordell–Weil group. The construction of modular curves, the winding quotient, the formal-immersion theorem, specialization at the cusp, and the N25 map/fiber calculation remain separate geometric obligations. Thus the honest conclusion is:

> This can be the single remaining **analytic-rank-to-arithmetic** axiom. It cannot honestly be called the single deep input of the whole Mazur proof unless all modular-curve/formal-immersion and Weil-pairing infrastructure is already proved and counted as non-axiomatic engineering.

# 1. The mathematically exact theorem

## 1.1 Newform-factor formulation

The safest general statement is factorwise.

Let `A/ℚ` be a modular abelian variety admitting a `ℚ`-isogeny

```text
A  ~_ℚ  ∏_{i∈I} A_{f_i}^{e_i},
```

where each `f_i` is a normalized weight-two newform on some `Γ₁(N_i)` and `A_{f_i}` is its associated newform quotient. Assume

```text
∀ i, ∀ σ : HeckeField(f_i) ↪ ℂ,  L(f_i^σ,1) ≠ 0.
```

Then

```text
A(ℚ) is finite.
```

This formulation covers oldforms, products, repeated factors, `Γ₀` quotients, and `Γ₁` quotients with character. It is more robust than saying only “`A` is attached to a newform,” which would exclude the winding quotient when it contains several simple factors.

## 1.2 Hasse–Weil formulation

Once the Hasse–Weil L-function of a modular quotient is defined and its factorization is proved, the same theorem is:

```text
A is a weight-two modular quotient
∧ L(A,1) ≠ 0
──────────────────────────────
A(ℚ) is finite.
```

This is the clean public theorem to cite.

## 1.3 What the theorem actually combines

The result packaged as “finite Mordell–Weil” combines two standard theorems:

1. **Kato's rank-zero Selmer theorem:** nonvanishing at the central critical point forces the relevant Bloch–Kato/Selmer group to have corank zero; in particular, the Mordell–Weil rank of `A_f(ℚ)` is zero.
2. **Mordell–Weil finite generation:** `A_f(ℚ)` is finitely generated.

A finitely generated abelian group of rank zero is finite.

It is therefore reasonable to call the Lean theorem

```lean
kato_finite_MW_of_analytic_rank_zero
```

provided the documentation says explicitly that it is the rank-zero corollary of Kato plus Mordell–Weil, rather than literally a theorem whose entire proof appears in one line of Kato's paper.

Primary reference:

- K. Kato, *p-adic Hodge theory and values of zeta functions of modular forms*, Astérisque **295** (2004), 117–290.

For the elliptic/rank-at-most-one predecessor:

- V. Kolyvagin and D. Logachev, the Gross–Zagier/Kolyvagin theory for modular elliptic curves and modular abelian varieties.

# 2. Future-facing Lean statement

Mathlib currently has no type representing a general modular abelian variety with its newform factorization and rational-point group. Once that layer exists, the ideal statement is essentially:

```lean
universe u

namespace FLT.ModularAV

/-- A modular abelian variety over `ℚ` whose simple factors arise from
weight-two newforms on `Γ₁(N)`. -/
class IsWeightTwoModularQuotient (A : AbelianVariety ℚ) : Prop where
  -- Concrete future fields should give a finite collection of newform orbits,
  -- a ℚ-isogeny to the associated product, and compatibility of L-functions.
  factorization : WeightTwoNewformFactorization A

/-- Analytic rank zero, stated by central-value nonvanishing. -/
def AnalyticRankZero (A : AbelianVariety ℚ) : Prop :=
  A.LFunction 1 ≠ 0

/-- Kato/Kolyvagin–Logachev rank-zero package. -/
axiom kato_finite_MW_of_analytic_rank_zero
    (A : AbelianVariety ℚ)
    [IsWeightTwoModularQuotient A]
    (hL : AnalyticRankZero A) :
    Finite A.rationalPoints

end FLT.ModularAV
```

This is the best final API. It states exactly the standard theorem and does not mention Mazur, `X₀(p)`, `X₁(25)`, or any particular quotient.

The names `AbelianVariety`, `rationalPoints`, `LFunction`, and `WeightTwoNewformFactorization` are deliberately future-facing: current Mathlib does not yet provide the required objects.

# 3. A compile-friendly temporary repository interface

Until modular abelian varieties are actual Lean objects, use a project-local certificate type. The crucial rule is that the predicate `ModularAVAnalyticRankZero` must eventually have a **concrete definition**; it must not remain an opaque arbitrary proposition.

```lean
universe u

namespace FLT.ModularRankZero

/-- A concrete certificate that the additive group `G` is the rational-point
Mordell–Weil group of a weight-two modular quotient whose central L-value is
nonzero.

The implementation should ultimately contain:
* a level and `Γ₀`/`Γ₁` quotient witness;
* the finite set of Galois orbits of weight-two newforms;
* the isogeny/product decomposition;
* identification of `G` with rational points;
* factorwise nonvanishing at `s = 1`.
-/
structure ModularAVAnalyticRankZero
    (G : Type u) [AddCommGroup G] : Prop where
  level : ℕ
  -- Replace these project-local placeholders by concrete witnesses as the
  -- modular-geometry layer lands.
  modularQuotientWitness : WeightTwoModularQuotientWitness G level
  LValueNonvanishing : modularQuotientWitness.LValueAtOne ≠ 0

/-- The single deep arithmetic package. -/
structure KatoPackage : Prop where
  finite_mw :
    ∀ {G : Type u} [AddCommGroup G],
      ModularAVAnalyticRankZero G → Finite G

/-- Axiom form, if the package is not passed explicitly. -/
axiom kato_finite_MW_of_analytic_rank_zero
    {G : Type u} [AddCommGroup G]
    (hG : ModularAVAnalyticRankZero G) :
    Finite G

end FLT.ModularRankZero
```

The explicit-parameter version is preferable for auditing:

```lean
variable (Kato : KatoPackage)

letI : Finite G := Kato.finite_mw hG
```

This makes every theorem that depends on the unformalized arithmetic input visibly carry a `KatoPackage` parameter.

Do **not** define

```lean
opaque ModularAVAnalyticRankZero (G : Type*) [AddCommGroup G] : Prop
```

and then allow arbitrary project proofs of it. That would merely rename an opaque axiom and would not improve the trust boundary.

# 4. Why one general theorem is better than two special axioms

## 4.1 Mathematically best interface

Once the modular quotient and L-value certificates are concrete, use the one general theorem:

```lean
kato_finite_MW_of_analytic_rank_zero
```

Advantages:

- it is a standard, independently citable theorem;
- it explains why the prime tail and N25 share an arithmetic input;
- it prevents the codebase from accumulating unrelated “database finiteness” axioms;
- it can later be discharged once, without modifying the two consumers;
- it applies to future modular factors encountered elsewhere in the Mazur build.

## 4.2 Minimal honest current axiom set

If the repository does **not yet define** modular abelian varieties, newform factorization, or L-values, two specific assumptions are logically narrower and more honest:

```lean
axiom windingQuotient_finite
    (p : ℕ) [Fact p.Prime] (hp : 19 < p) :
    Finite (PrimeTail.WindingQuotientQPoint p)

axiom n25_A4_finite :
    Finite N25.A4QPoint
```

These are two assumptions, not one, but they do not pretend that an undefined predicate encodes Kato's hypotheses.

The recommended transition plan is:

```text
Stage 1: use the two exact finite-point assumptions;
Stage 2: define concrete rank-zero modular certificates;
Stage 3: replace both assumptions by one KatoPackage field;
Stage 4: eventually prove KatoPackage in a separate foundational development.
```

A useful intermediate packaging is:

```lean
structure MazurModularRankZeroInputs : Prop where
  windingFinite :
    ∀ p [Fact p.Prime], 19 < p →
      Finite (PrimeTail.WindingQuotientQPoint p)
  n25A4Finite : Finite N25.A4QPoint
```

This gives one explicit parameter to the top-level theorem, but it must be described honestly as **two specialized consequences bundled together**, not as a formalization of Kato's theorem.

# 5. Prime-tail consumption

Assume the non-arithmetic modular geometry has exported:

```lean
namespace FLT.PrimeTail

/-- Rational points of the winding quotient `A_p`. -/
abbrev WindingQPoint (p : ℕ) := (windingQuotient p).rationalPoints

/-- Concrete modular/newform/L-value certificate. The winding quotient is
built from exactly the newform factors on which the winding element has
nonzero period, equivalently nonzero central L-value. -/
def winding_rankZero_certificate
    (p : ℕ) [Fact p.Prime] (hp : 19 < p) :
    ModularAVAnalyticRankZero (WindingQPoint p) := by
  -- No Kato input here.
  -- 1. Identify the Hecke-stable quotient of J₀(p).
  -- 2. Decompose it into newform factors.
  -- 3. Relate pairing with the winding element to L(f,1).
  -- 4. Prove every retained factor has nonzero central value.
  sorry

/-- Pure Mazur geometry: finiteness of the winding quotient's rational
points, together with specialization and formal immersion, rules out a
noncuspidal rational p-torsion modular point. -/
theorem no_prime_torsion_of_winding_finite
    {p : ℕ} [Fact p.Prime] (hp : 19 < p)
    (E : WeierstrassCurve ℚ) [E.IsElliptic]
    [Finite (WindingQPoint p)] :
    ¬ HasRationalPointOfOrder E p := by
  -- Tate/moduli bridge E,P ↦ x ∈ X₁(p)(ℚ)
  -- X₁(p) → X₀(p) → A_p
  -- reduction to the cusp
  -- torsion/specialization in A_p
  -- formal immersion at ∞
  -- contradiction with noncuspidality
  sorry

end FLT.PrimeTail
```

The Kato-consuming capstone is then one screen:

```lean
open FLT.ModularRankZero

/-- Uniform prime tail, with the arithmetic theorem explicit. -/
theorem no_rational_point_of_prime_order_gt_19
    (Kato : KatoPackage)
    {p : ℕ} [Fact p.Prime] (hp : 19 < p)
    (E : WeierstrassCurve ℚ) [E.IsElliptic] :
    ¬ HasRationalPointOfOrder E p := by
  letI : Finite (PrimeTail.WindingQPoint p) :=
    Kato.finite_mw (PrimeTail.winding_rankZero_certificate p hp)
  exact PrimeTail.no_prime_torsion_of_winding_finite hp E
```

If the global axiom form is used:

```lean
letI : Finite (PrimeTail.WindingQPoint p) :=
  kato_finite_MW_of_analytic_rank_zero
    (PrimeTail.winding_rankZero_certificate p hp)
```

Nothing in the formal-immersion proof should mention Kato directly. It should depend only on the typeclass

```lean
[Finite (WindingQPoint p)].
```

That separation is important: it lets the arithmetic proof be replaced without touching the geometry.

# 8. Does this one input complete all of `|T| ≤ 16`?

## 8.1 Conditional completeness

Yes, under the following already-proved or separately planned capstones:

1. rational torsion is finite;
2. the torsion structure is cyclic or `ℤ/2 × ℤ/2n` (`m ≤ 2`, via the Weil pairing or an equivalent theorem);
3. all fixed cyclic and noncyclic exclusions below the tail are proved, including N18 and the N25 geometric consumer;
4. `p=17` and `p=19` are handled explicitly, or included in the uniform tail;
5. the prime-tail modular curve, winding quotient, reduction, and formal-immersion theorem are proved;
6. the two analytic-rank-zero certificates are proved.

Then one `KatoPackage` field supplies both missing finite-Mordell–Weil instances, and the top-level Mazur cardinality theorem follows.

A schematic final theorem is:

```lean
theorem mazur_torsion_card_le_16
    (Kato : ModularRankZero.KatoPackage)
    (E : WeierstrassCurve ℚ) [E.IsElliptic] :
    Nat.card (RatTorsion E) ≤ 16 := by
  have h25 := no_rational_point_of_order_25 Kato E
  have htail := fun {p} [Fact p.Prime] hp =>
    no_rational_point_of_prime_order_gt_19 Kato hp E
  exact assemble_torsion_bound
    torsion_structure_m_le_two
    fixed_cyclic_exclusions
    fixed_noncyclic_exclusions
    h25
    no_rational_point_of_order_17
    no_rational_point_of_order_19
    htail
```

## 8.2 The necessary correction

It is not accurate, at the current Mathlib/repository state, to say:

```text
fixed-curve engineering + N18 + elementary Layer 3 + one Kato theorem
```

is the entire remaining proof.

Two other major foundations are presently absent or incomplete:

- the elliptic `n`-torsion/Weil-pairing package used in Layer 3 to force the first invariant factor `m ≤ 2`;
- the uniform modular-curve/winding-quotient/formal-immersion machinery used in the prime tail.

The latter is a large arithmetic-geometric theorem, not merely a fixed-curve computation. Kato does not construct `X₀(p)`, `J₀(p)`, the winding element, or prove formal immersion.

Therefore the strongest honest claim is:

> Once the structural and modular-geometric layers are formalized, **one standard Kato rank-zero theorem is the only remaining analytic/arithmetic black box**, and it closes both the winding quotient and N25's `A₄` factor.

That is still a major improvement over an opaque axiom such as

```lean
mazur_prime_torsion_bound
```

because the trust surface becomes a standard reusable theorem with explicit hypotheses, while the modular geometry, L-value certificates, and final contradictions remain inspectable Lean code.
