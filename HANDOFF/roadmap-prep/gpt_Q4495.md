# Mathlib infrastructure inventory for the uniform prime-torsion tail

**Mathlib revision checked:** `79aee35d9696d759b73eed71d7dde666750bc35e`, which is later than the repository baseline `96fd0fff3b8837985ae21dd02e712cb5df72ec05`.

## Executive verdict

A fully internal proof of

```lean
theorem no_rational_point_of_prime_order_gt_19
    {p : ℕ} [Fact p.Prime] (hp : 19 < p)
    (E : WeierstrassCurve ℚ) [E.IsElliptic] :
    ¬ HasRationalPointOfOrder E p
```

by the modular-curve/formal-immersion/winding-quotient route is **not presently a large but routine project on top of existing Mathlib APIs**. It is a genuine multi-year research-formalization programme.

Mathlib already has useful analytic and foundational pieces:

- the upper half-plane and the `SL₂(ℤ)`/`GL₂(ℝ)` slash action;
- congruence subgroups `Γ(N)`, `Γ₀(N)`, `Γ₁(N)`;
- analytic modular forms and cusp forms;
- analytic cusps and cusp orbits;
- complex `q`-expansions;
- an abstract double-coset Hecke-ring skeleton;
- schemes, group objects, properness, smoothness/unramifiedness infrastructure;
- a very small amount of generic abelian-variety scaffolding;
- a formal Euler-product definition of an elliptic-curve L-function.

However, essentially every **bridge used in Mazur's uniform argument** is missing:

```text
X₀(N), X₁(N) as compact algebraic curves / integral moduli schemes
→ moduli interpretation and cuspidal sections
→ Hecke action Tₙ on forms, homology, and Jacobians
→ modular symbols and the winding element {0,∞}
→ J₀(N), J₁(N), Pic⁰, quotient abelian varieties, Néron or local integral models
→ Eisenstein ideal / winding quotient
→ finiteness or rank zero of that quotient
→ cotangent-space calculation at the cusp
→ formal immersion over ℤ_(ℓ)
→ specialization and rational-point contradiction.
```

The strongest honest conclusion is:

> **From current Mathlib, a complete foundational formalization is plausibly 150,000–400,000 new Lean lines and several expert-years, with substantial mathematical design risk.**

A narrowly specialized proof for only `ℚ`, prime level, and `p > 19` can avoid some generality—especially general Néron models and a general Jacobian functor—but it remains a major project, plausibly **50,000–150,000 lines and one to three expert-years** if the mathematical route is frozen and several people work in parallel.

The only genuinely short shortcut is to isolate the uniform theorem itself as one trusted project theorem/axiom while completing all finite and structural layers around it.

# Status key and scale convention

For each ingredient:

- **HAS:** Mathlib has a directly reusable declaration.
- **PARTIAL:** relevant foundations exist, but not the theorem/object required by Mazur's proof.
- **BUILD:** the required layer is absent.

The size estimates are order-of-magnitude engineering estimates, not promises:

```text
S       1k–5k Lean lines
M       5k–20k
L       20k–60k
XL      60k–150k+
Research-scale: theorem design or dependencies are not yet stable enough for a reliable line count.
```

Many estimates overlap: for example, the integral modular-curve model supplies infrastructure used by both the Jacobian and formal-immersion blocks. The totals should therefore not be obtained by naively summing every upper bound.

## 3.2 Required construction

A useful modular-symbol block would need:

```text
1. P¹(ℚ) endpoints and oriented geodesic symbols {r,s};
2. boundary map and relative homology interpretation;
3. Manin relations;
```

# 4. `J₀(N)`, `J₁(N)`, abelian varieties, and Néron models

It proves that a proper geometrically integral group scheme over a field is commutative. This is useful abelian-variety scaffolding, but the file does not define or construct modular Jacobians.

Mathlib also has general schemes, group objects, proper morphisms, smooth/unramified morphisms, Picard groups of **rings**, and substantial category-theory infrastructure. These do not yield the curve Jacobian automatically.

## 4.2 Missing objects — **BUILD**

No current declarations provide:

```text

J₀(N), J₁(N)
Abel–Jacobi X₀(N) → J₀(N)
functorial pullback/pushforward on Jacobians
quotient abelian variety by a Hecke ideal
Eisenstein quotient or winding quotient
Néron model of J₀(N)
specialization on rational torsion
cotangent space of J₀(N) identified with S₂(Γ₀(N)).
```

A search for curve Jacobian/Picard-scheme declarations finds none. “Jacobian” occurrences in the elliptic-curve directory mostly refer to Jacobian **coordinates**, not Jacobian varieties.

Similarly, searches found no `NeronModel` declaration.

## 4.3 How much generality is actually needed?

A full general Jacobian and Néron-model theory is unnecessary for this one theorem. Three possible specializations are available:

### Option A: specialized divisor-class Jacobian

Construct `J₀(p)(K)` as degree-zero divisor classes on a concrete modular curve, together with:

- Hecke correspondences on divisors;
- the Abel–Jacobi map;
- enough quotient-group structure for the Eisenstein/winding ideal;
- an integral specialization map at one prime.

This avoids representability of the Picard functor but still requires a serious divisor/function-field library for the modular curve.

### Option B: Picard functor / abelian variety

Build a reusable `Pic⁰`/Jacobian construction and then use generic quotient-abelian-variety machinery. This is cleaner for Mathlib but much larger.

### Option C: modular-symbol quotient only

Avoid constructing the entire Jacobian as a scheme by defining the target finite/rank-zero group through a lattice/period or modular-symbol presentation, and prove only the local formal-immersion statement required. This is the narrowest route, but the comparison from a rational modular point to that quotient remains a substantial theorem.

### Scale

- Specialized divisor-class plus local specialization: **L–XL**, `30k–90k`.
- General Jacobian/Picard variety and quotient abelian varieties: **XL**, `80k–200k+`.
- General Néron models: **research-scale**, likely an independent multi-year library.

The recommended proof should avoid general Néron models unless another project needs them.

# 5. L-values and rank zero of the winding quotient

## 5.1 What Mathlib has — **VERY PARTIAL**

File:


Important declarations:

```lean
WeierstrassCurve.LFunction
WeierstrassCurve.LSeries
```

This defines the Euler factors and the L-function as a formal arithmetic function / L-series. It does **not** provide:

- analytic continuation or functional equation;
- modularity of elliptic curves;
- equality with a newform L-series;
- evaluation or nonvanishing at `s=1`;
- analytic rank;
- BSD implications;
- Gross–Zagier or Kolyvagin;
- `L(f,1) ≠ 0 → rank A_f(ℚ)=0`.

A search found no `Kolyvagin` declaration and no elliptic analytic-rank theorem.

Current open Mathlib work also confirms the stage of development: height infrastructure is still being developed, and an open affine-point PR describes its formulas as being needed “on the way to the Mordell–Weil Theorem.” Thus even ordinary Mordell–Weil finite generation is not yet a completed Mathlib theorem, much less Kolyvagin.

## 5.2 Kolyvagin route — **BUILD / RESEARCH-SCALE**

The implication

```text
L(f,1) ≠ 0
→ analytic rank zero
→ Mordell–Weil rank zero of A_f
```

through modularity and Kolyvagin–Logachev would require an enormous chain:

```text
newforms and attached abelian varieties
modular parametrizations
complex and p-adic height theory
Heegner points / Euler systems
Selmer groups and Galois cohomology
Kolyvagin classes and descent
rank and Sha conclusions.
```

This is **not** a sensible first route for this formalization. It is plausibly a `100k+`-line programme even after modular curves and Jacobians exist, and the mathematical dependency graph is itself a research project.

## 5.3 Mazur's Eisenstein-quotient alternative

For the rational torsion theorem one should investigate reproducing the original Eisenstein-ideal argument rather than importing Kolyvagin. This replaces the deep analytic-rank theorem by arithmetic specific to prime level:

```text
Hecke algebra and Eisenstein ideal
cuspidal and Shimura subgroups
structure of the Eisenstein quotient
specialization and component-group calculations
finiteness of the relevant rational quotient.
```

This is still large, but it is much closer to the actual `ℚ`-specific target and avoids formalizing Gross–Zagier/Kolyvagin.

A third option is to make the rank-zero/finiteness of the quotient a single narrow imported theorem:

```lean
axiom windingQuotient_rational_finite (p : ℕ) [Fact p.Prime] :
  Finite (WindingQuotient p).RationalPoints
```

and formalize the geometric/formal-immersion deduction around it. This gives a meaningful partial formalization while exposing exactly one deep arithmetic seam.

### Scale

- Kolyvagin route: **research-scale**, multiple years after prerequisites.
- Prime-level Eisenstein quotient specialized to this theorem: **L–XL**, perhaps `30k–100k` beyond modular-curve/Hecke/Jacobian foundations.
- Treat quotient finiteness as a single theorem/axiom: the surrounding formal-immersion project becomes much smaller, though still not small.

# 6. q-expansions and the formal-immersion criterion

## 6.1 Analytic q-expansions — **HAS**

As above, Mathlib has complex analytic `q`-expansions:

```lean
UpperHalfPlane.qExpansion
UpperHalfPlane.hasSum_qExpansion
UpperHalfPlane.qExpansion_coeff_unique
ModularForm.qExpansionRingHom
```

This is a real reusable block.

## 6.2 Integral q-expansion principle — **BUILD**

The formal-immersion proof needs more than a complex Taylor series. It needs an integral/local statement such as:

```text
weight-two cusp forms over ℤ_(ℓ)
↔ regular differentials on an integral model of X₀(p),
```

with the differential at the cusp represented by

```text
f(q) dq/q = (Σ a_n q^n) dq/q.
```

Missing pieces include:

- modular forms over `ℤ`, `ℤ_(ℓ)`, and finite fields;
- an integral q-expansion principle;
- base change and saturation of lattices of cusp forms;
- regular differentials on the compactified modular curve;
- identification of the cotangent space at infinity with the first q-coefficient;
- Hecke action on these integral lattices.

## 6.3 Generic formally unramified infrastructure — **PARTIAL**

Mathlib has generic algebraic geometry for formally unramified morphisms, e.g.

```text
Mathlib/AlgebraicGeometry/Morphisms/FormallyUnramified.lean
```

and extensive cotangent/smoothness infrastructure. However, a search found no packaged `FormalImmersion` declaration matching the arithmetic-geometric criterion used by Mazur/Kamienny.

Even if a generic formal-immersion predicate is added, the hard theorem is the concrete one:

```text
the map X₀(p) → J_e(p)
is a formal immersion at the cusp modulo ℓ.
```

That requires proving the appropriate cotangent map is surjective, normally by a q-expansion/Hecke/modular-symbol calculation.

## 6.4 Required local contradiction

After the cotangent calculation, one still needs the arithmetic lemma:

```text
if two ℤ_(ℓ)-sections reduce to the same smooth point,
their images in the quotient agree,
and the map is a formal immersion there,
then the two sections agree.
```

This can be built from completed local rings or infinitesimal lifting. Generic formally-unramified results help, but the exact section-equality theorem and its hypotheses need to be assembled.

### Scale

- Generic formal-immersion predicate and local section lemma: **M**, `5k–15k`.
- Integral q-expansion/differential/cotangent comparison: **L**, `20k–50k` after modular curves exist.
- Concrete uniform linear-independence theorem for the chosen quotient: **M–L**, `10k–30k`, depending on how much modular-symbol/Hecke infrastructure is already available.

# 8. Is the project “large but bounded” or “multi-year research formalization”?

## Full reusable Mathlib route

Building a polished reusable theory from the present library is a **multi-year research-formalization effort**. It is not comparable to adding one large theorem to a mature subject area.

The reason is not merely volume. Several interfaces do not yet have a settled formal design:

```text
analytic modular quotient ↔ algebraic modular curve
moduli stack/coarse scheme and compactification
curve ↔ Jacobian/Pic⁰
Hecke correspondences across forms, divisors, homology, and Jacobians
integral structures and reduction
Eisenstein/winding quotient
formal immersion at a cusp.
```

Each interface can invalidate design decisions in neighboring blocks. The project would need staged upstream Mathlib development, likely by multiple experts.

A realistic rough estimate for a reusable development is:

```text
150k–400k new Lean lines,
3–7 expert-years,
with high uncertainty and substantial upstream review time.
```

This estimate excludes a from-scratch Kolyvagin formalization. Adding that route could itself consume several additional expert-years.

## Narrow theorem-specific route

A deliberately specialized `ℚ`, prime-level, one-cusp, one-reduction-prime proof can avoid:

- general stacks of generalized elliptic curves;
- arbitrary level structures;
- a general Jacobian functor;
- general quotient abelian varieties;
- general Néron models;
- Kolyvagin, if Mazur's Eisenstein arithmetic is used or quotient finiteness is isolated.

One could build only:

```text
1. a prime-level modular-symbol complex;
2. enough moduli/Tate-curve data to attach a symbol to a rational p-torsion point;
3. an explicitly presented Eisenstein/winding quotient group;
4. a certified finite Hecke computation;
5. an integral q-expansion/cotangent certificate at infinity;
6. the formal-immersion section contradiction.
```

This is still approximately:

```text
50k–150k lines,
1–3 expert-years,
```

assuming the mathematical proof is frozen, the finite certificates are generated externally and checked by Lean, and no attempt is made to generalize every component immediately.

# 9. Possible shortcuts for the specific goal `p > 19`

## Shortcut 1: isolate the uniform theorem — recommended for the current build

Use exactly one explicit seam:

```lean
axiom no_rational_point_of_prime_order_gt_19
    {p : ℕ} [Fact p.Prime] (hp : 19 < p)
    (E : WeierstrassCurve ℚ) [E.IsElliptic] :
    ¬ HasRationalPointOfOrder E p
```

or a theorem imported from a separately audited package. Keep every other part of the Mazur `|T| ≤ 16` proof axiom-free. This honestly identifies the irreducible mountain instead of hiding it behind Mordell–Weil or an oversized “modular curves” axiom.

A good long-term refinement is to split it into two seams:

```lean
uniform_modular_point_to_cusp
winding_or_eisenstein_quotient_finite
```

so the geometric formal-immersion argument and the deep quotient-finiteness theorem can be replaced independently.

## Shortcut 2: assume only quotient finiteness, formalize the formal immersion

This is the most meaningful intermediate target:

```lean
axiom windingQuotient_finite_rational_points (p : ℕ) [Fact p.Prime] : ...
```

Then formally build:

```text
rational p-torsion point
→ integral modular point
→ reduction to the cusp
→ equal image in finite quotient
→ formal immersion
→ point is the cusp.
```

This still requires modular curves, integral q-expansions, a quotient target, and the local argument, but avoids Kolyvagin/Eisenstein finiteness. It turns the project from “several giant theories” into one very large geometric programme.

## Shortcut 3: original Eisenstein quotient rather than Kolyvagin

For `ℚ`, prime torsion, and no interest in general number fields, formalizing Mazur's Eisenstein-ideal arithmetic is more targeted than proving

```text
L(f,1) ≠ 0 → rank A_f(ℚ)=0
```

through Kolyvagin. It is still not a small shortcut; it shifts the hard work from Euler systems to Hecke/Eisenstein/cuspidal subgroup arithmetic.

## Shortcut 4: finite computations plus a uniform external bound

A theorem such as Parent's explicit degree-one bound would reduce the remaining primes to a finite list—or directly eliminate them. But **formalizing the bound is itself the uniform modular-curve theorem**. An externally computed list cannot certify all primes without some formally trusted uniform theorem.

Thus there is no route of the form

```text
check finitely many raw Tate equations
and obtain all p > 19
```

without importing a deep bound.

## Shortcut 5: rational-isogeny classification

Rational `p`-torsion implies a rational `p`-isogeny, but formalizing Mazur's prime-isogeny theorem requires essentially the same modular-curve/Eisenstein machinery and leaves exceptional isogeny primes whose fibers must still be checked. It is not an economy for this target unless the isogeny classification is independently required.

## Shortcut 6: purely local reduction arguments

There is no known elementary argument using only reductions at a fixed handful of primes that gives Mazur's prime tail for arbitrary elliptic curves over `ℚ`. Bad reduction can occur at any prescribed finite set, and the missing global restriction is precisely what the modular-curve theorem supplies. A local-only formalization would be attempting to prove a false strengthening or rediscover the deep global theorem in disguised form.

# 10. Recommended project plan

## Immediate architecture

Freeze this interface now:

```lean

theorem no_rational_point_of_prime_order_gt_19
    {p : ℕ} [Fact p.Prime] (hp : 19 < p)
    (E : WeierstrassCurve ℚ) [E.IsElliptic] :
    ¬ HasRationalPointOfOrder E p
```

Do not substitute either of these stronger and less relevant axioms:

```lean
mordell_weil_fg
mazur_torsion_classification
```

The narrow theorem states exactly what the current finite proof needs.

## Parallel research tracks

1. **Modular-symbol/Hecke track:** extend the abstract Hecke ring to certified prime-level Manin symbols and `T_n`.
2. **Integral cusp track:** build only the prime-level cusp neighborhood, Tate curve, q-parameter, and reduction needed for the formal immersion.
3. **Quotient track:** decide early between:
   - Mazur Eisenstein quotient;
   - winding quotient with one rank-zero axiom;
   - a purely modular-symbol finite quotient sufficient for the contradiction.
4. **Local algebra track:** generic formal-immersion and equality-of-sections theorem using completed local rings/cotangent spaces.
5. **Certificate track:** external generation plus Lean checking of finite matrices, Hecke relations, and mod-ℓ linear independence.

# Bottom line

Mathlib's current modular-form library is valuable, and the new abstract Hecke-ring work is encouraging. Nevertheless, the uniform `p > 19` tail sits almost entirely **above** the currently formalized layer.

The missing core is not one lemma. It is the connected arithmetic-geometric ecosystem:

```text
integral modular curves
+ modular symbols and classical Hecke action
+ modular Jacobian/quotient
+ quotient finiteness
+ integral q-expansion/cotangent comparison
+ formal immersion and specialization.
```

Therefore:

- **Full reusable proof:** genuine multi-year formalization research.
- **Narrow specialized proof:** still a major one-to-three-year project.
- **Practical current build:** isolate `no_rational_point_of_prime_order_gt_19` as the single explicit uniform seam, finish every finite case and all surrounding group theory, and return to this seam as a separate research programme.
