# Problem 2.5: why the proposed rigidity argument does not yet prove \(L=G\)

## Executive verdict

The proposed argument is **not valid as stated**.

The normalized Poincaré polynomial

\[
(\xi-1)(\xi^2-34\xi+1)
\]

and the three formal powers

\[
(\tau_+,\tau_0,\tau_-)=(0,-3,0)
\]

are important and highly suggestive, but they are only asymptotic data at one end of a **difference equation**. They do not determine the global Birkhoff connection matrix, the extension class, or the framed connection coefficient

\[
L=\frac{p_0w_+(0)}{q_0w_+(0)}.
\]

There are four independent gaps.

1. The CMF gives an additive difference module, whereas Katz rigidity concerns local systems or meromorphic differential connections on a curve. An exact functor or transform between the two modules must first be constructed.
2. Poincaré roots and formal indices are much less than the full local formal type, and they are given only at infinity. Even full formal data at infinity do not determine the global connection matrix.
3. The integrated elliptic module is a reducible, non-semisimple extension
   \[
   0\longrightarrow \mathbf 1\longrightarrow M_{\rm int}
   \longrightarrow M_K\longrightarrow0.
   \]
   Rigidity of the rank-two quotient does not determine the extension class.
4. Even an isomorphism of the unframed rank-three modules would not identify the numerical value \(Y(1)\). One must also match the normalized solution, the base point, and the numerator/denominator functionals.

There is a useful rigidity strategy, but it is a **rigidity-plus-extension-plus-framing** argument. The extension and framing are precisely where Catalan's constant occurs.

---

# 1. The category mismatch

Let the normalized CMF trajectory be written in row convention as

\[
X_{n+1}=X_nA(n),
\qquad A(n)\in\operatorname{GL}_3(\mathbf C(n)).
\]

Its formal solutions at \(n=\infty\) have Birkhoff--Trjitzinsky form

\[
W_i(n)\sim \xi_i^n n^{\tau_i}
\left(v_i+\frac{v_{i,1}}n+\frac{v_{i,2}}{n^2}+\cdots\right).
\]

The numbers \(\xi_i\) and \(\tau_i\) describe the first part of the formal classification at infinity. They do **not** specify the canonical solution evaluated at a finite point. In particular,

\[
w_+(0)
\]

is a global connection datum. It depends on transporting the canonical dominant solution from infinity to the initial point through the complete recurrence.

This can be seen without any sophisticated theory: two discrete systems can agree for every sufficiently large integer \(n\), and hence have identical formal asymptotics to every order at infinity, while differing in one finite transition matrix. Their values of \(w_+(0)\), and therefore their connection coefficients, are generally different.

In analytic difference-equation language, local formal solutions must be supplemented by a Birkhoff connection matrix or analogous global analytic data. The exponents are not the full classifying object. The same phenomenon appears explicitly in the regular-singular \(q\)-difference theory, where the classifying data consist of exponents together with a connection matrix; see Sauloy's work cited below. The additive-shift problem requires its own difference-module version of this statement.

Katz's theorem cannot be applied directly to

\[
X_{n+1}=X_nA(n).
\]

One must first construct an exact meromorphic differential connection from the CMF, for example by a generating-function, Mellin, Borel, or other certified transform, and prove that this transform preserves the relevant solution and framing.

# 2. What Katz rigidity actually requires

Let \(U=\mathbf P^1\setminus S\), let \(j:U\hookrightarrow\mathbf P^1\), and let \(\mathcal M\) be a rank-\(r\) regular-singular local system.

For a tame local system the rigidity index is

\[
\operatorname{rig}(\mathcal M)
:=\chi\!\left(\mathbf P^1,j_*\operatorname{End}(\mathcal M)\right)
=(2-|S|)r^2+
\sum_{s\in S}\dim Z(M_s),
\]

where \(M_s\) is the local monodromy and \(Z(M_s)\) is its centralizer.

If \(\mathcal M\) is irreducible, then

\[
H^0\cong H^2\cong\mathbf C,
\]

so

\[
\operatorname{rig}(\mathcal M)
=2-\dim H^1\!\left(\mathbf P^1,j_*\operatorname{End}(\mathcal M)\right).
\]

Thus an irreducible regular-singular system is cohomologically rigid exactly when

\[
H^1\!\left(\mathbf P^1,j_*\operatorname{End}(\mathcal M)\right)=0,
\]

or equivalently when its rigidity index is \(2\).

The local data that must be prescribed are the conjugacy classes of the local monodromies at **every** singular point, including their full Jordan forms. Eigenvalues at one point are not enough.

For an irregular meromorphic connection the analogous hypotheses are stricter:

- the connection must be irreducible for the standard Katz--Bloch--Esnault--Arinkin theorem;
- the complete formal isomorphism class must be fixed at every singularity;
- this includes slopes, ramification, exponential factors, formal monodromy, and Jordan data;
- one must prove
  \[
  H^1_{\rm dR}\!\left(\mathbf P^1,j_{!*}\operatorname{End}(\mathcal M)\right)=0.
  \]

Rigidity means that the Stokes and global connection data are then forced by the full collection of local formal types. It is not a consequence of merely matching leading eigenvalues and powers at infinity.

Arinkin states the relevant definition explicitly: an irreducible connection is rigid when it is determined by the formal types of **all** its singularities. Bloch and Esnault prove the corresponding cohomological criterion for meromorphic connections.

# 3. The natural integrated elliptic module is not rigid on the \(k\)-line

The complete elliptic integral satisfies

\[
k(1-k^2)K''+(1-3k^2)K'-kK=0.
\tag{3.1}
\]

If

\[
Y'(k)=K(k),
\]

then \(Y\) satisfies

\[
\boxed{
 k(1-k^2)Y'''+(1-3k^2)Y''-kY'=0.}
\tag{3.2}
\]

This is a regular-singular rank-three equation on

\[
U=\mathbf P^1_k\setminus\{0,1,-1,\infty\}.
\]

Its local Frobenius data are easy to read directly from (3.2).

- At \(k=0,1,-1\), the indicial roots are
  \[
  0,1,1,
  \]
  and the logarithmic local monodromy has Jordan type
  \[
  J_2\oplus J_1.
  \]
- At \(k=\infty\), the three solutions have logarithmic degrees \(0,1,2\), and the unipotent monodromy has Jordan type
  \[
  J_3.
  \]

For a \(3\times3\) matrix of Jordan type \((2,1)\), the centralizer has dimension \(5\). For type \((3)\), it has dimension \(3\). Therefore

\[
\begin{aligned}
\operatorname{rig}(M_{\rm int})
&=(2-4)\cdot 3^2
 +3\cdot5+3\\
&=-18+18\\
&=\boxed{0}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{3.3}
\]

This already rules out the proposed direct Katz-rigidity argument in the natural \(k\)-coordinate. Since the endomorphism local system has at least the scalar global endomorphism, Poincaré duality gives

\[
\dim H^1\ge2.
\]

So the natural integrated module is not cohomologically rigid.

There is also a more elementary obstruction: \(M_{\rm int}\) contains the constant solution and is reducible. The standard Katz and Arinkin reduction theorems are stated for irreducible rigid connections.

## 3.1 Where the rigid elliptic system does appear

Put

\[
x=k^2,
\qquad \theta=x\frac d{dx}.
\]

Then

\[
K(\sqrt x)
=\frac\pi2\,{}_2F_1\!\left(\frac12,\frac12;1;x\right)
\]

is annihilated by the rigid Gauss operator

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal L_K=\theta^2-x\left(\theta+\frac12\right)^2.}
\tag{3.4}
\]

The normalized primitive is

\[
\boxed{
\frac{Y(\sqrt x)}{\sqrt x}
=\frac\pi4\,{}_3F_2\!\left(
\frac12,\frac12,\frac12;
1,\frac32;x
\right).}
\tag{3.5}
\]

Its hypergeometric operator is

\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal L_{\rm int}
&=\theta^2\left(\theta+\frac12\right)
-x\left(\theta+\frac12\right)^3\\
&=\boxed{
\left[\theta^2-x\left(\theta+\frac12\right)^2\right]
\left(\theta+\frac12\right).}
\end{aligned}
\tag{3.6}
\]

Equation (3.6) displays the extension structure explicitly. The rank-two quotient is the rigid Gauss module, while the right factor is the rank-one Kummer/constant direction after the quadratic descent.

It is possible that the descended rank-three hypergeometric object is physically rigid once its **complete** local monodromy types at \(0,1,\infty\) are fixed. That would help identify an unframed differential module. It still would not justify an identification from the CMF Poincaré polynomial and the three powers alone.

# 4. The extension class is extra data

Choose a basis adapted to

\[
0\longrightarrow\mathbf1\longrightarrow M_{\rm int}
\longrightarrow M_K\longrightarrow0.
\]

The monodromy matrices then have block form

\[
\widetilde\rho(\gamma)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&c_\gamma\\
0&\rho_K(\gamma)
\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{4.1}
\]

The row vectors \(c_\gamma\) form a group-cohomology cocycle. Changing the splitting changes \(c_\gamma\) by a coboundary. Thus the extension is measured by a class

\[
\boxed{
[c]\in
\operatorname{Ext}^1(M_K,\mathbf1)
\simeq H^1_{\rm dR}(U,M_K^\vee),}
\tag{4.2}
\]

with the precise version depending on the chosen regular-singular or parabolic category.

The local eigenvalues and the formal powers of the three asymptotic modes do not determine this class. Rigidity of \(M_K\) fixes the quotient, not the cocycle \(c\).

This is exactly where the integrated period lives. Analytically, the cocycle entries are period integrals of elliptic solutions along loops and relative paths. Catalan's constant is a relative period of this extension, not an eigenvalue of the semisimplification.

# 5. An unframed module isomorphism still does not imply \(L=G\)

Suppose, much more strongly than is currently known, that one proved an abstract isomorphism

\[
M_{\rm CMF}\simeq M_{\rm int}.
\]

That would still not identify the displayed connection coefficient.

The value

\[
Y(1)=G
\]

uses the following framing:

1. the holomorphic elliptic solution is normalized as
   \[
   K(0)=\frac\pi2;
   \]
2. the primitive is normalized by
   \[
   Y(0)=0;
   \]
3. the path from \(0\) to \(1\) is fixed;
4. the factor \(1/2\) in
   \[
   Y(k)=\frac12\int_0^kK(s)\,ds
   \]
   is fixed.

The differential module by itself does not select the horizontal section \(Y\). Replacing a primitive by

\[
Y\longmapsto Y+C
\]

leaves the same differential equation but changes \(Y(1)\). Similarly, rescaling the elliptic solution rescales its primitive.

On the CMF side, the number

\[
L=\frac{p_0w_+(0)}{q_0w_+(0)}
\]

depends on the two initial row functionals, the normalization of the dominant canonical solution, and the connection from infinity to \(n=0\). These are framed data, not invariants of the abstract module.

Therefore the implication

\[
M_{\rm CMF}\simeq M_{\rm int}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad L=G
\]

is false without an explicit compatibility of framings.

# 6. A valid rigidity-based proof strategy

Rigidity can still reduce the amount of computation. A complete proof could follow the steps below.

## Step 1: construct the exact differential connection attached to the CMF

Starting from the fully normalized recurrence, derive a minimal differential operator or first-order connection

\[
\frac{dZ}{dz}=A_{\rm CMF}(z)Z
\]

by an exact generating-function, Mellin, Borel, or Hadamard transform. One must prove that the two CMF rows and the dominant Birkhoff solution map to specified solutions of this connection.

Matching only the principal polynomial

\[
(1-z)(1-34z+z^2)
\]

does not perform this step.

## Step 2: compute every local type

List every singular point of \(A_{\rm CMF}\), including apparent singularities, and compute:

- local exponents;
- Jordan blocks and logarithmic ranks;
- irregular slopes and exponential factors, if any;
- determinant and local monodromy conjugacy classes.

These must be compared with the complete local data of the descended integrated elliptic module, not merely with the three modes at infinity.

## Step 3: identify the rigid quotient

Prove an exact quotient isomorphism

\[
M_{\rm CMF}/\mathbf1\simeq M_K.
\]

Here Katz rigidity of the Gauss module can be genuinely useful: once the three local monodromy classes on \(\mathbf P^1\setminus\{0,1,\infty\}\) are matched and irreducibility is checked, the rank-two quotient is forced.

## Step 4: identify the extension class

Compute

\[
\operatorname{Ext}^1(M_K,\mathbf1)
\]

in the relevant category and determine the class of both extensions. In monodromy language, compute the cocycles \(c_\gamma\) in (4.1) and prove that they differ by a coboundary.

If the admissible extension space is one-dimensional, rigidity may reduce this comparison to one nonzero scalar. But that scalar must still be determined from a residue, a boundary map, a local logarithmic coefficient, or an exact period normalization. It cannot be read from the Poincaré roots.

## Step 5: match the framing

Finally prove that the CMF numerator and denominator rows correspond to the normalized de Rham/Betti data

\[
K(0)=\frac\pi2,
\qquad Y(0)=0,
\]

and that the CMF endpoint corresponds to \(k=1\). Only then does

\[
L=Y(1)=G
\]

follow.

## Equivalent finite certificates

Any one of the following would close the argument.

1. A rational differential gauge \(U(z)\) satisfying
   \[
   A_{\rm CMF}
   =U A_{\rm int}U^{-1}+U'U^{-1},
   \]
   together with exact initial-vector matching.
2. Equality of the full monodromy representations, including the extension cocycle, plus the framing at the base point.
3. Equality of the rank-three scalar differential operators after explicit pullback/gauge, followed by equality of three initial jets.
4. A one-dimensional \(\operatorname{Ext}^1\) calculation, equality of one nonzero local extension invariant, and exact normalization of the chosen primitive.

These are genuine rigidity-style certificates. They are substantially stronger than equality of the Poincaré polynomial and formal indices.

# 7. Direct answers to the three proposed steps

## Claim 1

> The Poincaré polynomial and formal indices determine the Birkhoff normal form up to finitely many choices.

**False.** They specify only leading formal asymptotics at infinity. They do not determine the full formal series, finite singularity data, Birkhoff connection matrix, or analytic connection coefficient.

## Claim 2

> The integrated elliptic module has the same Poincaré polynomial and formal indices.

Even if this has been verified exactly, it is only a necessary spectral compatibility. It is not an isomorphism criterion.

## Claim 3

> Katz rigidity then makes the two modules isomorphic and implies \(L=G\).

**False as stated.** Katz rigidity does not directly apply to the additive difference module; the natural integrated module on the \(k\)-line has rigidity index \(0\), not \(2\); the rank-three object is reducible; the extension class is additional data; and an unframed module isomorphism does not determine the value of a selected period.

The correct conclusion is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{Rigidity may identify the rank-two elliptic quotient, but the extension class and framing must still be computed.}}
\]

The 200-digit agreement is compelling evidence that those data match, but it is not replaced by the proposed rigidity argument.
