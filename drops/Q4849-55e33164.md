# Problem 2.5: what the exact moment lift proves—and what it does not

## Executive verdict

The existing moment formulas are exact, useful, and **not a Padé certificate**. In fact, once one writes

\[
R_{N,j}(s)=a_{N,j}+b_{N,j}s,
\]

the three formulas reduce to the two coefficient identities

\[
Q_{N,j}=a_{N,j}-b_{N,j},
\qquad
P_{N,j}=-b_{N,j}.
\]

Consequently

\[
\boxed{R_{N,j}(s)=Q_{N,j}-(1+s)P_{N,j}.}
\tag{1}
\]

With

\[
d\mu(s)=\frac{-\log s}{4\sqrt s}\,ds,
\]

one has

\[
\int_0^1d\mu(s)=1,
\qquad
\int_0^1\frac{d\mu(s)}{1+s}=G.
\]

Therefore

\[
\begin{aligned}
\int_0^1\frac{R_{N,j}(s)}{1+s}\,d\mu(s)
&=Q_{N,j}\int_0^1\frac{d\mu(s)}{1+s}
-P_{N,j}\int_0^1d\mu(s)\\
&=GQ_{N,j}-P_{N,j}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{2}
\]

Equation (2) holds for **arbitrary numbers** \(P_{N,j},Q_{N,j}\). It uses no recurrence, no orthogonality, and no special property of the challenge matrix. Thus Theorem 2 is a fixed two-dimensional moment realization of the linear form \(GQ-P\), not an increasing-order Padé construction.

The main conclusions are:

1. No fixed algebraic change of variable can turn the degree-one polynomial \(R_{N,j}\) into a genuine degree-\(N\) Padé denominator. A separate spectral lift or constant-term integral representation is required.
2. The displayed \(2\times3\) array is not, by itself, a Hermite–Padé system. A genuine type-II interpretation would require explicit Markov functions or measures and growing families of multiple-orthogonality conditions.
3. Poincaré roots determine the possible asymptotic scales, but not which scales occur for the particular initial rows. The missing statement is the annihilation of the leading mode in \(GQ-P\).
4. For the raw degree-pattern \((28,21,14,7)\) recurrence, a nonzero linear form \(GQ_N-P_N\) is not expected to tend to zero absolutely: every formal mode contains factorial-order-seven growth. The correct target is
   \[
   \frac{GQ_N-P_N}{Q_N}\to0,
   \]
   or decay after division by the common Pochhammer twist.
5. The smallest honest additional certificate is either:
   - a dominant-mode cancellation/stable-subspace certificate;
   - a true scalar or multiple-orthogonality certificate of growing degree;
   - or an \(N\)-dependent integral kernel whose phase directly bounds the normalized error.

So the moment lift is an exact bookkeeping identity, but it does not yet close convergence.

# 1. Exact reduction of the three moment formulas

Fix one column and suppress the index \(j\). Write

\[
R_N(s)=a_N+b_Ns.
\tag{3}
\]

The first moment formula gives

\[
\begin{aligned}
Q_N
&=\int_0^1(10-18s)(a_N+b_Ns)\,ds\\
&=a_N\left(10-\frac{18}{2}\right)
+b_N\left(\frac{10}{2}-\frac{18}{3}\right)\\
&=a_N-b_N.
\end{aligned}
\tag{4}
\]

The second gives

\[
\begin{aligned}
P_N
&=\int_0^1(6-12s)(a_N+b_Ns)\,ds\\
&=a_N\left(6-\frac{12}{2}\right)
+b_N\left(\frac{6}{2}-\frac{12}{3}\right)\\
&=-b_N.
\end{aligned}
\tag{5}
\]

Hence

\[
b_N=-P_N,
\qquad
a_N=Q_N-P_N,
\]

which proves (1).

This can also be read directly from the initial row:

\[
R_0(s)=q_0-(1+s)p_0.
\]

Indeed,

\[
q_0-p_0=(2829,-3028,760)
\]

and

\[
-p_0=(-30921,32972,-8240),
\]

so the displayed

\[
R_0(s)=(2829-30921s,-3028+32972s,760-8240s)
\]

is exactly \(q_0-(1+s)p_0\). Since right multiplication by \(M_N\) is independent of \(s\),

\[
\boxed{
R_N(s)=R_0(s)M_N=q_N-(1+s)p_N.
}
\tag{6}
\]

Thus all three moment formulas are consequences of (6) and two elementary integrals.

## 1.1 The Stieltjes measure calculation

Set \(s=t^2\). Then

\[
dt=\frac{ds}{2\sqrt s},
\qquad
-\log t=-\frac12\log s,
\]

so

\[
\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}R_N(t^2)\,dt
=
\int_0^1\frac{R_N(s)}{1+s}\,d\mu(s),
\]

where

\[
\boxed{
d\mu(s)=\frac{-\log s}{4\sqrt s}\,ds.}
\tag{7}
\]

Its moments are

\[
\int_0^1s^m\,d\mu(s)
=\frac1{(2m+1)^2}.
\tag{8}
\]

In particular,

\[
\mu([0,1])=1.
\tag{9}
\]

Moreover,

\[
\int_0^1\frac{d\mu(s)}{1+s}
=
\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}\,dt
=G.
\tag{10}
\]

Equations (1), (9), and (10) prove (2).

## 1.2 Functional form of the tautology

On the two-dimensional space \(\mathbb Q[s]_{\le1}\), define

\[
\mathcal L_Q(R)=\int_0^1(10-18s)R(s)\,ds,
\]

\[
\mathcal L_P(R)=\int_0^1(6-12s)R(s)\,ds,
\]

and

\[
\mathcal L_G(R)=\int_0^1\frac{R(s)}{1+s}\,d\mu(s).
\]

On the basis \((1,s)\),

\[
\mathcal L_Q=(1,-1),
\qquad
\mathcal L_P=(0,-1),
\]

and

\[
\mathcal L_G=(G,1-G).
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{\mathcal L_G=G\mathcal L_Q-\mathcal L_P}
\tag{11}
\]

on all linear polynomials. The theorem is precisely (11) applied to \(R_N\).

This observation is decisive: the same construction works for any pair of scalar sequences \(P_N,Q_N\), regardless of whether their ratio converges.

# 2. Why this is not classical Padé orthogonality

For comparison, let

\[
\widehat\mu(z)=\int_0^1\frac{d\mu(s)}{z-s},
\qquad z\notin[0,1].
\]

A genuine denominator \(\pi_n\) of the \([n-1/n]\) Padé approximant is a degree-\(n\) polynomial satisfying

\[
\boxed{
\int_0^1s^k\pi_n(s)\,d\mu(s)=0,
\qquad k=0,\ldots,n-1.}
\tag{12}
\]

If

\[
p_{n-1}(z)
=
\int_0^1
\frac{\pi_n(z)-\pi_n(s)}{z-s}\,d\mu(s),
\]

then the exact remainder is

\[
\boxed{
\pi_n(z)\widehat\mu(z)-p_{n-1}(z)
=
\int_0^1\frac{\pi_n(s)}{z-s}\,d\mu(s).}
\tag{13}
\]

After the usual reciprocal-polynomial change, this is the standard Stieltjes Padé remainder for

\[
\mathcal C(z)=\int_0^1\frac{d\mu(s)}{1+zs}.
\]

The load-bearing feature is not merely the integral in (13). It is the growing family of \(n\) orthogonality conditions (12). Those conditions force cancellation of the first \(n\) moments and control zeros, signs, and asymptotics.

For the challenge lift:

- \(\deg R_N=1\) for every \(N\);
- there is no increasing list of vanished moments;
- neither \(\mathcal L_Q(R_N)\) nor \(\mathcal L_P(R_N)\) is an orthogonality condition;
- the remainder identity holds before the recurrence is mentioned.

Thus the resemblance to a Padé remainder is formal but not structural.

A useful diagnostic is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{A Padé certificate must contain information whose dimension grows with }N.
}
\tag{14}
\]

The current degree-one moment space has fixed dimension two.

# 3. Can the degree-one polynomial be converted into a degree-\(N\) polynomial?

## 3.1 Not by a fixed change of variable

Let \(s=\varphi(x)\) be any fixed rational or algebraic substitution independent of \(N\). Then

\[
R_N(\varphi(x))=a_N+b_N\varphi(x)
\]

has numerator and denominator degrees bounded independently of \(N\). It cannot become a polynomial of degree \(N\).

Multiplying by an artificial factor such as \(x^{N-1}\) produces a degree-\(N\) expression, but it changes the moment problem and supplies no orthogonality. An \(N\)-dependent substitution can manufacture arbitrary degree and is likewise not a certificate.

Therefore the literal \(R_N\) is not a hidden scalar Padé denominator.

## 3.2 A spectral lift is possible, but it is extra structure

Normalize a scalar order-three recurrence to

\[
u_{n+3}+a_nu_{n+2}+b_nu_{n+1}+d_nu_n=0.
\tag{15}
\]

Choose a fixed spectral point \(x_*\), and define a monic polynomial family by a four-term recurrence

\[
\boxed{
\Pi_{n+3}(x)
=
\bigl(x-x_*-a_n\bigr)\Pi_{n+2}(x)
-b_n\Pi_{n+1}(x)-d_n\Pi_n(x).
}
\tag{16}
\]

At \(x=x_*\), equation (16) reduces to (15). With suitable initial polynomials, a scalar challenge solution can therefore be realized as an evaluation \(\Pi_n(x_*)\).

This is a formal **2-orthogonal polynomial candidate**: a four-term recurrence is the natural recurrence for a type-II multiple orthogonal polynomial associated with two linear functionals.

But (16) alone does not provide:

- positive measures;
- a Nikishin system;
- normality or perfectness;
- zero location on real intervals;
- or a Markov convergence theorem.

A generalized Favard theorem can produce abstract linear functionals under nondegeneracy assumptions, but arbitrary functionals do not give the analytic convergence needed here. One must identify the actual measures and prove the relevant positivity/Nikishin conditions.

## 3.3 The Schläfli/constant-term carrier is another possible lift

The central Delannoy number has the coefficient representation

\[
D_N=[w^N]\left(\frac{w^2+6w+8}{2}\right)^N.
\tag{17}
\]

Thus a genuine degree-\(2N\) polynomial already exists before coefficient extraction. Squaring gives a two-variable constant-term carrier for \(D_N^2\).

To use this for the printed CMF one needs a new exact identity of the form

\[
\boxed{
R_{N,j}(s)
=
\operatorname{CT}_{u,v}
\left[
\Phi(u,v)^N\,\mathcal P_{N,j}(u,v,s)
\right],}
\tag{18}
\]

or an equivalent contour integral whose integrand has an explicit \(N\)-dependent phase. Equation (18) would expose the hidden degree and could lead to AZ or steepest-descent bounds.

No such identity follows from the degree-one formula (6). Producing it is essentially the matrix-valued AZ certificate sought in Q4821/Q4826.

## 3.4 A Lax/spectral-parameter certificate

A third route is to find matrices \(\mathcal M(n,x)\), polynomial in \(x\), and a gauge \(U(n)\) such that

\[
U(n)^{-1}M(n)U(n+1)=\mathcal M(n,x_*)
\tag{19}
\]

for a fixed \(x_*\), while the product

\[
\mathcal M(0,x)\cdots\mathcal M(N-1,x)
\]

generates degree-\(N\) polynomial entries. This is the transfer-matrix version of (16).

Again, the spectral deformation \(\mathcal M(n,x)\) is additional data. It is not determined by the matrix at one value \(x=x_*\).

# 4. What a genuine Hermite–Padé interpretation would require

The fact that the initial matrix is \(2\times3\) does not by itself make the construction Hermite–Padé. It means only that two row functionals are being applied to a rank-three solution space.

For two Markov functions

\[
\widehat\mu_1(z)=\int\frac{d\mu_1(s)}{z-s},
\qquad
\widehat\mu_2(z)=\int\frac{d\mu_2(s)}{z-s},
\]

a type-II Hermite–Padé denominator for a multi-index

\[
\mathbf n=(n_1,n_2)
\]

is a polynomial \(\Pi_{\mathbf n}\) of degree at most \(|\mathbf n|=n_1+n_2\), together with numerators \(P_{\mathbf n,1},P_{\mathbf n,2}\), such that

\[
\Pi_{\mathbf n}(z)\widehat\mu_j(z)-P_{\mathbf n,j}(z)
=O(z^{-n_j-1})
\qquad(z\to\infty).
\tag{20}
\]

Equivalently,

\[
\boxed{
\int s^k\Pi_{\mathbf n}(s)\,d\mu_j(s)=0,
\qquad
k=0,\ldots,n_j-1,\quad j=1,2.}
\tag{21}
\]

On a step line such as

\[
(n_1,n_2)=(m,m)
\quad\text{or}\quad(m+1,m),
\]

the denominators satisfy a four-term recurrence, and a three-dimensional transfer system is natural. This is the genuine rank match with the challenge.

To invoke Nikishin theory one would therefore need to exhibit:

1. two explicit measures \(\mu_1,\mu_2\);
2. the exact multi-index assigned to each \(N\);
3. a polynomial \(\Pi_{\mathbf n}\) satisfying (21);
4. identities expressing the CMF entries as evaluations of \(\Pi_{\mathbf n}\) and its second-kind functions;
5. proof that the measures form a perfect Nikishin system or another class with a suitable convergence theorem.

The existing formulas supply none of the growing orthogonality conditions (21).

## 4.1 Why the three columns are not automatically three functions

All three column ratios approximate the same scalar constant. The columns may simply be three contiguous channels or three evaluations of one transfer system. In a type-II Hermite–Padé problem, by contrast, one common denominator simultaneously approximates two or more nonconstant functions.

A plausible integrated-elliptic pair would be

\[
F(z)=\sum_{n\ge0}D_n^2z^n
\]

and a primitive/normal function built from \(F\). But the measures and approximation conditions must be written explicitly before a Nikishin theorem applies.

## 4.2 Matrix-valued Padé

One may also approximate a matrix Markov function

\[
\mathcal F(z)=\int\frac{W(s)}{z-s}\,ds
\]

by matrix polynomials. The corresponding conditions are matrix orthogonality relations such as

\[
\int \Pi_N(s)W(s)s^k\,ds=0,
\qquad k=0,\ldots,N-1.
\tag{22}
\]

But the challenge's rectangular \(2\times3\) numerical output is not itself a square matrix polynomial or a matrix-valued measure. An explicit realization must first be supplied.

# 5. Why the Poincaré roots do not prove cancellation

Let

\[
R_N(s)=a_N+b_Ns,
\]

so that

\[
Q_N=a_N-b_N,
\qquad
P_N=-b_N,
\qquad
E_N:=GQ_N-P_N=Ga_N+(1-G)b_N.
\tag{23}
\]

After removing the common factorial/Pochhammer twist, the normalized characteristic roots are

\[
\lambda_+=17+12\sqrt2,
\qquad
\lambda_0=1,
\qquad
\lambda_-=17-12\sqrt2.
\tag{24}
\]

Suppose, for illustration, that the leading mode present in \(a_N,b_N\) is \(\Phi_+(N)\), and write

\[
a_N=A_+\Phi_+(N)+\text{smaller modes},
\]

\[
b_N=B_+\Phi_+(N)+\text{smaller modes}.
\]

Then

\[
Q_N=(A_+-B_+)\Phi_+(N)+\cdots,
\]

\[
P_N=-B_+\Phi_+(N)+\cdots,
\]

and

\[
E_N=\bigl(GA_++(1-G)B_+\bigr)\Phi_+(N)+\cdots.
\tag{25}
\]

The dominant mode disappears from \(E_N\) if and only if

\[
\boxed{GA_++(1-G)B_+=0.}
\tag{26}
\]

Provided \(A_+\ne B_+\), equation (26) is equivalent to

\[
\boxed{
-\frac{B_+}{A_+-B_+}=G.}
\tag{27}
\]

But the left side of (27) is exactly the limiting ratio \(P_N/Q_N\). Thus proving dominant cancellation in the error is not a consequence of the desired theorem; it is an equivalent formulation of it.

The characteristic roots determine \(\Phi_+,\Phi_0,\Phi_-\). They do not determine the connection amplitudes \(A_+,B_+\), which depend on every finite coefficient and on the initial rows.

## 5.1 If only one coefficient contains the dominant mode

The proposed scenario in which one coefficient of \(R_N\) is dominant and the other is purely subdominant cannot produce Catalan's constant.

- If \(A_+\ne0\) and \(B_+=0\), then \(P_N/Q_N\to0\).
- If \(A_+=0\) and \(B_+\ne0\), then \(P_N/Q_N\to1\).

Since \(0<G<1\) and \(G\ne0,1\), both coefficients must share the relevant leading mode, with the specific cancellation (26).

The same analysis applies if the initial rows happen to annihilate \(\lambda_+\) and their leading mode is \(\lambda_0\). One simply replaces \(+\) by the first mode actually present. The observed ratio

\[
17-12\sqrt2
\]

cannot distinguish these two possibilities, because

\[
\frac{\lambda_0}{\lambda_+}
=
\frac{\lambda_-}{\lambda_0}
=17-12\sqrt2.
\tag{28}
\]

So even the exact convergence rate does not identify which asymptotic coefficients vanish.

## 5.2 A triangle inequality cannot see the needed cancellation

From the positive measure,

\[
|E_N|
\le
\int_0^1\frac{|R_N(s)|}{1+s}\,d\mu(s)
\le
G\,\|R_N\|_{L^\infty[0,1]}.
\tag{29}
\]

But \(\|R_N\|_\infty\) is controlled by the same dominant coefficients as \(P_N,Q_N\). Estimate (29) therefore gives, at best, the dominant growth scale. The small error comes from signed cancellation between the constant and linear terms of \(R_N\), and absolute values destroy it.

Indeed,

\[
\frac{R_N(s)}{Q_N}
=1-\frac{P_N}{Q_N}(1+s).
\tag{30}
\]

If \(P_N/Q_N\to G\), the limiting polynomial is

\[
1-G(1+s),
\]

which is not small on \([0,1]\); it changes sign once. Its weighted integral vanishes. Thus the desired mechanism is cancellation of a nonzero limiting shape, not pointwise decay of the integrand.

# 6. The raw linear form should not be expected to tend to zero

The original scalar recurrence has Newton slope seven. Every nonzero formal mode has

\[
\frac{u_{N+1}}{u_N}
\sim cN^7
\qquad(c\ne0).
\tag{31}
\]

Hence the magnitude of a nonzero pure mode eventually increases superexponentially. Even the smallest raw Poincaré root is multiplied by \(N^7\).

Therefore the natural statement is not

\[
GQ_N-P_N\to0.
\]

The natural statement is

\[
\boxed{
\frac{GQ_N-P_N}{Q_N}\to0.}
\tag{32}
\]

Equivalently, divide by the common hypergeometric twist

\[
H_N=(-16)^N(2)_N^2(3)_N^2
\left(\frac52\right)_N
\left(\frac72\right)_N^2.
\tag{33}
\]

After this normalization the three exponential roots are (24). If, for example,

\[
\frac{Q_N}{H_N}\sim C_Q\lambda_+^N
\]

and

\[
\frac{E_N}{H_N}=O(N^{-3}),
\]

then

\[
\boxed{
\frac{E_N}{Q_N}
=O\!\left(N^{-3}(17-12\sqrt2)^N\right).}
\tag{34}
\]

This is the type of bound that would complete convergence. The moment identity does not prove either asymptotic assignment.

# 7. Minimal additional certificates that are sufficient

The full matrix AZ certificate is not logically necessary. Any one of the following would close the gap.

## 7.1 Dominant-mode annihilation certificate

After the twist (33) and a balancing gauge, prove

\[
\widehat Q_N=C_Q\lambda_d^NN^\alpha(1+O(N^{-1})),
\qquad C_Q\ne0,
\tag{35}
\]

and

\[
\widehat E_N=O(\lambda_s^NN^\beta),
\qquad |\lambda_s|<|\lambda_d|.
\tag{36}
\]

Then

\[
\frac{E_N}{Q_N}
=O\!\left(
N^{\beta-\alpha}
\left|\frac{\lambda_s}{\lambda_d}\right|^N
\right).
\tag{37}
\]

The missing finite statement is that the dominant connection coefficient of \(E_N\) is zero and that of \(Q_N\) is nonzero.

This can be certified by:

- an explicit adjoint/Casoratian projector onto the dominant mode;
- a block-triangular gauge with the error row in the stable block;
- or a rigorously computed connection matrix between \(N=0\) and infinity.

The projector need not be rational, but its defining integral or special-function value must be exact.

## 7.2 Generating-function analyticity certificate

Let

\[
\widehat E_N=E_N/H_N,
\qquad
\widehat Q_N=Q_N/H_N.
\]

It is enough to prove that the generating function of \(\widehat E_N\) is analytic in a disk strictly larger than the disk of convergence of the dominant denominator generating function. For example, analyticity of the error through the first elliptic singularity while the denominator has a singularity at

\[
\rho=17-12\sqrt2
\]

would force the required coefficient separation.

This is the differential/generating-function version of the stable-mode certificate.

## 7.3 True Padé or multiple orthogonality

Produce a degree-growing polynomial \(\Pi_N\) and prove either

\[
\int s^k\Pi_N(s)\,d\mu(s)=0,
\qquad k<N,
\tag{38}
\]

or the two-measure conditions (21). Then identify the CMF numerators and denominators with the corresponding first- and second-kind functions at a fixed exterior point.

Markov's theorem or its Nikishin extension then supplies convergence and usually the sign and rate of the error.

## 7.4 An \(N\)-dependent Beukers/AZ kernel

Find

\[
\boxed{
\frac{E_N}{H_N}
=
\int_\Omega \Phi(x)^N W_N(x)\,dx}
\tag{39}
\]

with a rigorously bounded phase and moderate numerator growth. If

\[
\sup_{x\in\Omega}|\Phi(x)|<|\lambda_d|,
\]

then (37) follows directly.

The current formula is not of this form because all \(N\)-dependence sits in two coefficients of a degree-one polynomial. A matrix AZ certificate would manufacture precisely the missing \(N\)-dependent phase representation.

## 7.5 A sign-definite Padé error formula

An exact formula such as

\[
G-\frac{P_N}{Q_N}
=
\frac{1}{Q_N^2}
\int \frac{\Pi_N(s)^2}{1+s}\,d\mu(s)
\tag{40}
\]

would be ideal. It gives positivity and convergence once orthogonal-polynomial asymptotics are known. But obtaining (40) again requires a true degree-\(N\) orthogonal polynomial; it cannot be deduced from the linear \(R_N\).

# 8. A practical next computation

The lowest-cost decisive test is a spectral/multiple-orthogonality lift, not another numerical integral.

## Test A: construct a four-term polynomial recurrence

From the normalized scalar recurrence, construct \(\Pi_N(x)\) using (16). Check whether the three challenge columns are, after fixed gauges and shifts,

\[
\Pi_N(x_*),
\qquad
\Pi_{N+1}(x_*),
\qquad
\Pi_{N+2}(x_*),
\]

or associated-polynomial values.

## Test B: recover candidate functionals

Compute the first moments of the formal 2-orthogonal functionals produced by the recurrence. Test whether they match recognizable positive measures related to

\[
\frac{-\log s}{4\sqrt s}\,ds
\]

and the Brafman elliptic measure.

## Test C: exact mode selection

Using the full normalized order-three recurrence, compute high-precision formal bases for the three modes and reconstruct the dominant amplitude of the exact rational initial rows. Then seek an exact special-function expression for the amplitude ratio. Numerics alone are reconnaissance, but this reveals whether the denominator uses the \(\lambda_+\) mode and the error uses \(\lambda_0\), or whether the initial plane kills \(\lambda_+\).

## Test D: normalized error generating function

Generate exact coefficients of

\[
\widehat E_N=(GQ_N-P_N)/H_N
\]

symbolically as a linear form in \(1,G\). Guess its minimal differential equation or identify it as the integrated elliptic solution. A certified order-three differential factor and initial-value match would close the proof without a full matrix AZ computation.

# 9. Direct answers

## 9.1 Does the remainder formula already give Padé convergence?

No. It is the universal identity

\[
R_N(s)=Q_N-(1+s)P_N
\]

inserted into two fixed moments. It holds for arbitrary \(P_N,Q_N\).

## 9.2 Can the displayed \(R_N\) be made degree \(N\)?

Not by any fixed change of variable. A true degree-\(N\) object requires a new spectral lift, multiple-orthogonal recurrence, or constant-term/AZ representation.

## 9.3 Is a matrix/Hermite–Padé proof possible?

Yes in principle. For two measures, a step-line type-II system naturally has a four-term recurrence and a three-dimensional transfer matrix. The required certificate is the explicit orthogonality (21) and an identification of the challenge entries with its second-kind functions. The mere \(2\times3\) shape does not establish this.

## 9.4 Do the moment identity and Poincaré roots prove decay?

No. They identify possible rates but not the connection amplitudes. The exact condition that removes the leading mode is equation (26), which is equivalent to the desired limiting value.

If one separately proves the mode assignments (35)–(36), then Poincaré–Perron/Levinson theory immediately proves convergence. But proving those assignments is the missing certificate.

## 9.5 Does \(|GQ_N-P_N|\to0\) hold in the raw normalization?

It should not be used as the target. Because of factorial-order-seven growth, a nonzero recessive raw solution may still grow rapidly. The correct statement is relative decay (32), or decay after dividing by the common twist (33).

---

# Final conclusion

The exact moment lift does not yet constitute a complete proof. Its mathematical content is

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal L_G=G\mathcal L_Q-\mathcal L_P
\quad\text{on }\mathbb Q[s]_{\le1},}
\]

and this identity is independent of the CMF recurrence.

The challenge is therefore not to estimate a degree-one integral. It is to prove that the initial rows select different asymptotic sectors of the normalized rank-three system:

\[
\boxed{
Q_N\text{ contains the leading mode, while }GQ_N-P_N
\text{ does not}.}
\]

A true Padé/Nikishin identification would prove this through growing orthogonality. A generating-function identification would prove it through analyticity. A matrix AZ certificate would prove it through an \(N\)-dependent phase. Any one of those is sufficient; the present fixed moment formulas alone are not.
