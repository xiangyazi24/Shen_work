# Problem 2.5: an explicit Delannoy-square WZ certificate, an explicit Catalan extension, and the remaining CMF gap

## Executive verdict

There are two different questions hidden in the prompt.

1. **For the exact symmetric-square Delannoy carrier**, there is a completely explicit hypergeometric summand and a completely explicit Zeilberger certificate. In fact, one does not need the double sum: Clausen's identity collapses \(D_n^2\) to the single sum
   
   \[
   \boxed{
   D_n^2=
   \sum_{k=0}^n
   2^k\binom{2k}{k}\binom nk\binom{n+k}{k}.
   }
   \tag{1}
   \]
   
   If
   
   \[
   F(n,k)=2^k\binom{2k}{k}\binom nk\binom{n+k}{k},
   \tag{2}
   \]
   
   then the exact telescoping identity is
   
   \[
   \boxed{
   \begin{aligned}
   &(n+2)^2(2n+1)F(n+2,k)\\
   &\quad-(2n+3)(35n^2+70n+26)F(n+1,k)\\
   &\quad+(2n+1)(35n^2+70n+26)F(n,k)\\
   &\quad-(2n+3)n^2F(n-1,k)\\
   &=\mathcal G(n,k+1)-\mathcal G(n,k),
   \end{aligned}}
   \tag{3}
   \]
   
   with
   
   \[
   \boxed{
   \mathcal G(n,k)=
   -\frac{4(n+1)(2n+1)(2n+3)k^3}
   {(n-k+1)(n-k+2)(n+k)}F(n,k).
   }
   \tag{4}
   \]
   
   Summing (3) proves the stated order-three recurrence for \(D_n^2\).

2. **For the actual Problem 2.5 CMF denominator \(q_n\)**, no explicit finite hypergeometric sum, WZ mate, rational Ore intertwiner, or Beukers error integral is currently certified by the supplied data. The facts
   
   \[
   \chi_{25}(c)=(c+16)(c^2+544c+256)
   \]
   
   and
   
   \[
   -16^{-1}\chi_{25}\sim
   (t-1)(t^2-34t+1)
   \]
   
   identify the formal spectrum at infinity, but do not prove a global rational equivalence with \(\operatorname{Sym}^2\) of the Delannoy module.

The prompt's proposed statement

\[
\text{“Problem 2.5 = a scalar \(-16\)-twist of }D_n^2
\text{ plus one inhomogeneous term”}
\]

is therefore not yet a theorem. A scalar twist is insufficient in general; a non-scalar Ore transformation

\[
U=u_0(n)+u_1(n)S+u_2(n)S^2
\]

may be required, and existence of such a \(U\) has not been established.

What *can* be done completely is to construct an explicit Catalan extension of the Delannoy-square sum. Define

\[
C_k=\sum_{j=0}^k\frac{(-1)^j}{(2j+1)^2}
\tag{5}
\]

and

\[
\boxed{
Q_n=\sum_{k=0}^nF(n,k)=D_n^2,
\qquad
P_n=\sum_{k=0}^nF(n,k)C_k.
}
\tag{6}
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{\frac{P_n}{Q_n}\longrightarrow G}
\tag{7}
\]

exponentially, and the error has the exact Beukers-type integral

\[
\boxed{
P_n-GQ_n
=\int_0^1
\frac{x^2(-\log x)}{1+x^2}
P_n^{\mathrm{Leg}}\!\left(\sqrt{1-8x^2}\right)^2\,dx,
}
\tag{8}
\]

where \(P_n^{\mathrm{Leg}}\) denotes the Legendre polynomial. Moreover,

\[
\boxed{
\frac{P_n}{Q_n}-G
=O\!\left(
 n\rho^n
\right),
\qquad
\rho=rac{15+4\sqrt{14}}{17+12\sqrt2}
=0.882135211270208\ldots .
}
\tag{9}
\]

This gives a complete \(\operatorname{Sym}^2\)-Delannoy construction for Catalan's constant. It is, however, **not yet identified with the printed Problem 2.5 CMF**, whose observed projective rate is much faster,

\[
17-12\sqrt2=0.0294372515\ldots .
\]

The difference in rates is another warning that the challenge construction is not merely the elementary model (6).

# 1. A correction about the form of a WZ identity

The equation requested in part (a),

\[
F(n+1,k)-F(n,k)=G(n,k+1)-G(n,k),
\tag{10}
\]

is the WZ form appropriate to a sum that is independent of \(n\), apart from boundary terms. If both \(k\)-boundary terms vanish, summing (10) gives

\[
\sum_kF(n+1,k)=\sum_kF(n,k).
\]

A denominator sequence \(q_n\) that grows with \(n\) therefore cannot normally be certified by (10). The correct Zeilberger form is

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{r=0}^{m}a_r(n)F(n+r,k)
=G(n,k+1)-G(n,k),
}
\tag{11}
\]

where the polynomial or rational coefficients \(a_r(n)\) are the recurrence coefficients of the sum.

For \(D_n^2\), formula (3) is precisely such a certificate, with \(m=3\) after the usual index shift.

For the actual CMF sequence \(q_n\), the corresponding \(F\) and \(G\) are not known because the finite Ore transformation from the Delannoy-square carrier to the challenge scalar operator has not been found.

# 2. The central Delannoy WZ pair

The central Delannoy numbers are

\[
D_n=P_n^{\mathrm{Leg}}(3)
=\sum_{k=0}^n\binom nk\binom{n+k}{k}.
\tag{12}
\]

They satisfy

\[
(n+1)D_{n+1}=3(2n+1)D_n-nD_{n-1}.
\tag{13}
\]

This recurrence itself has an elementary WZ certificate.

Put

\[
d(n,k)=\binom nk\binom{n+k}{k}.
\tag{14}
\]

The two relevant ratios are

\[
\frac{d(n+1,k)}{d(n,k)}
=\frac{n+k+1}{n-k+1},
\qquad
\frac{d(n,k+1)}{d(n,k)}
=\frac{(n-k)(n+k+1)}{(k+1)^2}.
\tag{15}
\]

Define

\[
\boxed{
R_D(n,k)=
-\frac{2(2n+1)k^2}{(n-k+1)(n+k)}
}
\tag{16}
\]

and

\[
G_D(n,k)=R_D(n,k)d(n,k).
\tag{17}
\]

Direct division by \(d(n,k)\) gives

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&(n+1)d(n+1,k)-3(2n+1)d(n,k)+nd(n-1,k)\\
&\qquad=G_D(n,k+1)-G_D(n,k).
\end{aligned}}
\tag{18}
\]

For completeness, the rational identity behind (18) is

\[
\begin{aligned}
&(n+1)\frac{n+k+1}{n-k+1}
-3(2n+1)
+n\frac{n-k}{n+k}\\
&=-2(2n+1)
\left[
1-\frac{k^2}{(n-k+1)(n+k)}
\right].
\end{aligned}
\]

The right side is exactly

\[
\frac{d(n,k+1)}{d(n,k)}R_D(n,k+1)-R_D(n,k).
\]

Summing over \(k\) proves (13).

# 3. Double-sum and single-sum formulas for \(D_n^2\)

The immediate double sum is

\[
\boxed{
D_n^2=
\sum_{j=0}^n\sum_{k=0}^n
\binom nj\binom{n+j}{j}
\binom nk\binom{n+k}{k}.
}
\tag{19}
\]

There is a much better one-dimensional sum. The Legendre hypergeometric formula is

\[
P_n^{\mathrm{Leg}}(x)
={}_2F_1\!\left(
\begin{matrix}-n,n+1\\1\end{matrix};
\frac{1-x}{2}
\right).
\tag{20}
\]

Clausen's square identity gives the terminating identity

\[
\boxed{
P_n^{\mathrm{Leg}}(x)^2
={}_3F_2\!\left(
\begin{matrix}-n,n+1,\tfrac12\\1,1\end{matrix};
1-x^2
\right).
}
\tag{21}
\]

At \(x=3\), the argument is \(1-3^2=-8\), so

\[
D_n^2
=\sum_{k=0}^n
\frac{(-n)_k(n+1)_k(1/2)_k}{(k!)^3}(-8)^k.
\tag{22}
\]

Now

\[
\frac{(-n)_k(n+1)_k}{(k!)^2}
=(-1)^k\binom nk\binom{n+k}{k}
\]

and

\[
\frac{(1/2)_k}{k!}
=\frac1{4^k}\binom{2k}{k}.
\]

Substitution into (22) proves (1):

\[
D_n^2=\sum_{k=0}^n
2^k\binom{2k}{k}\binom nk\binom{n+k}{k}.
\]

Thus the square has a single proper-hypergeometric summand, not merely a double sum.

# 4. The explicit creative-telescoping certificate for \(D_n^2\)

Set

\[
F(n,k)=2^k\binom{2k}{k}\binom nk\binom{n+k}{k}.
\]

The elementary shift ratios are

\[
\boxed{
\frac{F(n+1,k)}{F(n,k)}
=\frac{n+k+1}{n-k+1},
}
\tag{23}
\]

and

\[
\boxed{
\frac{F(n,k+1)}{F(n,k)}
=\frac{4(2k+1)(n-k)(n+k+1)}{(k+1)^3}.
}
\tag{24}
\]

Put

\[
P(n)=35n^2+70n+26.
\tag{25}
\]

Define the recurrence summand

\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal L F(n,k):={}&
(n+2)^2(2n+1)F(n+2,k)\\
&-(2n+3)P(n)F(n+1,k)\\
&+(2n+1)P(n)F(n,k)\\
&-(2n+3)n^2F(n-1,k).
\end{aligned}
\tag{26}
\]

Dividing by \(F(n,k)\) and simplifying gives

\[
\boxed{
\frac{\mathcal LF(n,k)}{F(n,k)}
=
\frac{4(n+1)(2n+1)(2n+3)\,A(n,k)}
{(n-k+1)(n-k+2)(n+k)},
}
\tag{27}
\]

where

\[
A(n,k)=
9k^3-12k^2-8(n+1)^2k-4n(n+2).
\tag{28}
\]

Now take

\[
\boxed{
R(n,k)=
-\frac{4(n+1)(2n+1)(2n+3)k^3}
{(n-k+1)(n-k+2)(n+k)}.
}
\tag{29}
\]

Using (24),

\[
\begin{aligned}
&\frac{F(n,k+1)}{F(n,k)}R(n,k+1)-R(n,k)\\
&= -\frac{16(n+1)(2n+1)(2n+3)(2k+1)}{n-k+1}\\
&\qquad
+\frac{4(n+1)(2n+1)(2n+3)k^3}
{(n-k+1)(n-k+2)(n+k)}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{30}
\]

After bringing (30) to the common denominator in (27), its numerator is

\[
4(n+1)(2n+1)(2n+3)
\left[
-4(2k+1)(n-k+2)(n+k)+k^3
\right].
\]

The bracket is exactly

\[
\begin{aligned}
&-4(2k+1)(n-k+2)(n+k)+k^3\\
&=9k^3-12k^2-8(n+1)^2k-4n(n+2)\\
&=A(n,k).
\end{aligned}
\tag{31}
\]

Equations (27)--(31) prove the certificate (3)--(4).

It is often cleaner to remove the apparent poles from \(\mathcal G=RF\). Using factorials,

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal G(n,k)=
-4(n+1)(2n+1)(2n+3)k^3
2^k\binom{2k}{k}
\frac{(n+k-1)!}{(k!)^2(n-k+2)!}.
}
\tag{32}
\]

With the reciprocal-gamma convention, \(\mathcal G(n,0)=0\) and \(\mathcal G(n,n+3)=0\). Summing (3) over \(0\le k\le n+2\) therefore gives

\[
\begin{aligned}
&(n+2)^2(2n+1)D_{n+2}^2\\
&\quad-(2n+3)(35n^2+70n+26)D_{n+1}^2\\
&\quad+(2n+1)(35n^2+70n+26)D_n^2\\
&\quad-(2n+3)n^2D_{n-1}^2=0.
\end{aligned}
\tag{33}
\]

Replacing \(n\) by \(n+1\) gives the recurrence quoted in the prompt:

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&(n+3)^2(2n+3)u_{n+3}\\
&\quad-(2n+5)(35n^2+140n+131)u_{n+2}\\
&\quad+(2n+3)(35n^2+140n+131)u_{n+1}\\
&\quad-(2n+5)(n+1)^2u_n=0.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{34}
\]

This answers part (b) with a complete finite certificate.

# 5. An explicit Catalan extension of the Delannoy-square sum

Define the Catalan partial sums

\[
C_k=\sum_{j=0}^k\gamma_j,
\qquad
\gamma_j=\frac{(-1)^j}{(2j+1)^2}.
\tag{35}
\]

They satisfy the first-order inhomogeneous relation

\[
\boxed{C_k-C_{k-1}=\gamma_k.}
\tag{36}
\]

This hypergeometric increment is the canonical rank-one extension source carrying Catalan's constant.

Take \(F(n,k)\) from (2), and define \(Q_n,P_n\) by (6). Then \(Q_n=D_n^2\), while \(P_n\) is rational.

Apply the recurrence operator \(\mathcal L\) from (26) to \(P_n\). Since \(C_k\) is independent of \(n\), formula (3) gives

\[
\mathcal LP_n
=\sum_k\bigl(\mathcal G(n,k+1)-\mathcal G(n,k)\bigr)C_k.
\tag{37}
\]

Discrete summation by parts, with the boundary terms zero, yields

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal LP_n
=-\sum_k\mathcal G(n,k)\gamma_k.
}
\tag{38}
\]

Using (32) and (35), the source is the explicit finite hypergeometric sum

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal LP_n={}&
4(n+1)(2n+1)(2n+3)\\
&\times\sum_{k=1}^{n+2}
\frac{(-2)^k k^3}{(2k+1)^2}
\binom{2k}{k}
\frac{(n+k-1)!}{(k!)^2(n-k+2)!}.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{39}
\]

Equation (39) is an explicit answer to the mathematical content of part (c): it is the inhomogeneous extension term produced by adjoining Catalan partial sums to the symmetric-square WZ kernel.

However, (39) belongs to the explicit model (6). It has **not** been identified with the extension hidden in the printed Problem 2.5 CMF.

A homogeneous closure can be obtained by finding an annihilator of the finite sum in (39) and left-multiplying the inhomogeneous recurrence. Its order will generally exceed three. Thus this elementary extension is not automatically the challenge's rank-three homogeneous recurrence.

# 6. The exact partial-sum error and a Beukers-type integral

The Catalan tail has the elementary integral representation

\[
\boxed{
G-C_k
=(-1)^{k+1}
\int_0^1
\frac{x^{2k+2}(-\log x)}{1+x^2}\,dx.
}
\tag{40}
\]

Indeed,

\[
\frac1{(2j+1)^2}
=\int_0^1x^{2j}(-\log x)\,dx
\]

and summing the geometric tail gives (40).

Therefore

\[
\begin{aligned}
P_n-GQ_n
&=\sum_{k=0}^nF(n,k)(C_k-G)\\
&=\int_0^1
\frac{x^2(-\log x)}{1+x^2}
\sum_{k=0}^n(-1)^kF(n,k)x^{2k}\,dx.
\end{aligned}
\tag{41}
\]

By Clausen's identity (21),

\[
\begin{aligned}
\sum_{k=0}^n(-1)^kF(n,k)x^{2k}
&={}_3F_2\!\left(
\begin{matrix}-n,n+1,\tfrac12\\1,1\end{matrix};8x^2
\right)\\
&=P_n^{\mathrm{Leg}}\!\left(\sqrt{1-8x^2}\right)^2.
\end{aligned}
\tag{42}
\]

Substitution into (41) proves the error integral (8).

This is a genuine Beukers-type formula: it writes a rational linear form in \(1\) and \(G\) as one explicit integral with an \(n\)-dependent orthogonal-polynomial kernel.

# 7. Exponential convergence of the explicit model

The denominator satisfies the standard Legendre asymptotic

\[
D_n=P_n^{\mathrm{Leg}}(3)
\asymp
\frac{(3+2\sqrt2)^n}{\sqrt n},
\tag{43}
\]

and hence

\[
Q_n=D_n^2
\asymp
\frac{(17+12\sqrt2)^n}{n}.
\tag{44}
\]

For the numerator of the error integral, split the interval at \(x=1/\sqrt8\).

- If \(0\le x\le1/\sqrt8\), then \(\sqrt{1-8x^2}\in[0,1]\), so
  
  \[
  \left|P_n^{\mathrm{Leg}}\!\left(\sqrt{1-8x^2}\right)\right|\le1.
  \]

- If \(1/\sqrt8\le x\le1\), put
  
  \[
  z=i\sqrt{8x^2-1}.
  \]
  
  The Laplace integral representation for Legendre polynomials gives the elementary bound
  
  \[
  |P_n^{\mathrm{Leg}}(z)|
  \le
  \left(
  |z|+|\sqrt{z^2-1}|
  \right)^n.
  \]
  
  Here
  
  \[
  |z|=\sqrt{8x^2-1},
  \qquad
  |\sqrt{z^2-1}|=2\sqrt2\,x.
  \]
  
  The sum is increasing in \(x\), so its maximum at \(x=1\) is
  
  \[
  \sqrt7+2\sqrt2.
  \]

Consequently,

\[
\left|P_n^{\mathrm{Leg}}\!\left(\sqrt{1-8x^2}\right)^2\right|
\le
(\sqrt7+2\sqrt2)^{2n}
=(15+4\sqrt{14})^n.
\tag{45}
\]

Since

\[
\int_0^1\frac{x^2(-\log x)}{1+x^2}\,dx<1,
\]

formula (8) gives

\[
|P_n-GQ_n|
\le(15+4\sqrt{14})^n.
\tag{46}
\]

Combining (44) and (46) proves

\[
\boxed{
\left|\frac{P_n}{Q_n}-G\right|
=O\!\left(
 n
 \left(
 \frac{15+4\sqrt{14}}{17+12\sqrt2}
 \right)^n
\right).
}
\tag{47}
\]

The ratio is

\[
\frac{15+4\sqrt{14}}{17+12\sqrt2}
=0.882135211270208\ldots<1.
\]

Thus the explicit construction converges exponentially.

# 8. A second exact period link through the elliptic generating function

The square generating function is Brafman's specialization

\[
\boxed{
U(z):=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}D_n^2z^n
=\frac1{1-z}
{}_2F_1\!\left(
\frac12,\frac12;1;
\frac{32z}{(1-z)^2}
\right).
}
\tag{48}
\]

In terms of the complete elliptic integral

\[
K(k)=\int_0^{\pi/2}
\frac{d\theta}{\sqrt{1-k^2\sin^2\theta}},
\]

this is

\[
\boxed{
U(z)=\frac{2}{\pi(1-z)}
K\!\left(\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z}\right).
}
\tag{49}
\]

Catalan's constant satisfies

\[
\boxed{G=\frac12\int_0^1K(k)\,dk.}
\tag{50}
\]

To verify (50), interchange the two integrals:

\[
\int_0^1K(k)\,dk
=\int_0^{\pi/2}
\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\,d\theta
=2G.
\]

Let

\[
z_0=17-12\sqrt2=(\sqrt2-1)^4
\]

and

\[
k(z)=\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z}.
\]

Then \(k(0)=0\) and \(k(z_0)=1\). Substituting (49) into (50) gives the exact integral transform

\[
\boxed{
G=
\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\int_0^{z_0}
\frac{1+z}{(1-z)\sqrt z}
U(z)\,dz.
}
\tag{51}
\]

This explains why the silver-ratio singularity and Catalan's constant can coexist naturally in one period calculation. It does not identify the challenge CMF matrix with the coefficient sequence \(D_n^2\).

# 9. Why this is not yet the Problem 2.5 CMF certificate

The actual scalar operator extracted from the challenge CMF has degree pattern

\[
(28,21,14,7)
\]

and Poincare polynomial

\[
(c+16)(c^2+544c+256).
\]

After removal of a factorial slope, its roots are proportional to

\[
1,\quad17+12\sqrt2,\quad17-12\sqrt2.
\]

This establishes only a formal match with the roots of the Delannoy symmetric square.

To push the WZ certificate (3) to the challenge, one must first prove an identity of Ore modules. In scalar form it would look like

\[
\boxed{
L_{25}\,U=V\,L_{D^2}^{[h]},
}
\tag{52}
\]

where

\[
U=u_0(n)+u_1(n)S+u_2(n)S^2,
\]

\(V\) is another Ore operator, and \(L_{D^2}^{[h]}\) is a possible scalar hypergeometric twist of (34).

Endpoint-divisor balancing suggests, in one normalization, the necessary scalar candidate

\[
\boxed{
h_n=(-16)^n(n!)^4(3/2)_n^3,}
\tag{53}
\]

with

\[
\frac{h_{n+1}}{h_n}
=-16(n+1)^4\left(n+\frac32\right)^3.
\]

This removes a determinant obstruction, but does not prove (52). A rational \(U\) may still fail to exist, and a polynomial ansatz can miss it because universal denominators are needed.

If (52) is eventually found, the challenge denominator summand will be obtained mechanically from (2):

\[
\boxed{
F_{25}(n,k)=
\sum_{r=0}^2u_r(n)h_{n+r}F(n+r,k).
}
\tag{54}
\]

Composing (52) with the certificate (3) then gives a creative-telescoping certificate for \(q_n\). Formula (54) is the exact conditional answer; the missing data are the rational functions \(u_0,u_1,u_2\).

Until those are printed and verified, it is not justified to claim

\[
q_n=\sum_kF_{25}(n,k)
\]

for the actual CMF sequence.

# 10. Answer to part (d): the exact \(H(n,k)\) in the Delannoy model

For the explicit model, take

\[
\boxed{
H(n,k)=F(n,k)
=2^k\binom{2k}{k}\binom nk\binom{n+k}{k}.
}
\tag{55}

\]

Then

\[
Q_n=\sum_kH(n,k),
\qquad
P_n=\sum_kH(n,k)C_k,
\]

and (8) proves \(P_n/Q_n\to G\).

There is also a useful general identity. For any finite-support weights \(H(n,k)\), put

\[
Q_n=\sum_kH(n,k),
\qquad
P_n=\sum_kH(n,k)C_k.
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{
P_n-GQ_n
=-\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}
\frac{(-1)^j}{(2j+1)^2}
\sum_{0\le k<j}H(n,k).
}
\tag{56}
\]

This follows by exchanging the order in the Catalan tails.

To make the *challenge* \(p_n,q_n\) appear in this way, one must find weights \(H_{25}(n,k)\) satisfying both

\[
q_n=\sum_kH_{25}(n,k)
\]

and the full challenge recurrence, together with a companion certificate for \(H_{25}(n,k)C_k\). Such an \(H_{25}\) is not currently known.

# 11. What the “extension term” means in Ore-module language

A pure symmetric square is already a rank-three homogeneous difference module. It does not contain an additional inhomogeneous term.

Catalan's constant enters only after adjoining the d'Alembertian sequence \(C_k\), whose increment is the hypergeometric term \(\gamma_k\). At the summand level the exact extension equation is

\[
(S_k-1)C_{k-1}=\gamma_k.
\tag{57}
\]

A full Chyzak-style certificate would seek rational functions \(A(n,k),B(n,k)\) satisfying

\[
\boxed{
L_n\bigl(H(n,k)C_k\bigr)
=\Delta_k\bigl(A(n,k)C_k+B(n,k)\bigr).
}
\tag{58}
\]

Using (57), equation (58) reduces to a finite rational system involving \(H,A,B,\gamma_k\). For the elementary model, equations (38)--(39) show exactly what source remains.

For the challenge CMF, the corresponding source or normal function has not been identified. It may be an integral extension rather than a simple partial-sum extension.
