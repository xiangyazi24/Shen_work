# Problem 2.7: what actually has to cancel, the likely CMF proof mechanism, and the arithmetic caveat

## Executive verdict

The public recurrence and its Poincaré roots explain the **speed** of convergence, but they do not identify the limit. The decisive identity is a connection-coefficient statement:

\[
\boxed{\mathcal A_p-(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))\mathcal A_q=0,}
\]

where \(\mathcal A_p,\mathcal A_q\) are the coefficients of the dominant Poincaré solution in the asymptotic expansions of \(p_n,q_n\). Neither the characteristic polynomial nor the Cooper level-11 spectral factorization proves this equality.

The Ramanujan Challenge manuscript places Problem 2.7 in its section of problems whose proofs or proof procedures are known to the authors but were not yet public. The public statement contains only the four-term recurrence and the two initial triples. It does **not** publish the source multivariate period, a CMF generating the recurrence, an Ore intertwiner, an integral kernel, or a WZ certificate. Consequently, the literal unpublished proof cannot presently be reconstructed uniquely from the statement.

There is, however, a very precise proof architecture consistent with both the recurrence and the CMF construction:

1. Introduce the one-parameter mixed period
   \[
   J(s)=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1\frac{(xy)^s}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
   \]
   Then
   \[
   \boxed{\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)=J(0)-\frac12J'(0).}
   \]
   Thus \(\zeta(3)\) enters through a derivative in an exponent/deformation parameter, not through differentiation of the discrete modular level.

2. Embed this period and its parameter derivative in a finite-dimensional Ore module. The shift matrices of a basis of that module form a CMF.

3. Choose an optimized trajectory in that CMF. After a rational coboundary to companion form, its scalar coordinate is the published order-three recurrence. The large initial values are the transformed boundary row/functionals at the starting point.

4. Use CMF path independence to compare the optimized trajectory with a simple boundary trajectory whose limit is evaluated by the period above. This comparison is what proves the exact dominant-mode cancellation.

5. Alternatively, produce an explicit error integral
   \[
   \ell_n=p_n-(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))q_n
   \]
   and prove that it satisfies the recurrence and has only the two recessive complex modes.

The level-11 relation is real and useful, but secondary to the connection coefficient. It identifies the homogeneous carrier, or at least its formal spectral image. The mixed period/boundary extension identifies the constant.

The claimed number \(1.0228\) cannot be an upper bound for the **classical irrationality exponent** of an irrational real number, because every irrational real has classical irrationality exponent at least \(2\). It may be a sequence-specific CMF/Dirichlet exponent or an empirical arithmetic score. The analytic rate of \(2.91\) digits per step alone gives no irrationality theorem; one must also control the reduced rational heights and common divisors.

# 1. The exact asymptotic statement

The recurrence is

\[
u_{n+1}
=\frac{B_n}{A_n}u_n
-\frac{C_{n-1}}{A_{n-1}}u_{n-1}
+\frac{D_{n-2}}{A_{n-2}}u_{n-2}.
\tag{1}
\]

From the leading coefficients of the published degree-12 polynomials,

\[
\frac{B_n}{A_n}\longrightarrow\frac{55}{64},\qquad
\frac{C_n}{A_n}\longrightarrow\frac1{2048},\qquad
\frac{D_n}{A_n}\longrightarrow\frac1{2^{20}}.
\tag{2}
\]

Hence the raw Poincaré polynomial is

\[
\boxed{
\chi(r)=r^3-\frac{55}{64}r^2+\frac1{2048}r-\frac1{2^{20}}.
}
\tag{3}
\]

Putting \(\mu=64r\) gives

\[
\boxed{F(\mu)=4\mu^3-220\mu^2+8\mu-1.}
\tag{4}
\]

Let \(\mu_0\) be the unique real root and let \(\mu_\pm\) be the complex conjugate roots. Put

\[
r_0=\frac{\mu_0}{64},\qquad r_\pm=\frac{\mu_\pm}{64}.
\]

Numerically,

\[
r_0\approx0.8588,
\]

while \(|r_\pm|\approx1.05\times10^{-3}\).

Because the product of the roots of \(F\) is \(1/4\),

\[
\mu_0|\mu_\pm|^2=\frac14.
\]

Therefore the exact modulus ratio is

\[
\boxed{
\rho:=\frac{|r_\pm|}{r_0}
=\frac{|\mu_\pm|}{\mu_0}
=\frac1{2\mu_0^{3/2}}.
}
\tag{5}
\]

This is approximately

\[
\rho\approx0.0012270,
\]

so

\[
-\log_{10}\rho\approx2.91.
\tag{6}
\]

This is the source of the observed digits per step.

## 1.1 Poincaré decomposition

Under the usual nonresonance/regularity hypotheses, choose a formal basis

\[
Y_n^{(0)},\quad Y_n^{(+)},\quad Y_n^{(-)}
\]

with exponential bases \(r_0,r_+,r_-\). Then

\[
q_n=A_qY_n^{(0)}+A_q^+Y_n^{(+)}+A_q^-Y_n^{(-)},
\tag{7}
\]

\[
p_n=A_pY_n^{(0)}+A_p^+Y_n^{(+)}+A_p^-Y_n^{(-)}.
\tag{8}
\]

The constants \(A_q,A_p\) depend linearly on the initial triples. If \(A_q\ne0\), then

\[
\frac{p_n}{q_n}\longrightarrow\frac{A_p}{A_q}.
\tag{9}
\]

Thus the desired theorem is exactly

\[
\boxed{
A_p=(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))A_q.
}
\tag{10}
\]

Equivalently, for

\[
\ell_n:=p_n-(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))q_n,
\tag{11}
\]

the coefficient of \(Y_n^{(0)}\) must vanish.

If (10) is proved, then

\[
\ell_n=A_\ell^+Y_n^{(+)}+A_\ell^-Y_n^{(-)},
\tag{12}
\]

and consequently

\[
\boxed{
\frac{p_n}{q_n}-(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))
=O\!\left(n^M\rho^n\right)
}
\tag{13}
\]

for an explicitly computable Birkhoff power \(M\). The equal moduli of the two recessive roots predict an oscillatory factor.

The key logical point is that (3)--(6) prove only the conditional statement

\[
\text{dominant cancellation}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\text{2.91 digits per step}.
\]

They do not prove the cancellation itself.

# 2. The exact mixed period

Define, initially for \(\Re(s)>-1\),

\[
J(s)=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{(xy)^s}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
\tag{14}
\]

Expanding \((1-xy)^{-1}\) and integrating termwise gives

\[
\begin{aligned}
J(s)
&=\sum_{m=0}^\infty
\int_0^1x^{m+s}\,dx
\int_0^1y^{m+s}\,dy\\
&=\sum_{m=0}^\infty\frac1{(m+s+1)^2}\\
&=\zeta(2,s+1),
\end{aligned}
\tag{15}
\]

where the last expression is the Hurwitz zeta function.

At \(s=0\),

\[
J(0)=\zeta(2),
\tag{16}
\]

and

\[
J'(0)
=-2\sum_{m=0}^\infty\frac1{(m+1)^3}
=-2\zeta(3).
\tag{17}
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)=J(0)-\frac12J'(0).
}
\tag{18}
\]

Equivalently,

\[
\boxed{
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{1-\frac12\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
}
\tag{19}
\]

This formula gives the cleanest explanation of the mixed weights:

- \(J(0)\) is the weight-two period;
- the parameter derivative \(J'(0)\) supplies the weight-three extension.

There is no natural derivative with respect to the integer level \(11\). The appropriate derivative is with respect to a continuous exponent, hypergeometric parameter, or deformation coordinate.

# 3. The likely CMF proof mechanism

The general CMF construction starts from a multivariate D-finite function and a finite basis of its Ore-module image. Matrices representing integer parameter shifts in that basis satisfy the conservative/cocycle relation. Different bases give rationally coboundary-equivalent CMFs.

For Problem 2.7, the structurally natural source is a deformation of (14), possibly multiplied by a level-11 modular or hypergeometric kernel. Schematically, one expects a period family

\[
\mathcal I(\mathbf m;s)
=\int_{[0,1]^d}
\mathcal K(\mathbf m;\mathbf x)
\frac{(x_1\cdots x_d)^s}{1-x_1\cdots x_d}\,d\mathbf x,
\tag{20}
\]

with a finite basis containing, at least implicitly,

\[
1,\qquad
\mathcal I(\mathbf m;0),\qquad
\partial_s\mathcal I(\mathbf m;s)|_{s=0}.
\tag{21}
\]

A parameter shift \(\mathbf m\mapsto\mathbf m+e_i\) is represented by a rational matrix. The cocycle identity makes the product independent of the path connecting two lattice points.

## 3.1 Simple path versus optimized path

The most convincing mechanism is that two trajectories in one CMF are compared.

- A **simple boundary trajectory** reduces to an elementary sum or integral. Its ratio is evaluated as (18), perhaps via the slower Problem 2.6 series.
- An **optimized trajectory** has a much better spectral gap. After scalarization it produces the degree-12 four-term recurrence of Problem 2.7.

Path independence shows that the two trajectories evaluate the same boundary functional. Thus the optimized recurrence inherits exactly the limit \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\).

This would explain why Problems 2.6 and 2.7 can have the same constant without their scalar recurrence operators being Ore-equivalent. They can be different trajectories, projections, or cyclic vectors inside a larger conservative system.

## 3.2 Companion coboundary

Let \(M_{\mathrm{traj}}(n)\) be the rank-three trajectory matrix and let \(C_{27}(n)\) be the companion matrix of the published recurrence. The relevant finite certificate would be

\[
\boxed{
M_{\mathrm{traj}}(n)U(n+1)=U(n)C_{27}(n)
}
\tag{22}
\]

for a rational coboundary matrix \(U(n)\).

Fixed boundary rows \(\lambda_p,\lambda_q\) then give

\[
p_n=\lambda_pM_{\mathrm{traj}}(0)\cdots M_{\mathrm{traj}}(n-1)v,
\tag{23}
\]

\[
q_n=\lambda_qM_{\mathrm{traj}}(0)\cdots M_{\mathrm{traj}}(n-1)v.
\tag{24}
\]

Equation (22) converts these into solutions of the scalar companion recurrence. The exact period identity on the simple path proves (10).

This is the CMF version of a Beukers proof: the cancellation is a boundary/path identity, not a consequence of the eigenvalues.

## 3.3 Why the initial values are large

The official initial triples are

\[
\begin{aligned}
p_0&=-612218384750,\\
p_1&=-\frac{9525021973931919}{18100},\\
p_2&=-\frac{29561828382772029}{65380},
\end{aligned}
\qquad
\begin{aligned}
q_0&=-215040420000,\\
q_1&=-\frac{167282265043404}{905},\\
q_2&=-\frac{964185327658080}{6071}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{25}
\]

In the CMF mechanism these numbers are not mysterious free parameters. They are the coordinates of simple boundary vectors after:

1. evaluation at a base lattice point;
2. a rational change of Ore-module basis;
3. passage to a cyclic/companion basis;
4. clearing convenient common denominators.

A modest period basis can therefore produce very large companion coordinates. Conversely, the scalar recurrence and the six numbers in (25) do not determine a unique source CMF: infinitely many state-space realizations and rational coboundaries produce the same scalar data.

The actual unpublished origin of (25) can be recovered only from the source period, trajectory, and coboundary matrix used by the authors.

# 4. A precise integral certificate that would finish the proof

One sufficient certificate is an explicit family \(K_n(x,y;s)\) such that

\[
I_n(s)=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1K_n(x,y;s)\,dx\,dy
\tag{26}
\]

satisfies the Problem 2.7 recurrence for \(s\) near zero.

A creative-telescoping identity would have the form

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&A_nK_{n+1}(x,y;s)-B_nK_n(x,y;s)\\
&\quad+C_{n-1}K_{n-1}(x,y;s)
-D_{n-2}K_{n-2}(x,y;s)\\
&=\partial_xU_n(x,y;s)+\partial_yV_n(x,y;s).
\end{aligned}
}
\tag{27}
\]

If the boundary terms vanish, integration proves

\[
A_nI_{n+1}-B_nI_n+C_{n-1}I_{n-1}-D_{n-2}I_{n-2}=0.
\tag{28}
\]

Differentiate (27) with respect to \(s\), set \(s=0\), and form

\[
E_n=I_n(0)-\frac12I_n'(0).
\tag{29}
\]

Then \(E_n\) satisfies the same recurrence. If direct evaluation of three initial values gives

\[
E_j=p_j-(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))q_j,
\qquad j=0,1,2,
\tag{30}
\]

uniqueness of solutions proves

\[
\boxed{E_n=p_n-(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))q_n\quad\text{for all }n.}
\tag{31}
\]

Finally, a saddle-point or positivity estimate showing

\[
|E_n|\le Cn^a|r_+|^n
\tag{32}
\]

and a dominant asymptotic

\[
|q_n|\ge c n^b r_0^n
\tag{33}
\]

gives

\[
\left|\frac{p_n}{q_n}-(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))\right|
\le C'n^{a-b}\rho^n.
\tag{34}
\]

Equations (27), (30), (32), and (33) would be a complete traditional proof.

No explicit \(K_n,U_n,V_n\) of this kind is currently present in the public challenge statement or the public Cooper analysis.

# 5. What the Cooper level-11 relation proves

Cooper's self-starting level-11 sequence satisfies

\[
\begin{aligned}
(n+1)^3T_{n+1}={}&2(2n+1)(5n^2+5n+2)T_n\\
&-8n(7n^2+1)T_{n-1}\\
&+22n(2n-1)(n-1)T_{n-2}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{35}
\]

Its characteristic polynomial is

\[
C_{11}(t)=t^3-20t^2+56t-44.
\tag{36}
\]

The exact polynomial identity

\[
\boxed{
16F\!\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{4}\right)
=-C_{11}(t)C_{11}(4-t)
}
\tag{37}
\]

shows that the Problem 2.7 spectrum is a centered quadratic image of the level-11 spectrum.

## 5.1 Centered binomial/even transform

Define

\[
W_n=\frac1{256^n}\sum_{k=0}^{2n}
\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}T_k.
\tag{38}
\]

For a pure mode \(T_k=t^k\), the binomial theorem gives

\[
W_n=\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}\right)^n.
\tag{39}
\]

This is exactly the raw root transform.

Let

\[
Z(X)=\sum_{k\ge0}T_kX^k.
\]

For the unscaled binomial transform

\[
b_m=\sum_{k=0}^m\binom mk(-2)^{m-k}T_k,
\]

one has

\[
\sum_{m\ge0}b_mr^m
=\frac1{1+2r}Z\!\left(\frac r{1+2r}\right).
\tag{40}
\]

Taking the even part and scaling gives a trace of two algebraic branches. With \(z=r^2\),

\[
\widehat Z(z)
=\frac12\left[
B\!\left(\frac{\sqrt z}{16}\right)
+B\!\left(-\frac{\sqrt z}{16}\right)
\right],
\tag{41}
\]

where

\[
B(r)=\frac1{1+2r}Z\!\left(\frac r{1+2r}\right).
\]

The associated rational pullback is

\[
\boxed{z=\frac{256X^2}{(1-2X)^2}.}
\tag{42}
\]

Thus the public level-11 connection is stronger than a numerical coincidence: it is an exact quadratic pullback plus even trace at the homogeneous generating-function level.

## 5.2 What remains beyond the spectrum

The sequence \(W_n\) is not the published \(q_n\). The missing finite comparison is:

1. compute the minimal scalar operator \(L_K\) annihilating \(W_n\), by differential pullback or holonomic creative telescoping;
2. find a possible rank-one twist \(h_n\) and a rational Ore homomorphism
   \[
   \boxed{
   L_{27}U=V L_K^{[h]},
   \qquad
   U=u_0(n)+u_1(n)S+u_2(n)S^2;
   }
   \tag{43}
   \]
3. compare three initial values.

No explicit \(U,V,h\) is publicly known from the supplied data.

Even a proof of (43) would identify only the homogeneous solution modules. It would still not prove the value of \(A_p/A_q\). The period extension or boundary functional in Sections 2--4 is additionally necessary.

# 6. How \(\zeta(3)\) can occur in a level-11 setting

The level-11 homogeneous system is modular, while

\[
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)=\frac{\pi^2}{6}+\zeta(3)
\]

mixes periods of different weights. Therefore it should not be expected to be a single critical value of one pure cusp form.

The correct conceptual object is mixed:

- a pure level-11 local system supplies the fast homogeneous carrier;
- a Tate/Eisenstein extension or parameter derivative supplies the boundary constants;
- the initial row selects the linear combination \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\).

As an illustration, the nonconstant part of the weight-four Eisenstein series satisfies

\[
L(E_4^\circ,s)=240\zeta(s)\zeta(s-3).
\]

At \(s=3\),

\[
L(E_4^\circ,3)=-120\zeta(3).
\]

For the level-11 Eisenstein combination

\[
f_{11}(\tau)=E_4(\tau)-121E_4(11\tau),
\]

one obtains, with this normalization,

\[
L(f_{11}^\circ,3)=-\frac{1200}{11}\zeta(3).
\tag{44}
\]

This shows that \(\zeta(3)\) is entirely compatible with a level-11 Eisenstein extension. It does **not** identify the particular extension behind Problem 2.7.

The elementary parameter-derivative identity (18) is more precise for the actual mixed value and should be regarded as the first proof target.

# 7. The number \(1.0228\) is not a classical irrationality-measure upper bound

For an irrational real number \(\alpha\), the classical irrationality exponent is

\[
\mu(\alpha)=\sup\left\{\mu:
\left|\alpha-\frac pq\right|<q^{-\mu}
\text{ for infinitely many }p/q\right\}.
\]

Dirichlet's theorem implies

\[
\mu(\alpha)\ge2.
\tag{45}
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{
\mu(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))\le1.0228
}
\]

cannot be a valid statement about the classical irrationality exponent of an irrational number.

What can legitimately be approximately \(1.0228\) is a **sequence-specific exponent** attached to these particular approximants, or a normalized CMF arithmetic score. Suppose, after reduction,

\[
H(p_n/q_n)=\exp(hn+o(n))
\tag{46}
\]

and

\[
\left|\frac{p_n}{q_n}-L\right|
=\exp(-\delta n+o(n)).
\tag{47}
\]

Then this particular sequence has approximation exponent

\[
\nu=\frac{\delta}{h}.
\tag{48}
\]

A value just above \(1\) may be sufficient for a Dirichlet-style irrationality proof if suitable denominator clearing and nonvanishing are proved. It is not an upper bound on the classical exponent.

## 7.1 Analytic scale is not arithmetic height

The dominant root \(r_0<1\) describes the size of the rational numbers \(q_n\) in the displayed normalization. It does not describe the size of their reduced denominators. The recurrence coefficients contain large polynomial denominators, and clearing them can introduce exponential arithmetic height even while the numerical value of \(q_n\) tends to zero.

Thus the \(2.91\)-digit analytic rate cannot be inserted directly into an irrationality-measure formula.

A genuine arithmetic result needs:

1. explicit integers \(P_n,Q_n\) obtained from \(p_n,q_n\);
2. a height estimate for \(P_n,Q_n\) after reduction;
3. control of \(\gcd(P_n,Q_n)\);
4. a nonzero linear form \(P_n-LQ_n\) tending to zero.

The public Problem 2.7 statement asks only for convergence and does not assert an irrationality result. The CMF paper also labels much of its general arithmetic behavior as experimental/conjectural.

It is not enough to note that \(\zeta(2)\) and \(\zeta(3)\) are individually irrational: that fact alone does not establish the irrationality of their sum. Public simultaneous-approximation results construct rational linear forms in \(1,\zeta(2),\zeta(3)\) and prove the irrationality of each value separately, but they do not turn the displayed recurrence automatically into a classical irrationality-measure bound of \(1.0228\).

# 8. WZ and creative telescoping

No public one-line WZ certificate is presently attached to the Problem 2.7 recurrence.

There are two appropriate certificate formats.

## 8.1 Certificate for the centered Cooper transform

Let

\[
K(n,k)=256^{-n}\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}.
\tag{49}
\]

Because \(T_k\) is P-recursive rather than a single hypergeometric term, the correct identity is a vector Chyzak certificate:

\[
\boxed{
L_K^{(n)}\bigl(K(n,k)T_k\bigr)
=\Delta_k\left[
K(n,k)\sum_{j=0}^2R_j(n,k)T_{k+j}
\right].
}
\tag{50}
\]

Summing over \(k\) would prove \(L_KW=0\). The next task is the Ore comparison (43).

## 8.2 Certificate for the mixed period

For an integral family \(K_n(x,y;s)\), use (27), or a discrete sum analogue

\[
\boxed{
L_{27}^{(n)}F(n,k;s)
=G(n,k+1;s)-G(n,k;s).
}
\tag{51}
\]

Differentiating with respect to \(s\) at zero generates logarithms and hence \(\zeta(3)\). A single hypergeometric WZ summand may not exist; a holonomic vector certificate is sufficient.

The spectral factorization is exactly what one would see from the leading symbol of such a certificate. It is not the certificate itself.

# 9. Why the official initial conditions encode the proof

For a generic order-three recurrence, any regular initial triple gives a solution. The six numbers in (25) are special because they select two boundary functionals in the hidden period module.

At the asymptotic level they enforce the exact relation (10). At the CMF level they should be recoverable from a formula of the form

\[
\begin{pmatrix}
p_n\\q_n\\ *
\end{pmatrix}
=U(n)^{-1}
M_{\mathrm{traj}}(0)\cdots M_{\mathrm{traj}}(n-1)
\begin{pmatrix}v_1\\v_2\\v_3\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{52}
\]

The entries of \(U(0)^{-1}\), together with denominator clearing, can readily create the observed large integers and nontrivial denominators \(905,6071,18100,65380\).

The scalar data do not reveal which of the following generated them:

- an Euler/Beukers integral basis;
- a hypergeometric parameter-derivative basis;
- an Eisenstein/Eichler extension of the level-11 module;
- a different simple trajectory in a higher-dimensional CMF.

Publishing the source CMF and the coboundary matrix would answer this question immediately.

# 10. A complete proof checklist

A complete, independently checkable proof can follow either the CMF route or the integral route.

## CMF route

1. **Source:** give the multivariate D-finite period \(\mathcal I(\mathbf m;s)\).
2. **Basis:** give the finite Ore-module basis containing the value and derivative directions.
3. **Shift matrices:** print the CMF generators and verify the cocycle relations.
4. **Simple trajectory:** evaluate its boundary ratio as \(J(0)-\tfrac12J'(0)\).
5. **Optimized trajectory:** print its matrix and the rational coboundary to the Problem 2.7 companion matrix.
6. **Initial data:** derive the six published values from the boundary vectors and the basepoint coboundary.
7. **Asymptotics:** prove that the denominator has nonzero dominant coefficient.
8. **Rate:** use (5) to obtain \(O(n^M\rho^n)\).

## Integral/creative-telescoping route

1. Print \(K_n(x,y;s)\).
2. Verify the telescoping identity (27).
3. Check the three initial identities (30).
4. Bound the error integral as in (32).
5. Prove the denominator lower asymptotic (33).
6. Conclude (34), with the exact root ratio (5).

## Level-11 route

1. Compute and certify the transformed operator \(L_K\) from (38).
2. Find the twist and Ore intertwiner (43).
3. Transport a period/extension solution, not only Cooper's holomorphic sequence.
4. Match the initial triples.
5. Apply Poincaré asymptotics.

At present the public data complete the spectral parts of these programs, but not the connection-coefficient step.

# Final answers to the five questions

## 1. Is there an integral representation for \(p_n,q_n\)?

An exact integral for the target constant is

\[
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{1-\frac12\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
\]

Equivalently it is \(J(0)-\tfrac12J'(0)\). No public explicit \(n\)-dependent integral representing the published \(p_n,q_n\) or their error is currently available. Equations (26)--(34) give the exact certificate required.

## 2. What is the precise Cooper connection?

The centered binomial/even transform gives an exact quadratic pullback and maps every Cooper exponential mode to the corresponding Problem 2.7 mode. This proves the spectral identity. It does not prove equality of finite operators or identify the period. The missing finite object is an operator \(L_K\) plus a twist/intertwiner (43), or an equivalent differential-equation gauge.

## 3. Does \(1.0228\) match a known irrationality measure?

Not as a classical irrationality-exponent upper bound. Such an upper bound would be impossible below \(2\) for an irrational number. The value may be a special-sequence/CMF score. To obtain any irrationality theorem one must prove arithmetic height, integrality, gcd, and nonvanishing estimates; the analytic root ratio alone is insufficient.

## 4. What modular/L-value object gives \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\)?

It is naturally a mixed period, not a pure critical value of one modular form. The level-11 carrier can be modular, while \(\zeta(3)\) enters through an Eisenstein/Eichler or parameter-derivative extension and \(\zeta(2)\) through a Tate/boundary period. Formula (18) gives the simplest exact realization.

## 5. Is there a known WZ proof?

No public explicit WZ/creative-telescoping certificate for the published recurrence is known from the supplied sources. The appropriate targets are the vector holonomic certificate (50) for the centered Cooper transform and the parameter-dependent telescoping identity (27) or (51) for the mixed period.
