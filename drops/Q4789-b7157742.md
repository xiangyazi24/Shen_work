# 1. Why the raw Ore identity is impossible

Write

\[
L_{25}=\sum_{i=0}^3c_i(n)S^i,
\qquad
L_D^{\langle2\rangle}=\sum_{i=0}^3a_i(n)S^i.
\]

The degrees of the challenge coefficients are

\[
\deg c_i=28-7i.
\]

Hence every formal solution of the raw challenge equation has factorial slope seven:

\[
\frac{q_{n+1}}{q_n}\sim c\,n^7.
\tag{1.1}
\]

The Delannoy symmetric square has all coefficient degrees equal to \(3\), so its solutions have slope zero:

\[
\frac{U_{n+1}}{U_n}\sim\lambda.
\tag{1.2}
\]

Let

\[
T=\sum_{j=0}^2t_j(n)S^j,\qquad t_j\in\mathbf Q(n).
\]

For a formal mode \(U_n\sim\lambda^n n^\beta\), every term

\[
t_j(n)U_{n+j}
\]

is still of the form

\[
\lambda^n n^{\beta+d_j}(\text{Laurent series in }n^{-1}),
\]

where \(d_j\in\mathbf Z\). Thus \(TU\) still has factorial slope zero. It cannot be a nonzero formal solution of \(L_{25}\).

If \(T\) killed all three solutions of \(L_D^{\langle2\rangle}\), then \(T\) would be a right multiple of the order-three operator. Since \(\operatorname{ord}T\le2\), this would force \(T=0\). This proves the slope obstruction to (0.3).

# 2. The correct factorial gauge

The naive gauge \((-16)^n(n!)^7\) removes the Newton slope, but it leaves an inconvenient common half-integral formal exponent. The determinant normalization from the invariant-conic calculation gives a better exact gauge.

Put

\[
\boxed{

=-16(n+2)^2(n+3)^2
\left(n+\frac52\right)
\left(n+\frac72\right)^2.}
\tag{2.1}

Equivalently,

\[
\Delta(n)=-2(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(2n+5)(2n+7)^2.

Choose \(H_n\) by

\[
\frac{H_{n+1}}{H_n}=\Delta(n),

\[
\boxed{

\tag{2.2}

\[
\widehat L_{25}=H^{-1}L_{25}H

\tag{2.3}

where

\[
\boxed{

=c_i(n)\prod_{s=0}^{i-1}\Delta(n+s).}
\tag{2.4}

All four \(\widehat c_i\) have degree \(28\), and their Poincaré roots are

\tag{2.5}

By contrast, all three modes of the Delannoy symmetric square have power \(-1\).

\sum_{i+j=k}\widehat c_i\,\sigma^it_j,

# 6. What the shifted sextic implies

The polynomials supplied in the question satisfy

\[
P_6(n)=\widetilde P_6(n+1),
\]

with

\[
\widetilde P_6(n)=
3072n^6+55680n^5+414064n^4+1615610n^3
+3483853n^2+3929280n+1806156.
\]

Thus the trailing scalar coefficient contains \(\widetilde P_6(n+1)\), while the leading coefficient contains \(\widetilde P_6(n)\). The shift dispersion is one and both multiplicities are one.

This is exactly the pattern in which a single power of a shifted sextic is the first denominator to test in an Abramov--Barkatou desingularization. It is strong evidence that the sextic is an apparent cyclic-vector singularity, but the shift identity alone is **not** a proof of apparentness. Apparentness is proved only when the rational desingularizer or intertwiner is actually exhibited.

The economical common-denominator ansatz is (0.7). Since

\[
\deg\widetilde P_6=6
\]

and the exact degree at infinity of \(t_j\) is \(1\), the numerator degree is forced to be \(7\).

Write

\[
N_j(n)=A_jn^7+B_jn^6+C_jn^5+\cdots.

Using

\[
\operatorname{lc}\widetilde P_6=3072,

conditions (4.5), (5.5), and (5.6) give the exact leading constraints

\boxed{
(A_0,A_1,A_2)

\tag{6.1}

\boxed{B_0+B_1+B_2=-49152\kappa,}
