# Problem 2.5: pullback exponents, the actual recurrence-to-ODE operator, and why Katz rigidity does not close the proof

## Executive verdict

The proposed rigidity argument does **not** work.

The exact conclusions are:

1. The pullback of
   \[
   k(1-k^2)Y'''+(1-3k^2)Y''-kY'=0
   \]
   through
   \[
   k(z)=\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z}
   \]
   is the third-order equation
   \[
   \begin{aligned}
   &4z^2(1-z^2)^2(z^2-34z+1)y'''\\
   &\quad+8z(1-z^2)(1-54z+103z^2+48z^3-2z^4)y''\\
   &\quad+(1-158z+1127z^2-852z^3-529z^4-110z^5+9z^6)y'=0.
   \end{aligned}
   \tag{0.1}
   \]

2. Its scalar cyclic equation has one additional apparent singularity at \(z=-1\). Its complete Riemann scheme is
   \[
   \boxed{
   \left\{
   \begin{array}{c|rrrrrr}
   z&0&-1&\rho&1&\rho^{-1}&\infty\\ \hline
   &0&0&0&0&0&0\\
   &\frac12&2&1&0&1&\frac12\\
   &\frac12&4&1&0&1&\frac12
   \end{array}
   \right\},
   \qquad \rho=17-12\sqrt2.}
   \tag{0.2}
   \]

3. The raw Problem 2.5 scalar recurrence does have the first formal ratio corrections
   \[
   \boxed{\sigma_0=\frac{33}{2},\qquad
   \sigma_+=\sigma_-=\frac{39}{2},}
   \tag{0.3}
   \]
   provided \(\sigma\) is defined by
   \[
   \frac{u_{n+1}}{u_n}
   =c\,n^7\left(1+\frac{\sigma}{n}+O(n^{-2})\right).
   \tag{0.4}
   \]
   If instead \(\sigma\) denotes the power of \(n\) in
   \(u_n\sim c^n(n!)^7n^\tau\), then the powers are
   \[
   \tau_0=\frac{19}{2},\qquad
   \tau_\pm=\frac{25}{2}.
   \tag{0.5}
   \]

4. After division by
   \[
   H_n=(-16)^n(2)_n^2(3)_n^2
   \left(\frac52\right)_n\left(\frac72\right)_n^2,
   \tag{0.6}
   \]
   the three Birkhoff powers are
   \[
   \boxed{0,-3,0.}
   \tag{0.7}
   \]

5. The phrase “convert the order-three recurrence into a rank-three differential operator by the standard generating-function correspondence” is false here. The raw ordinary generating series has radius zero because of the \((n!)^7\) growth. After the necessary coefficientwise normalization by \(H_n\), the standard Euler operator has **differential order 13**, not 3. For the selected denominator germ an order-5 annihilator has also been found in the preceding computation, but no order-3 equality follows from the difference order.

6. The recurrence-derived order-13 operator has a Riemann scheme incompatible with (0.2). More decisively, the analytic Catalan section
   \[
   U(z)=\frac{Y(k(z))}{k(z)}
   \]
   fails the normalized CMF recurrence already at \(n=0\), with exact residual
   \[
   \boxed{1\pmod {13}.}
   \tag{0.8}
   \]
   Hence its order-three operator is not a right factor of the recurrence-derived Euler operator.

7. Katz rigidity cannot be invoked. The integrated module contains the constant submodule, so it is reducible. Moreover, the pulled-back local system has rigidity index
   \[
   \boxed{\operatorname{rig}=-8,}
   \tag{0.9}
   \]
   not 2. It is not cohomologically rigid.

8. Even an isomorphism of differential modules would not identify the limit as \(G\): replacing \(Y\) by \(Y+C\) preserves the equation and all local exponents but changes \(Y(1)\) to \(G+C\). Exact matching of the CMF initial rows with the normalized section \(Y(0)=0\) remains indispensable.

Thus the formal corrections in (0.3) are correct, but they do not prove that the module is the integrated elliptic module, and the suggested local-exponent/Katz argument cannot complete the Catalan proof.

# 1. Pullback of the integrated elliptic equation

Let
\[
y(z)=Y(k(z)),\qquad
k(z)=\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z},\qquad
\Delta(z)=z^2-34z+1.
\]
Then
\[
k^2=\frac{32z}{(1-z)^2},
\qquad
1-k^2=\frac{\Delta(z)}{(1-z)^2},
\]
and
\[
k'(z)=\frac{2\sqrt2(1+z)}{\sqrt z(1-z)^2}.
\]
Using
\[
Y_k=\frac{y'}{k'},
\]
\[
Y_{kk}=\frac{y''}{(k')^2}-\frac{k''}{(k')^3}y',
\]
and
\[
Y_{kkk}=\frac{y'''}{(k')^3}
-3\frac{k''}{(k')^4}y''
+\left(3\frac{(k'')^2}{(k')^5}-\frac{k'''}{(k')^4}\right)y',
\]
direct substitution gives (0.1).

In Euler notation \(\theta=zD_z\), define
\[
A(z)=(1-z^2)^2\Delta(z),
\]
\[
B(z)=(1-z^2)(1-54z+103z^2+48z^3-2z^4),
\]
\[
C(z)=1-158z+1127z^2-852z^3-529z^4-110z^5+9z^6.
\]
Then an equivalent operator is
\[
\boxed{
L_{Y,z}=
\bigl[4A(z)(\theta-1)(\theta-2)
+8B(z)(\theta-1)+C(z)\bigr]\theta.}
\tag{1.1}
\]
The right factor \(\theta\) is the constant solution.

# 2. Singular map and local exponents

The map has the following behavior:

\[
\begin{array}{c|c|c}
z&k(z)&\text{local behavior}\\ \hline
0&0&k\asymp z^{1/2}\\
\rho&1&\text{unramified}\\
\rho^{-1}&-1&\text{unramified}\\
1&\infty&k\asymp (1-z)^{-1}\\
\infty&0&k\asymp z^{-1/2}\\
-1&2i\sqrt2&k-k(-1)\asymp(z+1)^2.
\end{array}
\tag{2.1}
\]

The point \(z=-1\) lies over an ordinary point of the original equation and is therefore apparent.

## 2.1 Exponents of the original equation in the \(k\)-plane

At \(k=0\), substituting \(Y=k^r\) gives
\[
r(r-1)^2=0,
\]
so the exponents are \(0,1,1\).

At each of \(k=1,-1\), the same indicial polynomial occurs, hence again
\[
0,1,1.
\]

At infinity, put \(Y\sim k^s\). The leading equation is
\[
-s^3k^s=0,
\]
so the Riemann exponents at infinity are
\[
0,0,0.
\]
The corresponding local solutions contain \(1,\log k,(\log k)^2\).

## 2.2 Exponents after pullback

Ramification multiplies local exponents by the ramification index. This gives:

- at \(z=0\): \(0,\frac12,\frac12\);
- at \(z=\infty\): \(0,\frac12,\frac12\);
- at \(z=\rho\) and \(z=\rho^{-1}\): \(0,1,1\);
- at \(z=1\): \(0,0,0\);
- at the apparent point \(z=-1\): \(0,2,4\).

The corresponding indicial polynomials, up to nonzero constants, are
\[
\begin{array}{c|c}
z&\text{indicial polynomial}\\ \hline
0&r(2r-1)^2\\
-1&r(r-2)(r-4)\\
\rho&r(r-1)^2\\
1&r^3\\
\rho^{-1}&r(r-1)^2\\
\infty&r(2r-1)^2.
\end{array}
\tag{2.2}
\]
The exponent sum is
\[
1+6+2+0+2+1=12,
\]
which is exactly the order-three Fuchs sum for six displayed singular points:
\[
(6-2)\frac{3\cdot2}{2}=12.
\]

# 3. Exact formal corrections of the Problem 2.5 recurrence

Let the primitive scalar recurrence be
\[
\sum_{j=0}^3c_j(n)u_{n+j}=0,
\qquad
\deg c_j=28-7j.
\tag{3.1}
\]
Write
\[
c_j(n)=a_jn^{28-7j}+b_jn^{27-7j}+O(n^{26-7j}).
\]
Assume
\[
\frac{u_{n+1}}{u_n}
=c n^7\left(1+\frac\sigma n+O(n^{-2})\right).
\]
Then
\[
\frac{u_{n+j}}{u_n}
=c^jn^{7j}
\left(1+\frac{j\sigma+\frac72j(j-1)}n+O(n^{-2})\right).
\]
The leading equation is
\[
P(c):=\sum_{j=0}^3a_jc^j=0.
\]
The next equation gives the general formula
\[
\boxed{
\sigma(c)=
-\frac{\displaystyle
\sum_{j=0}^3
\left(b_j+\frac72j(j-1)a_j\right)c^j}
{cP'(c)}.}
\tag{3.2}
\]
For the CMF recurrence,
\[
P(c)\doteq(c+16)(c^2+544c+256).
\]
Exact substitution into (3.2) gives
\[
\boxed{
\sigma(-16)=\frac{33}{2},
\qquad
\sigma(c)=\frac{39}{2}
\quad\text{when }c^2+544c+256=0.}
\tag{3.3}
\]

This confirms the numerical values quoted in the question, but clarifies their meaning. If
\[
u_n\sim Cc^n(n!)^7n^\tau,
\]
then
\[
\sigma=7+\tau.
\]
Thus (0.5), not (0.3), gives the powers in the usual Birkhoff ansatz.

## 3.1 Effect of the Pochhammer normalization

The ratio of (0.6) is
\[
\frac{H_{n+1}}{H_n}
=-2(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(2n+5)(2n+7)^2
\]
and therefore
\[
\frac{H_{n+1}}{H_n}
=-16n^7\left(1+\frac{39}{2n}+O(n^{-2})\right).
\tag{3.4}
\]
Consequently, for \(v_n=u_n/H_n\),
\[
\frac{v_{n+1}}{v_n}
=\begin{cases}
(17\pm12\sqrt2)(1+O(n^{-2})),&c=c_\pm,\\[1mm]
1-\dfrac3n+O(n^{-2}),&c=-16.
\end{cases}
\]
This proves the normalized formal powers
\[
\boxed{\widehat\tau_+=0,\qquad
\widehat\tau_0=-3,\qquad
\widehat\tau_-=0.}
\tag{3.5}
\]

These are Birkhoff data at \(n=\infty\) for a difference equation. They are not the Frobenius exponents of (0.1) at \(z=0\).

# 4. The actual standard generating-function operator

The raw solutions have factorial order seven, so
\[
\sum_{n\ge0}u_nz^n
\]
has radius zero. The standard ordinary-generating-function construction becomes analytic only after the coefficientwise hypergeometric normalization (0.6).

Let
\[
\ell_0(n)v_n+\ell_1(n)v_{n+1}
+\ell_2(n)v_{n+2}+\ell_3(n)v_{n+3}=0
\tag{4.1}
\]
be the primitive normalized recurrence. All four \(\ell_j\) have degree 13. Its endpoint factors are
\[
\begin{aligned}
\ell_0(n)={}&4(n+1)(n+3)^2(n+4)
(2n+3)(2n+5)(2n+7)P_6(n),\\
\ell_3(n)={}&-4(n+2)(n+3)(n+4)^2
(2n+7)(2n+11)^2P_6'(n),
\end{aligned}
\tag{4.2}
\]
where, after monic normalization,
\[
P_6(n)=P_6'(n+1),
\qquad\text{equivalently}\qquad
P_6'(n)=P_6(n-1).
\tag{4.3}
\]
The middle coefficients contain indispensable degree-9 and degree-10 factors.

For
\[
V(z)=\sum_{n\ge0}v_nz^n,
\qquad \theta=zD_z,
\]
the standard Euler operator is
\[
\boxed{
\mathscr D_{25}=
 z^3\ell_0(\theta)
+z^2\ell_1(\theta-1)
+z\ell_2(\theta-2)
+\ell_3(\theta-3).}
\tag{4.4}
\]
It has differential order 13. The order-three **difference** rank has not become differential order three.

The coefficient of \(\theta^{13}\) is, up to a nonzero scalar,
\[
\boxed{
1-35z+35z^2-z^3
=(1-z)(z^2-34z+1).}
\tag{4.5}
\]
Thus the characteristic finite singularities are
\[
z=\rho,\qquad z=1,
\qquad z=\rho^{-1}.
\]

# 5. Local exponents of the recurrence-derived order-13 operator

Let \(\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_6\) be the roots of \(P_6(n)\).

## 5.1 At \(z=0\)

The indicial polynomial is
\[
I_0(r)=\ell_3(r-3).
\]
Using (4.2)-(4.3), its roots are
\[
\boxed{
\left\{
1,0,-1,-1,-\frac12,-\frac52,-\frac52,
\alpha_1+4,\ldots,\alpha_6+4
\right\}.}
\tag{5.1}
\]
This already proves that the standard recurrence-derived operator is not the order-three operator (0.1).

## 5.2 At infinity

The indicial polynomial at infinity is
\[
I_\infty(r)=\ell_0(-r).
\]
Hence the exponents are
\[
\boxed{
\left\{
1,3,3,4,\frac32,\frac52,\frac72,
-\alpha_1,\ldots,-\alpha_6
\right\}.}
\tag{5.2}
\]

## 5.3 At \(z=\rho,1,\rho^{-1}\)

At a simple nonzero zero of the leading coefficient of an order-13 Fuchsian operator, twelve exponents are automatically
\[
0,1,\ldots,11.
\]
The thirteenth exponent is determined by the corresponding coefficient asymptotic. If
\[
v_n\sim \lambda^nn^\tau,
\]
then near \(z=1/\lambda\) the singular exponent is
\[
\gamma=-\tau-1.
\]
Using (3.5) gives
\[
\boxed{
\begin{array}{c|c}
 z&\text{local exponents of }\mathscr D_{25}\\ \hline
 \rho&\{-1,0,1,2,\ldots,11\}\\[1mm]
 1&\{0,1,2,\ldots,11\}\cup\{2\}\\[1mm]
 \rho^{-1}&\{-1,0,1,2,\ldots,11\}.
\end{array}}
\tag{5.3}
\]
At \(z=1\), the repeated exponent 2 corresponds to the neutral behavior
\[
v_n\sim n^{-3}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
V(z)\sim \text{analytic}+(1-z)^2\log(1-z).
\]

## 5.4 Fuchs check

The sums at \(\rho,1,\rho^{-1}\) are respectively
\[
65,68,65,
\]
for a total of 198. From (5.1)-(5.2) and (4.3), the sums at \(0\) and \(\infty\) total 36. Hence the total is
\[
234=(5-2)\frac{13\cdot12}{2},
\]
which is exactly the order-13 Fuchs relation for five singular points.

This gives the complete Riemann scheme of the direct recurrence-to-Euler operator without pretending that it has rank three.

# 6. Direct local mismatch with the integrated Catalan section

The branch relevant to Catalan's constant is more naturally
\[
U(z)=\frac{Y(k(z))}{k(z)}.
\]
It is analytic at zero and satisfies
\[
U(0)=\frac\pi4,
\qquad U(\rho)=G.
\]
Near \(z=\rho\), the logarithmic behavior of \(K\) gives
\[
\boxed{
U(z)=G+\frac{3\sqrt2}{32}
\left(1-\frac z\rho\right)
\log\left(1-\frac z\rho\right)
+O\left(1-\frac z\rho\right).}
\tag{6.1}
\]
Therefore
\[
[z^n]U(z)
\sim\frac{3\sqrt2}{32}\frac{\rho^{-n}}{n^2}.
\tag{6.2}
\]
The normalized CMF dominant branch instead has
\[
\widehat Q_n\sim C\rho^{-n}n^0.
\tag{6.3}
\]
Thus the relevant singular exponents are
\[
\boxed{1\text{ with a logarithm for }U,
\qquad -1\text{ for the CMF dominant branch}.}
\tag{6.4}
\]
They do not match as scalar germs.

There is also an exact finite obstruction. Normalize
\[
U(z)=\frac\pi4\sum_{n\ge0}u_nz^n.
\]
The first coefficients are
\[
(u_0,u_1,u_2,u_3)
=\left(1,\frac83,\frac{512}{15},\frac{20312}{35}\right)
\equiv(1,7,9,5)\pmod {13}.
\]
For the normalized CMF recurrence at \(n=0\),
\[
(\ell_0(0),\ell_1(0),\ell_2(0),\ell_3(0))
\equiv(11,9,5,5)\pmod {13}.
\]
Hence
\[
\begin{aligned}
\sum_{j=0}^3\ell_j(0)u_j
&\equiv 11+9\cdot7+5\cdot9+5\cdot5\\
&\equiv\boxed{1}\pmod {13}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{6.5}
\]
Therefore the analytic integrated-period operator is not a right factor of \(\mathscr D_{25}\). The direct identity proposed in the question is false.

A nontrivial Ore intertwiner of order at most two is a different question and is not ruled out by (6.5).

# 7. Why Katz rigidity is unavailable

There are three independent obstructions.

## 7.1 Reducibility

The integrated equation has the constant solution. Its local system fits into
\[
0\longrightarrow\mathbf1
\longrightarrow M_{\mathrm{int}}
\longrightarrow M_K
\longrightarrow0.
\tag{7.1}
\]
It is therefore reducible. The standard Katz uniqueness theorem for rigid irreducible local systems cannot simply be applied to this extension.

## 7.2 The rigidity index is not 2

Remove the apparent point \(z=-1\). The five genuine local monodromies have the following Jordan types and centralizer dimensions:

\[
\begin{array}{c|c|c}
z&\text{Jordan type}&\dim Z(M_z)\\ \hline
0&1\oplus(-J_2)&3\\
\rho&J_2\oplus1&5\\
1&J_3&3\\
\rho^{-1}&J_2\oplus1&5\\
\infty&1\oplus(-J_2)&3.
\end{array}
\tag{7.2}
\]
For rank 3 and five punctures,
\[
\begin{aligned}
\operatorname{rig}
&=(2-5)3^2+(3+5+3+5+3)\\
&=-27+19\\
&=\boxed{-8}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{7.3}
\]
Including the apparent point adds one puncture and an identity centralizer of dimension 9, leaving the same value \(-8\).

Cohomological rigidity would require rigidity index 2, not \(-8\).

## 7.3 Exponents do not determine an extension class

Even for a rigid semisimple quotient, an extension
\[
0\to\mathbf1\to M\to M_K\to0
\]
contains extension data not recorded by the exponent sets. The transformation
\[
Y(k)\longmapsto Y(k)+C
\]
preserves the differential equation, the monodromy conjugacy classes, and every local exponent, but changes
\[
Y(1)=G
\quad\text{to}\quad G+C.
\]
Likewise, replacing a numerator sequence by
\[
P_n\longmapsto P_n+C Q_n
\]
preserves the recurrence and all local data while shifting the limiting ratio by \(C\).

Therefore no local-exponent table can identify the Catalan connection coefficient without initial-row matching.

# 8. What would actually complete the proof

A valid differential proof needs all of the following:

1. the exact normalized scalar recurrence extracted from the full \(3\times3\) matrix;
2. a certified minimal differential operator or a certified rank-three differential factor;
3. an explicit rational differential/Ore intertwiner between that factor and the integrated elliptic module;
4. exact matching of the two CMF initial rows with the normalized period section
   \[
   Y(0)=0,\qquad Y_k(0)=\frac\pi4;
   \]
5. a proof that the denominator row has a nonzero dominant coefficient.

Only Step 4 identifies
\[
Y(k(\rho))=Y(1)=G.
\]
Local exponents and rigidity do not replace it.

# 9. Sage code for exact verification

The following code reconstructs the scalar recurrence, verifies the formal corrections, builds the order-13 Euler operator, and checks the pulled-back indicial polynomials. It uses exact arithmetic only.

```python
from sage.all import *
from functools import reduce

# ================================================================
# A. Pullback operator and finite indicial polynomials
# ================================================================
K2.<rt2> = QuadraticField(2)
Rz.<z> = PolynomialRing(K2)
RT.<r> = PolynomialRing(K2)

rho = 17 - 12*rt2
rho_inv = 17 + 12*rt2
Delta = z^2 - 34*z + 1

a3 = 4*z^2*(1-z^2)^2*Delta
a2 = 8*z*(1-z^2)*(1-54*z+103*z^2+48*z^3-2*z^4)
a1 = 1-158*z+1127*z^2-852*z^3-529*z^4-110*z^5+9*z^6
a0 = Rz(0)


def falling(x, j):
    ans = RT(1)
    for k in range(j):
        ans *= x-k
    return ans


def valuation_and_lc_at(p, s):
    p = Rz(p)
    if p == 0:
        return None
    v = 0
    q = p
    while q(s) == 0:
        q, rem = q.quo_rem(z-s)
        assert rem == 0
        v += 1
    return v, K2(q(s))


def indicial_at(s):
    coeffs = [a0, a1, a2, a3]
    data = []
    for j, aj in enumerate(coeffs):
        if aj != 0:
            v, lc = valuation_and_lc_at(aj, s)
            data.append((j, v, lc))
    m = min(v-j for j, v, lc in data)
    I = sum(lc*falling(r, j)
            for j, v, lc in data if v-j == m)
    return I.factor()

print('z=0      :', indicial_at(K2(0)))
print('z=-1     :', indicial_at(K2(-1)))
print('z=rho    :', indicial_at(rho))
print('z=1      :', indicial_at(K2(1)))
print('z=rho^-1 :', indicial_at(rho_inv))

# Infinity has the same exponents as zero because
# k(1/z) = -k(z) up to the chosen square-root branch.

# ================================================================
# B. Printed CMF and scalar recurrence
# ================================================================
Rn.<n> = PolynomialRing(QQ)
Kn = Rn.fraction_field()


def M_of(x):
    m11 = (-2*x-5)*(x+3)^2*(
        136*x^4+1424*x^3+5548*x^2+9551*x+6141)
    m12 = (384*x^6+6384*x^5+44168*x^4+162698*x^3
           +336377*x^2+369933*x+169011)
    m13 = -(480*x^4+4980*x^3+19210*x^2+32690*x+20730)

    m21 = (x+2)^2*(x+3)^2*(4*x+10)*(
        48*x^3+386*x^2+1017*x+879)
    m22 = (x+2)^2*(-272*x^5-3848*x^4-21732*x^3
                    -61184*x^2-85761*x-47808)
    m23 = (x+2)^2*(320*x^3+2540*x^2+6610*x+5640)

    m31 = (-4*x-10)*(x+2)^2*(x+3)^2*(
        32*x^4+302*x^3+1037*x^2+1530*x+813)
    m32 = (x+2)^2*(192*x^6+2984*x^5+19116*x^4
                    +64452*x^3+120256*x^2+117279*x+46476)
    m33 = (x+2)^2*(-16*x^5-408*x^4-2912*x^3
                    -8884*x^2-12254*x-6240)

    return matrix(Kn, [
        [m11,m12,m13],
        [m21,m22,m23],
        [m31,m32,m33],
    ])


def matrix_shift(A, j):
    return matrix(Kn, A.nrows(), A.ncols(),
                  [Kn(a(n=n+j)) for a in A.list()])


def determinant_of_columns(cols):
    return matrix(Kn, 3, 3,
                  lambda i, j: cols[j][i]).det()


def primitive_scalar_recurrence(column=0):
    e = vector(Kn, [1 if i == column else 0 for i in range(3)])
    vec = [e]
    P = identity_matrix(Kn, 3)
    for j in range(1, 4):
        P = P*M_of(n+j-1)
        vec.append(P*e)

    c = []
    for j in range(4):
        cols = [vec[k] for k in range(4) if k != j]
        c.append((-1)^j*determinant_of_columns(cols))

    assert sum((c[j]*vec[j] for j in range(4)),
               vector(Kn, 3)) == 0

    common = Rn(1)
    for f in c:
        common = lcm(common, Rn(Kn(f).denominator()))
    p = [Rn(common*f) for f in c]
    g = reduce(gcd, p)
    p = [q//g for q in p]
    if p[-1].leading_coefficient() < 0:
        p = [-q for q in p]
    return p

c = primitive_scalar_recurrence(0)
print('raw degrees:', [p.degree() for p in c])
assert [p.degree() for p in c] == [28,21,14,7]

# ================================================================
# C. Poincare polynomial and first formal correction
# ================================================================
Rc.<cvar> = PolynomialRing(QQ)

a = []
b = []
for j, p in enumerate(c):
    d = 28-7*j
    a.append(QQ(p[d]))
    b.append(QQ(p[d-1]))

P = sum(a[j]*cvar^j for j in range(4))
```

# Final answer

The critical formal-correction check is:
\[
\boxed{
\sigma_0=33/2,\qquad \sigma_\pm=39/2
}
\]
in the ratio convention (0.4).

But the proposed conclusion is false:

\[
\boxed{
\begin{gathered}
\text{the standard normalized recurrence-to-ODE operator has order 13, not 3;}\\
\text{its local exponents do not match the pulled-back integrated equation;}\\
\text{the analytic Catalan section fails the CMF recurrence exactly;}\\
\text{and the integrated local system has rigidity index }-8.
\end{gathered}}
\]

The remaining viable route is an explicit nontrivial Ore/differential intertwiner together with exact initial-section matching. Local exponents plus Katz rigidity cannot replace those certificates.
