ANSWER Q9 00a96348

# Apéry collision energy: exact reductions, two audit corrections, and a new Racah/secant-direction program

## Executive assessment

I do not know a complete proof of

\[
E(p):=\sum_{a\in\mathbf F_p}N_p(a)^2\ll p,
\qquad
N_p(a)=\#\{0\le n<p:b_n=a\},
\]

from the presently established Apéry identities. The numerical law

\[
E(p)=3p+O(\sqrt p)
\]

is extraordinarily convincing, but none of the standard facts—palindromy, a large differential Galois group, the square/defect-square factorization of the generating polynomial, or one modular-form coefficient—alone supplies the required horizontal anti-concentration.

There is, however, a more specific route that I think deserves priority over another generic Weil-bound attempt:

> **Treat the Apéry numbers as diagonal Racah/6j coefficients, and seek a bounded-complexity model for the nonforced divided-difference curve.**

The route has three exact ingredients.

1. Palindromy implies that the interpolation polynomial has the form
   \[
   B_p(X)=Q_p(X(X+1)).
   \]
   Hence
   \[
   B_p(X+H)-B_p(X)
   =H(2X+H+1)D_p(X,H).
   \]
   The first two factors account for the diagonal and reflected collisions. The entire problem is exactly
   \[
   \#\{(X,H)\in\mathbf F_p^2:H\ne0,\ 2X+H+1\ne0,\ D_p(X,H)=0\}=O(p).
   \]

2. The Apéry number is the **diagonal of a symmetric Racah kernel**:
   \[
   b_n={}_4F_3\!\left(\begin{matrix}-n,n+1,-n,n+1\\1,1,1\end{matrix};1\right)
   =\mathcal R(n,n),
   \]
   where
   \[
   \mathcal R(r,s)
   ={}_4F_3\!\left(\begin{matrix}-r,r+1,-s,s+1\\1,1,1\end{matrix};1\right).
   \]
   Thus the hard curve is a diagonal divided difference of a self-dual, bispectral 6j-symbol kernel, not a generic degree-\(p\) polynomial curve.

3. The rank-two recurrence has an exact **secant-direction budget**. If \(f,g\) are fundamental solutions and
   \[
   v_n=(f_n,g_n),
   \]
   then the collision energy of any solution \(u_n=cf_n+dg_n\) is a fiber of the secant-direction map
   \[
   \Psi(n,m)=[-(g_n-g_m):f_n-f_m].
   \]
   Summing the collision energies over all \([c:d]\in\mathbf P^1(\mathbf F_p)\) gives an exact formula. Palindromy alone makes the average approximately \(3p\). The remaining task is to prove that the distinguished Apéry direction \([1:5]\) is not an exceptional rich direction.

These reductions do not yet prove the theorem. They identify two concrete missing lemmas that are much more specific than “apply Katz”:

- a **low-conductor/low-genus theorem for the Racah divided-difference curve**; or
- a **uniform secant-direction bound for the distinguished rank-two solution curve**.

Both are falsifiable by moderate computations, and either would prove \(E(p)=O(p)\).

Before developing them, two logical corrections are important.

# 0. Two audit corrections

## 0.1 Energy is not equivalent to uniform square-root cancellation

Let

\[
S_p(t)=\sum_{n=0}^{p-1}e_p(tb_n).
\]

Orthogonality gives

\[
\boxed{
E(p)=\frac1p\sum_{t\in\mathbf F_p}|S_p(t)|^2.
}
\tag{0.1}
\]

Since \(S_p(0)=p\), the conjecture \(E(p)\ll p\) is equivalent to

\[
\sum_{t\ne0}|S_p(t)|^2\ll p^2.
\tag{0.2}
\]

It is **not** equivalent to

\[
|S_p(t)|\ll\sqrt p
\qquad(t\ne0),
\tag{0.3}
\]

which is a substantially stronger uniform statement. Energy permits a sparse set of larger Fourier coefficients. This distinction matters strategically: a proof may exploit cancellation only after averaging over \(t\), without proving a Weil bound for each \(t\).

## 0.2 The all-\(n\) implication needs a separate moving-target step

The current repository note `working_notes/Q3.2_conditional_theorem.md` uses

\[
E(p)\ll p\Longrightarrow Z(p)\ll\sqrt p
\]

and then treats the event that a fixed moving digit lies in \(Z_p\) as having “probability” \(O(p^{-1/2})\) while \(p\) varies. That is not a deterministic consequence of the vertical cardinality bound. A family of sets \(Z_p\) of size \(\sqrt p\) can contain one prescribed moving residue for every prime.

Therefore, before describing \(E(p)\) as literally the single remaining gap in the all-\(n\) theorem, the downstream implication should be supplemented by a moving-target/large-prime incidence estimate. The energy conjecture remains an important and natural theorem, and may be a crucial input, but it does not by itself exclude cross-prime alignment.

This audit does not affect the internal question addressed here: prove \(E(p)\ll p\) for one fixed prime.

# 1. Exact shift decomposition: the right first target

Let \(B_p(X)\in\mathbf F_p[X]\) be the interpolation polynomial of degree at most \(p-1\) satisfying

\[
B_p(n)=b_n\qquad(n\in\mathbf F_p).
\]

For \(h\in\mathbf F_p\), define

\[
C_p(h)=\#\{x\in\mathbf F_p:B_p(x+h)=B_p(x)\}.
\tag{1.1}
\]

Every ordered pair \((x,y)\) has a unique difference \(h=y-x\), so

\[
\boxed{
E(p)=\sum_{h\in\mathbf F_p}C_p(h).
}
\tag{1.2}
\]

This is already the requested decomposition into \(p\) terms. The theorem follows from the strong statement

\[
C_p(h)=O(1)\qquad(h\ne0),
\tag{1.3}
\]

but only the averaged form

\[
\sum_{h\ne0}C_p(h)=O(p)
\tag{1.4}
\]

is necessary.

## 1.1 Palindromy gives an invariant-theory factorization

The identity

\[
b_{p-1-n}=b_n
\]

implies the polynomial identity

\[
B_p(-1-X)=B_p(X).
\tag{1.5}
\]

Indeed, the two degree-at-most-\(p-1\) polynomials agree at every point of \(\mathbf F_p\).

For odd \(p\), the invariant ring of the involution

\[
X\longmapsto-1-X
\]

is

\[
\mathbf F_p[X(X+1)].
\]

Consequently there is a polynomial \(Q_p\) of degree at most \((p-1)/2\) such that

\[
\boxed{B_p(X)=Q_p(U),\qquad U=X(X+1).}
\tag{1.6}
\]

This is stronger and cleaner than merely knowing that \(B_p(X)-B_p(Y)\) has the two obvious factors.

For an indeterminate \(H\), put

\[
U=X(X+1),
\qquad
V=(X+H)(X+H+1).
\]

Then

\[
V-U=H(2X+H+1),
\]

and therefore

\[
\boxed{
B_p(X+H)-B_p(X)
=H(2X+H+1)D_p(X,H),
}
\tag{1.7}
\]

where

\[
D_p(X,H)
=\frac{Q_p(V)-Q_p(U)}{V-U}
\in\mathbf F_p[X,H].
\tag{1.8}
\]

For each \(h\ne0\), the root

\[
x=-\frac{h+1}{2}
\]

is precisely the reflected collision \(x+h=-1-x\).

Define the nonforced collision set

\[
\mathcal C_p^{\mathrm{nf}}
=
\{(x,h)\in\mathbf F_p^2:
 h\ne0,
 2x+h+1\ne0,
 D_p(x,h)=0\}.
\]

Then the exact formula is

\[
\boxed{
E(p)=2p-1+\#\mathcal C_p^{\mathrm{nf}}.
}
\tag{1.9}
\]

The diagonal contributes \(p\); the reflection graph contributes another \(p\), and they meet once at \(x=(p-1)/2\).

Thus the empirical law \(E(p)\sim3p\) says, more sharply,

\[
\#\mathcal C_p^{\mathrm{nf}}\sim p.
\tag{1.10}
\]

This is the exact geometric target.

## 1.2 Connection with differential uniformity

In finite-field cryptography, a function \(F:\mathbf F_p\to\mathbf F_p\) is studied through

\[
\delta_F(h,c)
=\#\{x:F(x+h)-F(x)=c\}.
\]

Our quantity is the zero-differential spectrum

\[
C_p(h)=\delta_{B_p}(h,0).
\]

A planar function in odd characteristic has \(C_p(h)=1\) for every \(h\ne0\), which would give the minimal palindromic energy \(2p-1\). The Apéry function is empirically “almost planar at zero”: it has one forced derivative zero and about one additional root on average.

This terminology brings in a useful geometric toolkit—difference quotient curves, functional decomposition, exceptional planar functions, and CCZ-equivalence—but existing classification theorems generally require a fixed degree as \(p\to\infty\). Here \(\deg B_p\asymp p\), so they do not apply as black boxes.

A decisive computation is the complete spectrum

\[
\{C_p(h):h\ne0\},
\]

not just its sum. If

\[
\sup_{p,h\ne0}C_p(h)<\infty,
\tag{1.11}
\]

then the theorem is finished. If the maximum grows while the average remains bounded, the proof must preserve cancellation/average geometry rather than seek a uniform derivative bound.

# 2. A precise curve criterion

Let \(\widetilde{\mathcal C}_{p,i}\) be the normalizations of the geometrically irreducible components of the projective closure of the nonforced curve \(D_p=0\), after removing components contained in \(H=0\) or \(2X+H+1=0\).

## Proposition 2.1 — low-complexity curve criterion

Suppose there is an absolute \(R\) such that, for every \(p\):

1. the nonforced curve has at most \(R\) geometrically irreducible components defined over \(\mathbf F_p\);
2. the total number of points above the boundary and singular exceptional set is \(O(p)\); and
3. the total first Betti number satisfies
   \[
   \sum_i 2g(\widetilde{\mathcal C}_{p,i})=O(\sqrt p).
   \]

Then

\[
E(p)=O(p).
\]

If there is exactly one nonforced component and its genus and boundary complexity are \(O(1)\), then

\[
E(p)=3p+O(\sqrt p).
\]

### Proof

The Weil bound on each normalized component gives

\[
\#\widetilde{\mathcal C}_{p,i}(\mathbf F_p)
=p+O(g_i\sqrt p+1).
\]

Summing over the bounded number of components and adding the boundary/singularity corrections gives \(O(p)\). In the one-component bounded-genus case, the nonforced count is \(p+O(\sqrt p)\), and (1.9) gives the stated asymptotic. \(\square\)

The criterion explains why the naive bidegree/genus estimate fails: the plane model has degree comparable with \(p\). What is needed is not another bound in terms of that degree, but a **different normalization or cover whose actual topological complexity is small**.

The coefficient \(3\) makes a very concrete geometric prediction:

> The nonforced collision locus should behave as one geometrically connected one-dimensional family, after quotienting the obvious reflection symmetry.

This can be tested directly by factoring \(D_p(X,H)\) over \(\mathbf F_p(H)\), computing singularities, and estimating the genus of the normalization for moderate primes.

# 3. The Apéry-specific structure not yet exploited: a diagonal Racah kernel

The binomial summand has the hypergeometric identity

\[
\binom nk\binom{n+k}{k}
=
\frac{\prod_{j=0}^{k-1}(n(n+1)-j(j+1))}{(k!)^2}.
\tag{3.1}
\]

Define

\[
\Phi_k(U)
=
\frac{\prod_{j=0}^{k-1}(U-j(j+1))}{(k!)^2}.
\tag{3.2}
\]

Then

\[
\Phi_k(n(n+1))
=
\binom nk\binom{n+k}{k},
\]

with the convention that it vanishes for \(k>n\).

Consequently

\[
\boxed{
b_n=\sum_{k\ge0}\Phi_k(n(n+1))^2.}
\tag{3.3}
\]

Introduce the symmetric bivariate kernel

\[
\mathcal R(r,s)
=
\sum_{k\ge0}
\Phi_k(r(r+1))\Phi_k(s(s+1)).
\tag{3.4}
\]

Equivalently,

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal R(r,s)
={}_4F_3\!\left(
\begin{matrix}-r,r+1,-s,s+1\\1,1,1\end{matrix};1
\right).
}
\tag{3.5}
\]

The Apéry number is its diagonal:

\[
\boxed{b_n=\mathcal R(n,n).}
\tag{3.6}
\]

The expression in (3.5) is the \((\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\delta)=(0,0,0,0)\) Racah/6j kernel. Askey and Wilson's 1984 paper, *A recurrence relation generalizing those of Apéry*, places the Apéry recurrence precisely in the Racah/Wilson hierarchy. Racah kernels are self-dual and bispectral: each variable satisfies a second-order difference equation, and the 6j-symbol interpretation supplies tetrahedral and pentagon identities.

This is materially more structure than “a polynomial of degree \(p-1\).”

## 3.1 The proposed Racah divided-difference program

Write

\[
K(U,V)=\sum_k\Phi_k(U)\Phi_k(V).
\]

Then

\[
B_p(X)=K(U,U),
\qquad U=X(X+1),
\]

and the nonforced quotient is the diagonal divided difference

\[
\frac{K(V,V)-K(U,U)}{V-U}.
\tag{3.7}
\]

The concrete research question is:

> Does the diagonal divided difference of this self-dual Racah kernel admit a fixed-size determinant, a Christoffel--Darboux-type boundary expression, or a bounded-degree auxiliary cover?

For a standard orthogonal-polynomial reproducing kernel, Christoffel--Darboux converts a long sum into a \(2\times2\) determinant of boundary polynomials. The functions \(\Phi_k\) are in a Newton/Racah basis rather than an immediately orthonormal basis, so the ordinary formula cannot simply be quoted. But Racah polynomials possess their own second-order difference operator, duality, and 6j identities. Those are exactly the mechanisms from which a low-rank displacement identity could arise.

A successful identity might have one of the following forms:

\[
K(V,V)-K(U,U)
=(V-U)\frac{A(U,V,Z)}{B(U,V,Z)},
\qquad
P(U,V,Z)=0,
\tag{3.8}
\]

where \(A,B,P\) have degree bounded independently of \(p\), or

\[
K(V,V)-K(U,U)
=(V-U)\det
\begin{pmatrix}
F_1(U)&F_1(V)\\
F_2(U)&F_2(V)
\end{pmatrix}
\cdot U_p(U,V),
\tag{3.9}
\]

where \(U_p\) is a unit on the relevant finite-field domain and \(F_1,F_2\) come from a fixed-rank Racah difference module.

Either kind of formula would replace the high-degree plane curve by a bounded-conductor cover and make Proposition 2.1 applicable.

## 3.2 Why this route is plausible but uncertain

It is plausible because:

- the diagonal sum is a 6j/Racah coefficient, not a generic hypergeometric polynomial;
- the recurrence is the diagonal restriction of a bispectral system;
- the observed nonforced point count is exactly the size of one curve component;
- the degree-\(p\) plane model could have enormous singularity and a low-genus normalization hidden by its embedding.

It is uncertain because:

- fixed holonomic rank does not by itself bound finite-field value energy;
- a Christoffel--Darboux formula usually controls a sum over the polynomial degree while keeping the spectral variables independent, whereas here the degree and variable are identified on the diagonal;
- the relevant finite truncation reaches \(k\asymp p\), where boundary terms can retain characteristic-dependent complexity.

This is nevertheless a sharply testable avenue, unlike a general appeal to “big monodromy.”

## 3.3 Computations that should be run first

For primes small enough for exact symbolic work:

1. Express \(D_p\) in the variables
   \[
   U=X(X+1),\qquad V=(X+H)(X+H+1).
   \]
2. Factor the divided difference
   \[
   \frac{Q_p(V)-Q_p(U)}{V-U}
   \]
   over \(\mathbf F_p(U)\), \(\mathbf F_p(V)\), and \(\mathbf F_p(H)\).
3. Compute the singular locus and the genus of each normalization.
4. Compute the displacement rank of the coefficient matrix in the Racah basis \(\Phi_k\), not the monomial basis.
5. Use creative telescoping/Ore algebra to find the minimal bivariate difference ideal of
   \[
   \mathcal R(r,r)-\mathcal R(s,s).
   \]
6. Search explicitly for Biedenharn--Elliott/pentagon specializations that contain the diagonal difference.

If the normalization genus grows linearly or quadratically with \(p\), this route probably does not close by ordinary Weil. If it remains bounded or the curve has a bounded-degree parametrization, it is likely the proof.

References:

- R. Askey and J. A. Wilson, *A recurrence relation generalizing those of Apéry*, J. Austral. Math. Soc. Ser. A 36 (1984), 267--278, DOI 10.1017/S1446788700024691.
- R. Askey and J. Wilson, *A set of orthogonal polynomials that generalize the Racah coefficients or 6-j symbols*, SIAM J. Math. Anal. 10 (1979), 1008--1016.

# 4. A direct rank-two reformulation: secant directions

Let \(f,g\) be a fundamental basis of scalar solutions of the Apéry recurrence modulo \(p\), and write

\[
v_n=(f_n,g_n)\in\mathbf F_p^2.
\]

Every solution is

\[
u_n^{[c:d]}=cf_n+dg_n,
\qquad [c:d]\in\mathbf P^1(\mathbf F_p).
\]

The Apéry solution is one direction, for example \(b=f+5g\) in the repository's normalization.

For \(n,m\), if \(v_n\ne v_m\), define the secant-direction map

\[
\boxed{
\Psi(n,m)
=[-(g_n-g_m):f_n-f_m].
}
\tag{4.1}
\]

Then

\[
u_n^{[c:d]}=u_m^{[c:d]}
\iff
\Psi(n,m)=[c:d].
\tag{4.2}
\]

If \(v_n=v_m\), every solution direction collides.

Let

\[
D_{\mathrm{state}}
=\#\{(n,m):v_n=v_m\},
\]

and let \(E_{[c:d]}\) be the collision energy of \(u^{[c:d]}\). Summing over all solution directions gives the exact identity

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{[c:d]\in\mathbf P^1(\mathbf F_p)}E_{[c:d]}
=p^2+pD_{\mathrm{state}}.
}
\tag{4.3}
\]

### Proof

A nonzero difference \(v_n-v_m\) is annihilated by exactly one projective linear functional. A zero difference is annihilated by all \(p+1\) directions. Hence

\[
(p^2-D_{\mathrm{state}})\cdot1
+D_{\mathrm{state}}(p+1)
=p^2+pD_{\mathrm{state}}.
\]

## 4.1 Why the constant \(3\) appears without a random model

The established palindromy of both fundamental solutions gives

\[
v_{p-1-n}=v_n.
\]

Therefore

\[
D_{\mathrm{state}}\ge2p-1.
\]

If these are the only state repetitions, then the average over solution directions is

\[
\frac{p^2+p(2p-1)}{p+1}
=3p-4+\frac4{p+1}.
\tag{4.4}
\]

Thus \(3p\) is already the exact **direction-averaged** energy forced by rank two plus palindromy. This is a stronger structural explanation than the birthday heuristic.

The original theorem would follow from either of the following.

### Secant anti-concentration

\[
\boxed{
\#\Psi^{-1}(\xi)\ll p
\quad\text{for every }\xi\in\mathbf P^1(\mathbf F_p).
}
\tag{4.5}
\]

### Distinguished-direction typicality

\[
E_{[1:5]}
\ll
\frac1{p+1}\sum_{\xi}E_\xi.
\tag{4.6}
\]

The first is stronger but clean. The second uses only the Apéry direction.

## 4.2 What SL(2) monodromy would actually have to prove

The differential Galois or geometric monodromy group being \(\mathrm{SL}_2\) does not by itself prove (4.5). It says that the solution local system has no proper invariant algebraic subspace. It does not say that the ordered finite set

\[
\{v_0,\ldots,v_{p-1}\}
\]

has uniformly distributed secant slopes.

A directly useful monodromy theorem would need to construct the **secant sheaf** or difference correspondence associated with

\[
(n,m)\longmapsto[v_n-v_m]\in\mathbf P^1
\]

and prove bounded conductor plus geometric irreducibility after removing the diagonal and reflection components. Then Deligne's theorem on the corresponding two-dimensional family could control each direction fiber.

That is a materially different theorem from ordinary Sato--Tate for Frobenius traces.

## 4.3 A correction to the repository's zero-map equivalence

The zero map

\[
\varphi(n)=[-g_n:f_n]
\]

has the property that \(cf_n+dg_n=0\) precisely for \([c:d]=\varphi(n)\). Its collision energy counts pairs of indices at which **one homogeneous solution vanishes at both indices**, equivalently pairs with \(v_n\) and \(v_m\) proportional.

The original value energy counts

\[
cf_n+dg_n=cf_m+dg_m,
\]

which is governed by the **difference** map \(\Psi(n,m)\), not by \(\varphi\). Thus the statement that the zero-map energy is equivalent to \(E(p)\) needs correction. The secant-direction map (4.1) is the exact rank-two reformulation.

## 4.4 Decisive experiment

For each tested prime, compute all \(p+1\) energies

\[
E_{[c:d]}.
\]

Record:

- their mean and verify (4.3);
- their maximum;
- the percentile of the Apéry direction \([1:5]\);
- \(D_{\mathrm{state}}\) and whether state repetitions are only diagonal/reflection;
- the histogram of secant-direction fiber sizes.

If every direction has energy \(O(p)\) with a small absolute constant, the problem has become a finite-field incidence theorem for one explicit planar point set. If a few directions are much richer, identify their algebraic meaning before invoking monodromy.

# 5. A third-moment elimination route

Let

\[
M_3(p)=\sum_{a}N_p(a)^3
=\#\{(n_1,n_2,n_3):b_{n_1}=b_{n_2}=b_{n_3}\}.
\]

Cauchy--Schwarz gives

\[
E(p)=\sum_aN_p(a)^2
\le
\left(\sum_aN_p(a)^3\right)^{1/2}
\left(\sum_aN_p(a)\right)^{1/2}.
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{M_3(p)=O(p)\Longrightarrow E(p)=O(p).}
\tag{5.1}
\]

At first this seems harder. Rank two makes triple collisions algebraically special.

Let \(T_h(x)\) be the \(h\)-step transfer matrix taking the state at \(x\) to the state at \(x+h\), and let

\[
\ell_h(x)=e_1^t(T_h(x)-I).
\]

If

\[
b_x=b_{x+h}=b_{x+k},
\]

then the nonzero state vector at \(x\) is annihilated by both \(\ell_h(x)\) and \(\ell_k(x)\). Hence

\[
\boxed{
\Theta_{h,k}(x)
:=\det\begin{pmatrix}\ell_h(x)\\\ell_k(x)\end{pmatrix}=0.
}
\tag{5.2}
\]

After clearing denominators, \(\Theta_{h,k}\) is a universal polynomial independent of the chosen solution. This is the collision analogue of the gap polynomial: **two equality conditions eliminate the unknown rank-two state**.

The naive degree is \(O(h+k)\) after the appropriate cubic denominator bookkeeping, so summing root bounds over all \((h,k)\) is far too expensive. The route becomes useful only if the Racah/6j structure forces a dramatic factorization of \(\Theta_{h,k}\), or if the family of triple-gap curves has a bounded-complexity common normalization.

Concrete tasks:

1. compute \(\Theta_{h,k}\) for small \(h,k\);
2. remove diagonal and palindromic factors;
3. factor over \(\mathbf Q\) and over generic finite fields;
4. compare the residual factors with Racah pentagon identities;
5. test whether their genus/rank is bounded as \(h,k\) grow.

A bounded residual complexity would give a moment proof that bypasses individual additive character sums.

# 6. A useful self-adjoint form of the recurrence

The recurrence coefficient has the identity

\[
34n^3+51n^2+27n+5
=n^3+(n+1)^3+4(2n+1)^3.
\]

Thus the Apéry recurrence can be written as

\[
\boxed{
(n+1)^3(b_{n+1}-b_n)
-n^3(b_n-b_{n-1})
=4(2n+1)^3b_n.
}
\tag{6.1}
\]

This is a discrete Sturm--Liouville/Jacobi form. It explains the symmetric transfer structure and is compatible with the Racah interpretation.

Over \(\mathbf R\), such a form gives oscillation and interlacing. Over \(\mathbf F_p\), there is no order, so it does not immediately bound repeated values. Its likely role is structural: derive the correct Lax pair, Green kernel, or Christoffel--Darboux identity needed in Section 3.

# 7. Why the other advertised structures are not yet sufficient

## 7.1 Generating polynomial versus interpolation polynomial

Two different polynomials occur:

\[
A_p(t)=\sum_{n=0}^{p-1}b_nt^n
\]

and

\[
B_p(X)\quad\text{with}\quad B_p(n)=b_n.
\]

The CFVMZ square/defect-square theorem concerns \(A_p(t)\), the **generating variable**. The collision curve concerns \(B_p(X)\), the **index interpolation variable**. A factorization of the first does not factor the second.

CFVMZ prove, according to \(p\bmod24\),

\[
A_p(t)=C_p(t)^2
\]

or

\[
A_p(t)=(t^2-34t+1)C_p(t)^2.
\]

The additional assertion that all irreducible factors have degree at most two is an empirical splitting observation, not a consequence of the square theorem itself, and should be stated separately until proved.

## 7.2 Squareness alone permits maximal energy

Even reciprocal square polynomials can have almost all coefficients equal. For example,

\[
(1+t^{(p-1)/2})^2
\]

has only three nonzero coefficient positions. Its coefficient-value energy is quadratic in \(p\).

Therefore convolution

\[
b_n=\sum_k\beta_k\beta_{n-k}
\]

helps only if one proves an additional anti-concentration theorem for the particular square root \((\beta_k)\). Sparse support, reciprocal symmetry, and low splitting degree do not supply that theorem.

## 7.3 The modular form controls one coordinate

The congruence involving

\[
b_{(p-1)/2}
\]

and the coefficient of the weight-four level-eight newform is valuable arithmetic information about the central index. Collision energy involves all \(p\) indices. Sato--Tate for one Hecke trace does not control the growing vector

\[
(b_0,\ldots,b_{p-1})\bmod p.
\]

A modular approach would need a fixed bounded-conductor family whose Frobenius trace at a parameter encoding \(n\) is exactly \(b_n\bmod p\), together with a local-limit theorem for equality of two such traces. No such horizontal realization is currently established.

## 7.4 The Picard--Fuchs rank must be stated carefully

The usual Apéry generating differential operator is of order three. It is related to the symmetric square of an underlying rank-two system. The \(\mathrm{SL}_2\) statement belongs to that underlying system; the raw Apéry local system is rank three and naturally has an orthogonal/symmetric-square representation.

This distinction does not invalidate the monodromy philosophy, but it matters when constructing the actual sheaf on a collision or secant parameter space.

## 7.5 Closed transfer products do not imply mixing

The identity that the product of all one-step matrices closes after one \(p\)-block does not imply that the deterministic prefix path is equidistributed. A deterministic sequence of generators can generate all of \(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbf F_p)\) while its prescribed prefixes remain concentrated.

A useful warning is the scalar recurrence

\[
u_{n+1}=(n+1)u_n,
\]

whose orbit is \(u_n=n!\bmod p\). It has extremely simple degree-one nonautonomous steps, yet proving that \(n!\) occupies a positive proportion of \(\mathbf F_p\) is far beyond current results; the best unconditional lower bounds are only of square-root scale. See Grebennikov--Sagdeev--Semchankau--Vasilevskii, *On the sequence \(n!\bmod p\)*, arXiv:2204.01153.

Thus low-degree steps, group generation, and a closed orbit are not enough. The special Racah/6j structure must be used.

## 7.6 No evident recurrence from \(p\) to a smaller prime

The recurrence is in the index \(n\), while reduction modulo \(p\) changes the characteristic, the interpolation polynomial, and the finite state space. Lucas and Dwork congruences relate digit levels for one fixed \(p\); they do not yield a descent

\[
E(p)\longrightarrow E(p')
\]

for a smaller prime \(p'\).

# 8. The random palindromic constant, exactly

There are

\[
M=\frac{p-1}{2}
\]

reflection pairs of weight \(2\), and one central fixed index of weight \(1\). Assign independent uniform values in \(\mathbf F_p\) to these \(M+1\) orbits.

The self-pair contribution to energy is

\[
4M+1=2p-1.
\]

For two distinct orbits, equality occurs with probability \(1/p\). The total ordered cross weight is

\[
p^2-(2p-1).
\]

Hence

\[
\boxed{
\mathbf E E(p)
=2p-1+\frac{p^2-(2p-1)}p
=3p-3+\frac1p.
}
\tag{8.1}
\]

This is the exact null constant. It matches the decomposition (1.9): the first \(2p-1\) collisions are forced, and the random nonforced locus contributes about \(p\).

The numerical ratio \(3.000021\) at \(p\approx10^{10}\) is spectacular evidence for this model. It does not identify the proof mechanism; both the low-genus curve model and the secant-direction model predict the same constant.

# 9. A prioritized experimental program

The following experiments distinguish the candidate mechanisms rather than merely extending the range of \(E(p)\).

## 9.1 Differential spectrum

For each prime, compute

\[
C_p(h)=\#\{x:b_{x+h}=b_x\}
\]

for every \(h\ne0\). Record:

- \(\max_h C_p(h)\);
- the histogram of \(C_p(h)-1\);
- correlations with quadratic characters of \(h\), \(h+1\), and the singular parameters;
- whether the average nonforced count is \(1+O(p^{-1/2})\).

A bounded maximum suggests a direct differential-uniformity theorem. A slowly growing maximum but stable mean points to an irreducible-curve/trace proof.

## 9.2 Geometry of the nonforced curve

For moderate \(p\):

- construct \(Q_p\) and \(D_p\);
- factor \(D_p\) over \(\mathbf F_p(H)\);
- compute singularities and normalization genus;
- count components defined over \(\mathbf F_p\);
- compare \(\#\mathcal C_p^{\mathrm{nf}}-p\) with Frobenius traces of those components.

If the error sequence itself matches a fixed low-rank motive, that would reveal the hidden cover.

## 9.3 Racah-basis displacement rank

Represent the divided difference in the basis \(\Phi_j(U)\Phi_k(V)\). Compute the rank of the coefficient matrix and its displacement rank under the Racah second-order operators.

Bounded displacement rank is the signature of a Christoffel--Darboux/Lax identity even when the monomial degree grows.

## 9.4 All solution directions

Compute \(E_{[c:d]}\) for all \(p+1\) solutions. Verify (4.3), and measure

\[
\max_{[c:d]}\frac{E_{[c:d]}}p.
\]

If the maximum remains bounded and the Apéry direction is unexceptional, seek a direction-uniform incidence proof. If some directions have much higher energy, determine whether they correspond to algebraic, polynomial, or reducible solutions.

## 9.5 State repetitions and secant fibers

Compute

\[
D_{\mathrm{state}}=\#\{(n,m):v_n=v_m\}
\]

and factor out the forced reflection graph. Then compute the fibers of \(\Psi\). This tells whether the obstacle is already present in the rank-two state curve or only after projection to the Apéry direction.

## 9.6 Triple-gap determinants

Construct \(\Theta_{h,k}(x)\) from (5.2) for small gaps. Search for universal factors, restart identities, and Racah-pentagon patterns. A dramatic stable factorization would justify pursuing the third-moment route.

## 9.7 Functional decomposition and planar-function tests

Apply Ritt decomposition to \(Q_p\), and test whether \(B_p\) is linearly or CCZ-equivalent to a known low-differential-uniformity function. The observed image size near \((1-e^{-1/2})p\) argues against a simple permutation/Dickson map, but a bounded-degree outer factor or hidden exceptional quotient is still possible.

# 10. Paper-ready sufficient criteria

## Proposition 10.1 — zero-differential criterion

If there is an absolute \(C\) such that, for every prime \(p\) and every \(h\ne0\),

\[
\#\{x\in\mathbf F_p:b_{x+h}=b_x\}\le C,
\]

then

\[
E(p)\le p+C(p-1)=O(p).
\]

## Proposition 10.2 — averaged differential criterion

It is enough to prove

\[
\sum_{h\ne0}
\#\{x\ne-(h+1)/2:D_p(x,h)=0\}
\ll p.
\]

Then (1.9) gives \(E(p)=O(p)\).

## Proposition 10.3 — Racah curve criterion

If the normalization of the nonforced diagonal Racah divided-difference curve has \(O(1)\) components and total compact Betti number \(O(\sqrt p)\), uniformly in \(p\), then \(E(p)=O(p)\). If it is one bounded-genus component, then

\[
E(p)=3p+O(\sqrt p).
\]

## Proposition 10.4 — secant-direction criterion

Let \(v_n=(f_n,g_n)\) and \(\Psi\) be defined by (4.1). If

\[
\#\Psi^{-1}([1:5])\ll p
\]

and the state-equality count is \(O(p)\), then the Apéry value energy is \(O(p)\).

These statements isolate exactly what has to be shown. None follows from big monodromy alone.

# 11. Which route is most promising?

My ranking is:

## 1. Racah divided-difference / low-conductor cover

**Best chance of an existing-technology proof if the hidden identity exists.** It uses the most specific structure available: self-duality, bispectrality, and 6j-symbol identities. The decisive first step is computational algebra, not abstract monodromy.

## 2. Secant-direction geometry of the rank-two solution curve

**Best direct use of \(\mathrm{SL}_2\).** The exact average formula explains the constant \(3\). A proof still needs a two-parameter secant sheaf or incidence theorem; ordinary monodromy is insufficient.

## 3. Triple-collision elimination

**Potentially useful if the universal determinant factors.** Rank two removes the unknown state after two equality constraints. Without a new factorization, degree summation is too weak.

## 4. Crystalline/Mellin local limit

**Conceptually correct but presently a breakthrough-level theorem.** Bounded rank and complex equidistribution do not control exact reduction modulo the same prime at probability scale \(1/p\). Jacobi-sum examples show that even fixed rank one and optimal Weil bounds can coexist with a positive density of \(p\)-divisible twists.

## Routes unlikely to close the problem alone

- generic Weil/Lang--Weil applied to the degree-\(p\) plane model;
- the square factorization of the generating polynomial without a theorem on its square root;
- Sato--Tate for the single modular-form coefficient;
- group generation or random-walk expansion for a deterministic nonautonomous word;
- a recurrence relating \(E(p)\) to \(E(p')\).

# Final recommendation

The next theorem to try to prove is not a generic exponential-sum estimate. It is:

> **Racah anti-collision lemma.** After writing the Apéry diagonal as the Racah kernel \(\mathcal R(n,n)\), the nonforced divided difference
> \[
> \frac{\mathcal R(x+h,x+h)-\mathcal R(x,x)}{h(2x+h+1)}
> \]
> is represented by a curve or finite cover of uniformly bounded cohomological complexity.

This lemma would imply \(E(p)=O(p)\) by Weil, and a one-component bounded-genus version would explain the observed asymptotic \(3p+O(\sqrt p)\).

If the genus computations disprove bounded complexity, shift immediately to the secant-direction formulation and test whether all solution directions have uniformly bounded energy. That experiment will determine whether \(\mathrm{SL}_2\) can act as a genuine proof mechanism or is only a heuristic slogan.

The important conceptual change is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{Do not study }B_p\text{ as a generic degree-}p\text{ polynomial.}
}
\]

Study it as

\[
\boxed{
\text{the diagonal of a self-dual Racah/6j kernel, and study its secants.}
}
\]

That is the most concrete unexploited structure I can identify.
