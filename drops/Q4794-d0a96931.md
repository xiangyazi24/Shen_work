# Problem 3.2: what is known about \(p\)-adic digit laws, reflection, and horizontal sieving for Apéry-like sequences

## Executive verdict

As of July 2026, there is a strong and fairly systematic **local/digital theory** for classical Apéry numbers and for many Apéry-like sequences that are factorial-ratio multisums, diagonals, or constant terms of Laurent-polynomial powers. There is not yet a comparably general **pointwise horizontal large-sieve theorem** proving, for every fixed \(n\), a bound of the form

\[
\sum_{n^{\theta}<p\le n\atop A_{n\bmod p}\equiv0\pmod p}\log p=o(n).
\tag{1}
\]

That missing horizontal statement is genuinely different from the existing Lucas, Dwork, formal-group, Hasse-invariant, and Frobenius-distribution results.

Four corrections are essential.

1. The assertion that the classical Apéry valuations belong to a fixed finite set such as
   
   \[
   \nu_p(A_n)\in\{0,1,2,4\}
   \]
   
   is false. For fixed \(p\), the valuations can grow without bound as the number of special base-\(p\) digits grows.

2. What is known digit-wise is primarily a **lower bound** on \(v_p(A_n)\), together with finite-automaton descriptions modulo \(p^s\) for fixed \(p,s\). There is no general exact Granville-style formula for all primes and all Apéry-like sequences.

3. The reflection
   
   \[
   A_{p-1-r}\equiv A_r\pmod p
   \tag{2}
   \]
   
   is rigorous for the classical Apéry numbers. It gives reflection symmetry of the **mod-\(p\) zero set**, but not automatically equality of the full valuations.

4. A Conservative Matrix Field by itself does not imply Lucas congruences, automata, modularity, or a horizontal sieve. Those consequences require an additional realization—typically as a diagonal, a factorial-ratio sum, a Laurent-polynomial constant term, or a geometric Picard–Fuchs system.

The most realistic proof architecture for the gcd problem is therefore

```text
exact Casoratian/Wronskian
        -> local upper bounds for common p-adic content
        -> split small and large primes
        -> Lucas/Dwork reduction for large primes
        -> new horizontal discrepancy estimate for moving residue n mod p.
```

The first three steps are available in important cases. The last step is the present research frontier.

# 1. First fix the normalization

The statement of Problem 3.2 must specify exactly which two solutions are being used and which power of the least common multiple clears denominators.

For the classical Apéry recurrence

\[
(n+1)^3u_{n+1}
=(2n+1)(17n^2+17n+5)u_n-n^3u_{n-1},
\tag{3}
\]

the integral Apéry solution is

\[
A_n=\sum_{k=0}^n\binom nk^2\binom{n+k}{k}^2,
\qquad A_0=1,\ A_1=5.
\tag{4}
\]

A rational companion solution usually requires a denominator-clearing factor involving

\[
L_n^3,
\qquad L_n=\operatorname{lcm}(1,\ldots,n),
\]

not merely \(L_n\). For another CMF, the needed exponent may be different. Thus a claim about

\[
\gcd(d_na_n,d_nb_n)
\]

is meaningful only after the exact exponent in \(d_n\), the recurrence, and the initial conditions are fixed.

There is also a terminology point: a polynomial bound for the gcd would be more than enough, because

\[
G_n\le n^C
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\log G_n=O(\log n)=o(n).
\]

The difficulty is not that polynomial growth would be insufficient. The difficulty is proving any uniform bound substantially below \(e^{cn}\).

# 2. Classical Apéry numbers: the exact local picture

## 2.1 Lucas congruences

The classical Apéry numbers satisfy the \(p\)-Lucas congruence for every prime \(p\):

\[
A_{a+bp}\equiv A_aA_b\pmod p,
\qquad 0\le a<p.
\tag{5}
\]

Consequently, if

\[
n=n_0+n_1p+\cdots+n_mp^m,
\qquad 0\le n_j<p,
\]

then

\[
\boxed{
A_n\equiv\prod_{j=0}^m A_{n_j}\pmod p.
}
\tag{6}
\]

If

\[
Z_p:=\{r\in\{0,\ldots,p-1\}:A_r\equiv0\pmod p\},
\]

then

\[
p\mid A_n
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
n_j\in Z_p\text{ for at least one base-}p\text{ digit }n_j.
\tag{7}
\]

This is a complete description of divisibility by \(p\), but not of the exact value of \(v_p(A_n)\).

## 2.2 Delaygue's digit-count valuation theorem

Delaygue proved Beukers' valuation conjectures and a general theorem for suitable factorial-ratio multisums. In the Apéry case, the theorem gives a lower bound

\[
\boxed{
v_p(A_n)\ge \alpha_p(n),}
\tag{8}
\]

where \(\alpha_p(n)\) counts the base-\(p\) digits of \(n\) that lie in \(Z_p\).

For example,

\[
Z_5=\{1,3\},
\qquad
Z_{11}=\{5\}.
\tag{9}
\]

Hence, if

\[
n_m=1+5+5^2+\cdots+5^{m-1}=\frac{5^m-1}{4},
\]

then every base-\(5\) digit is \(1\), and Delaygue's theorem gives

\[
\boxed{v_5(A_{n_m})\ge m.}
\tag{10}
\]

Likewise, if

\[
m_s=5(1+11+\cdots+11^{s-1}),
\]

then

\[
v_{11}(A_{m_s})\ge s.
\tag{11}
\]

Therefore the valuations are not confined to a fixed finite set.

The important limitation is that (8) is generally a lower bound, not an exact universal formula. Additional carries and higher congruences can increase the valuation.

## 2.3 Automata modulo prime powers

Rowland and Yassawi proved that, when a sequence is the diagonal of a rational power series, one can algorithmically construct a finite automaton computing the sequence modulo \(p^\alpha\), for all but finitely many primes \(p\). They applied this framework to Apéry numbers and other combinatorial sequences.

Thus, for each fixed pair \((p,\alpha)\), the set

\[
\{n:A_n\equiv0\pmod{p^\alpha}\}
\]

is \(p\)-automatic and can in principle be analyzed exactly as a regular language in the base-\(p\) digits of \(n\).

This is powerful local information, but the number and structure of automaton states depend on \(p\) and \(\alpha\). It does not supply a uniform estimate as \(p\to\infty\), and it does not by itself control the moving condition \(A_{n\bmod p}\equiv0\pmod p\) for one fixed large \(n\).

## 2.4 Lucas congruences modulo \(p^2\)

Krattenthaler, Rowland, and Yassawi proved Lucas-type congruences modulo \(p^2\) for Apéry numbers when the base-\(p\) digits lie in a specified admissible set. This is substantially stronger than (5), but it is not a complete modulo-\(p^2\) digit theorem for arbitrary digits.

Therefore the current local hierarchy is roughly:

```text
mod p:        complete Lucas product;
valuation:    strong digit-count lower bounds;
mod p^s:      finite automata for fixed p,s;
mod p^2:      explicit stronger formulas on restricted digit sets;
uniform p:    no complete exact valuation law.
```

# 3. Reflection symmetry is rigorous modulo \(p\)

Let \(p\) be prime and \(0\le r<p\). We prove

\[
\boxed{A_{p-1-r}\equiv A_r\pmod p.}
\tag{12}
\]

Start with

\[
A_{p-1-r}
=
\sum_{k=0}^{p-1-r}
\binom{p-1-r}{k}^2
\binom{p-1-r+k}{k}^2.
\tag{13}
\]

For \(k\le r\),

\[
\binom{p-1-r}{k}
\equiv(-1)^k\binom{r+k}{k}\pmod p,
\tag{14}
\]

and

\[
\binom{p-1-r+k}{k}
\equiv(-1)^k\binom rk\pmod p.
\tag{15}
\]

For \(k>r\), the second binomial coefficient in (13) is divisible by \(p\), because one of its numerator factors is \(p\). Hence all terms with \(k>r\) vanish modulo \(p\). Squaring removes the signs, so

\[
A_{p-1-r}
\equiv
\sum_{k=0}^{r}
\binom{r+k}{k}^2\binom rk^2
=A_r\pmod p.
\]

Therefore

\[
r\in Z_p
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
p-1-r\in Z_p.
\tag{16}
\]

Equivalently, the truncated polynomial

\[
H_p(x)=\sum_{r=0}^{p-1}A_rx^r
\tag{17}
\]

is reciprocal modulo \(p\):

\[
\boxed{x^{p-1}H_p(x^{-1})\equiv H_p(x)\pmod p.}
\tag{18}
\]

This is the rigorous form of the reflection symmetry.

It does **not** imply

\[
v_p(A_{p-1-r})=v_p(A_r)
\]

as an equality of full valuations. Establishing such an equality would require congruences modulo arbitrarily high powers of \(p\), not merely (12).

For a general Apéry-like sequence, a reflection can follow from one of the following:

- a binomial or factorial-ratio representation with a complementary-index identity;
- a recurrence invariant under \(n\mapsto-1-n\) modulo \(p\);
- self-duality of the associated difference module plus matching boundary values;
- a reciprocal truncated Hasse polynomial.

None of these properties follows from the CMF axioms alone.

# 4. How far the theory extends beyond classical Apéry numbers

## 4.1 Factorial-ratio multisums

Delaygue developed an effective criterion, using Landau functions and differential-equation hypotheses, for factorial-ratio sequences to have the \(p\)-Lucas property. His valuation theorem applies to many Apéry-like sequences, including Apéry, Domb, Franel, and related multisums.

This is the closest existing result to a Granville-style theory for a broad class of Apéry-like objects.

## 4.2 Constant terms of Laurent-polynomial powers

Mellit and Vlasenko proved Dwork congruences for

\[
b_n=[\Lambda(\mathbf x)^n]_{\mathbf x^0}
\]

when the Newton polytope of \(\Lambda\) has the origin as its unique interior lattice point. Their theorem yields Lucas congruences and higher \(p\)-power congruences, as well as a \(p\)-adic unit-root function.

Gorodetsky found constant-term realizations for all fifteen sporadic Apéry-like sequences of Zagier, Almkvist–Zudilin, and Cooper. For fourteen of the fifteen, the Newton-polytope condition gives a strong Lucas property; the same framework also produces valuation lower bounds.

## 4.3 Diagonals

For diagonals of rational functions, the Rowland–Yassawi automaton method applies modulo fixed prime powers. Straub's multivariate Apéry construction and related work explain many supercongruences through diagonal representations.

## 4.4 Arbitrary D-finite and CMF sequences

There is no theorem saying that every D-finite sequence arising from a CMF is a diagonal or a factorial-ratio sum. Even the broad philosophy that every globally bounded D-finite series should be a diagonal is a deep unresolved circle of questions.

The 2025 CMF paper constructs CMFs from finite-dimensional Ore modules and formulates several arithmetic observations as conjectures; it does not prove a general Lucas or valuation theorem for CMF trajectories.

Therefore the implication

```text
CMF  -> p-Lucas  -> digit valuation law
```

is false without extra hypotheses.

A usable arithmetic theorem must begin with a certified realization such as

```text
CMF trajectory = diagonal / constant term / factorial-ratio sum / geometric period.
```

# 5. The modular and \(p\)-adic geometry: what it gives and what it does not

Modular or geometric origin is extremely useful locally.

For suitable Picard–Fuchs families, one obtains:

- a crystalline Frobenius matrix;
- a unit-root subcrystal on the ordinary locus;
- a Hasse invariant or Hasse–Witt polynomial;
- Dwork congruences for truncations;
- formal-group explanations of supercongruences;
- special information at CM or supersingular primes.

For the Apéry Laurent-polynomial family, Mellit and Vlasenko explain that

\[
H_p(t)=\sum_{r=0}^{p-1}A_rt^r\pmod p
\tag{19}
\]

is a Hasse invariant in the geometric parameter \(t\); its nonvanishing detects ordinary fibers, and Dwork's quotient of truncations gives the unit root.

But the condition needed in the gcd problem is

\[
A_r\equiv0\pmod p,
\tag{20}
\]

which says that one **coefficient** of the Hasse polynomial vanishes. It is not the geometric condition

\[
H_p(t)=0
\]

at a fiber \(t\in\mathbf F_p\).

This distinction is decisive. Frobenius equidistribution and Sato–Tate naturally count fibers for which a trace, determinant, or Hasse invariant takes a specified value. They do not automatically count zero coefficients of a polynomial whose degree itself grows with \(p\).

Recent work of Caruso, Fürnsinn, Vargas-Montoya, and Zudilin computes Galois groups of reductions modulo \(p\) of Apéry-like generating series. This gives important information about the algebraic functions obtained after reduction. It does not yet give a uniform density theorem for the zero coefficients \(A_r\bmod p\).

CM can help if the bad-prime condition is genuinely a Frobenius condition for a fixed motive or a fixed algebraic family. In the present moving-coefficient problem, that geometric reinterpretation is exactly what is missing.

# 6. Why standard large-sieve theorems do not directly prove the needed estimate

Kowalski's large sieve for Frobenius controls the distribution of Frobenius conjugacy classes as a geometric parameter varies in a family over a finite field. Fouvry–Kowalski–Michel prove power-saving estimates for sums of fixed algebraic trace functions over the primes.

Those frameworks require a uniformly bounded-conductor sheaf or family and a condition expressible through its Frobenius traces.

Our event is

\[
n\bmod p\in Z_p,
\qquad
Z_p=\{r:A_r\equiv0\pmod p\}.
\tag{21}
\]

Both the modulus and the set \(Z_p\) vary with the summation prime. No fixed sheaf of bounded conductor is currently known whose trace at \(r\in\mathbf F_p\) has zero set \(Z_p\) uniformly in \(p\).

Thus the phrase “horizontal large sieve” is an accurate description of the missing theorem, but it is not presently a standard theorem one can quote.

## 6.1 A precise sufficient horizontal estimate

For a dyadic interval \(P<p\le2P\), a sufficient estimate would be

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{P<p\le2P}
\left(
\mathbf 1_{\,n\bmod p\in Z_p}
-rac{|Z_p|}{p}
\right)\log p
\ll P^{1-\eta}
}
\tag{22}
\]

uniformly in the relevant range of \(n\), for some fixed \(\eta>0\), together with a vertical estimate on \(|Z_p|\).

If \(|Z_p|=O(1)\), the expected main term in each dyadic block is \(O(1)\), and (22) would give the desired \(o(n)\) after summation over \(P\ge n^{2/3}\).

## 6.2 Fourier formulation

Write

\[
\mathbf1_{Z_p}(r)
=
\frac{|Z_p|}{p}
+
\frac1p\sum_{h\ne0}
\widehat{Z_p}(h)e^{2\pi ihr/p},
\tag{23}
\]

where

\[
\widehat{Z_p}(h)=\sum_{z\in Z_p}e^{-2\pi ihz/p}.
\]

The pointwise problem becomes a sum over varying primes and varying additive characters

\[
\sum_p\frac{\log p}{p}
\sum_{h\ne0}\widehat{Z_p}(h)e^{2\pi ihn/p}.
\tag{24}
\]

A proof would need both:

1. uniform square-root-type information on the Fourier transforms of \(Z_p\);
2. cancellation in the prime sum with the modulus equal to the summation prime.

At present neither ingredient is available in a uniform geometric form for Apéry coefficient-zero sets.

# 7. Vertical zero-density results would give an almost-all theorem

Suppose one could prove

\[
|Z_p|\ll p^{1-\delta}
\tag{25}
\]

for some \(\delta>0\). This would already imply strong average results in \(n\), even without the horizontal discrepancy estimate.

Indeed, for \(X\le n\le2X\), define

\[
\mathcal Z(n)
=
\sum_{X^{2/3}<p\le2X\atop n\bmod p\in Z_p}\log p.
\]

Then

\[
\begin{aligned}
\sum_{X\le n\le2X}\mathcal Z(n)
&\ll
\sum_{X^{2/3}<p\le2X}
\log p\left(\frac{X|Z_p|}{p}+|Z_p|\right)\\
&\ll X^{2-\delta+o(1)}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{26}
\]

Hence

\[
\frac1X\sum_{X\le n\le2X}\mathcal Z(n)
\ll X^{1-\delta+o(1)}=o(X).
\tag{27}
\]

This proves the desired support estimate for almost all \(n\) in each dyadic interval.

If one had the much stronger generic expectation

\[
|Z_p|=O(1),
\tag{28}
\]

then the average size of \(\mathcal Z(n)\) would be only \(O(\log X)\).

But neither (25) nor (28) is presently known uniformly for the classical Apéry coefficient-zero sets. More importantly, an almost-all result still does not prove the required pointwise statement for every \(n\).

# 8. The gcd problem: the standard local-to-global reduction

Let

\[
X_n=D_na_n,
\qquad
Y_n=D_nb_n,
\qquad
G_n=\gcd(X_n,Y_n),
\]

with the exact denominator-clearing factor \(D_n\). Put

\[
c_p(n)=v_p(G_n).
\]

## 8.1 Casoratian or Wronskian control

For two solutions of a second-order recurrence, the discrete Wronskian satisfies a first-order product formula. In the classical Apéry companion system one obtains an identity of the form

\[
a_nb_{n-1}-a_{n-1}b_n=\frac{C}{n^3},
\tag{29}
\]

with a fixed nonzero rational constant \(C\).

After clearing denominators, this implies

\[
c_p(n)+c_p(n-1)
\le
v_p\!\left(\frac{C D_nD_{n-1}}{n^3}\right).
\tag{30}
\]

Thus each fixed-prime common valuation is at most logarithmic in \(n\). For higher-order systems the same role is played by the determinant of a fundamental solution matrix, although extracting a bound for a selected pair of coordinates can require additional minors.

This is the right uniform mechanism for the **depth** of the common divisibility.

## 8.2 Small primes

If

\[
c_p(n)\ll1+\log_p n,
\tag{31}
\]

then, for \(y=n^\theta\),

\[
\sum_{p\le y}c_p(n)\log p
\ll\pi(y)\log n
\ll_\theta y.
\tag{32}
\]

Hence every fixed \(\theta<1\) gives

\[
\sum_{p\le n^\theta}c_p(n)\log p=o(n).
\tag{33}
\]

The small-prime contribution is therefore not the obstruction.

## 8.3 Large primes

For \(p>n^\theta\), the Wronskian bound usually makes \(c_p(n)\) uniformly bounded. The remaining contribution is then

\[
\ll
\sum_{n^\theta<p\le n\atop p\mid G_n}\log p.
\tag{34}
\]

Lucas or logarithmic-Lucas congruences often reduce the support condition \(p\mid G_n\) to

\[
A_{n\bmod p}\equiv0\pmod p,
\tag{35}
\]

possibly with an explicitly controlled exceptional condition involving the higher base-\(p\) digits.

This is exactly where the horizontal sieve is needed.

## 8.4 Conditional theorem

A clean sufficient theorem is the following.

> Assume:
> 
> 1. \(c_p(n)\le C(1+\log_p n)\) for all \(p,n\);
> 2. for \(p>n^{2/3}\), the condition \(p\mid G_n\) implies \(n\bmod p\in Z_p\), apart from a set with total logarithmic weight \(o(n)\);
> 3. the horizontal estimate
>    
>    \[
>    \sum_{n^{2/3}<p\le n\atop n\bmod p\in Z_p}\log p=o(n)
>    \]
>    
>    holds.
> 
> Then
> 
> \[
> \log G_n=o(n),
> \qquad
> G_n=e^{o(n)}.
> \]

The proof is immediate from the small/large-prime split above.

# 9. Why denominator lemmas in irrationality proofs are not enough

Classical Beukers, Rhin–Viola, Rivoal, and Zudilin constructions often prove statements such as

\[
L_n^s q_n\in\mathbf Z,
\qquad
L_n^s p_n\in\mathbf Z.
\tag{36}
\]

These are **denominator-clearing** results. They do not estimate

\[
\gcd(L_n^sp_n,L_n^sq_n).
\]

In hypergeometric constructions one can sometimes improve the arithmetic by dividing by an explicit product of primes. Such correction factors come from exact factorial-ratio valuations, Landau functions, or carefully optimized group actions on parameters. They are highly construction-specific.

There is no general “prime-free denominator” theorem for arbitrary CMFs.

A uniform CMF theorem would need at least:

1. an integral lattice in the Ore module;
2. a formula for its determinant/Casoratian;
3. a primitive reduction theorem modulo \(p\);
4. a horizontal sparsity theorem for the primes where the selected trajectory becomes nonprimitive.

Only the first two are formal consequences of sufficiently explicit difference-module data. The last two are arithmetic and geometric.

# 10. What modularity can realistically contribute

A modular parametrization can help in four ways.

## 10.1 Local Frobenius matrices

It can identify the differential equation with a geometric \(F\)-crystal and produce a vector-valued Dwork congruence rather than only a scalar Lucas congruence.

## 10.2 Unit-root and supersingular structure

It can identify exceptional primes or geometric parameters through the ordinary/supersingular decomposition. For CM fibers, these primes can sometimes be described by splitting conditions in an imaginary quadratic field.

## 10.3 Deligne bounds

If the required indicator can be represented by a bounded-conductor trace function, Deligne's Riemann-Hypothesis bounds can give square-root cancellation in vertical parameter sums.

## 10.4 Effective Chebotarev

If simultaneous divisibility of the numerator and denominator can be translated into an algebraic condition on a Frobenius conjugacy class of a fixed Galois representation, then effective Chebotarev becomes available.

But none of these steps is automatic. For the Apéry coefficient condition, the missing bridge is an identity of the form

\[
A_r\bmod p
=
\operatorname{tr}\bigl(\operatorname{Frob}_r\mid\mathcal F_p\bigr)
\tag{37}
\]

for a family of sheaves \(\mathcal F_p\) whose conductor is bounded independently of \(p\). No such uniform trace-function realization is currently known.

Without (37), the modular origin explains supercongruences but does not solve the horizontal support problem.

# 11. Answers to the four questions

## (1) Do Granville-type digit results extend to general CMFs?

For classical Apéry numbers, there is:

- a complete mod-\(p\) Lucas product;
- Delaygue's digit-count lower bound for valuations;
- automata modulo fixed \(p^s\);
- stronger modulo-\(p^2\) results for restricted digit sets.

For many factorial-ratio, diagonal, and Laurent-constant-term Apéry-like sequences, analogous Lucas and valuation theorems exist.

For a general CMF sequence, no. A CMF must first be shown to belong to one of those arithmetic classes.

## (2) Can the reflection be made rigorous?

Yes, for classical Apéry numbers:

\[
A_{p-1-r}\equiv A_r\pmod p.
\]

The proof is the binomial calculation in Section 3. It yields reciprocal symmetry of the truncated polynomial and reflection of the zero set.

It does not prove equality of full \(p\)-adic valuations. For a general CMF, reflection requires a separately proved self-duality or reciprocal identity.

## (3) Is there a uniform gcd method for CMFs?

There is a uniform **framework**, but not a finished general theorem:

```text
integral lattice
+ Casoratian/Wronskian
+ local p-adic congruences
+ horizontal support estimate.
```

The first three can often be derived from an explicit diagonal, constant-term, or modular realization. The horizontal estimate remains sequence-specific and currently open even in the classical coefficient-zero formulation.

## (4) Does modularity or CM solve the valuation problem?

It gives strong local structure—Frobenius, unit roots, Hasse invariants, formal groups, and supercongruences. CM can give precise splitting laws for supersingular primes of fixed fibers.

It does not directly control which **coefficients** \(A_r\) vanish modulo \(p\). To obtain the needed gcd estimate, one must still convert coefficient vanishing into a fixed-family Frobenius or trace-function condition and then prove a horizontal sieve estimate.

# 12. A concrete research target

The next theorem worth targeting is not an exact valuation formula. It is the following uniform discrepancy statement.

> **Horizontal Apéry-zero conjecture.** There exists \(\eta>0\) such that, uniformly for \(P\ge n^{2/3}\),
> 
> \[
> \sum_{P<p\le2P}
> \left(
> \mathbf1_{A_{n\bmod p}\equiv0\pmod p}
> -\frac{|Z_p|}{p}
> \right)\log p
> \ll P^{1-\eta}.
> \tag{38}
> \]

A viable proof program would be:

1. derive a uniform algebraic description of the functions
   
   \[
   r\longmapsto A_r\pmod p,
   \qquad r\in\mathbf F_p;
   \]

2. prove bounded conductor or controlled monodromy for that description;
3. obtain vertical estimates for \(|Z_p|\) and its additive Fourier coefficients;
4. combine them with a dispersion or large-sieve argument in the prime aspect;
5. insert the result into the Wronskian reduction of Section 8.

The recent Galois-group results for reductions of Apéry-like generating series may provide part of Step 1, but they do not yet provide Steps 2–4.

---

# 13. Bottom line

The state of the art is asymmetric:

- **local and digit-wise:** highly developed;
- **modular and crystalline:** highly developed for geometric realizations;
- **coefficient-zero density as \(p\to\infty\):** largely unknown;
- **pointwise moving-residue sieve:** no general theorem presently available.

Thus the desired estimate

\[
\gcd(D_na_n,D_nb_n)=e^{o(n)}
\]

is not presently a formal consequence of Lucas congruences, Dwork theory, Sato–Tate, or the CMF formalism. The correct remaining obstacle is a horizontal support theorem for the large common prime divisors.

The finite set \(\{0,1,2,4\}\) should not be used as a premise for classical Apéry valuations. The rigorous replacements are:

\[
v_p(A_n)\ge\alpha_p(n),
\]

fixed-\(p\) automaticity modulo \(p^s\), the reflection congruence (12), and the Wronskian-based local upper bound for the common gcd.
