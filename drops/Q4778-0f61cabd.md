# Problem 2.5: invariant conic, first-order obstruction, and determinant normalization

## Executive result

Use the balanced cocycle

\[
B(n)=\frac{1}{-16n^7}D_n^{-1}M(n)D_{n+1},
\qquad
D_n=\operatorname{diag}(1,n+1,(n+1)^2),
\]

so that

\[
B(n)=C+\frac{C_1}{n}+O(n^{-2}).
\]

The leading equation

\[
C^T J_0 C=J_0
\]

has a two-dimensional pencil of symmetric solutions:

\[
\boxed{
J_0(u,v)=
\begin{pmatrix}
u&0&-v\\
0&-2u+v&0\\
-v&0&2v
\end{pmatrix}.}
\tag{0.1}
\]

Its determinant is

\[
\boxed{\det J_0(u,v)=-v(2u-v)^2.}
\tag{0.2}
\]

A convenient smooth split member is

\[
\boxed{
J_{\mathrm{split}}=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&0&-1\\
0&1&0\\
-1&0&2
\end{pmatrix},}
\tag{0.3}
\]

corresponding to the conic

\[
Y^2-2XZ+2Z^2=0.
\]

It gives the exact leading symmetric-square conjugacy

\[
\boxed{CW_0=W_0\operatorname{Sym}^2(A_0),}
\tag{0.4}
\]

with

\[
W_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&0&2\\
0&2&0\\
0&0&2
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
A_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
-3&4\\
2&-3
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad \det A_0=1.
\tag{0.5}
\]

However, **no nondegenerate member** of (0.1) extends as

\[
J(n)=J_0+\frac{J_1}{n}+O(n^{-2}).
\]

The obstruction is exact:

* the determinant of \(B(n)\) forces the conic multiplier to have first coefficient \(37/n\);
* the fixed third eigenline forces the coefficient \(33/n\).

Thus the requested regular conic does not exist. The correct formal conic is singular at infinity. Its projective shape must be

\[
\boxed{
J(n)=J_{\mathrm{mid}}+\kappa n^{-6}J_{\mathrm{pair}}+\cdots,}
\tag{0.6}
\]

where

\[
J_{\mathrm{mid}}=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&0&-2\\
0&0&0\\
-2&0&4
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
J_{\mathrm{pair}}=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&0&0\\
0&-2&0\\
0&0&0
\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{0.7}
\]

This six-power degeneration is exactly what the formal exponent gap

\[
\frac{39}{2}-\frac{33}{2}=3
\]

predicts: a vector gauge loses three powers on the middle mode, and its quadratic form loses six.

Finally, if

\[
M(n)V(n+1)=V(n)\,\rho(n)\operatorname{Sym}^2(A(n)),
\tag{0.8}
\]

then the determinant factorization permits the minimal choice

\[
\boxed{\det V(n)=(n+1)(n+2)(2n+3)^2(2n+5)^2,}
\tag{0.9}
\]

for which

\[
\boxed{
\rho(n)\det A(n)
=-2(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(2n+5)(2n+7)^2.}
\tag{0.10}
\]

Only the product \(\rho\det A\) is fixed; \(\rho\) and \(\det A\) may be redistributed by a rank-two scalar normalization.

# 1. Complete solution of the leading equation

Write

\[
J=
\begin{pmatrix}
a&b&c\\
b&d&e\\
c&e&f
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

For

\[
C=
\begin{pmatrix}
17&-24&0\\
-12&17&0\\
8&-12&1
\end{pmatrix},
\]

the equation \(C^TJC=J\) gives

\[
b=0,\qquad e=0,\qquad c=-\frac f2,\qquad d=-2a+\frac f2.
\]

Putting \(u=a\) and \(v=f/2\) gives (0.1). Its determinant is

\[
(-2u+v)(2uv-v^2)=-v(2u-v)^2.
\]

Therefore:

* \(v=0\) gives a rank-two form;
* \(2u=v\) gives a rank-one form;
* all other members are smooth conics.

A zero determinant means a singular conic, not a split smooth conic. The member (0.3) is smooth and split because \([1:0:0]\) is a rational point.

Its rational parametrization is

\[
(X,Y,Z)=(s^2+2t^2,\,2st,\,2t^2).
\tag{1.1}
\]

# 2. Leading Veronese factorization

In the basis \((s^2,st,t^2)\),

\[
\operatorname{Sym}^2(A_0)=
\begin{pmatrix}
9&-24&16\\
-6&17&-12\\
4&-12&9
\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{2.1}
\]

Direct multiplication gives

\[
CW_0
=
\begin{pmatrix}
17&-48&34\\
-12&34&-24\\
8&-24&18
\end{pmatrix}
=W_0\operatorname{Sym}^2(A_0).
\tag{2.2}
\]

Equivalently, with

\[
\nu(z)=(z^2+2,2z,2)^T,
\]

one has

\[
C\nu(z)=(2z-3)^2\nu\!\left(\frac{4-3z}{2z-3}\right).
\tag{2.3}
\]

Thus the limiting matrix really is a rational symmetric square. The obstruction begins at order \(1/n\).

# 3. First-order obstruction

Assume a regular expansion

\[
J(n)=J_0+\frac{J_1}{n}+O(n^{-2}),
\qquad
\Lambda(n)=1+\frac{\lambda_1}{n}+O(n^{-2}),
\]

satisfying

\[
B(n)^TJ(n+1)B(n)=\Lambda(n)J(n).
\tag{3.1}
\]

The reversed index convention gives the same first-order conclusion because \(J(n+1)-J(n)=O(n^{-2})\).

## 3.1 Determinant calculation

One has

\[
C^{-1}=
\begin{pmatrix}
17&24&0\\
12&17&0\\
8&12&1
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

Using the supplied \(C_1\),

\[
\operatorname{tr}(C^{-1}C_1)
=-\frac{291}{2}+\frac{339}{2}+\frac{63}{2}
=\frac{111}{2}.
\]

Hence

\[
\det B(n)=1+\frac{111}{2n}+O(n^{-2}).
\tag{3.2}
\]

Taking determinants in (3.1), and using

\[
\frac{\det J(n+1)}{\det J(n)}=1+O(n^{-2})
\]

for nondegenerate \(J_0\), gives

\[
3\lambda_1=111,
\qquad
\boxed{\lambda_1=37.}
\tag{3.3}
\]

## 3.2 The third eigenline

Let \(e_3=(0,0,1)^T\). Then

\[
Ce_3=e_3,
\qquad
C_1e_3=
\begin{pmatrix}
30\\-20\\63/2
\end{pmatrix},
\]

and

\[
J_0(u,v)e_3=
\begin{pmatrix}
-v\\0\\2v
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

The \((3,3)\) entry of the first-order equation is

\[
2(C_1e_3)^TJ_0e_3
=\lambda_1(e_3^TJ_0e_3).
\]

The left side is

\[
2(-30v+63v)=66v,
\]

whereas the right side is \(2v\lambda_1\). Every nondegenerate member has \(v\neq0\), so

\[
\boxed{\lambda_1=33.}
\tag{3.4}
\]

This contradicts (3.3). Multiplying \(J(n)\) by a common power \(n^\gamma\) shifts both values by the same amount and does not remove the gap \(37-33=4\).

Therefore there is no invariant conic with a finite nondegenerate constant leading term.

# 4. The singular formal conic

Let the left eigenvectors be

\[
\ell_0=(-1,0,2),
\qquad
\ell_\pm=(1,\pm\sqrt2,0).
\]

The two degenerate generators are

\[
J_{\mathrm{mid}}=\ell_0^T\ell_0,
\]

and

\[
J_{\mathrm{pair}}
=\frac12(\ell_+^T\ell_-+\ell_-^T\ell_+).
\]

The formal exponent corrections from Q4772 are

\[
\sigma_0=\frac{33}{2},
\qquad
\sigma_+=\sigma_-=\frac{39}{2}.
\]

Therefore the induced first-order weights on the quadratic forms are

\[
2\sigma_0=33
\]

and

\[
\sigma_++\sigma_-=39.
\]

If

\[
J(n)=a_nJ_{\mathrm{pair}}+b_nJ_{\mathrm{mid}}+\cdots
\]

has a single projective multiplier, then

\[
\frac{b_{n+1}/a_{n+1}}{b_n/a_n}
=1+\frac6n+O(n^{-2}),
\]

so

\[
\boxed{\frac{b_n}{a_n}\asymp n^6.}
\tag{4.1}
\]

After normalizing \(b_n=1\), this gives (0.6).

Moreover,

\[
\det(J_{\mathrm{mid}}+\varepsilon J_{\mathrm{pair}})
=-8\varepsilon^2.
\tag{4.2}
\]

Thus

\[
\det J(n)\asymp n^{-12}.
\tag{4.3}
\]

If \(J(n)=V(n)^{-T}J_{\mathrm{std}}V(n)^{-1}\), then

\[
\det J(n)=\frac{\det J_{\mathrm{std}}}{\det V(n)^2},
\]

and therefore

\[
\boxed{\det V(n)\asymp n^6.}
\tag{4.4}
\]

This agrees with the exact determinant class below.

# 5. What the determinant of \(M(n)\) tells us about \(\rho(n)\)

Take determinants in

\[
M(n)V(n+1)=V(n)\,\rho(n)\operatorname{Sym}^2(A(n)).
\]

Since

\[
\det(\operatorname{Sym}^2A)=(\det A)^3,
\]

writing \(h(n)=\det V(n)\) gives

\[
[\rho(n)\det A(n)]^3
=\det M(n)\frac{h(n+1)}{h(n)}.
\tag{5.1}
\]

Choose

\[
h(n)=(n+1)(n+2)(2n+3)^2(2n+5)^2.
\tag{5.2}
\]

Then

\[
\frac{h(n+1)}{h(n)}
=\frac{n+3}{n+1}\frac{(2n+7)^2}{(2n+3)^2},
\]

and the given determinant becomes

\[
\begin{aligned}
\det M(n)\frac{h(n+1)}{h(n)}
={}&-8(n+2)^6(n+3)^6\\
&\times(2n+5)^3(2n+7)^6.
\end{aligned}
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{
\det M(n)\frac{h(n+1)}{h(n)}
=\Delta(n)^3,}
\]

with

\[
\boxed{
\Delta(n)=-2(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(2n+5)(2n+7)^2.}
\tag{5.3}
\]

Hence

\[
\boxed{\rho(n)\det A(n)=\Delta(n).}
\tag{5.4}
\]

Its asymptotic expansion is

\[
\boxed{
\Delta(n)
=-16n^7\left(1+\frac{39}{2n}+O(n^{-2})\right).}
\tag{5.5}
\]

This is precisely the common scalar correction of the two outer modes. The middle mode's \(33/2\) correction must come from the singular, mode-dependent gauge.

Only the product \(\rho\det A\) is fixed. For example:

* if \(\det A(n)=1\), then \(\rho(n)=\Delta(n)\);
* for the standard Delannoy companion
  \[
  A_D(n)=
  \begin{pmatrix}
  \dfrac{3(2n+1)}{n+1}&-\dfrac{n}{n+1}\\[2mm]
  1&0
  \end{pmatrix},
  \qquad \det A_D(n)=\frac{n}{n+1},
  \]
  one has
  \[
  \rho(n)=\Delta(n)\frac{n+1}{n};
  \]
* if the \(4i\) twist is included in \(A_D\), then
  \[
  \det(4iA_D(n))=-16\frac{n}{n+1},
  \]
  and
  \[
  \rho(n)=-\frac{\Delta(n)}{16}\frac{n+1}{n}.
  \]

# 6. Consequence for the Ore-intertwiner computation

The data \(C,C_1\) prove:

1. the limiting matrix is exactly a rational symmetric square;
2. no intertwiner with a finite invertible leading matrix can work;
3. any valid gauge must be singular at infinity;
4. it must shift the middle mode by three powers relative to the outer modes;
5. its determinant has a natural degree-six representative (5.2).

Therefore the conic search must not use a generic ansatz

\[
J_0+J_1/n+\cdots
\]

with nondegenerate \(J_0\). It should use a degree-profiled ansatz whose projective leading terms are

\[
J_{\mathrm{mid}}+n^{-6}(\text{nonzero multiple of }J_{\mathrm{pair}})+\cdots.
\]

For a scalar Ore intertwiner

\[
T=t_0(n)+t_1(n)S+t_2(n)S^2,
\]

the same exponent gap means that its leading symbol must vanish on the middle Poincaré root \(S=1\) through enough successive degree layers to lower that mode by three powers, while remaining nonzero at \(17\pm12\sqrt2\).

Once a rational conic \(J(n)\) with this degree profile is found, parameterize it and recover \(A(n)\) by evaluating the induced action on three points of the conic. The determinant identity (5.4) then fixes the remaining scalar normalization.

## Final verdict

The explicit leading answer is the pencil (0.1), and the split member (0.3) gives the exact leading matrix \(A_0\). But the first correction proves that the requested regular nondegenerate expansion does not exist. The finite certificate, if it exists, must be a singular degree-six coboundary, with determinant and scalar product constrained by (5.2)--(5.5).
