# Problem 2.5: invariant-conic calculation and the determinant normalization

## Executive conclusion

Let

\[
B(n)=\frac{1}{-16n^7}D_n^{-1}M(n)D_{n+1},
\qquad
D_n=\operatorname{diag}(1,n+1,(n+1)^2),
\]

so that

\[
B(n)=C+\frac{C_1}{n}+O(n^{-2})
\]

with the matrices in the question.

The leading conic equation

\[
C^T J_0C=J_0
\]

does **not** have a unique solution. Its complete symmetric solution space is the two-dimensional pencil

\[
\boxed{
J_0(u,v)=
\begin{pmatrix}
u&0&-v\\
0&-2u+v&0\\
-v&0&2v
\end{pmatrix}.}
\tag{0.1}
\]

Its determinant is

\[
\boxed{\det J_0(u,v)=-v(2u-v)^2.}
\tag{0.2}
\]

A particularly convenient smooth split member is

\[
\boxed{
J_{\rm split}=J_0(0,1)=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&0&-1\\
0&1&0\\
-1&0&2
\end{pmatrix},}
\tag{0.3}
\]

whose conic is

\[
Y^2-2XZ+2Z^2=0.
\]

It has the rational parametrization

\[
(X,Y,Z)=(s^2+2t^2,\,2st,\,2t^2).
\]

At the **leading-matrix level** one therefore has the exact rational symmetric-square conjugacy

\[
\boxed{CW_0=W_0\operatorname{Sym}^2(A_0),}
\tag{0.4}
\]

where

\[
W_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&0&2\\
0&2&0\\
0&0&2
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
A_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
-3&4\\
2&-3
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad \det A_0=1.
\tag{0.5}
\]

However, this split conic does **not** extend as a regular nondegenerate expansion

\[
J(n)=J_0+\frac{J_1}{n}+\cdots.
\]

In fact no nondegenerate member of the pencil (0.1) does. The first correction gives an exact obstruction:

* the determinant of \(B(n)\) forces the conic multiplier to have coefficient \(37/n\);
* the \((3,3)\) entry of the conic equation forces the coefficient \(33/n\).

Thus

\[
\boxed{
\text{there is no invariant conic with a finite, nondegenerate constant leading term.}}
\tag{0.6}
\]

This does **not** yet rule out a singular rational coboundary. Rather, it determines its required shape. The two degenerate invariant forms have first-order weights \(39\) and \(33\), so their coefficient ratio must vary like \(n^6\). Projectively, the correct formal form must begin as

\[
\boxed{
J(n)=J_{\rm mid}+\kappa n^{-6}J_{\rm pair}+\cdots,}
\tag{0.7}
\]

where

\[
J_{\rm mid}=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&0&-2\\
0&0&0\\
-2&0&4
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
J_{\rm pair}=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&0&0\\
0&-2&0\\
0&0&0
\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{0.8}
\]

The leading form in (0.7) is rank one; the transverse component first appears six powers later. Therefore a genuine \(V(n)\) cannot have a finite invertible limit after the balancing used in Q4772. It must be polynomially sheared.

The determinant factorization gives a very concrete normalization for that shearing. If

\[
M(n)V(n+1)=V(n)\,\rho(n)\operatorname{Sym}^2(A(n)),
\tag{0.9}
\]

and \(h(n)=\det V(n)\), then

\[
[\rho(n)\det A(n)]^3
=\det M(n)\frac{h(n+1)}{h(n)}.
\tag{0.10}
\]

A minimal polynomial representative of the required determinant class is

\[
\boxed{
h(n)=(n+1)(n+2)(2n+3)^2(2n+5)^2.}
\tag{0.11}
\]

It gives the exact cube

\[
\boxed{
\det M(n)\frac{h(n+1)}{h(n)}
=\left[-2(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(2n+5)(2n+7)^2\right]^3.}
\tag{0.12}
\]

Consequently the determinant data determine

\[
\boxed{
\rho(n)\det A(n)
=-2(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(2n+5)(2n+7)^2,}
\tag{0.13}
\]

up to multiplication by a rational coboundary/cube normalization. They do **not** determine \(ho\) and \(\det A\) separately.

# 1. Solving the leading invariant-form equation

Write a general symmetric matrix as

\[
J=
\begin{pmatrix}
a&b&c\\
b&d&e\\
c&e&f
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

The leading matrix is

\[
C=
\begin{pmatrix}
17&-24&0\\
-12&17&0\\
8&-12&1
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

Solving

\[
C^TJC=J
\]

gives

\[
b=0,\qquad e=0,\qquad c=-\frac f2,\qquad d=-2a+\frac f2.
\]

Putting \(u=a\) and \(v=f/2\) yields exactly (0.1):

\[
J_0(u,v)=
\begin{pmatrix}
u&0&-v\\
0&-2u+v&0\\
-v&0&2v
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

The determinant is

\[
(-2u+v)(2uv-v^2)=-v(2u-v)^2.
\]

Therefore:

* \(v=0\) gives a rank-two form;
* \(2u=v\) gives a rank-one form;
* every other member is a smooth conic.

A zero determinant means a **singular** conic, not a split smooth conic. A smooth conic is split when it has a rational point. The member \(J_{\rm split}\) in (0.3) is both nondegenerate and split because \([1:0:0]\) lies on it.

# 2. Exact leading symmetric-square conjugacy

For

\[
A_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
-3&4\\
2&-3
\end{pmatrix},
\]

the symmetric square in the basis \((s^2,st,t^2)\) is

\[
\operatorname{Sym}^2(A_0)=
\begin{pmatrix}
9&-24&16\\
-6&17&-12\\
4&-12&9
\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{2.1}
\]

A direct multiplication gives

\[
CW_0
=
\begin{pmatrix}
17&-48&34\\
-12&34&-24\\
8&-24&18
\end{pmatrix}
=W_0\operatorname{Sym}^2(A_0).
\tag{2.2}
\]

Projectively, \(A_0\) acts on \(z=s/t\) by

\[
z\longmapsto\frac{4-3z}{2z-3}.
\]

Indeed, if

\[
\nu(z)=(z^2+2,2z,2)^T,
\]

then

\[
C\nu(z)=(2z-3)^2\nu\!\left(\frac{4-3z}{2z-3}\right).
\]

Thus the limiting matrix really is a rational symmetric square. The obstruction appears only at the next asymptotic order.

# 3. First-order obstruction to a regular nondegenerate conic

Assume

\[
J(n)=J_0+\frac{J_1}{n}+O(n^{-2}),
\qquad
\Lambda(n)=1+\frac{\lambda_1}{n}+O(n^{-2}),
\]

and use the equation, in the orientation stated in the question,

\[
B(n)^TJ(n+1)B(n)=\Lambda(n)J(n).
\tag{3.1}
\]

The same first-order conclusion is obtained with the reversed index convention, because \(J(n+1)-J(n)=O(n^{-2})\) for a regular expansion.

## 3.1 The determinant forces \(\lambda_1=37\)

The inverse of \(C\) is

\[
C^{-1}=
\begin{pmatrix}
17&24&0\\
12&17&0\\
8&12&1
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

Using the supplied \(C_1\),

\[
\operatorname{tr}(C^{-1}C_1)
=-\frac{291}{2}+\frac{339}{2}+\frac{63}{2}
=\frac{111}{2}.
\]

Hence

\[
\det B(n)=1+\frac{111}{2n}+O(n^{-2}).
\tag{3.2}
\]

Taking determinants in (3.1) gives

\[
\det B(n)^2\det J(n+1)
=\Lambda(n)^3\det J(n).
\]

If \(J_0\) is nondegenerate, then

\[
\frac{\det J(n+1)}{\det J(n)}=1+O(n^{-2}).
\]

Therefore

\[
3\lambda_1=2\cdot\frac{111}{2}=111,
\]

so

\[
\boxed{\lambda_1=37.}
\tag{3.3}
\]

## 3.2 The fixed eigenline forces \(\lambda_1=33\)

Let \(e_3=(0,0,1)^T\). Since

\[
Ce_3=e_3,
\qquad
C_1e_3=
\begin{pmatrix}
30\\-20\\63/2
\end{pmatrix},
\]

and

\[
J_0(u,v)e_3=
\begin{pmatrix}
-v\\0\\2v
\end{pmatrix},
\]

the \((3,3)\) entry of the first-order equation is

\[
2(C_1e_3)^TJ_0e_3
=\lambda_1(e_3^TJ_0e_3).
\]

The left side is

\[
2(-30v+63v)=66v,
\]

while the right side is \(2v\lambda_1\). For a nondegenerate member of the pencil, \(v\ne0\), and hence

\[
\boxed{\lambda_1=33.}
\tag{3.4}
\]

Equations (3.3) and (3.4) contradict each other. Thus no smooth member of the leading pencil extends with a regular nondegenerate constant leading term.

Multiplying \(J(n)\) by a common scalar \(n^\gamma\) does not remove the contradiction: it shifts both determinant and entrywise values of \(\lambda_1\) by the same \(\gamma\), leaving the difference \(37-33=4\) unchanged.

# 4. What replaces the regular conic

The invariant pencil has two distinguished degenerate generators.

Let

\[
\ell_0=(-1,0,2),
\qquad
\ell_\pm=(1,\pm\sqrt2,0).
\]

Then

\[
J_{\rm mid}=\ell_0^T\ell_0
=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&0&-2\\
0&0&0\\
-2&0&4
\end{pmatrix},
\tag{4.1}
\]

and

\[
J_{\rm pair}
=\frac12(\ell_+^T\ell_-+\ell_-^T\ell_+)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&0&0\\
0&-2&0\\
0&0&0
\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{4.2}
\]

The formal exponent corrections from Q4772 are

\[
\sigma_0=\frac{33}{2},
\qquad
\sigma_+=\sigma_-=rac{39}{2}.
\]

Therefore the induced first-order multipliers on the two quadratic forms are

\[
2\sigma_0=33
\]

for \(J_{\rm mid}\), and

\[
\sigma_++\sigma_-=39
\]

for \(J_{\rm pair}\).

For a moving linear combination

\[
J(n)=a_nJ_{\rm pair}+b_nJ_{\rm mid}+\cdots
\]

to have one common projective multiplier, its coefficient ratio must satisfy

\[
\frac{b_{n+1}/a_{n+1}}{b_n/a_n}
=1+\frac6n+O(n^{-2}).
\]

Hence

\[
\boxed{\frac{b_n}{a_n}\asymp n^6.}
\tag{4.3}
\]

After normalizing the middle coefficient to one, this gives (0.7):

\[
J(n)=J_{\rm mid}+\kappa n^{-6}J_{\rm pair}+\cdots.
\]

This is a singular perturbation of a rank-one form. Indeed,

\[
\det(J_{\rm mid}+\varepsilon J_{\rm pair})=-8\varepsilon^2.
\tag{4.4}
\]

Thus

\[
\det J(n)\asymp n^{-12}.
\tag{4.5}
\]

If \(J(n)\) is obtained by pulling back a fixed standard discriminant form through a gauge \(V(n)\), then

\[
\det J(n)=\frac{\det J_{\rm std}}{\det V(n)^2}.
\]

Equation (4.5) therefore predicts

\[
\boxed{\det V(n)\asymp n^6,}
\tag{4.6}
\]

which agrees exactly with the degree of the determinant normalization found below.

This is the main computational lesson for the Ore-intertwiner search: an ansatz whose coefficients all have the same degree at infinity will miss the intertwiner. The map must lose three powers on the middle eigenmode, equivalently six powers on its quadratic form.

# 5. Determinant normalization and the scalar twist

Take determinants in

\[
M(n)V(n+1)=V(n)\,\rho(n)\operatorname{Sym}^2(A(n)).
\]

Because

\[
\det(\operatorname{Sym}^2A)=(\det A)^3,
\]

one obtains

\[
\det M(n)\det V(n+1)
=\det V(n)\,[\rho(n)\det A(n)]^3.
\]

Writing \(h(n)=\det V(n)\),

\[
[\rho(n)\det A(n)]^3
=\det M(n)\frac{h(n+1)}{h(n)}.
\tag{5.1}
\]

The non-cube exponents in

\[
\det M(n)
=-8(n+1)(n+2)^6(n+3)^5(2n+3)^2(2n+5)^3(2n+7)^4
\]

can be removed by the shift quotient of

\[
h(n)=(n+1)(n+2)(2n+3)^2(2n+5)^2.
\]

Indeed,

\[
\frac{h(n+1)}{h(n)}
=\frac{n+3}{n+1}\frac{(2n+7)^2}{(2n+3)^2},
\]

and therefore

\[
\begin{aligned}
\det M(n)\frac{h(n+1)}{h(n)}
={}&-8(n+2)^6(n+3)^6\\
&\times(2n+5)^3(2n+7)^6.
\end{aligned}
\]

This is the cube in (0.12).

Define

\[
\Delta(n):=-2(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(2n+5)(2n+7)^2.
\tag{5.2}
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{\rho(n)\det A(n)=\Delta(n).}
\tag{5.3}
\]

The asymptotic expansion is

\[
\boxed{
\Delta(n)
=-16n^7\left(1+\frac{39}{2n}+O(n^{-2})\right).}
\tag{5.4}
\]

The coefficient \(39/2\) is precisely the correction of the two outer modes. The lower correction \(33/2\) of the middle mode must therefore be produced by the singular, mode-dependent part of \(V(n)\), not by the common scalar twist.

## 5.1 Normalization choices for \(\rho\)

Only the product \(\rho\det A\) is fixed.

* If \(A(n)\) is normalized to lie in \(\mathrm{SL}_2\), then
  \[
  \rho(n)=\Delta(n).
  \]

* For the standard central-Delannoy companion matrix
  \[
  A_D(n)=
  \begin{pmatrix}
  \dfrac{3(2n+1)}{n+1}&-\dfrac{n}{n+1}\\[2mm]
  1&0
  \end{pmatrix},
  \qquad
  \det A_D(n)=\frac{n}{n+1},
  \]
  one has
  \[
  \rho(n)=\Delta(n)\frac{n+1}{n}.
  \]

* If the \(4i\) twist is included inside the rank-two matrix, so that
  \[
  A_D^{(4i)}(n)=4iA_D(n),
  \qquad
  \det A_D^{(4i)}(n)=-16\frac{n}{n+1},
  \]
  then
  \[
  \rho(n)=-\frac{\Delta(n)}{16}\frac{n+1}{n}.
  \]

These are the same determinant identity in different scalar conventions.

# 6. Consequence for the requested \(V(n)\) and Ore intertwiner

The data \(C,C_1\) determine the following facts exactly.

1. A constant leading Veronese gauge exists and is given by \(W_0\) in (0.5).
2. No gauge of the form
   \[
   V(n)=V_0+\frac{V_1}{n}+\cdots,
   \qquad \det V_0\ne0,
   \]
   can realize the full cocycle as a symmetric square.
3. A possible gauge must be singular at infinity and have determinant degree six.
4. In the eigenbasis, it must shift the middle mode by three powers relative to the two outer modes.
5. Its determinant class can be normalized by (0.11), and then its scalar rank-two determinant factor is (0.13).

Therefore \(C\) and \(C_1\) are not sufficient to print the full rational matrix \(V(n)\), but they sharply constrain the ansatz. The next exact computation should solve the six-dimensional induced equation on symmetric forms while allowing the degree-six degeneration:

\[
M(n)^TJ(n+1)M(n)=\Lambda(n)J(n),
\]

with an ansatz whose projective leading behavior is

\[
J_{\rm mid}+n^{-6}(\text{nonzero multiple of }J_{\rm pair})+\cdots,
\]

not a nondegenerate constant matrix.

Once such a rational \(J(n)\) is found:

1. test whether its smooth generic conic has a rational point;
2. parameterize it by a quadratic map \(
u_n:\mathbf P^1\to\mathbf P^2\);
3. evaluate \(M(n)
u_{n+1}(0),M(n)
u_{n+1}(1),M(n)
u_{n+1}(\infty)\);
4. reconstruct the induced Möbius matrix \(A(n)\);
5. verify all nine entries of (0.9).

For the scalar Ore ansatz

\[
T=t_0(n)+t_1(n)S+t_2(n)S^2,
\]

the same exponent gap says that the leading symbol of \(T\) must vanish on the middle Poincaré root \(S=1\) through enough successive degree layers to lower that mode by three powers, while remaining nonzero at \(17\pm12\sqrt2\). This is a useful linear constraint before solving

\[
L_{25}T=R L_D^{\langle2\rangle}.
\]

# Final verdict

The explicit answer to the leading question is the pencil (0.1). A convenient split choice gives the exact leading conjugacy (0.4). But the full \(1/n\) data show that the requested regular nondegenerate conic does not exist.

The correct finite certificate, if it exists, must use a **singular degree-six coboundary**. Its determinant is naturally normalized by

\[
(n+1)(n+2)(2n+3)^2(2n+5)^2,
\]

and the scalar/determinant product is forced to be

\[
-2(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(2n+5)(2n+7)^2.
\]

These formulas reduce the remaining search from an unconstrained \(3\times3\) rational gauge problem to a degree-profiled invariant-conic or Ore-intertwiner computation.
