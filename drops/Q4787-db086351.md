# Problem 2.7: from the level-11 centered-binomial transform to a rigorous CMF proof

Let \(T_n\) denote Cooper's level-11 sequence

\[
\begin{aligned}
(n+1)^3T_{n+1}={}&2(2n+1)(5n^2+5n+2)T_n\\
&-8n(7n^2+1)T_{n-1}
+22n(2n-1)(n-1)T_{n-2},
\end{aligned}
\tag{0.1}
\]

and define

\[
W_n:=(KT)_n
=\frac1{256^n}\sum_{k=0}^{2n}
\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}T_k.
\tag{0.2}
\]

The Cooper Poincaré cubic is

\[
H_{11}(t)=t^3-20t^2+56t-44,
\tag{0.3}
\]

whereas the scaled Problem 2.7 cubic is

\[
F(\mu)=4\mu^3-220\mu^2+8\mu-1.
\tag{0.4}
\]

The exact spectral identity is

\[
\boxed{
16F\!\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{4}\right)
=-H_{11}(t)H_{11}(4-t).
}
\tag{0.5}
\]

Consequently, a pure Cooper mode \(t^n\) is sent by \(K\) to the mode

\[
\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}\right)^n,
\tag{0.6}
\]

which is exactly the Problem 2.7 Poincaré-root map.

## Executive conclusions

1. The quotient
   \[
   g_n:=\frac{q_n}{W_n}
   \]
   is **not automatically the rank-one gauge between the two rank-three difference modules**. A rational module isomorphism may mix \(W_n,W_{n+1},W_{n+2}\). Thus \(g_n\) need not be hypergeometric even if the two modules are equivalent after a hypergeometric twist.
2. The asymptotic relation
   \[
   \frac{g_{n+1}}{g_n}=1+\frac{3}{2n}+O(n^{-2})
   \]
   implies only
   \[
   g_n=Cn^{3/2}(1+O(n^{-1})),
   \]
   not an exact Pochhammer formula.
3. There is a finite decision procedure for the exact scalar gauge. First compute the minimal operator \(L_K\) annihilating \(W_n\). Then three explicit coefficient identities either determine the rational ratio \(ho(n)=h_{n+1}/h_n\) or prove that no scalar hypergeometric gauge exists.
4. If the scalar test fails, the correct object is an Ore intertwiner
   \[
   L_{27}U=V L_K^{[\rho]}
   \]
   with \(\operatorname{ord}U<3\), or equivalently a rational \(3\times3\) companion-matrix coboundary.
5. The centered-binomial transform is not literally the coefficientwise Hadamard product with \(inom{2n}{n}/4^n\). It is a rational binomial substitution followed by even projection and rescaling. The proper WZ analogue is therefore a **holonomic vector creative-telescoping certificate**, not a one-summand hypergeometric WZ pair.
6. Cooper's source is a level-11 modular weight-two system. It is reasonable to call its third-order equation **K3-type** because it is a symmetric-square-type rank-three variation, but the statement “level 11 in Beauville's K3 classification” is not literally correct. Beauville's classical list concerns semistable elliptic surfaces over \(\mathbf P^1\) with four singular fibres; \(X_0(11)\) has genus \(1\).
7. Even a complete homogeneous module equivalence would identify the denominator period but would not yet prove the connection constant \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\). The \(\zeta(3)\) component requires a parameter derivative, normal function, Eisenstein extension, or explicit double-integral linear form.

# 1. What the coefficient \(3/2\) actually tells us

Suppose two nonzero solutions have the same dominant exponential root \(\lambda\) and asymptotics

\[
q_n\sim C_q\lambda^n n^{\sigma_q},
\qquad
W_n\sim C_W\lambda^n n^{\sigma_W}.
\tag{1.1}
\]

Then

\[
\frac{q_n}{W_n}\sim \frac{C_q}{C_W}n^{\sigma_q-\sigma_W}.
\tag{1.2}
\]

The observed ratio expansion therefore says

\[
\sigma_q-\sigma_W=\frac32.
\tag{1.3}
\]

It does not determine a unique exact sequence. For example,

\[
h_n^{(A)}=\frac{(5/2)_n}{n!}
\]

has

\[
\frac{h_{n+1}^{(A)}}{h_n^{(A)}}
=\frac{n+5/2}{n+1}
=1+\frac{3}{2n}-\frac{3}{2n^2}+O(n^{-3}),
\tag{1.4}
\]

and \(h_n^{(A)}\asymp n^{3/2}\). Infinitely many different gamma quotients have the same first coefficient \(3/2\).

More generally, if

\[
\rho(n)=c\,
\frac{\prod_i(n+\alpha_i)^{m_i}}
     {\prod_j(n+\beta_j)^{r_j}}
\tag{1.5}
\]

has equal numerator and denominator degrees, then

\[
h_n=h_0c^n
\frac{\prod_i(\alpha_i)_n^{m_i}}
     {\prod_j(\beta_j)_n^{r_j}}
\tag{1.6}
\]

satisfies \(h_{n+1}/h_n=\rho(n)\), and its power at infinity is

\[
\sum_i m_i\alpha_i-\sum_j r_j\beta_j.
\tag{1.7}
\]

Thus \(3/2\) is only one linear constraint on a possible gamma product.

## 1.1 Why \(g_n=q_n/W_n\) need not be the module gauge

Let \(M_K\) be the rank-three difference module generated by \(W_n\), and let \(M_{27}\) be the Problem 2.7 module. A rank-one twist and rational module isomorphism may have the scalar form

\[
q_n=h_n\bigl(u_0(n)W_n+u_1(n)W_{n+1}+u_2(n)W_{n+2}\bigr),
\tag{1.8}
\]

with \(u_j(n)\in\mathbf Q(n)\). Dividing by \(W_n\) gives

\[
g_n=h_n\left(
 u_0(n)+u_1(n)\frac{W_{n+1}}{W_n}
 +u_2(n)\frac{W_{n+2}}{W_n}
\right).
\tag{1.9}
\]

The bracket is generally neither rational nor hypergeometric. Hence failure to recognize \(g_n\) as a Pochhammer ratio is expected when the intertwiner is nontrivial.

This is the first major correction to the proposed strategy:

> The exact object to determine is not necessarily \(g_n\), but the pair consisting of a rank-one twist \(h_n\) and an order-at-most-two Ore intertwiner \(U\).

# 2. The exact scalar-gauge test

Write the primitive transformed-Cooper operator as

\[
L_K=\sum_{j=0}^3 b_j(n)S^j,
\qquad (Su)_n=u_{n+1},
\tag{2.1}
\]

and the Problem 2.7 operator as

\[
L_{27}=\sum_{j=0}^3 a_j(n)S^j.
\tag{2.2}
\]

Assume first that a pure scalar gauge exists:

\[
q_n=h_nW_n,
\qquad
\rho(n):=\frac{h_{n+1}}{h_n}\in\mathbf Q(n).
\tag{2.3}
\]

Set

\[
H_0(n)=1,
\qquad
H_j(n)=\prod_{r=0}^{j-1}\rho(n+r)\quad(j\ge1).
\tag{2.4}
\]

If \(L_KW=0\), then \(u_n=h_nW_n\) is annihilated by

\[
L_K^{[\rho]}
=\sum_{j=0}^3\frac{b_j(n)}{H_j(n)}S^j.
\tag{2.5}
\]

The scalar gauge exists exactly when \(L_{27}\) is a rational multiple of \(L_K^{[\rho]}\). The ratio is forced by the first two coefficients:

\[
\boxed{
\rho(n)=\frac{a_0(n)b_1(n)}{b_0(n)a_1(n)}.
}
\tag{2.6}
\]

It is valid if and only if the remaining two identities hold:

\[
\boxed{
\rho(n)\rho(n+1)
=\frac{a_0(n)b_2(n)}{b_0(n)a_2(n)},
}
\tag{2.7}
\]

\[
\boxed{
\rho(n)\rho(n+1)\rho(n+2)
=\frac{a_0(n)b_3(n)}{b_0(n)a_3(n)}.
}
\tag{2.8}
\]

These three rational-function identities are the complete certificate.

- If (2.7) and (2.8) hold, factor \(ho(n)\) and use (1.6) to obtain the exact gamma/Pochhammer formula for \(h_n\).
- If either identity fails, no scalar hypergeometric gauge exists, regardless of the asymptotic \(n^{3/2}\).

A purely rational gauge \(h_n\in\mathbf Q(n)\) is already impossible: a rational function has an integer power at infinity, whereas \(3/2\) is half-integral. A rational successive ratio \(ho(n)\) is still possible, but then \(h_n\) must be a genuine gamma quotient.

## 2.1 Why this test cannot be run from Poincaré roots alone

The Poincaré cubic records only the leading coefficient of the Riccati/gauge equations at infinity. Two operators can have identical Poincaré roots and still fail (2.7) or (2.8) because of lower-order coefficient data. Therefore the minimal transformed operator \(L_K\) must be computed first.

# 3. Computing the exact transformed operator \(L_K\)

Define the kernel

\[
K(n,k)=256^{-n}\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}.
\tag{3.1}
\]

Then

\[
W_n=\sum_{k=0}^{2n}K(n,k)T_k.
\tag{3.2}
\]

The kernel is hypergeometric and satisfies the exact first-order relations

\[
\boxed{
2(k+1)K(n,k+1)+(2n-k)K(n,k)=0,
}
\tag{3.3}
\]

and

\[
\boxed{
64(2n+2-k)(2n+1-k)K(n+1,k)
-(2n+2)(2n+1)K(n,k)=0.
}
\tag{3.4}
\]

Together with Cooper's order-three recurrence in \(k\), these generate a holonomic left ideal in

\[
\mathbf Q(n,k)\langle S_n,S_k\rangle.
\]

The correct telescoping certificate has the vector form

\[
\boxed{
L_K^{(n)}\bigl(K(n,k)T_k\bigr)
=(S_k-1)\left[
K(n,k)\sum_{j=0}^2R_j(n,k)T_{k+j}
\right],
}
\tag{3.5}
\]

where

- \(L_K\in\mathbf Q[n]\langle S_n\rangle\),
- \(R_0,R_1,R_2\in\mathbf Q(n,k)\).

Summing (3.5) over \(0\le k\le2n\), then checking the finitely many boundary terms, proves

\[
L_KW=0.
\tag{3.6}
\]

This is the rigorous WZ-style certificate. It is a Chyzak/Koutschan holonomic certificate rather than a classical scalar WZ pair because \(T_k\) is P-recursive, not known to be a single hypergeometric term.

## 3.1 Why a classical one-summand WZ pair is not expected

A classical WZ summand is hypergeometric in both variables. Here only \(K(n,k)\) is hypergeometric. The factor \(T_k\) satisfies an order-three recurrence. Therefore the certificate naturally lives in the three-dimensional \(k\)-state

\[
(T_k,T_{k+1},T_{k+2}).
\]

The rational vector \(R_0,R_1,R_2\) in (3.5) is the proper replacement for a single WZ certificate function.

## 3.2 An equivalent differential-operator computation

Let

\[
Z(X)=\sum_{k\ge0}T_kX^k,
\qquad \theta=X\frac{d}{dX}.
\]

Cooper's recurrence is equivalent to

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal L_{11}={}&\theta^3
-2X(2\theta+1)(5\theta^2+5\theta+2)\\
&+8X^2(\theta+1)\bigl(7(\theta+1)^2+1\bigr)\\
&-22X^3(\theta+1)(\theta+2)(2\theta+3).
\end{aligned}
}
\tag{3.7}
\]

The ordinary centered-binomial transform

\[
b_m=\sum_{k=0}^m\binom{m}{k}(-2)^{m-k}T_k
\]

has generating function

\[
\boxed{
B(r)=\frac1{1+2r}
Z\!\left(\frac{r}{1+2r}\right).
}
\tag{3.8}
\]

Taking the even part and applying the \(256^{-n}\) scale gives

\[
\boxed{
W(z)=\sum_{n\ge0}W_nz^n
=\frac12\left[
B\!\left(\frac{\sqrt z}{16}\right)
+B\!\left(-\frac{\sqrt z}{16}\right)
\right].
}
\tag{3.9}
\]

Equivalently,

\[
\begin{aligned}
W(z)=\frac12\Bigg[&
\frac1{1+\sqrt z/8}
Z\!\left(\frac{\sqrt z}{16+2\sqrt z}\right)\\
&+\frac1{1-\sqrt z/8}
Z\!\left(-\frac{\sqrt z}{16-2\sqrt z}\right)
\Bigg].
\end{aligned}
\tag{3.10}
\]

This gives a second exact route to \(L_K\):

1. pull back \(\mathcal L_{11}\) along each algebraic branch;
2. include the rational prefactors in (3.10);
3. take the least common left multiple;
4. restrict to the even/invariant subspace;
5. descend from \(r=\sqrt z\) to \(z=r^2\);
6. factor the resulting operator and select the minimal factor matching the first terms of \(W_n\).

The associated modular coordinate is

\[
\boxed{
z=\frac{256X^2}{(1-2X)^2}.
}
\tag{3.11}
\]

This differential method and the telescoping method must give the same minimal operator.

# 4. The Ore intertwiner

Assume \(L_K\) has now been computed. Let \(ho\) be either the scalar candidate from (2.6) or an unknown rational function to be solved simultaneously with the intertwiner.

Seek

\[
U=u_0(n)+u_1(n)S+u_2(n)S^2,
\tag{4.1}
\]

and an Ore operator \(V\) such that

\[
\boxed{
L_{27}U=V L_K^{[\rho]}.
}
\tag{4.2}
\]

All coefficients lie in \(\mathbf Q(n)\). Once denominator divisors and degree bounds are chosen, equating coefficients of powers of \(S\) gives a finite linear system.

If (4.2) holds and \(U\) is invertible in the quotient module, then every solution of \(L_K^{[\rho]}\) is carried to a solution of \(L_{27}\). A reverse intertwiner or a nonzero Casoratian verifies that the map is an isomorphism rather than a proper quotient.

For the distinguished denominator, one then checks three exact initial values:

\[
q_n=U(hW)_n,
\qquad n=0,1,2.
\tag{4.3}
\]

Because both sides satisfy the same order-three recurrence, (4.3) proves equality for all \(n\).

## 4.1 Equivalent \(3\times3\) companion-matrix equation

For

\[
L=\ell_0(n)+\ell_1(n)S+\ell_2(n)S^2+\ell_3(n)S^3,
\]

define the column companion matrix

\[
C_L(n)=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&1&0\\
0&0&1\\
-\ell_0/\ell_3&-\ell_1/\ell_3&-\ell_2/\ell_3
\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{4.4}
\]

If

\[
Y_n=(W_n,W_{n+1},W_{n+2})^T,
\qquad
X_n=(q_n,q_{n+1},q_{n+2})^T,
\]

and

\[
X_n=h_nR(n)Y_n,
\qquad R(n)\in\operatorname{GL}_3(\mathbf Q(n)),
\]

then the exact coboundary equation is

\[
\boxed{
C_{27}(n)R(n)
=\rho(n)R(n+1)C_K(n).
}
\tag{4.5}
\]

This is often easier to search than the scalar Ore equation.

Taking determinants gives the necessary Gosper-type condition

\[
\boxed{
\frac{\det R(n+1)}{\det R(n)}
=\frac{\det C_{27}(n)}
       {\rho(n)^3\det C_K(n)}.
}
\tag{4.6}
\]

Before solving nine matrix equations, one should test whether the right-hand side of (4.6) is a rational shift quotient. This sharply restricts the possible \(ho(n)\).

## 4.2 Why the asymptotic \(3/2\) is still useful

Although it does not determine \(h_n\), it constrains the rank-one twist:

\[
\rho(n)=1+\frac{3}{2n}+O(n^{-2}).
\tag{4.7}
\]

It also constrains the local exponent difference of the two generating functions at the dominant singularity. Any candidate \(ho\), matrix \(R(n)\), or differential prefactor should reproduce this exponent shift.

# 5. What the period interpretation should be

Cooper's level-11 modular data are

\[
g_{11}(\tau)=\eta(\tau)^2\eta(11\tau)^2,
\]

\[
Z_{11}(\tau)=\Theta_{11}(\tau)^2,
\qquad
X(\tau)=\frac{g_{11}(\tau)}{Z_{11}(\tau)},
\tag{5.1}
\]

with

\[
B(X)^2=1-20X+56X^2-44X^3.
\tag{5.2}
\]

The curve obtained by setting \(t=1/X\) is

\[
v^2=tH_{11}(t).
\tag{5.3}
\]

It is birational to \(X_0(11)\), an elliptic curve. The third-order equation for \(Z_{11}\) is a rank-three weight-two modular local system and is naturally of symmetric-square/K3 type.

## 5.1 Correction to the Beauville claim

Beauville's classical classification is the list of semistable elliptic surfaces over \(\mathbf P^1\) with four singular fibres. It is not a classification of “level-11 K3 surfaces”, and level \(11\) does not appear as a genus-zero Beauville base because \(X_0(11)\) has genus \(1\).

What can legitimately be said is:

- Cooper's third-order modular equation is of the same formal type as Picard-Fuchs equations arising from Kummer or lattice-polarized K3 families;
- because \(Z_{11}\) is a square of a weight-one theta function, a symmetric-square interpretation is natural;
- an actual K3 theorem requires an explicit algebraic family, a holomorphic \(2\)-form, and verification that its Picard-Fuchs operator equals \(\mathcal L_{11}\).

Thus “K3-type” is justified; “the Beauville level-11 K3 surface” is not yet a precise object.

## 5.2 Geometry of the Problem 2.7 transform

The transform (3.9) has a clean local-system interpretation:

1. pull the level-11 rank-three local system back along the degree-two map
   \[
   z=\frac{256X^2}{(1-2X)^2};
   \]
2. take the trace/invariant part under the two algebraic branches;
3. apply a possible rank-one Kummer twist \(H\);
4. change the rational basis by \(R(n)\) or \(U\).

If the Cooper system is realized by a Kummer/K3 family, this operation gives a base-changed and twisted K3-type variation. The denominator \(q_n\) should be a period of that pure rank-three variation.

## 5.3 Why \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\) is not explained by the pure K3 period alone

The target has the exact mixed-weight integral

\[
\boxed{
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{1-\frac12\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
}
\tag{5.4}
\]

Introduce

\[
J(a,c)=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{x^{a-1}y^{c-1}}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
\tag{5.5}
\]

Then

\[
J(1,1)=\zeta(2),
\qquad
-\frac12(\partial_a+\partial_c)J(a,c)\big|_{a=c=1}=\zeta(3).
\tag{5.6}
\]

Therefore

\[
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
=\left[1-\frac12(\partial_a+\partial_c)\right]
J(a,c)\big|_{a=c=1}.
\tag{5.7}
\]

This strongly suggests that the numerator/error system is a **parameter derivative or normal-function extension** of the pure level-11 period system. A homogeneous module equivalence identifies the denominator geometry, but the limit requires one more extension class.

# 6. The Hadamard-product issue

The standard central-binomial Hadamard product is

\[
\left(\sum a_nz^n\right)
\star
\frac1{\sqrt{1-4z}}
=\sum \binom{2n}{n}a_nz^n.
\tag{6.1}
\]

That is not the transform (0.2), because (0.2) mixes all \(T_k\) with \(0\le k\le2n\).

The exact structure is instead

\[
\text{binomial substitution}
\longrightarrow
\text{even projection}
\longrightarrow
\text{rescaling}.
\tag{6.2}
\]

A useful constant-term form is

\[
\boxed{
W_n=256^{-n}\operatorname{CT}_x
\left((x-2)^{2n}Z(x^{-1})\right).
}
\tag{6.3}
\]

Thus \(W_n\) is a diagonal/constant-term construction. If \(T_n\) itself is represented as a diagonal of a rational function, then \(W_n\) is again a diagonal after adjoining one variable. This gives another route to creative telescoping and arithmetic properties.

So the answer to part (d) is:

> There should be a finite creative-telescoping certificate, but its natural form is the vector identity (3.5), not a classical scalar WZ pair and not the naive central-binomial Hadamard product.

No such explicit \(R_0,R_1,R_2\) certificate appears to be published for this exact Problem 2.7 transform. Producing it is a concrete computer-algebra task, not an open-ended conceptual obstacle.

# 7. How the full limit proof would close

Let

\[
L:=\zeta(2)+\zeta(3),
\qquad
E_n=p_n-Lq_n.
\]

A complete proof can be organized into five finite certificates.

## Certificate A: transformed Cooper recurrence

Produce \(L_K\) and rational functions \(R_0,R_1,R_2\) satisfying (3.5), including all boundary checks.

## Certificate B: rank-one twist and Ore/CMF intertwiner

Produce \(ho(n)\), \(U,V\) satisfying (4.2), or equivalently \(R(n)\) satisfying (4.5), and verify invertibility.

## Certificate C: denominator initial values

Verify the three initial identities mapping the transformed Cooper period to \(q_n\). Recurrence uniqueness then proves the identity for all \(n\).

## Certificate D: mixed period/error representation

Construct an integral or parameter-derivative family \(I_n\) such that

\[
I_n=E_n
\]

for \(n=0,1,2\), and prove \(L_{27}I=0\) by creative telescoping. The natural seed is (5.5)-(5.7).

## Certificate E: decay

Use saddle-point analysis, Poincaré-Perron theory, or the integral kernel to prove

\[
\frac{E_n}{q_n}\longrightarrow0.
\]

Then

\[
\frac{p_n}{q_n}
=L+\frac{E_n}{q_n}
\longrightarrow
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3).
\]

This is the rigorous route from the established spectral correspondence to the challenge limit.

# 8. Direct answers to the four questions

## (a) Exact closed form of \(g_n\)

No exact closed form follows from the current data, and \(g_n\) is probably not the correct module gauge. The \(3/2\) coefficient determines only the power \(n^{3/2}\). Compute \(L_K\) and apply (2.6)-(2.8). If those identities hold, they produce the exact gamma-product gauge. If they fail, solve the non-scalar intertwiner problem; then \(g_n\) need not be hypergeometric at all.

## (b) Ore intertwiner

The required certificate is

\[
L_{27}U=V L_K^{[\rho]},
\qquad \operatorname{ord}U<3,
\]

or the matrix equation

\[
C_{27}(n)R(n)=\rho(n)R(n+1)C_K(n).
\]

This is a finite rational-function calculation. It has not yet been supplied by the spectral identity alone.

## (c) Period interpretation

Problem 2.7 should be interpreted as a quadratic pullback, trace/even projection, and possible Kummer twist of Cooper's level-11 rank-three modular local system. If the source is realized as a Kummer/K3 Picard-Fuchs system, the denominator is a period of the transformed K3-type family. The value \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\) belongs to a mixed extension/normal function, not merely to the pure K3 period.

## (d) WZ certificate

Yes in the generalized holonomic sense: seek the vector certificate (3.5). The kernel relations (3.3)-(3.4) and Cooper's recurrence are the complete input. A classical scalar WZ pair is not expected because \(T_k\) is P-recursive rather than hypergeometric.
