# Problem 2.7: Cooper’s exact level-11 recurrence, the quadratic trace transform, initial data, and the mixed zeta period

## Executive answer

There are three corrections that matter before doing the computation.

1. **Cooper’s level-11 recurrence has five parameters, not the four-parameter shape written in the question.** In Cooper’s notation the general self-starting four-term form is
   \[
   (n+1)^3T_{n+1}
   =(2n+1)(an^2+an+b)T_n
   +n(cn^2+d)T_{n-1}
   +e\,n(2n-1)(n-1)T_{n-2}.
   \]
   For level 11,
   \[
   \boxed{(a,b,c,d,e)=(10,4,-56,-8,22).}
   \]

2. **The relation to Problem 2.7 is not a symmetric square.** Both systems have rank three. The exact operation suggested by the spectral algebra is a centered binomial transform, followed by the even section and a scale by \(256^{-n}\).

3. **The official target is**
   \[
   \boxed{\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
   =2.846990970007820721872153328\ldots,}
   \]
   not \(1+\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\). The latter is
   \[
   3.846990970007820721872153328\ldots.
   \]
   Thus the numerical value quoted in the question corresponds to the sum without the extra \(1\).

The exact sequence transform is
\[
\boxed{
(\mathcal K T)_n
=\frac1{256^n}\sum_{k=0}^{2n}
\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}T_k.
}
\tag{E1}
\]
For a pure mode \(T_k=t^k\), it gives
\[
(\mathcal K T)_n
=\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}\right)^n,
\]
which is exactly the Problem 2.7 Poincaré-root map. This is an exact transform of sequences and generating functions. What is **not yet certified** is that its minimal finite-\(n\) operator is Ore-equivalent to the printed degree-18 challenge operator. That last statement still requires the explicit Ore intertwiner described in Section 4 below.

# 1. Cooper’s exact level-11 four-term recurrence

Cooper’s paper gives the level-11 parameters in Table 7 of arXiv:2302.00757v2 and derives the recurrence again in Section 5. The correct general form is
\[
(n+1)^3T_{n+1}
=(2n+1)(an^2+an+b)T_n
+n(cn^2+d)T_{n-1}
+e\,n(2n-1)(n-1)T_{n-2}.
\tag{1.1}
\]
For level 11,
\[
(a,b,c,d,e)=(10,4,-56,-8,22).
\tag{1.2}
\]
Therefore
\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
(n+1)^3T_{n+1}={}&
2(2n+1)(5n^2+5n+2)T_n\\
&-8n(7n^2+1)T_{n-1}\\
&+22n(2n-1)(n-1)T_{n-2}.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{1.3}
\]

The template in the question,
\[
-c\,n(4n^2-1)T_{n-1}+d\,n^2(n-1)T_{n-2},
\]
does not fit with constant \(c,d\). The actual second coefficient is
\[
n(-56n^2-8),
\]
and the terminal coefficient is
\[
22n(2n-1)(n-1).
\]

The recurrence is self-starting from \(T_0=1\). Its first terms are
\[
\boxed{
T_0,T_1,T_2,T_3,T_4,T_5,T_6
=1,4,28,268,3004,36784,476476.
}
\tag{1.4}
\]

The Poincaré cubic is
\[
\boxed{H_{11}(t)=t^3-20t^2+56t-44.}
\tag{1.5}
\]

The associated generating function \(Z(X)=\sum_{n\ge0}T_nX^n\) is annihilated by
\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal D_{11}={}&\theta^3
-2X(2\theta+1)(5\theta^2+5\theta+2)\\
&+8X^2(\theta+1)\bigl(7(\theta+1)^2+1\bigr)\\
&-22X^3(\theta+1)(\theta+2)(2\theta+3),
\end{aligned}}
\qquad \theta=X\frac{d}{dX}.
\tag{1.6}
\]

For an Ore computation with a forward shift \(S_kT_k=T_{k+1}\), an equivalent polynomial operator is
\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
L_{11}^{(k)}={}&(k+3)^3S_k^3
-2(2k+5)(5k^2+25k+32)S_k^2\\
&+8(k+2)(7k^2+28k+29)S_k\\
&-22(k+2)(2k+3)(k+1).
\end{aligned}}
\tag{1.7}
\]

# 2. Exact spectral algebra

Write the scaled Problem 2.7 Poincaré polynomial as
\[
F(\mu)=4\mu^3-220\mu^2+8\mu-1.
\tag{2.1}
\]
The exact norm identity is
\[
\boxed{
16F\!\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{4}\right)
=-H_{11}(t)H_{11}(4-t).
}
\tag{2.2}
\]
Indeed, putting \(u=t-2\),
\[
H_{11}(2+u)=u^3-14u^2-12u-4,
\]
\[
H_{11}(2-u)=-u^3-14u^2+12u-4,
\]
and hence
\[
-H_{11}(2+u)H_{11}(2-u)
=u^6-220u^4+32u^2-16.
\]
This equals
\[
16F(u^2/4).
\]

Thus every root \(t_i\) of \(H_{11}\) gives a root
\[
\mu_i=\frac{(t_i-2)^2}{4}
\tag{2.3}
\]
of \(F\). In the normalization of the printed challenge recurrence, the actual Poincaré root is \(\lambda_i=\mu_i/64\), so
\[
\boxed{
\lambda_i=\frac{(t_i-2)^2}{256}.
}
\tag{2.4}
\]

This explains exactly why the shifted-binomial/even-section transform is the right operation.

## Why this is not \(\operatorname{Sym}^2\)

A rational symmetric square of a rank-two system has modes
\[
r^2,\quad rs,\quad s^2.
\]
The middle mode \(rs\) is the determinant and is rational over the coefficient field. The Problem 2.7 cubic \(F\) is irreducible over \(\mathbf Q\), so it has no rational Poincaré root. Therefore the challenge operator is not a rational symmetric square of a rank-two operator.

The correct description is:

> a quadratic algebraic trace/even-section transform between two rank-three systems.

# 3. The exact binomial-transform operator

Define first the shifted binomial transform
\[
B_m:=\sum_{k=0}^{m}\binom{m}{k}(-2)^{m-k}T_k.
\tag{3.1}
\]
For a pure exponential \(T_k=t^k\), the binomial theorem gives
\[
B_m=(t-2)^m.
\tag{3.2}
\]
Now take the even section and the challenge scale:
\[
\boxed{
S_n:=\frac{B_{2n}}{256^n}
=\frac1{256^n}\sum_{k=0}^{2n}
\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}T_k.
}
\tag{3.3}
\]
Then
\[
T_k=t^k
\quad\Longmapsto\quad
S_n=\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}\right)^n.
\tag{3.4}
\]

The kernel
\[
K(n,k)=256^{-n}\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}
\tag{3.5}
\]
is hypergeometric in both variables. Its exact shift quotients are
\[
\boxed{
\frac{K(n,k+1)}{K(n,k)}
=-\frac{2n-k}{2(k+1)},
}
\tag{3.6}
\]
and
\[
\boxed{
\frac{K(n+1,k)}{K(n,k)}
=\frac{(2n+2)(2n+1)}
{64(2n+2-k)(2n+1-k)}.
}
\tag{3.7}
\]
These two rational quotients, together with \(L_{11}^{(k)}\), are the complete input for a Chyzak/Zeilberger creative-telescoping calculation.

## 3.1 Generating-function form

Let
\[
Z(X)=\sum_{k\ge0}T_kX^k,
\qquad
B(r)=\sum_{m\ge0}B_mr^m.
\]
Then
\[
\boxed{
B(r)=\frac1{1+2r}
Z\!\left(\frac{r}{1+2r}\right).
}
\tag{3.8}
\]
The scaled even section is
\[
\boxed{
\mathscr S(z)=\sum_{n\ge0}S_nz^n
=\frac12\left[
B\!\left(\frac{\sqrt z}{16}\right)
+B\!\left(-\frac{\sqrt z}{16}\right)
\right].
}
\tag{3.9}
\]
Equivalently, with \(r=\sqrt z\),
\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathscr S(z)=\frac12\Bigg[&
\frac1{1+r/8}
Z\!\left(\frac{r}{16+2r}\right)\\
&+\frac1{1-r/8}
Z\!\left(-\frac{r}{16-2r}\right)
\Bigg].
\end{aligned}}
\tag{3.10}
\]
Solving
\[
X=\frac{r}{16+2r}
\]
for \(r\) gives
\[
r=\frac{16X}{1-2X},
\]
so the exact quadratic generating coordinate is
\[
\boxed{
z=\frac{256X^2}{(1-2X)^2}.}
\tag{3.11}
\]

In Cooper’s modular parametrization,
\[
X(\tau)=
\frac{\eta(\tau)^2\eta(11\tau)^2}
{\Theta_{11}(\tau)^2},
\qquad
\Theta_{11}(\tau)=
\sum_{j,k\in\mathbf Z}q^{j^2+jk+3k^2},
\]
so (3.11) is already an explicit level-11 modular function.

# 4. The missing finite Ore certificate

Equation (3.3) is an exact sequence transform and equations (2.2)–(2.4) prove its spectral effect. They do **not** by themselves prove that its image is the solution space of the full degree-18 challenge operator.

Let \(L_{27}^{(n)}\) denote the primitive Problem 2.7 operator. The desired mixed-Ore creative-telescoping certificate has the form
\[
\boxed{
L_{27}^{(n)}\,\mathcal K
=\mathcal U\,L_{11}^{(k)}
+(S_k-1)\mathcal V,
}
\tag{4.1}
\]
where \(\mathcal U\) and \(\mathcal V\) have explicit rational-function coefficients in \(n,k\) and the two shift operators. Summing over \(k=0,\ldots,2n\), checking the moving-boundary terms, and using \(L_{11}T=0\) would prove
\[
L_{27}S=0.
\]

A slightly more flexible two-stage computation is usually easier:

1. Creative telescope the kernel (3.5) against \(L_{11}^{(k)}\) to obtain the minimal transformed operator \(L_{\mathrm{tr}}^{(n)}\).
2. Seek an Ore intertwiner
   \[
   R=r_0(n)+r_1(n)S_n+r_2(n)S_n^2
   \]
   and an operator \(Q\) such that
   \[
   \boxed{
   L_{27}R=Q L_{\mathrm{tr}}.
   }
   \tag{4.2}
   \]
   Since both modules have rank three, an intertwiner of shift order at most two is the natural first ansatz.
3. Verify the resulting map on three initial values.

This is the finite certificate that Q4763 correctly identified as missing. No published source, and no current repository file I found, contains explicit \(\mathcal U,\mathcal V\) or \(R,Q\). Therefore it would be incorrect to claim that (3.3) has already been proved to equal the printed challenge recurrence. What is proved is the kernel transform, the generating pullback, and the exact spectral norm identity.

A further conceptual point is important: the homogeneous transform (3.3) transports solution spaces, but it does not by itself create the weight-three constant \(\zeta(3)\). The numerator/connection solution must come from a parameter derivative, an Eichler integral, or another extension of the level-11 module.

# 5. Explicit initial values

There are three different normalizations in circulation. Stating which one is meant removes an apparent contradiction.

## 5.1 Cooper’s level-11 sequence

The self-starting Cooper sequence has
\[
\boxed{T_0=1,\quad T_1=4,\quad T_2=28,\quad T_3=268.}
\tag{5.1}
\]

## 5.2 The integer even-binomial transform

Define the unscaled even transform
\[
C_n:=B_{2n}
=\sum_{k=0}^{2n}\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}T_k.
\tag{5.2}
\]
Using the Cooper values through \(T_6\),
\[
\boxed{
C_0=1,\quad C_1=16,\quad C_2=1420,\quad C_3=178444.
}
\tag{5.3}
\]
For example,
\[
C_1=4T_0-4T_1+T_2=4-16+28=16,
\]
\[
C_2=16T_0-32T_1+24T_2-8T_3+T_4=1420,
\]
and
\[
\begin{aligned}
C_3={}&64T_0-192T_1+240T_2-160T_3\\
&+60T_4-12T_5+T_6
=178444.
\end{aligned}
\]

## 5.3 Centered and challenge-scaled normalizations

If the centered transform includes the factor \(2^{-m}\), its even section is
\[
W_n=4^{-n}C_n,
\]
so
\[
\boxed{
W_0=1,\quad W_1=4,\quad
W_2=\frac{355}{4},\quad
W_3=\frac{44611}{16}.
}
\tag{5.4}
\]

The normalization matching the challenge Poincaré roots is
\[
S_n=256^{-n}C_n,
\]
so
\[
\boxed{
S_0=1,\quad
S_1=\frac1{16},\quad
S_2=\frac{355}{16384},\quad
S_3=\frac{44611}{4194304}.
}
\tag{5.5}
\]

Thus, if the notation \(S_0,S_1,S_2,S_3\) in the question refers to the transformed Cooper seed, (5.5) is the challenge-scale answer; (5.3) is the integral normalization.

## 5.4 The actual Problem 2.7 initial data

The official recurrence does not specify a single sequence called \(S_n\). It specifies two solutions \(p_n,q_n\), and because the recurrence has order three it supplies three initial values for each:
\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
p_0&=-612218384750,\\
p_1&=-\frac{9525021973931919}{18100},\\
p_2&=-\frac{29561828382772029}{65380},
\end{aligned}}
\tag{5.6}
\]
\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
q_0&=-215040420000,\\
q_1&=-\frac{167282265043404}{905},\\
q_2&=-\frac{964185327658080}{6071}.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{5.7}
\]
These are the values used in `scripts/verify_2_7_2_8.py` in the Ramanujan Challenge repository. A fourth term is determined by the recurrence and is not an independent initial value.

The very large values (5.6)–(5.7) also show that the direct transformed seed (5.5) is not literally the challenge denominator or numerator. A nontrivial rational change of basis/Ore intertwiner is required.

# 6. Integral representations of the target period

The cleanest exact representation is
\[
\boxed{
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{1-\tfrac12\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
}
\tag{6.1}
\]
To verify it, expand
\[
\frac1{1-xy}=\sum_{m\ge0}(xy)^m.
\]
Then
\[
\int_0^1\!\int_0^1\frac{dx\,dy}{1-xy}
=\sum_{m\ge0}\frac1{(m+1)^2}=\zeta(2),
\]
while
\[
-\frac12\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy
=\sum_{m\ge0}\frac1{(m+1)^3}=\zeta(3).
\]

Using
\[
\int_0^1\!\int_0^1 f(xy)\,dx\,dy
=\int_0^1 f(t)(-\log t)\,dt,
\]
(6.1) becomes the one-variable moment
\[
\boxed{
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
=\int_0^1
\frac{-\log t+\tfrac12\log^2t}{1-t}\,dt.
}
\tag{6.2}
\]

The logarithm can also be rationalized to obtain a triple integral:
\[
\boxed{
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
=\int_{[0,1]^3}
\left[
\frac1{1-xy}
+\frac1{2\bigl(1-(1-xy)s\bigr)}
\right]dx\,dy\,ds.
}
\tag{6.3}
\]

If one deliberately wants the shifted constant in the question, then simply add \(1\):
\[
1+\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
=1+\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{1-\tfrac12\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
\tag{6.4}
\]
But this is not the target used by the Problem 2.7 recurrence in the challenge repository.

# 7. What is now proved and what remains

The following formulas are exact and verified:

- Cooper’s level-11 parameters
  \[
  (a,b,c,d,e)=(10,4,-56,-8,22);
  \]
- the four-term recurrence (1.3);
- the spectral norm identity (2.2);
- the kernel transform (3.3);
- the generating coordinate
  \[
  z=256X^2/(1-2X)^2;
  \]
- the transformed initial values (5.3)–(5.5);
- the period integrals (6.1)–(6.3).

The one genuinely missing statement is the finite operator identity (4.1), or equivalently the two-stage certificate consisting of the transformed telescoper and the Ore intertwiner (4.2). Until that identity is printed and checked, one should say:

> Problem 2.7 is an exact quadratic **spectral** descendant of Cooper’s level-11 system, with an explicit binomial/even-section candidate transform, but the full degree-18 recurrence equivalence is not yet certified.
