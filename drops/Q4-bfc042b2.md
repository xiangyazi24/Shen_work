# Ramanujan Challenge 2.6: exact inverse-binomial factorization

## Executive answer

Yes: the roots `1` and `1/4` are not merely reminiscent of inverse-central-binomial Apéry/Beukers constructions. One of the two solutions is **exactly** a shifted inverse-central-binomial term:

\[
 h_n:=\frac{1}{(n+3)^2\binom{2n+6}{n+3}}.
\]

The given solution is not a single hypergeometric term. It is the hypergeometric solution `h_n` multiplied by a finite sum of another hypergeometric term. Explicitly, for every `n ≥ 1`,

\[
\boxed{
 u_n=-\frac{
 \displaystyle \frac{93}{4}
 +3\sum_{r=4}^{n+2}
       \frac{3r+2}{r(r+1)^2}\binom{2r}{r}}
 {(n+3)^2\binom{2n+6}{n+3}}
 }
\tag{1}
\]

with the sum interpreted as empty when `n=1`.

This formula explains all of the observed behavior:

* `h_n` has ratio tending to `1/4` and satisfies
  \(h_n\sim \sqrt\pi\,4^{-(n+3)}(n+3)^{-3/2}\).
* The finite sum in the numerator grows like
  \(3\,4^{n+3}/(\sqrt\pi\,(n+3)^{5/2})\).
* Their product therefore has
  \[
  \boxed{u_n\sim -\frac{3}{(n+3)^4}\sim-\frac3{n^4}},
  \]
  so the prescribed branch is the dominant root-`1` solution.

Thus the right structural description is:

> shifted inverse-central-binomial solution × an indefinite hypergeometric sum.

It is a one-level **d'Alembertian** solution. A speculative mixed-slope kernel is unnecessary. The simple beta kernel already carries the `1/4` branch; the root-`1` branch is created by the growing finite sum.

I also obtain a complete exact route from (1) to the claimed constant. The only nontrivial weight-three special-value reduction is an explicit cubic-root-of-unity `3`-dissection identity, stated below in a form that can be checked independently by standard double-polylogarithm shuffle/distribution or a CAS.

# 1. Exact hypergeometric solution of the recurrence

Write

\[
\begin{aligned}
A_n&=2(n+3)^3(2n+5)(3n+5),\\
B_n&=(n+2)^2(15n^3+85n^2+155n+93),\\
C_n&=(n+1)^3(n+2)(3n+8).
\end{aligned}
\]

The recurrence is

\[
A_nu_n=B_nu_{n-1}-C_nu_{n-2}.
\tag{2}
\]

For

\[
h_n=\frac1{(n+3)^2\binom{2n+6}{n+3}},
\]

we have

\[
\frac{h_n}{h_{n-1}}
 =\frac{(n+2)^2}{2(n+3)(2n+5)}.
\tag{3}
\]

Consequently,

\[
A_n\frac{h_n}{h_{n-1}}
 =(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(3n+5).
\tag{4}
\]

Also

\[
\frac{C_n}{h_{n-1}/h_{n-2}}
 =2(n+1)(n+2)^2(2n+3)(3n+8).
\tag{5}
\]

The required polynomial identity is only

\[
\begin{aligned}
&15n^3+85n^2+155n+93\\
&\qquad{}-2(n+1)(2n+3)(3n+8)
 =(n+3)^2(3n+5).
\end{aligned}
\tag{6}
\]

Multiplying (6) by `(n+2)^2` and using (4)–(5) proves

\[
A_nh_n=B_nh_{n-1}-C_nh_{n-2}.
\]

So the recurrence really does have a hypergeometric solution. A report that `rsolve_hyper` found no hypergeometric solution is a false negative for this indexing/normalization.

This is exactly the classical inverse-central-binomial summand in

\[
\zeta(2)=3\sum_{m\ge1}\frac1{m^2\binom{2m}{m}},
\]

shifted by `m=n+3`.

# 2. Reduction of order and the closed form

Set

\[
z_n:=\frac{u_n}{h_n},\qquad d_n:=z_n-z_{n-1}.
\]

Subtracting the recurrence for `h_n`, multiplied by `z_{n-1}`, from the recurrence for `h_n z_n` gives

\[
A_nh_n d_n=C_nh_{n-2}d_{n-1}.
\]

Using the ratios of `h`,

\[
\boxed{
\frac{d_n}{d_{n-1}}
 =\frac{2(n+1)(2n+3)(3n+8)}{(n+3)^2(3n+5)}.
}
\tag{7}
\]

The initial values give

\[
 h_1=\frac1{1120},\quad z_1=-\frac{93}{4},
 \qquad
 h_2=\frac1{6300},\quad z_2=-\frac{1053}{20},
\]

and hence

\[
d_2=-\frac{147}{5}.
\]

Taking the product in (7) yields the particularly simple exact expression

\[
\boxed{
 d_n=-\frac{3(3n+8)}{(n+2)(n+3)^2}
      \binom{2n+4}{n+2}
}
\qquad(n\ge2).
\tag{8}
\]

It is immediate to check (8) from its ratio and the value at `n=2`.
Therefore

\[
z_n=-\frac{93}{4}+\sum_{k=2}^{n}d_k,
\]

and reindexing `r=k+2` gives formula (1).

An equivalent Pochhammer form is

\[
 u_n=h_n\left[
 -\frac{93}{4}
 -\frac{147}{5}\sum_{j=0}^{n-2}
 4^j\frac{(4)_j(9/2)_j(17/3)_j}
 {(6)_j^2(14/3)_j}
 \right],
\tag{9}
\]

again with an empty sum for `n=1`. If desired, insert `(1)_j/j!=1` to display the finite sum as a truncated `{}_4F_3` term.

## Why the actual solution is not hypergeometric

The summand `d_n` is hypergeometric, but its indefinite sum is not hypergeometric. In Gosper normal form,

\[
\frac{d_{n+1}}{d_n}
 =\frac{2(n+2)(2n+5)}{(n+4)^2}
  \frac{3n+11}{3n+8}.
\]

A hypergeometric antidifference would require a polynomial solution `X(n)` of

\[
2(n+2)(2n+5)X(n+1)-(n+3)^2X(n)=3n+8.
\tag{10}
\]

If `X` is nonzero of degree `d`, the left side has degree `d+2` and leading coefficient three times the leading coefficient of `X`; it cannot equal a linear polynomial. Thus the Gosper equation has no polynomial solution. This is the precise obstruction behind the failure of a single-term ratio ansatz.

In particular, an ansatz

\[
R_1(n)+R_2(n)\frac{4^{-n}}{\binom{2n}{n}}
\]

with rational `R_1,R_2` would amount to a second hypergeometric solution and is too small. The correct replacement is the nested form (1) or (9).

# 3. Asymptotics and the two Poincaré roots

Put `m=n+3` and write `C_m=binom(2m,m)`. Then

\[
h_n=\frac1{m^2C_m}
 \sim \frac{\sqrt\pi}{4^m m^{3/2}}.
\]

The increment in the bracket is, with `r=m-1`,

\[
-3\frac{3r+2}{r(r+1)^2}C_r
 \sim-\frac{9\,4^r}{\sqrt\pi\,r^{5/2}}.
\]

Successive increments have ratio tending to `4`, so their partial sum is asymptotic to `4/3` times the last increment. Hence the bracket in (1) is

\[
-\frac{3\,4^m}{\sqrt\pi\,m^{5/2}}(1+o(1)),
\]

and

\[
u_n=-\frac3{m^4}(1+o(1)).
\]

This gives a more informative explanation than the bare characteristic polynomial:

* the recessive solution is the inverse-binomial `h_n`;
* discrete integration of its reduction-of-order quotient introduces a factor of `4^n`;
* that factor cancels the `4^{-n}` in `h_n`, leaving the power-law root-`1` branch.

So the mixed-decay phenomenon is genuinely Apéry/inverse-binomial in origin, but it is **not** evidence for a single mixed-slope positive double-integral kernel.

# 4. A simple Beukers-type integral, not the proposed mixed-slope kernel

For `m≥1`,

\[
\frac1{m^2\binom{2m}{m}}
 =\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
 x^{m-1}(1-x)^m y^{m-1}\,dx\,dy.
\tag{11}
\]

Indeed the `x` integral is `Beta(m,m+1)=1/(m binom(2m,m))`, and the `y` integral contributes another `1/m`.

Define, for `m≥4`,

\[
B_m:=-\frac{93}{4}
 -3\sum_{r=4}^{m-1}
   \frac{3r+2}{r(r+1)^2}\binom{2r}{r}.
\tag{12}
\]

Then `u_{m-3}=B_m/(m^2 C_m)`, so

\[
\boxed{
 u_n=B_{n+3}\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
 x^{n+2}(1-x)^{n+3}y^{n+2}\,dx\,dy.
}
\tag{13}
\]

The `1/4` slope is already encoded by `x(1-x)≤1/4`. The root `1` comes from the growth of `B_m`, not from doubling the powers of `y` and `(1-xy)`.

Thus a kernel of the proposed form

\[
\frac{x^{n+a}(1-x)^{n+b}y^{2n+c}(1-y)^{n+d}}
     {(1-xy)^{2n+e}}
\]

is not the shortest route. It may be possible to manufacture one after additional integral transforms, but it would obscure the exact first-order factorization (7).

# 5. Generating function of the finite-sum multiplier

Let

\[
g_r:=3\frac{3r+2}{r(r+1)^2}\binom{2r}{r}.
\]

The partial fraction

\[
\frac{3r+2}{r(r+1)^2}
 =\frac2r-\frac2{r+1}+\frac1{(r+1)^2}
\]

and the standard central-binomial generating functions give

\[
\boxed{
G(t):=\sum_{r\ge1}g_rt^r
 =3+\frac{3(1-4t)}{t}
   \log\!\left(\frac{1+\sqrt{1-4t}}2\right).
}
\tag{14}
\]

The first coefficients are

\[
g_1=\frac{15}{2},\qquad g_2=8,\qquad g_3=\frac{55}{4}.
\]

Now put

\[
H(t):=\sum_{m\ge4}B_mt^{m-1}.
\]

Since `B_4=-93/4` and `B_{m+1}-B_m=-g_m`,

\[
\boxed{
H(t)=\frac{
 -3-\dfrac{3(1-4t)}{t}
       \log\!\left(\dfrac{1+\sqrt{1-4t}}2\right)
 +\dfrac{15}{2}t+8t^2-\dfrac{19}{2}t^3
}{1-t}.
}
\tag{15}
\]

Combining (11) with absolute convergence gives

\[
S:=\sum_{n\ge1}u_n
 =\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
 (1-x)H(x(1-x)y)\,dx\,dy.
\tag{16}
\]

This is already an exact Beukers-style connection formula. It is also the cleanest starting point for a proof of the constant evaluation.

Absolute convergence is elementary: `g_r=O(4^r r^{-5/2})`, hence `B_m=O(4^m m^{-5/2})`, while `1/(m^2C_m)=O(4^{-m}m^{-3/2})`; therefore `u_{m-3}=O(m^{-4})`.

# 6. Exact evaluation of the sum

This section records a complete reduction to elementary logarithmic integrals and one standard cubic `3`-dissection at weight three.

Integrate first in `y` and interchange the order of integration. With

\[
s=\sqrt{1-4t},
\]

one obtains

\[
S=2\int_0^{1/4}H(t)\operatorname{artanh}(\sqrt{1-4t})\,dt.
\tag{17}
\]

Use the rationalizing substitution

\[
z=\frac{1-s}{1+s},\qquad
 t=\frac{z}{(1+z)^2},\qquad
 \log\frac{1+s}{2}=-\log(1+z).
\tag{18}
\]

Set

\[
q(z)=1+z+z^2,\qquad
R(z)=\frac{1-z}{(1+z)q(z)},\qquad
P(t)=\frac{15}{2}t+8t^2-\frac{19}{2}t^3.
\]

Then (17) becomes

\[
S=-\int_0^1 R(z)
 \left[-3+P\!\left(\frac{z}{(1+z)^2}\right)
 +3\frac{(1-z)^2}{z}\log(1+z)
 \right]\log z\,dz.
\tag{19}
\]

Split `S=S_2+S_3`, where `S_2` is the part without `log(1+z)` and `S_3` is the logarithmic part.

## 6.1 The weight-two part

Define

\[
J(z):=R(z)\left[-3+P\!\left(\frac{z}{(1+z)^2}\right)\right].
\]

A rational partial fraction calculation gives

\[
J(z)=-3\frac{2z+1}{q(z)}+L(z),
\tag{20}
\]

where

\[
\begin{aligned}
L(z)={}&\frac6{1+z}+\frac6{(1+z)^2}
 -\frac{27}{2(1+z)^3}-\frac5{(1+z)^4}\\
&+\frac{35}{(1+z)^5}
 -\frac{95}{2(1+z)^6}+\frac{19}{(1+z)^7}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{21}
\]

First,

\[
\int_0^1\frac{2z+1}{q(z)}\log z\,dz
 =-\int_0^1\frac{\log q(z)}z\,dz
 =-\frac23\zeta(2),
\tag{22}
\]

because `q(z)=(1-z^3)/(1-z)`.

For `k≥2`, set

\[
I_k:=\int_0^1\frac{\log z}{(1+z)^k}\,dz.
\]

Integration by parts with a primitive vanishing at zero gives

\[
I_k=-\frac1{k-1}\left[
\log2+\sum_{r=1}^{k-2}\frac{1-2^{-r}}r
\right],
\tag{23}
\]

while

\[
I_1=-\frac12\zeta(2).
\]

In particular,

\[
\begin{array}{c|c}
k&I_k\\ \hline
2&-\log2\\
3&-\frac12\log2-\frac14\\
4&-\frac13\log2-\frac7{24}\\
5&-\frac14\log2-\frac7{24}\\
6&-\frac15\log2-\frac{269}{960}\\
7&-\frac16\log2-\frac{1531}{5760}.
\end{array}
\]

Substitution into (21) cancels every `log 2` term and gives

\[
\int_0^1L(z)\log z\,dz
 =-3\zeta(2)+\frac{2077}{720}.
\tag{24}
\]

Equations (20), (22), and (24) therefore yield

\[
\boxed{S_2=\zeta(2)-\frac{2077}{720}.}
\tag{25}
\]

This calculation explains the otherwise mysterious rational number `2077/720`: it is exactly the rational residue of the seven elementary `(1+z)^{-k}` logarithmic integrals in (21).

## 6.2 The weight-three part

The coefficient of `log z log(1+z)` in (19) is

\[
K(z):=3\frac{(1-z)^3}{z(1+z)q(z)}.
\]

Its partial fraction decomposition is exceptionally simple:

\[
\boxed{
K(z)=\frac3z-\frac{24}{1+z}
 +9\frac{2z+1}{q(z)}.
}
\tag{26}
\]

The three needed integrals are

\[
\begin{aligned}
A&:=\int_0^1\frac{\log z\log(1+z)}z\,dz
   =-\frac34\zeta(3),\\
B&:=\int_0^1\frac{\log z\log(1+z)}{1+z}\,dz
   =-\frac18\zeta(3),\\
C&:=\int_0^1\frac{2z+1}{1+z+z^2}
       \log z\log(1+z)\,dz
   =-\frac7{36}\zeta(3).
\end{aligned}
\tag{27}
\]

The first follows by the Mercator series. For the second, integrate by parts and use

\[
\int_0^1\frac{\log^2(1+z)}z\,dz=\frac14\zeta(3).
\]

For completeness, the third is the standard weight-three cubic dissection. Let

\[
\omega=e^{2\pi i/3},
\qquad
\operatorname{Li}_{2,1}(a,b)
 :=\sum_{N>m\ge1}\frac{a^N b^m}{N^2m}.
\]

Expanding the integrand in (27) gives

\[
C=-\operatorname{Li}_{2,1}(\omega,-\omega^2)
  -\operatorname{Li}_{2,1}(\omega^2,-\omega).
\tag{28}
\]

The cubic distribution/double-shuffle identity is

\[
\boxed{
\operatorname{Li}_{2,1}(\omega,-\omega^2)
+\operatorname{Li}_{2,1}(\omega^2,-\omega)
=\frac7{36}\zeta(3).
}
\tag{29}
\]

An entirely real equivalent certificate is as follows. Define

\[
\begin{aligned}
T&:=\sum_{N>m\ge1}\frac{(-1)^m}{N^2m},\\
D&:=\sum_{\substack{N>m\ge1\\N\equiv m\pmod3}}
       \frac{(-1)^m}{N^2m}.
\end{aligned}
\]

Root-of-unity filtering gives the left side of (29) as `3D-T`, and the standard alternating Euler-sum evaluations are

\[
T=\zeta(3)-\frac{\pi^2}{4}\log2,
\qquad
D=\frac{43}{108}\zeta(3)-\frac{\pi^2}{12}\log2.
\tag{30}
\]

Thus `3D-T=7ζ(3)/36`. Formula (30) follows directly by the cubic root-of-unity filter, the stuffle relation for `Li_2 Li_1`, and

\[
\sum_{j=0}^{2}\operatorname{Li}_3(\xi\omega^j)
 =\frac19\operatorname{Li}_3(\xi^3).
\]

This is a small, self-contained special-value lemma; no unproved connection formula is being hidden here.

Finally, (26)–(27) give

\[
S_3=-3A+24B-9C=\zeta(3).
\tag{31}
\]

Combining (25) and (31),

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}u_n
 =\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)-\frac{2077}{720}.
}
\]

Equivalently,

\[
\boxed{
\frac{2077}{720}+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}u_n
 =\zeta(2)+\zeta(3).
}
\]

# 7. Exact checks suitable for Mathematica, Maple, or Sage

The recurrence factorization can be checked without any numerical approximation.

```Mathematica
Clear[n, r];
A[n_] := 2 (n + 3)^3 (2 n + 5) (3 n + 5);
B[n_] := (n + 2)^2 (15 n^3 + 85 n^2 + 155 n + 93);
C[n_] := (n + 1)^3 (n + 2) (3 n + 8);

h[n_] := 1/((n + 3)^2 Binomial[2 n + 6, n + 3]);
d[n_] := -3 (3 n + 8) Binomial[2 n + 4, n + 2]/
          ((n + 2) (n + 3)^2);

FullSimplify[A[n] h[n] - B[n] h[n - 1] + C[n] h[n - 2],
  Assumptions -> Element[n, Integers] && n >= 3]

FullSimplify[d[n]/d[n - 1] -
  2 (n + 1) (2 n + 3) (3 n + 8)/((n + 3)^2 (3 n + 5)),
  Assumptions -> Element[n, Integers] && n >= 3]

uClosed[n_] := h[n] (-93/4 + Sum[d[k], {k, 2, n}]);

FullSimplify[uClosed[1] + 93/4480]
FullSimplify[uClosed[2] + 117/14000]
FullSimplify[A[n] uClosed[n] - B[n] uClosed[n - 1] +
  C[n] uClosed[n - 2],
  Assumptions -> Element[n, Integers] && n >= 3]
```

The two key partial fractions in the connection calculation are also purely rational checks:

```Mathematica
q[z_] := 1 + z + z^2;
t[z_] := z/(1 + z)^2;
R[z_] := (1 - z)/((1 + z) q[z]);
P[x_] := 15 x/2 + 8 x^2 - 19 x^3/2;

J[z_] := R[z] (-3 + P[t[z]]);
K[z_] := 3 (1 - z)^3/(z (1 + z) q[z]);

Apart[J[z] + 3 (2 z + 1)/q[z], z]
Apart[K[z], z]
```

The expected outputs are respectively (21) and (26).

For the only cyclotomic special-value lemma, an exact CAS target is

```Mathematica
FullSimplify[
 Integrate[(2 z + 1) Log[z] Log[1 + z]/(z^2 + z + 1),
   {z, 0, 1}, GenerateConditions -> False]
 + 7 Zeta[3]/36
]
```

A more robust symbolic-summation route is to use the real sums `T,D` in (30), apply a third-root-of-unity filter to `D`, and reduce the resulting weight-three colored multiple polylogarithms using shuffle/stuffle plus the trilogarithm distribution relation.

# 8. Relation to Apéry/Beukers literature

The recognition is now precise:

1. The recessive solution is literally the shifted summand in Euler's inverse-central-binomial acceleration
   \[
   \zeta(2)=3\sum_{m\ge1}\frac1{m^2\binom{2m}{m}}.
   \]
2. The dominant solution is produced by one discrete antidifferentiation whose increments contain `binom(2m,m)` in the numerator.
3. The square-root/logarithm in (14) is the standard analytic signature of central-binomial generating functions.
4. The extra logarithm in the beta/Hadamard transform raises the final weight from two to three, producing the `ζ(3)` component.

This is adjacent to Apéry–Beukers and inverse-binomial/WZ technology, but it is not the classical Apéry recurrence for `ζ(3)`, nor is it simply the well-known alternating identity

\[
\zeta(3)=\frac52\sum_{m\ge1}
 \frac{(-1)^{m-1}}{m^3\binom{2m}{m}}.
\]

The closest accurate label is:

> a d'Alembertian central-binomial acceleration of `ζ(2)+ζ(3)`.

# 9. Do we know the actual historical/source formula?

I did not locate this exact recurrence or the initial values in the public Apéry/Beukers/inverse-binomial literature. Exact-string searches led only to the Ramanujan Challenge statement and mirrors of it.

This is consistent with the official challenge description: Section 2 consists of formulas whose proofs or algorithmic proof procedures are known to the organizers but were intentionally not made public during the initial challenge period. The official sources are:

* Challenge page: <https://www.ramanujanmachine.com/ramanujan-challenge/>
* Challenge PDF: <https://www.ramanujanmachine.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/07/The_Ramanujan_Challenge__Clean_.pdf>

Accordingly, it would be irresponsible to claim that this is a named pre-existing acceleration or to attribute a specific hidden derivation. Structurally, it is very likely to have arisen from one of the following equivalent computational viewpoints:

* a central-binomial Euler/Abel transform;
* a WZ/creative-telescoping construction;
* a conservative-matrix-field construction subsequently scalarized;
* a holonomic connection problem built from the inverse-binomial `ζ(2)` kernel and one logarithmic lift.

The exact factorization above is strong evidence for the first two. It may well be close to the intended encrypted proof, but that cannot presently be verified from a public source.

# Bottom line

The shortest productive route is not to search a broad family of doubled Beukers kernels. It is:

```text
spot h_n = 1 / ((n+3)^2 * C(2n+6,n+3))
    ↓
reduce order: u_n/h_n has hypergeometric first difference
    ↓
obtain the exact nested-binomial formula (1)
    ↓
use the beta representation of 1/(m^2 C(2m,m))
    ↓
central-binomial generating function (14)
    ↓
rationalize sqrt(1-4t)
    ↓
weight 2 = ζ(2) - 2077/720
weight 3 = ζ(3).
```

This simultaneously supplies the closed form, the correct integral architecture, the explanation of the two Poincaré roots, and an exact proof route for the claimed sum.
