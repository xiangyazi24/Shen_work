# Package II: torsion in the ramified \(3\)-adic formal kernel

## Executive verdict

The desired conclusion is correct, but the proposed calculation needs two important corrections.

1. The coefficient tuple
   
   \[
   (a_1,a_2,a_3,a_4,a_6)=(1,-1,1,-5,5)
   \]
   
   is the original rational model
   
   \[
   E_0:\quad y^2+xy+y=x^3-x^2-5x+5.
   \]
   
   That equation has discriminant \(-648\), so it is not the good integral model at the prime above \(3\). Its formal parameter at infinity also does not contain the visible order-\(3\) point \(T=(1,0)\): at that point \(y=0\), so the rational function \(-x/y\) is not a small local coordinate. The formal-kernel statement must be made on the explicit good \(L_\pi\)-model, or after transporting points and the parameter through the explicit isomorphism to that model.

2. At valuation \(k=1\), the relevant two valuations are **\(4\) and \(4\)**, not \(4\) and \(3\). Unequal valuations cannot cancel. On the good model, the cubic coefficient has valuation exactly \(1\), so its contribution has valuation
   
   \[
   1+3k.
   \]
   
   Thus at \(k=1\),
   
   \[
   3+k=4=1+3k,
   \]
   
   and cancellation is possible. For \(k\ge2\),
   
   \[
   3+k<1+3k,
   \]
   
   so the linear contribution is uniquely minimal and no cancellation is possible.

With that correction, the precise result is:

> Let \(z\ne0\) be a point of the formal kernel of the good model, and put \(k=v_\pi(z)\ge1\). Then
> 
> \[
> k\ge2\quad\Longrightarrow\quad v_\pi([3](z))=3+k<\infty.
> \]
> 
> Consequently \([3]\) is injective on the subgroup with \(v_\pi(z)\ge2\). Multiplication by every integer prime to \(3\) is also injective there. Hence
> 
> \[
> \widehat E_0(\mathfrak m^2)
> \]
> 
> is torsion-free. Equivalently, every nonzero torsion point in
> 
> \[
> \widehat E_0(\mathfrak m)
> \]
> 
> has \(v_\pi(z)=1\).

In particular, after transporting the known point \(T=(1,0)\) to the good model, its formal parameter has valuation exactly \(1\).

# 2. Actual computation of the low \([3]\)-series

I will derive the coefficients for a general generalized Weierstrass equation, with the convention \(t=-x/y\), and then specialize.

Let

\[
E:\quad y^2+a_1xy+a_3y=x^3+a_2x^2+a_4x+a_6.
\]

## 2.1 The \(w\)-equation

Put

\[
w=-1/y.
\]

Then

\[
x=t/w,\qquad y=-1/w.
\]

Substituting in the Weierstrass equation and multiplying by \(w^3\) gives the exact identity

\[
\boxed{
 w=t^3+a_1tw+a_2t^2w+a_3w^2+a_4tw^2+a_6w^3.
}
\]

In particular,

\[
w=t^3+O(t^4).
\]

## 2.2 The invariant differential

The invariant differential is

\[
\omega=\frac{dx}{2y+a_1x+a_3}.
\]

In the \(t,w\) coordinates one obtains

\[
\omega=q(t)\,dt,
\]

with

\[
q(t)=
\frac{1}{1-a_1t-a_2t^2-2a_3w-2a_4tw-3a_6w^2}.
\]

Using \(w=t^3+O(t^4)\), inversion of the denominator gives

\[
\boxed{
q(t)=1+a_1t+(a_1^2+a_2)t^2
 +(a_1^3+2a_1a_2+2a_3)t^3+O(t^4).
}
\]

No logarithm is being introduced here; this is only the invariant differential written in the local parameter.

## 2.3 Pullback under multiplication by \(3\)

Write

\[
h(t)=[3](t)=3t+b_2t^2+b_3t^3+b_4t^4+O(t^5).
\]

Translation-invariance of \(\omega\) gives the algebraic identity

\[
[3]^*\omega=3\omega.
\]

In the parameter \(t\), this is

\[
\boxed{q(h(t))h'(t)=3q(t).}
\]

Set

\[
A=a_1,\qquad B=a_1^2+a_2,
\qquad C=a_1^3+2a_1a_2+2a_3.
\]

Then

\[
q(h)=1+3At+(Ab_2+9B)t^2
 +(Ab_3+6Bb_2+27C)t^3+O(t^4),
\]

and

\[
h'=3+2b_2t+3b_3t^2+4b_4t^3+O(t^4).
\]

Comparing coefficients in \(q(h)h'=3q\):

### Coefficient of \(t\)

\[
9a_1+2b_2=3a_1,
\]

hence

\[
\boxed{b_2=-3a_1.}
\]

### Coefficient of \(t^2\)

\[
9a_1b_2+27(a_1^2+a_2)+3b_3
 =3(a_1^2+a_2).
\]

Substituting \(b_2=-3a_1\) gives

\[
\boxed{b_3=a_1^2-8a_2.}
\]

### Coefficient of \(t^3\)

The equation is

\[
12a_1b_3+36(a_1^2+a_2)b_2+2a_1b_2^2
 +81C+4b_4=3C.
\]

Substituting the preceding values and simplifying gives

\[
\boxed{b_4=3(4a_1a_2-13a_3).}
\]

Therefore, for the parameter \(t=-x/y\), the exact low-order formula is

\[
\boxed{
[3](t)
 =3t-3a_1t^2+(a_1^2-8a_2)t^3
 +3(4a_1a_2-13a_3)t^4+O(t^5).
}
\]

This derivation uses only the \(w\)-equation, the invariant differential, and the identity \([3]^*\omega=3\omega\).

# 3. Specialization to the two models

## 3.1 Why the original rational tuple does not give Package II

For

\[
(a_1,a_2,a_3)=(1,-1,1),
\]

the formula gives

\[
\boxed{
[3](t)=3t-3t^2+9t^3-51t^4+O(t^5).
}
\]

Thus, over \(K\),

\[
v_\pi(3)=3,\qquad
v_\pi(-3)=3,\qquad
v_\pi(9)=6,\qquad
v_\pi(-51)=3.
\]

In particular, the cubic coefficient is not a unit and does not have valuation \(1\); it has valuation \(6\). This is another direct indication that the rational model is not the local chart in which the visible order-\(3\) point sits in the formal kernel.

## 3.2 Coefficients on the good model

For the good model, the first coefficients of \([3](t)\) are

\[
\begin{aligned}
c_1&=3,\\
c_2&=-3A_1,\\
c_3&=A_1^2-8A_2,\\
c_4&=3(4A_1A_2-13A_3).
\end{aligned}
\]

First,

\[
A_1A_2=A_3.
\]

This is a direct calculation using \(a^3=3a+1\). Hence

\[
c_4=3(4-13)A_3=-27A_3.
\]

For the cubic coefficient,

\[
\begin{aligned}
c_3
 &=A_1^2-8A_2\\
 &=10a^2-3a-13.
\end{aligned}
\]

Substituting \(a=1+\pi\) gives

\[
c_3=10\pi^2+17\pi-6.
\]

From

\[
3=\pi^2(\pi+3)
\]

we obtain

\[
-6=-2\pi^2(\pi+3),
\]

and therefore

\[
\boxed{
c_3=\pi u_3,
\qquad
u_3:=17+4\pi-2\pi^2.
}
\]

Modulo \(\pi\),

\[
u_3\equiv17\equiv2\pmod\pi,
\]

so \(u_3\) is a unit. Consequently

\[
\boxed{v_\pi(c_3)=1.}
\]

The relevant coefficient table is therefore

| degree | coefficient | valuation |
|---:|---|---:|
| \(1\) | \(3\) | \(3\) |
| \(2\) | \(-3A_1\) | \(3\) |
| \(3\) | \(\pi u_3\), \(u_3\in\mathcal O^\times\) | \(1\) |
| \(4\) | \(-27A_3\) | \(9\) |

Thus

\[
\boxed{
[3](t)
 =3t-3A_1t^2+\pi u_3t^3-27A_3t^4+O(t^5).
}
\]

The reduction is supersingular: modulo \(\pi\), the \(t^3\)-coefficient vanishes, and the first nonzero term of the reduced \(3\)-series occurs in degree \(9\). That is fully compatible with \(v_\pi(c_3)=1\). Package II uses the first-order \(\pi\)-divisibility of \(c_3\), not a unit \(t^3\)-term modulo \(\pi\).

# 4. Exact decomposition \([3](T)=3f(T)+g(T^3)\)

Let

\[
[3](T)=\sum_{n\ge0}c_nT^n\in\mathcal O[[T]].
\]

The decomposition requested in the question is valid, but the valuation of the first coefficient of \(g\) must be recorded.

Reduce the invariant-differential identity modulo \(3\mathcal O\). In the characteristic-\(3\) ring \(\mathcal O/3\mathcal O\),

\[
q([3](T))[3]'(T)=0.
\]

The series \(q([3](T))\) has constant coefficient \(1\), so it is a unit. Hence

\[
[3]'(T)=0\quad\text{in }(\mathcal O/3\mathcal O)[[T]].
\]

For every \(n\) not divisible by \(3\), multiplication by \(n\) is a unit in a characteristic-\(3\) ring. Therefore

\[
3\nmid n\quad\Longrightarrow\quad c_n\in3\mathcal O.
\]

Define coefficientwise

\[
f(T)=\sum_{3\nmid n}\frac{c_n}{3}T^n,
\qquad
g(U)=\sum_{j\ge0}c_{3j}U^j.
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{[3](T)=3f(T)+g(T^3).}
\]

Because \(c_1=3\),

\[
f(T)=T+T^2F(T)
\]

for some \(F\in\mathcal O[[T]]\). More explicitly, the beginning is

\[
f(T)=T-A_1T^2-9A_3T^4+\cdots.
\]

Because \(c_0=0\) and \(c_3=\pi u_3\),

\[
g(U)=\pi u_3U+U^2G(U)
\]

for some \(G\in\mathcal O[[U]]\).

Thus the exact useful shape is

\[
\boxed{
[3](T)
 =3\bigl(T+T^2F(T)\bigr)
  +\pi u_3T^3+T^6G(T^3),
\qquad u_3\in\mathcal O^\times.
}
\]

The factor \(\pi\) in the cubic coefficient is the missing datum in the proposed comparison.

# 5. Valuation comparison

Let \(z\in\mathfrak m_K\) be nonzero and put

\[
k=v_\pi(z)\in\mathbf Z_{\ge1}.
\]

Evaluation of an integral power series at \(z\in\mathfrak m_K\) remains integral. Thus

\[
v_\pi(F(z))\ge0,
\qquad
v_\pi(G(z^3))\ge0.
\]

## 5.1 The \(3f(z)\) contribution

Since

\[
f(z)=z\bigl(1+zF(z)\bigr)
\]

and

\[
v_\pi(zF(z))\ge k\ge1,
\]

the factor \(1+zF(z)\) is a unit. Therefore

\[
v_\pi(f(z))=k,
\]

and hence

\[
\boxed{v_\pi(3f(z))=3+k.}
\]

## 5.2 The \(g(z^3)\) contribution

We have

\[
g(z^3)=\pi u_3z^3+z^6G(z^3).
\]

The first term has valuation

\[
v_\pi(\pi u_3z^3)=1+3k.
\]

The second has valuation at least

\[
6k.
\]

For every \(k\ge1\),

\[
1+3k<6k.
\]

Therefore the two terms inside \(g(z^3)\) have unequal valuations, and the ultrametric equality gives

\[
\boxed{v_\pi(g(z^3))=1+3k.}
\]

In particular, the weaker estimate requested in the question is true:

\[
v_\pi(g(z^3))\ge3k,
\]

but the exact value \(1+3k\) is what explains the boundary case.

## 5.3 The case \(k=1\)

Now

\[
v_\pi(3f(z))=3+1=4,
\]

and

\[
v_\pi(g(z^3))=1+3=4.
\]

Thus the two summands can cancel. The ultrametric inequality gives only

\[
v_\pi([3](z))\ge4,
\]

and the value may be strictly larger or \(+\infty\).

There is even a useful first-graded-piece check. Write \(z=\pi s\) with \(s\) a unit. Since

\[
\frac{3}{\pi^3}=(1-\pi^2)^{-1}\equiv1\pmod\pi
\]

and

\[
\frac{c_3}{\pi}=u_3\equiv2\pmod\pi,
\]

the coefficient of \(\pi^4\), after dividing by \(\pi^4\), is

\[
\bar s+2\bar s^3.
\]

In \(\mathbf F_3\), \(ar s^3=ar s\), so this is

\[
\bar s+2\bar s=0.
\]

Thus first-order cancellation at level \(4\) is not merely permitted; it is forced for every unit residue \(s\). Further cancellation is what distinguishes an actual \(3\)-torsion root from a general valuation-\(1\) point.

The statement in the question saying “\(4\) versus \(3\), so cancellation is possible” must be replaced by this \(4\)-versus-\(4\) calculation. If the valuations were \(4\) and \(3\), cancellation would be impossible and the sum would have valuation \(3\).

## 5.4 The case \(k\ge2\)

For \(k\ge2\),

\[
(1+3k)-(3+k)=2(k-1)>0.
\]

Hence

\[
3+k<1+3k.
\]

The two summands in

\[
[3](z)=3f(z)+g(z^3)
\]

have unequal valuations, so

\[
\boxed{v_\pi([3](z))=3+k.}
\]

This is finite, so

\[
\boxed{[3](z)\ne0.}
\]

At the boundary \(k=2\), the exact comparison is

\[
3+2=5,
\qquad
1+3\cdot2=7.
\]

If one uses only the coarse estimate \(v(g(z^3))\ge3k\), the requested boundary comparison is

\[
5<6.
\]

Both prove the result, but the exact cubic calculation gives \(5<7\).

The complete valuation statement is therefore

\[
\boxed{
\begin{cases}
 k=1:&v_\pi([3](z))\ge4,\text{ with cancellation possible};\\
 k\ge2:&v_\pi([3](z))=3+k<\infty.
\end{cases}}
\]

# 6. Torsion-freeness of \(\widehat E_0(\mathfrak m^2)\)

Let

\[
\widehat E_0(\mathfrak m^2)
 =\{P\in\widehat E_0(\mathfrak m):v_\pi(z(P))\ge2\}.
\]

This is a subgroup because the formal group law has integral coefficients and begins \(X+Y\).

## 6.1 No \(3\)-power torsion

The valuation theorem proves

\[
[3]P=0,\quad P\in\widehat E_0(\mathfrak m^2)
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad P=0.
\]

Thus multiplication by \(3\) is injective on this subgroup. Since \([3]\) preserves \(\widehat E_0(\mathfrak m^2)\), iteration shows that multiplication by \(3^r\) is injective for every \(r\ge0\). Hence there is no nonzero \(3\)-power torsion in \(\widehat E_0(\mathfrak m^2)\).

## 6.2 No prime-to-\(3\) torsion

For every positive integer \(n\), the formal \(n\)-series has the universal form

\[
[n](T)=nT+T^2A_n(T),
\qquad A_n(T)\in\mathcal O[[T]].
\]

If \(3\nmid n\), then \(n\) is a unit of \(\mathcal O\), so \(v_\pi(n)=0\). For nonzero \(z\in\mathfrak m\) with \(v_\pi(z)=k\ge1\),

\[
v_\pi(nz)=k,
\]

while

\[
v_\pi(z^2A_n(z))\ge2k>k.
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{v_\pi([n](z))=k}
\]

and in particular \([n](z)\ne0\). Thus there is no nonzero torsion of order prime to \(3\), even in the full first formal neighborhood.

## 6.3 Arbitrary torsion order

Let \(P\in\widehat E_0(\mathfrak m^2)\) satisfy

\[
[n]P=0
\]

for some \(n>0\). Write

\[
n=3^r m,
\qquad 3\nmid m.
\]

Set \(Q=[m]P\). Then \(Q\in\widehat E_0(\mathfrak m^2)\) and

\[
[3^r]Q=0.
\]

Injectivity of \([3]\), iterated \(r\) times, gives \(Q=0\). Injectivity of \([m]\) then gives \(P=0\). Hence

\[
\boxed{\widehat E_0(\mathfrak m^2)\text{ is torsion-free}.}
\]

Equivalently, if \(P\ne0\) is torsion in \(\widehat E_0(\mathfrak m)\), then \(v_\pi(z(P))\ge1\), but it cannot be at least \(2\). Since the value group is \(\mathbf Z\),

\[
\boxed{v_\pi(z(P))=1.}
\]

## 6.4 The known order-\(3\) point

The rational-model point

\[
T=(1,0)
\]

has exact order \(3\). Transport it to the good \(L_\pi\)-model. Its reduction is killed by \(3\), because \(3T=0\). The good reduction group has order and exponent \(7\), so the same reduction is also killed by \(7\). Since \(\gcd(3,7)=1\), its reduction is zero. Thus the transported point lies in the formal kernel.

It is nonzero torsion, so the preceding theorem forces

\[
\boxed{v_\pi(z(T))=1.}
\]

This proves the exact assertion about the visible order-\(3\) point without evaluating its transformed coordinates explicitly.

## 7.2 Low-series coefficient theorem

Let `e0ThreeSeries : O⟦X⟧` be the multiplication-by-\(3\) series of the good model. Prove

```lean
@[simp] lemma coeff_e0ThreeSeries_zero :
    PowerSeries.coeff 0 e0ThreeSeries = 0 := ...

@[simp] lemma coeff_e0ThreeSeries_one :
    PowerSeries.coeff 1 e0ThreeSeries = 3 := ...

@[simp] lemma coeff_e0ThreeSeries_two :
    PowerSeries.coeff 2 e0ThreeSeries = -3 * A1 := ...

@[simp] lemma coeff_e0ThreeSeries_three :
    PowerSeries.coeff 3 e0ThreeSeries = c3 := ...

@[simp] lemma coeff_e0ThreeSeries_four :
    PowerSeries.coeff 4 e0ThreeSeries = -27 * A3 := ...
```

The proofs of degrees \(2,3,4\) can be organized around the coefficient calculation in Section 2. A convenient project-local theorem is the truncated invariant-differential identity

```lean
qSeries.subst e0ThreeSeries * PowerSeries.derivative O e0ThreeSeries
  = PowerSeries.C 3 * qSeries
```

followed by `congrArg (PowerSeries.coeff n)` for `n = 1,2,3` and `ring_nf`. It is unnecessary to construct a formal logarithm.

For the minimal valuation proof, package only the first four terms:

```lean
theorem e0ThreeSeries_low :
    ∃ H : O⟦X⟧,
      e0ThreeSeries =
          PowerSeries.C 3 * PowerSeries.X
        + PowerSeries.C (-3 * A1) * PowerSeries.X ^ 2
        + PowerSeries.C c3 * PowerSeries.X ^ 3
        + PowerSeries.X ^ 4 * H := by
  -- Let R be the series after subtracting the first three nonzero monomials.
  -- Its coefficients in degrees 0,1,2,3 vanish.
  -- Define H by coefficient shift: coeff n H := coeff (n+4) R.
  -- Finish by PowerSeries.ext and split n as 0,1,2,3 or m+4.
  ...
```

A direct definition of the shifted tail is

```lean
noncomputable def shiftFour (R : O⟦X⟧) : O⟦X⟧ :=
  PowerSeries.mk fun n => PowerSeries.coeff (n + 4) R
```

and the proof is coefficient extensionality using `PowerSeries.coeff_mk` and the coefficient formulas for powers of `X`.

```lean
lemma ordPi_eval_nonneg
    (H : O⟦X⟧) {z : K}
    (hz : 1 ≤ ordPi z) :
    0 ≤ ordPi (PowerSeries.eval₂ (algebraMap O K) z H) := ...
```

The exact topology proof belongs in the existing evaluation toolbox. Package II should consume this lemma, not reopen convergence details.

```lean
    norm_num
    exact_mod_cast (show 3 + k < 1 + 3 * k by omega)

  have htail :
      ((3 + k : ℤ) : WithTop ℤ) <
        ordPi (z ^ 4 * PowerSeries.eval₂ (algebraMap O K) z H) := by
    rw [ordPi_mul, ordPi_pow, hk]
    -- use hHz and 3+k < 4*k for k ≥ 2
    ...

  have hrest :
      ((3 + k : ℤ) : WithTop ℤ) <
        ordPi
          (((-3 * A1 : K) * z ^ 2)
            + (c3 : K) * z ^ 3
            + z ^ 4 * PowerSeries.eval₂ (algebraMap O K) z H) := by
    -- Repeatedly use the ultrametric lower bound.
    exact ordPi_add_gt_of_gt hquad
      (ordPi_add_gt_of_gt hcubic htail)

  rw [threeEval, hH]
  simp only [map_add, map_mul, PowerSeries.eval₂_C,
    PowerSeries.eval₂_X, map_pow]
  exact ordPi_add_eq_left_of_lt hlin hrest
```

The displayed body is schematic only in the names of the project-local `ordPi` wrappers. The proof state after each valuation rewrite is elementary integer arithmetic, and `omega` should close it.

```lean
        have hm0 : m ≠ 0 := by omega
        have hm_lt : m < 3 * m := by omega

          three_nsmul_eq_zero_on_m2 (m • P) h3m

          intro hP0
          subst hP0
          simpa using hnP)
```

# 8. Minimal dependency list

## 8.1 Existing project-local infrastructure actually needed

Only the following curve-specific interfaces are load-bearing:

1. the explicit good integral model and its isomorphism with the base-changed rational curve;
2. the formal parameter equivalence between the good-reduction kernel and the maximal ideal;
3. compatibility of scalar multiplication with evaluation of the formal \([n]\)-series;
4. `ordPi pi = 1`, `ordPi 3 = 3`, and the standard additive-valuation rules;
5. integral power-series evaluation at \(z\in\mathfrak m\);

No point-count or supersingularity theorem is needed for the abstract \(\mathfrak m^2\)-torsion-free result. The reduction exponent \(7\) is used only to place the known order-\(3\) point in the formal kernel without computing its transformed coordinates.

Mathlib's raw `Valuation` is multiplicative and has the visually reversed order convention. Do not mix that order directly with additive statements such as \(v(x+y)\ge\min(v(x),v(y))\). The project should expose an additive wrapper

# 9. Self-contained logical dependency

The proof is elementary in exactly the Silverman AEC IV.6 / VII formal-group sense:

```text
explicit good Weierstrass equation
    ↓
w-equation and invariant differential
    ↓
[3](T) = 3T - 3A1*T^2 + (pi*unit)*T^3 + T^4*H(T)
    ↓
for v(T)=k≥2:
  3+k < 3+2k,
  3+k < 1+3k,
  3+k < 4k
    ↓
v([3](T)) = 3+k < infinity
    ↓
[3] injective on m^2
    +
[n] valuation-preserving for 3∤n
    ↓
formal m^2 subgroup is torsion-free
    ↓
nonzero torsion in formal m has valuation exactly 1.
```

There is no use of:

- convergence of the formal logarithm;
- a Mathlib formal-logarithm object;
- the radius \(e/(p-1)\) as a black-box theorem;
- analytic \(p\)-adic integration.

The number \(e/(p-1)=3/2\) is only a heuristic threshold. The actual fixed-curve balance is controlled by the valuation-\(1\) cubic coefficient:

\[
3+k=1+3k,
\]

whose integral solution is \(k=1\). That is the exact elementary reason the order-\(3\) torsion can occur in \(\mathfrak m\setminus\mathfrak m^2\), but not in \(\mathfrak m^2\).
