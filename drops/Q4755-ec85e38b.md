# Ramanujan Challenge 2.7: the exact level-11 spectral connection, the period, and the gauge obstruction

## Executive conclusion

There is an exact and quite striking connection with Shaun Cooper's level-11 four-term recurrence, but it is **not** an equality of recurrences and it is **not** an ordinary factorial/Pochhammer gauge transformation.

Let

\[
C(t)=t^3-20t^2+56t-44
\]

be the Poincaré polynomial of Cooper's level-11 recurrence, and let

\[
F(\mu)=4\mu^3-220\mu^2+8\mu-1
\]

be the Poincaré polynomial extracted from Problem 2.7. Then

\[
\boxed{\mu=\frac{(t-2)^2}{4}}
\]

maps the three roots of \(C\) bijectively onto the three roots of \(F\). Equivalently, one has the exact polynomial certificate

\[
\boxed{
16F\!\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{4}\right)=-C(t)C(4-t).
}
\tag{1}
\]

This identifies the challenge recurrence with the **quadratic spectral image** of Cooper's level-11 case. It does not, by itself, identify the full finite-\(n\) Ore operator.

The direct answers to the four questions are:

1. **Cooper entry.** The relevant entry is Cooper's level \(11\) sequence \(T_{11}(n)\), with parameter tuple
   
   \[
   (a,b,c,d,e)=(10,4,-56,-8,22),
   \]
   
   not a separately needed Greek-labelled case. The challenge polynomial is the quadratic spectral image (1) of its characteristic polynomial.
2. **Solutions.** Both recurrences are order \(3\), so their solution spaces are three-dimensional, not two-dimensional. Cooper has a canonical integer solution \(T_{11}(n)\). For the challenge, the two distinguished solutions are the supplied denominator and numerator sequences \(q_n,p_n\); the period-normalized solution is
   
   \[
   e_n=p_n-(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))q_n.
   \]
   
   No exact transformation carrying Cooper's \(T_{11}(n)\) to \(q_n\) has yet been established.
3. **Period.** A completely explicit double period is
   
   \[
   \boxed{
   \zeta(2)+\zeta(3)=
   \int_0^1\!\int_0^1
   \frac{1-\frac12\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
   }
   \tag{2}
   \]
   
   What is still missing for a proof of Problem 2.7 is an \(n\)-dependent integral kernel whose creative telescoping recurrence is exactly the challenge recurrence.
4. **Gauge from 2.6.** There is no scalar factorial/Pochhammer gauge from Problem 2.6 to Problem 2.7. The former is order \(2\), while the latter is order \(3\); their Poincaré data also rule out a summation lift or symmetric-square-plus-scalar-gauge explanation. Any genuine relation must be a non-scalar transform, such as an algebraic pullback, a matrix/Darboux intertwiner, or an integral transform.

## 1. The precise Cooper level-11 entry

In Cooper's paper *Apéry-like sequences defined by four-term recurrence relations* (arXiv:2302.00757v2, Tables 3, 4, and 7, and Section 5), the level-11 modular parametrization is

\[
Z(q)=\Theta_{1,1,3}(q)^2,
\qquad
\Theta_{1,1,3}(q)=\sum_{j,k\in\mathbf Z}q^{j^2+jk+3k^2},
\]

and

\[
X(q)=\frac{\eta(q)^2\eta(q^{11})^2}{Z(q)}.
\]

Writing

\[
Z(q)=\sum_{n\ge0}T_{11}(n)X(q)^n,
\]

Cooper obtains

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
(n+1)^3T_{11}(n+1)
={}&2(2n+1)(5n^2+5n+2)T_{11}(n)\\
&-8n(7n^2+1)T_{11}(n-1)\\
&+22n(2n-1)(n-1)T_{11}(n-2).
\end{aligned}
}
\tag{3}
\]

The self-starting solution begins

\[
\boxed{
T_{11}(n)=1,4,28,268,3004,36784,476476,6418192,\ldots
}
\tag{4}
\]

The parameter tuple in Cooper's general four-term template is

\[
\boxed{(a,b,c,d,e)=(10,4,-56,-8,22).}
\tag{5}
\]

The accompanying genus-one equation in Cooper's table is

\[
B^2=1-20X+56X^2-44X^3,
\qquad
H=4X(1-8X+11X^2).
\tag{6}
\]

Because \(X_0(11)\) has genus \(1\), \(X\) should not be described as a genus-zero Hauptmodul. The pair \((X,B)\) lies on the elliptic curve (6).

Taking the leading part of (3) and substituting \(T_{11}(n)\sim t^n\) gives

\[
t^3-20t^2+56t-44=0.
\]

Thus Cooper's level-11 characteristic polynomial is exactly

\[
\boxed{C(t)=t^3-20t^2+56t-44.}
\tag{7}
\]

There is no need to guess a label such as “case \((\eta)\)”: the invariant identification is **level \(11\), tuple (5), recurrence (3)**.

## 2. Exact derivation of the quadratic spectral map

Set

\[
y=t-2.
\]

Then Cooper's equation \(C(t)=0\) becomes

\[
y^3-14y^2-12y-4=0.
\tag{8}
\]

Now put

\[
\mu=\frac{y^2}{4}=\frac{(t-2)^2}{4}.
\tag{9}
\]

Using \(y^2=4\mu\), equation (8) becomes

\[
(4\mu-12)y=56\mu+4,
\]

or

\[
y=\frac{14\mu+1}{\mu-3}.
\]

Squaring and using \(y^2=4\mu\) yields

\[
(14\mu+1)^2=4\mu(\mu-3)^2.
\]

After expansion this is

\[
4\mu^3-220\mu^2+8\mu-1=0.
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{C(t)=0\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
F\!\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{4}\right)=0.}
\tag{10}
\]

The stronger identity (1) follows by direct expansion:

\[
16F\!\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{4}\right)
=-C(t)C(4-t).
\]

A compact Sage certificate is:

```sage
Qt.<t> = PolynomialRing(QQ)
C = t^3 - 20*t^2 + 56*t - 44

def F(z):
    return 4*z^3 - 220*z^2 + 8*z - 1

assert 16*F((t - 2)^2/4) == -C*C(4 - t)
assert C.discriminant() == -2^4 * 11^3

Qm.<m> = PolynomialRing(QQ)
Fm = 4*m^3 - 220*m^2 + 8*m - 1
assert Fm.discriminant() == -2^4 * 11^3 * 43^2
assert Fm.discriminant() == C.discriminant() * 43^2
```

### Why the factor \(43\) appears

Let \(t_1,t_2,t_3\) be the roots of \(C\), and define

\[
\mu_i=\frac{(t_i-2)^2}{4}.
\]

Then

\[
\mu_i-\mu_j
=\frac{(t_i-t_j)(t_i+t_j-4)}{4}.
\]

Since \(t_1+t_2+t_3=20\), if \(k\) is the complementary index then

\[
t_i+t_j-4=16-t_k.
\]

Hence the extra discriminant factor introduced by the quadratic map is controlled by

\[
\prod_{k=1}^3(16-t_k)=C(16).
\]

But

\[
C(16)=16^3-20\cdot16^2+56\cdot16-44=-172=-4\cdot43.
\tag{11}
\]

Accounting for the denominators in (9) and for the leading coefficient \(4\) of \(F\) gives

\[
\boxed{\operatorname{disc}(F)=43^2\operatorname{disc}(C).}
\tag{12}
\]

Explicitly,

\[
\operatorname{disc}(C)=-2^4\,11^3,
\qquad
\operatorname{disc}(F)=-2^4\,11^3\,43^2.
\tag{13}
\]

Thus both cubics have the same quadratic resolvent field

\[
\boxed{\mathbf Q(\sqrt{-11}).}
\tag{14}
\]

This is the clean reason that \(43\) appears in the transformed problem: it is the resultant/branch-separation factor \(C(16)/(-4)\) of the map \(t\mapsto(t-2)^2/4\). The phrase “odd index of the quadratic transform” is not needed and is not, by itself, a standard invariant.

The coefficient

\[
946=2\cdot11\cdot43
\]

is therefore arithmetically suggestive: it combines the level \(11\) conductor with the transform factor \(43\). However, this factorization alone does not prove an equality of finite-\(n\) operators.

The endpoint quadratic

\[
R(n)=946n^2+6407n+10860
\]

has

\[
\operatorname{disc}(R)=6407^2-4\cdot946\cdot10860
=-44591=-17\cdot43\cdot61.
\tag{15}
\]

Its shifted occurrence \(R(n-1)\) is strong evidence of an adjoint or reciprocal normalization of the **challenge operator itself**. The factors \(17\) and \(61\), however, do not follow from Cooper's characteristic polynomial alone; assigning them a modular meaning requires the complete operator, not only its endpoints.

## 3. What has and has not been identified

The identity (1) proves an exact match at the Poincaré/spectral level. It is a necessary and highly nontrivial invariant, but it is not a complete Ore-operator identification.

To prove that Problem 2.7 is an actual pullback, Darboux transform, or algebraic correspondence of Cooper's recurrence, one needs one of the following finite-\(n\) certificates:

1. an Ore intertwiner
   
   \[
   L_{2.7}\,T=S\,L_{11},
   \]
   
   with explicit \(T,S\in\mathbf Q(n)\langle E\rangle\), possibly after an algebraic extension;
2. a generating-function identity
   
   \[
   G_{2.7}(x)=r(x)G_{11}(\phi(x))
   \]
   
   or a related integral/Hadamard transform;
3. a rational transfer-matrix conjugacy whose limiting matrix applies the polynomial map
   
   \[
   M\longmapsto\frac{(M-2I)^2}{4}.
   \]

No such certificate follows from the data currently supplied, and Cooper's paper does not state that his \(T_{11}\) sequence has Apéry limit \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\).

There is also a normalization discrepancy that must be settled before an exact comparison: the present question says all four challenge coefficients have degree \(13\), while an earlier working note in the challenge repository describes a primitive scalar operator with degree \(18\) coefficients. A common polynomial factor or a shifted/nonprimitive normalization can change displayed degrees without changing the solution space or the Poincaré polynomial. The exact primitive coefficients must be fixed before testing an intertwiner. The spectral identity (1) is unaffected by this issue.

So the strongest justified statement is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{Problem 2.7 is spectrally the quadratic image of Cooper level 11.}
}
\]

It is premature to replace “spectrally” by “is the same recurrence as.”

## 4. The solution space and the role of \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\)

### 4.1 An order-three recurrence has three independent solutions

A four-term scalar recurrence is order \(3\). Once a nonsingular starting index is fixed, an arbitrary initial triple determines a solution. Thus one needs three independent sequences for a fundamental basis.

This corrects the premise that there should be only two independent solutions \(u_n,v_n\).

### 4.2 Cooper's canonical integer solution

For Cooper's recurrence (3), the modular expansion singles out the canonical integer sequence \(T_{11}(n)\) in (4). Two additional independent solutions can be produced by choosing two other independent initial triples, for example

\[
(0,1,0),\qquad (0,0,1),
\]

at three consecutive regular indices. Cooper's paper does not give those companions as simple binomial sums, nor does it identify one of them with a \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\) connection coefficient.

The modular generating equation has the usual logarithmic companion solutions at the maximally unipotent point, but those are generating-function/Frobenius companions; they should not be confused with a proved Apéry numerator sequence for the challenge.

### 4.3 The two distinguished challenge solutions

Let \(q_n\) and \(p_n\) denote the denominator and numerator solutions specified by the challenge's two initial triples. They both satisfy the same order-three recurrence. Put

\[
L=\zeta(2)+\zeta(3).
\]

Then the period-normalized error solution is

\[
\boxed{e_n=p_n-Lq_n.}
\tag{16}
\]

It satisfies the recurrence and has initial triple

\[
(e_0,e_1,e_2)
=(p_0-Lq_0,\ p_1-Lq_1,\ p_2-Lq_2).
\]

The desired limit is equivalent to

\[
\frac{e_n}{q_n}\longrightarrow0.
\tag{17}
\]

This is the standard and precise way in which the period enters: the numerator and denominator can both have rational or integral initial data, while \(L\) is the connection coefficient that cancels their common dominant asymptotic mode.

If \(Y^{(1)},Y^{(2)},Y^{(3)}\) is a Poincaré basis associated with the three roots of \(F\), one expects decompositions of the form

\[
q_n=a_1Y_n^{(1)}+a_2Y_n^{(2)}+a_3Y_n^{(3)},
\]

\[
p_n=La_1Y_n^{(1)}+b_2Y_n^{(2)}+b_3Y_n^{(3)}.
\]

Then \(e_n\) contains only the two non-dominant modes. Because \(\operatorname{disc}(F)<0\), those two modes form a nonreal conjugate pair, which explains the expected oscillatory error.

The full initial triples \(p_0,p_1,p_2,q_0,q_1,q_2\) were not included in the question, so no unique numerical formulas for \(p_n,q_n\) can be reconstructed from the endpoint factors alone. In particular, one cannot identify \(q_n\) with a transformed \(T_{11}(n)\) without an explicit operator/intertwiner calculation.

## 5. The explicit double period for \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\)

The elementary mixed period is

\[
I=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{1-\frac12\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
\]

For \(0\le x,y<1\), expand

\[
\frac1{1-xy}=\sum_{m\ge0}(xy)^m.
\]

Termwise integration gives

\[
\int_0^1\!\int_0^1(xy)^m\,dx\,dy
=\frac1{(m+1)^2},
\]

and

\[
\begin{aligned}
-\frac12\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
(xy)^m\log(xy)\,dx\,dy
&=-\frac12\left(
-\frac1{(m+1)^2}\frac1{m+1}
-\frac1{m+1}\frac1{(m+1)^2}
\right)\\
&=\frac1{(m+1)^3}.
\end{aligned}
\]

Therefore

\[
I=\sum_{m\ge0}\frac1{(m+1)^2}
 +\sum_{m\ge0}\frac1{(m+1)^3}
=\zeta(2)+\zeta(3),
\]

which proves (2).

An equivalent Beukers--Hadjicostas form is

\[
\boxed{
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
=\frac32+
\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{1-x}{1-xy}
\left(1-\frac12\log(xy)\right)dx\,dy.
}
\tag{18}
\]

Indeed,

\[
\zeta(2)=1+\int_0^1\!\int_0^1\frac{1-x}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy
\]

and

\[
\zeta(3)=\frac12+rac12
\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{(1-x)(-\log(xy))}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
\]

### What is still needed for the recurrence

Equation (2) evaluates the target period, but it does not yet prove that the challenge recurrence converges to it. The missing certificate should have the form

\[
\boxed{
 p_n-Lq_n=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1 K_n(x,y)\,dx\,dy,
}
\tag{19}
\]

where \(K_n\) is explicit and creative telescoping proves

\[
A_nK_{n+1}-B_nK_n+C_{n-1}K_{n-1}-D_{n-2}K_{n-2}
=\Delta_x U_n+\Delta_y V_n.
\tag{20}
\]

Boundary integration would then yield the four-term recurrence, and a positivity or saddle-point estimate would prove (17).

Because \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\) mixes weights \(2\) and \(3\), a level-11 modular realization should be expected to be an **extension/Eichler period**, not merely the period of the holomorphic elliptic differential on \(X_0(11)\). This is another reason the characteristic-polynomial match alone cannot supply the connection coefficient.

## 6. Why there is no factorial/Pochhammer gauge from Problem 2.6

Problem 2.6 has the order-two recurrence

\[
2(n+3)^3(2n+5)(3n+5)u_n
=(n+2)^2(15n^3+85n^2+155n+93)u_{n-1}
-(n+1)^3(n+2)(3n+8)u_{n-2},
\tag{21}
\]

with Poincaré roots

\[
\boxed{1,\ \frac14.}
\tag{22}
\]

It also has a hypergeometric recessive solution satisfying

\[
\frac{v_{n+1}}{v_n}
=\frac{(n+3)^2}{2(n+4)(2n+7)}.
\tag{23}
\]

Suppose one applies a scalar hypergeometric gauge

\[
y_n=g_nu_n,
\qquad
\frac{g_{n+1}}{g_n}
=\kappa
\frac{\prod_i(n+a_i)}{\prod_j(n+b_j)}.
\tag{24}
\]

Such a gauge has two elementary invariants:

1. it preserves the order of the scalar recurrence;
2. at a fixed Newton slope it multiplies all Poincaré roots by the same asymptotic scalar (up to the reciprocal convention for \(u_n=y_n/g_n\)).

Consequently, (24) cannot turn an order-two system into the order-three Problem 2.7 system, and it cannot implement the nonlinear map

\[
t\longmapsto\frac{(t-2)^2}{4}.
\]

Two common attempts also fail:

### Summation lift

Multiplying an order-two operator by \(E-1\) produces an order-three operator with a rational linear Poincaré factor. But

\[
F(\mu)=4\mu^3-220\mu^2+8\mu-1
\]

has no rational root and is irreducible over \(\mathbf Q\). Thus Problem 2.7 is not a scalar summation lift of Problem 2.6.

### Symmetric square

The symmetric square of an order-two recurrence with roots \(1\) and \(1/4\) has roots proportional to

\[
1,\quad\frac14,\quad\frac1{16}.
\]

After any common scalar gauge, the resulting cubic still splits over \(\mathbf Q\). The challenge cubic \(F\) is irreducible and has one real root and a nonreal conjugate pair. Hence it is not a scalar-gauged symmetric square of Problem 2.6 either.

Therefore

\[
\boxed{
\text{there is no factorial/Pochhammer factor relating 2.6 to 2.7.}
}
\tag{25}
\]

The shifted endpoint pair \(R(n),R(n-1)\) may provide a useful adjoint gauge **inside Problem 2.7**, but it is not a gauge from Problem 2.6.

The two problems can still evaluate the same period. Distinct Picard--Fuchs, creative-telescoping, or conservative-matrix constructions often have the same connection constant without being gauge-equivalent.

## 7. The appropriate next exact computation

The spectral discovery sharply narrows the search. The following computation would decide whether the level-11 connection lifts from infinity to the complete recurrence.

1. Put both recurrences in primitive Ore form over \(\mathbf Q(n)\langle E\rangle\), resolving the degree-\(13\)/degree-\(18\) normalization issue.
2. Compute singularity orbits, local exponent data, exterior/symmetric powers, and \(p\)-curvatures of \(L_{2.7}\) and Cooper's \(L_{11}\).
3. Search for a rational or algebraic homomorphism
   
   \[
   \operatorname{Hom}(L_{11},L_{2.7})
   \]
   
   after allowing a degree-two pullback or a rank-three transfer-matrix polynomial transform.
4. If an intertwiner exists, apply it to the modular solution \(T_{11}\) and compare the resulting first three terms with the challenge denominator sequence.
5. Independently run creative telescoping on deformations of the mixed kernel in (18), with the goal of producing the exact endpoint factors \(R(n)\) and \(R(n-1)\).

At the constant-coefficient level, the target matrix relation is especially transparent. If \(M_{11,\infty}\) is a companion matrix with characteristic polynomial \(C\), then

\[
\frac{(M_{11,\infty}-2I)^2}{4}
\]

has characteristic polynomial \(F\). The open problem is to lift this limiting matrix polynomial to a rational finite-\(n\) relation.

## Final answers, item by item

### (1) Precise Cooper connection

The exact entry is Cooper's **level-11** sequence \(T_{11}\), parameter tuple

\[
(10,4,-56,-8,22),
\]

with recurrence (3) and characteristic polynomial \(C(t)\). Problem 2.7 does not have the same polynomial directly; rather,

\[
\mu=(t-2)^2/4
\]

and identity (1) give the exact quadratic spectral correspondence. The factor \(43\) is explained by \(C(16)=-4\cdot43\), and

\[
\operatorname{disc}(F)=43^2\operatorname{disc}(C).
\]

### (2) Independent solutions

The recurrence is order \(3\), so there are three independent solutions. Cooper's canonical integral solution is \(T_{11}(n)\). For the challenge, the relevant pair is \(q_n,p_n\), and the period solution is

\[
e_n=p_n-(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))q_n.
\]

No explicit transform from \(T_{11}\) to \(q_n\), and no Cooper companion with connection coefficient \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\), has been proved from the supplied data.

### (3) Double integral

The exact target period is

\[
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)=
\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{1-\frac12\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
\]

To prove the recurrence identity one still needs an \(n\)-dependent telescoping kernel as in (19)--(20).

### (4) Gauge from Problem 2.6

There is no scalar factorial/Pochhammer gauge. Order, irreducibility, and Poincaré-root invariants rule it out. A connection, if it exists beyond the shared period and the level-11 spectral map, must be matrix-valued or integral/algebraic rather than a multiplicative sequence gauge.
