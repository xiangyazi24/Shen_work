# Problem 2.5: how Catalan’s constant can arise from the Delannoy-square carrier

## Executive answer

There are two different constants and two different constructions in play.

The canonical second solution of the central-Delannoy recurrence gives

\[
\boxed{
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{E_n}{D_n}=\frac{\log 2}{2}.
}
\]

That statement is exact. It comes from the Legendre function of the second kind at \(x=3\). No natural renormalization of that same second solution changes the intrinsic connection constant to Catalan’s constant.

Catalan’s constant enters one level higher. If

\[
F(z):=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}D_n^2z^n,
\]

then Brafman’s product-generating-function identity gives

\[
\boxed{
F(z)=\frac{2}{\pi(1-z)}
K\!\left(\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z}\right),
}
\tag{1}
\]

where \(K(k)\) is the complete elliptic integral in the modulus convention. Put

\[
\rho:=17-12\sqrt2=(\sqrt2-1)^4.
\]

The change of variables

\[
k=\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z}
\]

maps \(0\le z\le\rho\) to \(0\le k\le1\). Since

\[
G=\frac12\int_0^1K(k)\,dk,
\]

one obtains the exact Delannoy-square period formula

\[
\boxed{
G=
\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\int_0^\rho
\frac{1+z}{\sqrt z\,(1-z)}
\left(\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}D_n^2z^n\right)dz.
}
\tag{2}
\]

Thus \(G\) is not the ratio of the two elementary Delannoy solutions. It is an **integrated elliptic period**—equivalently, a normal-function or summation-extension value—built from the holomorphic \(D_n^2\) solution.

This distinction resolves the puzzle:

- \(E_n/D_n\to\tfrac12\log2\) is the basic rank-two Legendre connection constant;
- \(G\) is the endpoint value of an additional integration of the elliptic period;
- the common \((-16)^n\) twist only rescales the independent variable and moves the singular point; it creates no new period;
- the Poincaré roots identify the elliptic/Delannoy carrier and the convergence rate, but not the limiting constant;
- the full finite-\(n\) CMF together with its two initial rows must select the integrated-period connection coefficient.

There is also a useful obstruction. If the gauged Problem 2.5 operator were genuinely rationally isomorphic to the **pure homogeneous** symmetric square of the Delannoy recurrence, then limits of ratios arising from rational initial data would lie in the connection field generated by \(\log2/2\) and \(\sqrt2\). A rational Ore intertwiner by itself therefore does not explain \(G\); one still needs an extension, an integral transform, or an explicit moment functional such as (2).

The most plausible exact proof architecture is therefore

\[
\boxed{
\text{Delannoy elliptic period}
\longrightarrow
\text{integrated/normal-function extension}
\longrightarrow
\text{CMF initial-condition connection coefficient }G.
}
\]

# 1. The canonical Delannoy second solution really gives \(\log 2/2\)

The central Delannoy numbers satisfy

\[
(n+1)D_{n+1}=3(2n+1)D_n-nD_{n-1},
\qquad D_0=1,\quad D_1=3,
\tag{3}
\]

and

\[
D_n=P_n(3),
\]

where \(P_n\) is the Legendre polynomial.

Let \(\mathsf Q_n(x)\) denote the Legendre function of the second kind. It obeys the same three-term recurrence and has

\[
\mathsf Q_0(x)=\frac12\log\frac{x+1}{x-1},
\qquad
\mathsf Q_1(x)=x\mathsf Q_0(x)-1.
\]

At \(x=3\), define

\[
q:=\mathsf Q_0(3)=\frac12\log2.
\tag{4}
\]

Now put

\[
\boxed{
E_n:=qD_n-\mathsf Q_n(3).
}
\tag{5}
\]

Then

\[
E_0=0,
\qquad
E_1=1,
\]

and \(E_n\in\mathbb Q\) for every \(n\). This is the standard rational second solution associated with the initial data \((0,1)\).

The discrete Wronskian is

\[
W_n:=D_nE_{n+1}-D_{n+1}E_n.
\]

Using (3),

\[
W_n=\frac{n}{n+1}W_{n-1},
\qquad W_0=1,
\]

so

\[
\boxed{W_n=\frac1{n+1}.}
\tag{6}
\]

Consequently

\[
\frac{E_{n+1}}{D_{n+1}}-
\frac{E_n}{D_n}
=
\frac1{(n+1)D_nD_{n+1}},
\]

and hence

\[
\boxed{
\frac{E_n}{D_n}
=
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}
\frac1{(k+1)D_kD_{k+1}}.
}
\tag{7}
\]

For \(x>1\), \(P_n(x)\) is dominant and \(\mathsf Q_n(x)\) is recessive. At \(x=3\),

\[
\frac{\mathsf Q_n(3)}{D_n}
=O\!\left((17-12\sqrt2)^n\right).
\]

Equation (5) therefore gives

\[
\boxed{
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{E_n}{D_n}
=q=\frac{\log2}{2}.
}
\tag{8}
\]

So the numerical value \(0.34657359\ldots\) is not merely close to \(\log2/2\); it is exactly \(\log2/2\).

# 2. Can a different normalization of \(E_n\) give \(G\)?

Formally, yes, but only tautologically.

Every second solution is of the form

\[
E_n^{(a,b)}=aE_n+bD_n.
\]

Its limiting ratio is

\[
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{E_n^{(a,b)}}{D_n}
=a\frac{\log2}{2}+b.
\tag{9}
\]

If one fixes the Wronskian normalization (6), then \(a=1\), while adding \(bD_n\) leaves the Wronskian unchanged. If one also imposes \(E_0=0\), then \(b=0\), and the solution is unique.

One could of course define

\[
E_n^{G}:=E_n+\left(G-\frac{\log2}{2}\right)D_n,
\]

so that \(E_n^G/D_n\to G\). But this merely inserts \(G\) into the initial condition. The sequence is no longer rational-valued, and it gives no proof or structural explanation.

More generally, among rational-valued second solutions, \(a,b\in\mathbb Q\), so the possible elementary connection constants are rational affine combinations of \(\log2/2\), not a canonically selected Catalan constant.

Therefore the answer to part (a) is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{There is no natural rational normalization of the Delannoy second solution whose ratio is }G.
}
\]

# 3. What the pure symmetric square can and cannot contain

The symmetric-square solution space is

\[
\operatorname{span}
\{D_n^2,\ D_nE_n,\ E_n^2\}.
\tag{10}
\]

Let

\[
U_n=A D_n^2+B D_nE_n+C E_n^2,
\]

with \(A,B,C\in\mathbb Q\). Since \(E_n/D_n\to q=\log2/2\),

\[
U_n
\sim
D_n^2\bigl(A+Bq+Cq^2\bigr).
\tag{11}
\]

Thus, for two generic rational solutions \(U_n,V_n\),

\[
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{U_n}{V_n}
=
\frac{A+Bq+Cq^2}{A'+B'q+C'q^2}.
\tag{12}
\]

The same conclusion survives a finite rational Ore transformation. To see this, write

\[
E_n=qD_n-\mathsf Q_n(3).
\]

A rational combination of finitely many shifts of \(D^2,DE,E^2\) becomes a combination of

\[
D_n^2,\qquad D_n\mathsf Q_n(3),\qquad \mathsf Q_n(3)^2
\]

whose coefficients belong to \(\mathbb Q(\sqrt2,q)\) at infinity. Since

\[
\frac{\mathsf Q_n(3)}{D_n}=O(\rho^n),
\qquad \rho=17-12\sqrt2,
\]

any finite nonzero projective limit produced by such a rational map belongs to

\[
\mathbb Q\!\left(\sqrt2,\frac{\log2}{2}\right).
\tag{13}
\]

This yields an important conditional diagnostic:

> If the gauged Problem 2.5 module is rationally isomorphic to the pure homogeneous Delannoy symmetric square, and the relevant initial data are rational, then the limiting ratio is controlled by the Delannoy connection constant \(\log2/2\), not by a new period.

It is not known how to prove that \(G\notin\mathbb Q(\sqrt2,\log2)\), so this is not a transcendence-theoretic contradiction. But it shows that a pure Ore equivalence would not by itself prove the challenge identity; it would merely replace the challenge by an unexplained identity between \(G\) and \(\log2\).

This is why the exact finite-\(n\) distinction between

- a semisimple symmetric square, and
- a nontrivial summation/normal-function extension

is load-bearing.

# 4. The exact elliptic generating function for \(D_n^2\)

Set

\[
F(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}D_n^2z^n.
\tag{14}
\]

A specialization of Brafman’s product generating function gives

\[
\boxed{
F(z)=\frac{2}{\pi(1-z)}
K\!\left(k(z)\right),
\qquad
k(z)=\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z}.
}
\tag{15}
\]

Here

\[
K(k)=\int_0^{\pi/2}
\frac{d\theta}{\sqrt{1-k^2\sin^2\theta}}
\tag{16}
\]

is the complete elliptic integral in the modulus convention.

The first terms check immediately:

\[
F(z)=1+9z+169z^2+3969z^3+\cdots,
\]

because

\[
D_0,D_1,D_2,D_3=1,3,13,63.
\]

The elliptic degeneration \(k=1\) occurs when

\[
(1-z)^2=32z,
\]

i.e.

\[
z^2-34z+1=0.
\]

The smaller root is

\[
\boxed{
\rho=17-12\sqrt2=(\sqrt2-1)^4,
}
\tag{17}
\]

and the larger root is \(1/\rho=17+12\sqrt2\).

This is precisely the quadratic factor appearing in the normalized Poincaré polynomial. Thus the silver-ratio spectrum has an exact analytic meaning:

\[
\boxed{
\rho\text{ is both the reciprocal dominant Sym}^2\text{ root and the point where the elliptic modulus reaches }1.
}
\]

This is the first genuine bridge from the Delannoy structure to Catalan’s constant.

# 5. A self-contained proof that \(G=\frac12\int_0^1K(k)\,dk\)

Using

\[
K(k)=\int_0^1
\frac{dt}{\sqrt{(1-t^2)(1-k^2t^2)}},
\]

Tonelli’s theorem gives

\[
\begin{aligned}
\int_0^1K(k)\,dk
&=
\int_0^1
\frac1{\sqrt{1-t^2}}
\left(\int_0^1\frac{dk}{\sqrt{1-k^2t^2}}\right)dt\\
&=
\int_0^1
\frac{\arcsin t}{t\sqrt{1-t^2}}\,dt.
\end{aligned}
\]

Put \(t=\sin\theta\). Then

\[
\int_0^1K(k)\,dk
=
\int_0^{\pi/2}\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\,d\theta.
\]

Integrating by parts with

\[
\int\csc\theta\,d\theta
=\log\tan\frac\theta2
\]

gives

\[
\int_0^{\pi/2}\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\,d\theta
=-2\int_0^{\pi/4}\log(\tan u)\,du.
\]

The classical Fourier-series evaluation is

\[
-\int_0^{\pi/4}\log(\tan u)\,du=G.
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{
G=\frac12\int_0^1K(k)\,dk.
}
\tag{18}
\]

No modular or conjectural input is needed for this identity.

# 6. The exact Delannoy-square integral for \(G\)

Differentiate

\[
k(z)=\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z}.
\]

One obtains

\[
\frac{dk}{dz}
=
\frac{2\sqrt2(1+z)}{\sqrt z\,(1-z)^2}.
\tag{19}
\]

From (15),

\[
K(k(z))=\frac\pi2(1-z)F(z).
\]

Substituting (19) into (18) gives

\[
\boxed{
G=
\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\int_0^\rho
\frac{1+z}{\sqrt z\,(1-z)}F(z)\,dz.
}
\tag{20}
\]

Equivalently,

\[
\boxed{
G=
\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\int_0^\rho
\frac{1+z}{\sqrt z\,(1-z)}
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}D_n^2z^n\,dz.
}
\tag{21}
\]

This answers part (b) in a concrete form: there is a direct classical integral identity connecting the square-generating function of the central Delannoy numbers to \(G\).

It also identifies the correct object. The ordinary generating function

\[
\sum D_nz^n=\frac1{\sqrt{1-6z+z^2}}
\]

is only algebraic. Its Hadamard square \(F(z)\) is elliptic, and integrating that elliptic period to its first degeneration produces Catalan’s constant.

# 7. An explicit coefficient identity involving \(D_n^2\)

Because all terms in (21) are nonnegative on \(0\le z\le\rho\), monotone convergence permits termwise integration. Define

\[
\mu_n:=
\int_0^\rho
z^{n-1/2}\frac{1+z}{1-z}\,dz.
\tag{22}
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{
G=\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}D_n^2\mu_n.
}
\tag{23}
\]

Let

\[
a:=\sqrt\rho=3-2\sqrt2=(\sqrt2-1)^2.
\]

With \(z=t^2\),

\[
\mu_n
=2\int_0^a t^{2n}\frac{1+t^2}{1-t^2}\,dt.
\tag{24}
\]

Since

\[
\frac{1+t^2}{1-t^2}
=-1+\frac2{1-t^2},
\]

one obtains

\[
\mu_n
=
-\frac{2a^{2n+1}}{2n+1}
+4\left(
\operatorname{artanh}a
-\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}
\frac{a^{2j+1}}{2j+1}
\right).
\]

Now

\[
\frac{1+a}{1-a}=\sqrt2,
\qquad
\operatorname{artanh}a=\frac14\log2,
\]

so

\[
\boxed{
\mu_n
=
\log2
-4\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}
\frac{a^{2j+1}}{2j+1}
-\frac{2a^{2n+1}}{2n+1}.
}
\tag{25}
\]

A manifestly positive tail form is

\[
\boxed{
\mu_n
=
\frac{2a^{2n+1}}{2n+1}
+4\sum_{j=n+1}^{\infty}
\frac{a^{2j+1}}{2j+1}.
}
\tag{26}
\]

Equations (23), (25), and (26) are an explicit exact series mechanism by which \(G\) is obtained from the Delannoy-square sequence.

The convergence is only polynomial after the dominant exponential factors cancel: \(D_n^2\) grows like \(ho^{-n}/n\), while \(\mu_n\) decays like \(ho^n/n\), so the product is of order \(n^{-2}\). An Apéry or CMF transformation can accelerate this boundary-period series without changing its value.

# 8. A Beukers-type algebraic double integral

Equation (18) already gives

\[
\boxed{
G=rac12
\int_0^1\int_0^1
\frac{dt\,dk}
{\sqrt{(1-t^2)(1-k^2t^2)}}.
}
\tag{27}
\]

This is a two-dimensional period with an algebraic square-root denominator.

To expose the Delannoy-square discriminant, substitute

\[
k=\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z}.
\]

Using (19) and

\[
1-k^2t^2
=
\frac{(1-z)^2-32zt^2}{(1-z)^2},
\]

one obtains

\[
\boxed{
G=
\sqrt2\int_0^\rho\int_0^1
\frac{1+z}
{\sqrt z\,(1-z)
\sqrt{(1-t^2)\bigl((1-z)^2-32zt^2\bigr)}}
\,dt\,dz.
}
\tag{28}
\]

This is the requested Beukers-type representation.

The relevant quadratic is not merely the ordinary Delannoy discriminant

\[
1-6z+z^2.
\]

For the square/Hadamard-square family, the elliptic discriminant is

\[
(1-z)^2-32z=z^2-34z+1,
\]

obtained from (28) at \(t=1\). Its roots are \(17\pm12\sqrt2\), exactly the symmetric-square Poincaré roots.

Thus the denominator geometry and the recurrence spectrum agree perfectly.

# 9. What the \((-16)^n\) twist does

At rank two, define

\[
\widetilde D_n=(4i)^nD_n,
\qquad
\widetilde E_n=(4i)^nE_n.
\]

Then

\[
\frac{\widetilde E_n}{\widetilde D_n}
=rac{E_n}{D_n},
\]

so the connection constant remains \(\log2/2\).

At symmetric-square level,

\[
\widetilde F(w)
:=\sum_{n\ge0}(-16)^nD_n^2w^n
=F(-16w).
\tag{29}
\]

Therefore the twist merely rescales the generating variable. The elliptic degeneration at \(z=\rho\) moves to

\[
w=-\frac\rho{16}.
\]

The twist explains the common factor \(-16\) in the Poincaré roots and the alternating orientation of the dominant modes. It does **not** introduce a new period.

Likewise, the projective convergence factor is unchanged:

\[
\frac{|-16|}{|-16(17+12\sqrt2)|}
=17-12\sqrt2=\rho.
\]

So the answer to part (c) is:

\[
\boxed{
(-16)^n\text{ relocates the singularity and fixes the Newton scale; it does not create }G.
}
\]

# 10. The normal-function / summation-extension mechanism

Define

\[
J(k):=\int_0^kK(s)\,ds.
\tag{30}
\]

Then

\[
J'(k)=K(k),
\qquad
J(1)=2G.
\tag{31}
\]

The complete elliptic integral satisfies

\[
k(1-k^2)K''+(1-3k^2)K'-kK=0.
\tag{32}
\]

Therefore \(J\) satisfies the third-order equation

\[
\boxed{
k(1-k^2)J'''+(1-3k^2)J''-kJ'=0.}
\tag{33}
\]

Equation (33) has a neutral constant solution because it contains only derivatives of \(J\). Conceptually it is an extension

\[
0\longrightarrow
\text{elliptic rank-two period system}
\longrightarrow
\text{rank-three integrated-period system}
\longrightarrow
\mathbf1
\longrightarrow0.
\tag{34}
\]

This is exactly the structure needed for a Catalan connection coefficient:

- the rank-two quotient contains the elliptic period \(K\);
- the extra direction records an indefinite integral of \(K\);
- the endpoint value of that integral is \(2G\).

The cubic asymptotic polynomial

\[
(t-1)(t^2-34t+1)
\]

is compatible both with a formal symmetric-square interpretation and with a rank-two elliptic block plus a neutral extension. Poincaré data alone cannot distinguish these two possibilities.

For the challenge, the second interpretation is the one that naturally explains \(G\).

This also clarifies why simply finding an Ore intertwiner to the homogeneous symmetric-square recurrence is not the end of the proof. One must identify the extension class or period functional selected by the CMF.

# 11. How the initial matrix can select Catalan’s constant

Let \(p_n\) and \(q_n\) be the numerator and denominator sequences selected by the two rows of the initial CMF matrix. For a Poincaré-regular order-three system, write their asymptotic decompositions as

\[
p_n=C_pY_n^{(+)}+\text{smaller modes},
\]

\[
q_n=C_qY_n^{(+)}+\text{smaller modes},
\]

where \(Y^{(+)}\) is the dominant mode. Then

\[
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{p_n}{q_n}
=\frac{C_p}{C_q}.
\tag{35}
\]

The Poincaré roots determine the growth of \(Y^{(+)}\) and the rate of convergence. They do not determine \(C_p/C_q\). The coefficients \(C_p,C_q\) are global connection coefficients determined by

1. all finite-\(n\) coefficients of the matrix or scalar recurrence;
2. the precise initial rows;
3. the choice of asymptotic sector and normalization.

Rational recurrences with rational initial conditions routinely have transcendental connection coefficients; Apéry’s \(\zeta(3)\) recurrence is the standard example.

Thus part (e) has a qualified yes:

\[
\boxed{
G\text{ can enter through the initial rows as the selected connection coefficient, but only because the full finite-}n\text{ system encodes the appropriate period functional.}
}
\]

The initial rows do not contain \(G\) explicitly. They select the unique linear combination for which

\[
p_n-Gq_n
\]

lies entirely in the recessive sector. The proof obligation is to identify that recessive linear form with an integral whose value is known to be \(G\).

# 12. The exact certificate still needed for Problem 2.5

Equation (20) suggests a concrete target functional

\[
\Lambda[f]
:=
\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\int_0^\rho
\frac{1+z}{\sqrt z(1-z)}f(z)\,dz,
\tag{36}
\]

for which

\[
\Lambda[F]=G.
\]

A self-contained CMF proof would be completed by producing one of the following equivalent certificates.

## 12.1 Moment/Hermite–Padé certificate

Construct explicit rational or polynomial functions \(R_n(z)\) such that

\[
\boxed{
p_n-Gq_n=\Lambda[R_n],}
\tag{37}
\]

and prove

\[
\Lambda[R_n]\to0
\]

with the observed rate \(ho^n\).

The recurrence identities should then follow from creative telescoping or integration by parts.

## 12.2 Block-extension certificate

After the correct common factorial/twist gauge, find a rational matrix \(U(n)\) putting the CMF into block-triangular form

\[
U(n)^{-1}M(n)U(n+1)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
A_2(n)&v(n)\\
0&1
\end{pmatrix},
\tag{38}
\]

where \(A_2(n)\) is the elliptic/Delannoy block and \(v(n)\) is the extension cocycle. Then compute the accumulated cocycle and identify it with (36).

## 12.3 Generating-function certificate

Convert the gauged scalar recurrence to a differential operator. Prove that its rank-two quotient is the pullback of the \(K\)-equation under

\[
m=\frac{32z}{(1-z)^2},
\]

and that the remaining solution is the primitive \(J\) from (30), with the CMF initial data selecting \(J(1)/2=G\).

Any of these would explain both the silver-ratio spectrum and the Catalan limit.

By contrast, the following facts are insufficient on their own:

- equality of Poincaré roots;
- the determinant factorization;
- a common \((-16)^n\) twist;
- a rational intertwiner to the homogeneous symmetric square;
- numerical convergence to 51 digits.

# 13. Direct answers to the five questions

## (a) A different normalization of \(E_n\)?

The canonical rational solution with \(E_0=0,E_1=1\) satisfies

\[
E_n/D_n\to\log2/2.
\]

Adding \(cD_n\) changes the limit to \(\log2/2+c\), but obtaining \(G\) this way simply inserts \(c=G-\log2/2\) by hand. It is not a structural derivation.

## (b) A classical identity involving \(\sum D_n^2z^n\)?

Yes. Equations (15) and (20) give

\[
\sum D_n^2z^n
=\frac{2}{\pi(1-z)}K\!\left(\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z}\right)
\]

and

\[
G=\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\int_0^\rho
\frac{1+z}{\sqrt z(1-z)}
\sum D_n^2z^n\,dz.
\]

This is the cleanest exact bridge.

The mixed series \(\sum D_nE_nz^n\) and \(\sum E_n^2z^n\) are logarithmic companion solutions. Their natural connection constant is \(\log2/2\); the new constant \(G\) appears after integrating the elliptic period in the modulus.

## (c) Does the \((-16)^n\) twist introduce \(G\)?

No. It sends \(F(z)\) to \(F(-16z)\) and moves the singularity from \(z=\rho\) to \(z=-\rho/16\). Ratios such as \(E_n/D_n\) are unchanged. The twist fixes scale and alternation, not the period value.

## (d) A Beukers-type integral?

Yes. Equation (28) is an explicit algebraic double integral tied to the Delannoy-square discriminant:

\[
G=
\sqrt2\int_0^\rho\int_0^1
\frac{1+z}
{\sqrt z(1-z)
\sqrt{(1-t^2)((1-z)^2-32zt^2)}}
\,dt\,dz.
\]

The quadratic \(z^2-34z+1\) is the correct square-family discriminant.

## (e) Can \(G\) enter through the initial conditions?

Yes, as a global connection coefficient selected by the initial rows—but only in conjunction with the full finite-\(n\) recurrence. The spectrum determines the rate, while the initial rows select the period/normal-function value. The missing exact step is to identify those rows with the functional (36), or with an equivalent block-extension cocycle.

---

# Final conclusion

The equality

\[
\frac{E_n}{D_n}\longrightarrow\frac{\log2}{2}
\]

is not in conflict with the Catalan limit. It is simply the wrong period level.

The Delannoy rank-two recurrence has a logarithmic connection constant \(\log2/2\). Its Hadamard/symmetric square has the elliptic generating function (15). Integrating that elliptic period from the ordinary point \(k=0\) to the degeneration \(k=1\) gives

\[
\boxed{G=\frac12\int_0^1K(k)\,dk.}
\]

The silver-ratio number

\[
\rho=17-12\sqrt2
\]

is exactly the \(z\)-coordinate of that degeneration under the Brafman pullback. The common \(-16\) twist moves this point but introduces no new constant.

Therefore the structurally correct explanation is

\[
\boxed{
G\text{ is an integrated elliptic-period connection coefficient selected by the CMF initial data, not the basic Delannoy second-solution ratio.}
}
\]

A rigorous completion of Problem 2.5 should now seek an explicit moment, block-extension, or generating-function certificate connecting the printed CMF to equation (20).
