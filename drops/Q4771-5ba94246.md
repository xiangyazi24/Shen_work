# Problem 3.2: coefficient zeros of Apéry numbers and the exact missing sieve theorem

## Executive conclusion

Let

\[
 b_0=1,\qquad b_1=5,
\]

and

\[
 (n+1)^3b_{n+1}=P(n)b_n-n^3b_{n-1},
 \qquad
 P(n)=(2n+1)(17n^2+17n+5).
 \tag{1}
\]

For a prime \(p\), put

\[
 Z_p:=\{0\le r<p:b_r\equiv0\pmod p\},
 \qquad \nu_p:=|Z_p|,
\]

and define the large-prime contribution

\[
 \mathcal Z(n):=
 \sum_{\substack{n^{2/3}<p\le n\\ n\bmod p\in Z_p}}
 \log p.
 \tag{2}
\]

The proposed Dwork--Katz/Sato--Tate argument does **not** prove
\(\mathcal Z(n)=o(n)\) as stated. The decisive correction is:

> \(b_r\equiv0\pmod p\) is a **coefficient-vanishing condition** in the
> truncated Apéry period. It is not the condition that the Hasse--Witt
> matrix, or the Hasse polynomial evaluated at a geometric parameter, is
> zero.

The geometric object controlled by Dwork theory is

\[
 H_p(t):=\sum_{r=0}^{p-1}b_rt^r\in\mathbf F_p[t].
 \tag{3}
\]

A Hasse/nonordinary point is detected by an equation of the form
\(H_p(t_0)=0\). By contrast, the condition in (2) asks whether one
**coefficient** of \(H_p\) vanishes. In fact, for interior indices it is
more naturally a vanishing **multiplicative Fourier coefficient** of the
function \(H_p(t)\) on \(\mathbf F_p^\times\).

What can be proved unconditionally is:

1. the exact reflection law
   \[
   \boxed{b_{p-1-r}\equiv b_r\pmod p};
   \tag{4}
   \]
2. two consecutive entries cannot both vanish modulo \(p\);
3. consequently
   \[
   \boxed{\nu_p\le\frac{p-1}{2}}.
   \tag{5}
   \]

This is far from the conjectural random-value estimate \(\nu_p=O(1)\).
Moreover, even \(\nu_p=O(1)\) would imply (2) immediately only **on
average over \(n\)**, not pointwise for every \(n\). The pointwise result
requires a new horizontal estimate correlating the varying prime \(p\)
with the distinguished residue \(n\bmod p\).

A precise sufficient statement is the following. Suppose that for some
\(\delta,\eta>0\),

\[
 \nu_p\ll p^{1-\delta}
 \tag{6}
\]

and, uniformly in \(n\) and in dyadic ranges
\(n^{2/3}\le P\le n\),

\[
 \sum_{P<p\le2P}\log p
 \left(
  \mathbf 1_{\,n\bmod p\in Z_p}-\frac{\nu_p}{p}
 \right)
 \ll P^{1-\eta}.
 \tag{7}
\]

Then

\[
 \boxed{\mathcal Z(n)\ll n^{1-\min(\delta,\eta)}=o(n).}
 \tag{8}
\]

Thus the correct missing input is not ordinary Chebotarev or ordinary
Sato--Tate. It is a **horizontal prime-aspect large-sieve theorem for the
Mellin coefficients of the Apéry Hasse polynomial**. No such theorem is
currently supplied by the standard Dwork--Katz framework.

# 1. The geometric object and the coefficient object are different

The Apéry generating function

\[
 f(t)=\sum_{n\ge0}b_nt^n
\]

satisfies the third-order Picard--Fuchs equation

\[
 \left[
 \theta^3-t(34\theta^3+51\theta^2+27\theta+5)
 +t^2(\theta+1)^3
 \right]f=0,
 \qquad \theta=t\frac d{dt}.
 \tag{9}
\]

This is the Picard--Fuchs equation of the Beukers--Peters family of K3
surfaces. It is an order-three K3 variation and is the symmetric square
of an order-two differential equation. Therefore it is reasonable to
speak of a rank-two square root, but the geometric variation attached to
(9) is not simply a single two-dimensional representation whose
Frobenius trace equals \(b_r\).

The Apéry numbers satisfy the \(p\)-Lucas congruence

\[
 b_{mp+r}\equiv b_mb_r\pmod p,
 \qquad 0\le r<p.
 \tag{10}
\]

Consequently, modulo \(p\),

\[
 f(t)\equiv H_p(t)f(t)^p,
 \qquad
 H_p(t)=\sum_{r=0}^{p-1}b_rt^r.
 \tag{11}
\]

This truncation is the object that enters the unit-root/Hasse theory.
Recent work of Caruso, Fürnsinn, Vargas-Montoya and Zudilin proves the
remarkable factorization

\[
 H_p(t)=B_p(t)^2
 \quad\text{if }p\equiv1,5,7,11\pmod{24},
 \tag{12}
\]

and

\[
 H_p(t)=(t^2-34t+1)B_p(t)^2
 \quad\text{if }p\equiv13,17,19,23\pmod{24}.
 \tag{13}
\]

These formulas concern the polynomial as a function of the geometric
parameter \(t\). They do not count its zero **coefficients**.

There is a simple exact Fourier reformulation of the coefficient
condition. First,

\[
 b_{p-1}\equiv1\pmod p.
 \tag{14}
\]

Indeed, in

\[
 b_{p-1}=\sum_{k=0}^{p-1}
 \binom{p-1}{k}^2\binom{p-1+k}{k}^2,
\]

the \(k=0\) term is \(1\), while every term with \(1\le k<p\)
contains a factor \(p^2\).

Set

\[
 \widetilde H_p(t):=H_p(t)-t^{p-1}
 =\sum_{j=0}^{p-2}b_jt^j.
\]

For \(0\le r\le p-2\), multiplicative-character orthogonality gives

\[
 \begin{aligned}
 \sum_{t\in\mathbf F_p^\times}
 \widetilde H_p(t)t^{-r}
 &=\sum_{j=0}^{p-2}b_j
   \sum_{t\in\mathbf F_p^\times}t^{j-r}\\
 &=(p-1)b_r=-b_r.
 \end{aligned}
\]

Hence

\[
 \boxed{
 b_r\equiv0\pmod p
 \iff
 \sum_{t\in\mathbf F_p^\times}
 \widetilde H_p(t)t^{-r}=0
 }
 \qquad(0\le r\le p-2).
 \tag{15}
\]

So the hard set \(Z_p\) is the zero set of the multiplicative Fourier
transform of a Hasse-type polynomial. It is not the supersingular locus
\(\{t:H_p(t)=0\}\).

This distinction invalidates the proposed direct application of
Sato--Tate to \(Z_p\).

# 2. Exact finite-field structure of the coefficient zeros

## 2.1 Reflection symmetry

The polynomial \(P(n)\) in (1) satisfies

\[
 P(-n-1)=-P(n).
 \tag{16}
\]

We already know \(b_{p-1}\equiv1\). Substituting \(n=p-1\) into
(1) gives

\[
 0=P(-1)b_{p-1}-(-1)^3b_{p-2}
 =-5+b_{p-2},
\]

so

\[
 b_{p-2}\equiv5\pmod p.
 \tag{17}
\]

Define

\[
 c_r:=b_{p-1-r}.
\]

Using (16), the recurrence at the reflected index shows that

\[
 (r+1)^3c_{r+1}=P(r)c_r-r^3c_{r-1}\pmod p.
 \tag{18}
\]

Thus \((c_r)\) satisfies the same recurrence as \((b_r)\), and by
(14), (17),

\[
 (c_0,c_1)=(1,5)=(b_0,b_1).
\]

Uniqueness of the forward recurrence for \(0\le r\le p-2\) proves

\[
 \boxed{b_{p-1-r}\equiv b_r\pmod p.}
 \tag{19}
\]

In particular, noncentral zeros occur in reflected pairs.

## 2.2 Zeros are isolated

Suppose that

\[
 b_r\equiv b_{r+1}\equiv0\pmod p
\]

for some \(1\le r\le p-2\). Equation (1) at \(r\) gives

\[
 r^3b_{r-1}\equiv0\pmod p,
\]

hence \(b_{r-1}\equiv0\). Repeating backwards would give
\(b_0\equiv0\), contradicting \(b_0=1\). Therefore no two consecutive
entries vanish.

Since the first and last entries are both \(1\), the zero set is an
independent set in a path with \(p\) vertices and nonzero endpoints.
Consequently

\[
 \boxed{\nu_p\le\frac{p-1}{2}.}
 \tag{20}
\]

This elementary bound is sharp at \(p=5\):

\[
 (b_0,b_1,b_2,b_3,b_4)\equiv(1,0,3,0,1)\pmod5.
\]

There is a little more local rigidity. If
\(b_r=b_{r+2}=0\), then \(b_{r+1}\ne0\), and the recurrence at
\(r+1\) forces

\[
 P(r+1)\equiv0\pmod p.
 \tag{21}
\]

Thus there are at most three pairs of zeros at distance two. More
generally, fixed zero gaps are controlled by explicit transfer
polynomials, but their degrees grow with the gap and this does not give
a uniform \(O(1)\) bound for \(\nu_p\).

The unconditional estimate (20) corresponds only to density at most
\(1/2\), not density \(O(1/p)\).

# 3. Why ordinary Sato--Tate and Chebotarev do not answer the question

There are three separate mismatches.

## 3.1 Geometric parameter versus coefficient index

Vertical Sato--Tate for a family studies Frobenius traces as a geometric
parameter \(t\in\mathbf F_p\) varies. Dwork's Hasse polynomial likewise
studies the values \(H_p(t)\). The variable \(r\) in \(b_r\) is a
coefficient index. Formula (15) shows that passing from \(t\) to \(r\)
is a global Mellin transform, not evaluation at a point.

Therefore a theorem about

\[
 \#\{t\in\mathbf F_p:H_p(t)=0\}
\]

is not a theorem about

\[
 \#\{r\in[0,p):b_r=0\}.
\]

## 3.2 Archimedean trace distribution versus divisibility by the
characteristic prime

Sato--Tate controls normalized real or complex Frobenius traces. The
condition \(b_r\equiv0\pmod p\) is a \(p\)-adic congruence in which the
modulus is the same prime as the residue characteristic. A continuous
Sato--Tate law does not by itself estimate exact divisibility by \(p\).

Similarly, standard Chebotarev fixes an \(\ell\)-adic or finite Galois
representation and varies Frobenius primes away from \(\ell\). Here both
the finite field and the modulus vary with \(p\), while the index
\(r=n\bmod p\) also varies. This is a horizontal crystalline problem,
not a standard fixed-representation Chebotarev problem.

## 3.3 Vertical density does not imply a pointwise moving-residue bound

Even suppose one could prove the very strong estimate

\[
 \nu_p=O(1),
 \tag{22}
\]

which is the proposed density \(O(1/p)\). For an integer chosen uniformly
from a long interval, the probability of landing in \(Z_p\) is indeed
\(\nu_p/p=O(1/p)\). This gives a strong **average-over-\(n\)** result.
It does not by itself control one specified residue \(n\bmod p\) for
every prime \(p\).

Partial summation can be used only after one has a prime-aspect estimate
for the actual selected primes, or an averaging theorem in \(n\). The
cardinality \(|Z_p|\) alone contains no information about the correlation
of \(Z_p\) with the moving residues \(n\bmod p\).

# 4. What a vertical zero-density theorem would prove

The next proposition makes the distinction quantitative.

## Proposition: an almost-all-\(n\) theorem

Assume that for some fixed \(0<\delta\le1\),

\[
 \nu_p\ll p^{1-\delta}.
 \tag{23}
\]

For \(N<m\le2N\), define

\[
 \mathcal Z_N(m)=
 \sum_{\substack{N^{2/3}<p\le2N\\m\bmod p\in Z_p}}
 \log p.
\]

Then, for \(0<\delta<1\),

\[
 \boxed{
 \sum_{N<m\le2N}\mathcal Z_N(m)\ll N^{2-\delta}.
 }
 \tag{24}
\]

For \(\delta=1\),

\[
 \boxed{
 \sum_{N<m\le2N}\mathcal Z_N(m)\ll N\log N.
 }
 \tag{25}
\]

### Proof

For each \(p\), every residue class in \(Z_p\) occurs at most
\(N/p+1\) times in \((N,2N]\). Hence

\[
 \begin{aligned}
 \sum_{N<m\le2N}\mathcal Z_N(m)
 &\le
 \sum_{N^{2/3}<p\le2N}
 \log p\,\nu_p\left(\frac Np+1\right)\\
 &\ll
 N\sum_{p\le2N}p^{-\delta}\log p
 +\sum_{p\le2N}p^{1-\delta}\log p.
 \end{aligned}
\]

Standard Chebyshev estimates for prime sums give (24) and (25). ∎

For example, a square-root vertical bound

\[
 \nu_p\ll p^{1/2}
 \tag{26}
\]

would imply average size \(O(N^{1/2})\) for \(\mathcal Z_N(m)\), and all
but \(O(N^{3/4})\) integers \(m\in(N,2N]\) would satisfy

\[
 \mathcal Z(m)\ll N^{3/4}=o(N).
\]

If the conjectural bound \(\nu_p=O(1)\) held, the average would be
\(O(\log N)\), and almost every \(m\) would satisfy a polylogarithmic
bound.

This is valuable, but it is not the pointwise estimate required by the
challenge.

# 5. The exact pointwise theorem that would finish estimate (11)

The required new input can be stated cleanly.

## Horizontal Frobenius-sieve hypothesis

Assume there exist \(\delta,\eta>0\) such that

\[
 \nu_p\ll p^{1-\delta}
 \tag{27}
\]

and, uniformly for every integer \(n\) and every dyadic
\(n^{2/3}\le P\le n\),

\[
 \boxed{
 \sum_{P<p\le2P}\log p
 \left(
  \mathbf 1_{\,n\bmod p\in Z_p}-\frac{\nu_p}{p}
 \right)
 \ll P^{1-\eta}.
 }
 \tag{28}
\]

Then

\[
 \sum_{\substack{P<p\le2P\\n\bmod p\in Z_p}}\log p
 \ll P^{1-\delta}+P^{1-\eta}.
 \tag{29}
\]

Summing over dyadic \(P\) gives

\[
 \boxed{
 \mathcal Z(n)\ll
 n^{1-\min(\delta,\eta)}=o(n).
 }
 \tag{30}
\]

This proves estimate (11).

A slightly weaker but still sufficient formulation is: for some
\(\eta>0\), uniformly in \(n,P\),

\[
 \#\{P<p\le2P:n\bmod p\in Z_p\}
 \ll\frac{P^{1-\eta}}{\log P}.
 \tag{31}
\]

The point is that (28) or (31) is a genuinely horizontal theorem over
varying prime fields. It is not a formal consequence of geometric
monodromy at one fixed prime.

# 6. What Dwork--Katz could realistically contribute

Dwork--Katz theory remains relevant, but in two stages rather than one.

## Stage A: construct the Mellin-side sheaf

Formula (15) suggests constructing a finite-field hypergeometric or
Mellin-transform sheaf whose trace in the character indexed by \(r\)
is related to \(b_r\). The factorization (12)--(13) and the identity
between the Apéry period and a square of a Franel period are strong
starting points.

One would need a construction uniform in \(p\), with controlled
conductor, and an exact comparison with the integer residue \(b_r\).
That comparison is not contained in the ordinary Hasse-polynomial
factorization.

## Stage B: vertical exponential sums

If one could prove, uniformly for \(u\ne0\),

\[
 \left|
 \sum_{r\in\mathbf F_p}
 e_p(ub_r)
 \right|\ll p^{1/2},
 \tag{32}
\]

then additive-character orthogonality would give

\[
 \nu_p
 =\frac1p\sum_{u\in\mathbf F_p}
   \sum_{r\in\mathbf F_p}e_p(ub_r)
 =1+O(p^{1/2}).
 \tag{33}
\]

Thus bounded-conductor Deligne estimates naturally lead first to
\(\nu_p=O(p^{1/2})\), not automatically to \(O(1)\).

This would already prove an almost-all-\(n\) form of (11), by Section 4.

## Stage C: horizontal prime-aspect cancellation

To prove the challenge for every \(n\), one still needs cancellation in
an expression schematically like

\[
 \sum_{P<p\le2P}\frac{\log p}{p}
 \sum_{u\in\mathbf F_p}
 e_p\!\left(u\,b_{n\bmod p}\right),
 \tag{34}
\]

uniformly in \(n\). The finite field, the additive character and the
index all change with \(p\). Standard Deligne bounds are vertical
bounds at a fixed \(p\); they do not sum (34) over primes.

This is the genuinely new analytic-number-theoretic step.

# 7. Relation with the Lucas reduction from Q4765

The ordinary Lucas congruence gives, for

\[
 n=qp+r,\qquad0\le r<p,
\]

\[
 b_n\equiv b_qb_r\pmod p.
 \tag{35}
\]

For \(p>n^{2/3}\), one has \(q<n^{1/3}\). Outside the already isolated
exceptional primes dividing one of the small numbers
\(b_0,\ldots,b_{\lfloor n^{1/3}\rfloor}\), the condition

\[
 b_r\equiv0\pmod p
\]

is equivalent to

\[
 p\mid b_n.
\]

Thus estimate (11) is also a statement about the logarithmic weight of
prime divisors \(p\le n\) of the **single exponentially large integer**
\(b_n\), restricted to \(p>n^{2/3}\). This is a horizontal prime-divisor
problem. Generic monodromy of the differential equation does not by
itself control the small-prime radical of one coefficient \(b_n\).

This reformulation explains why the remaining estimate is genuinely
difficult despite the very strong local supercongruences.

# 8. Final answer

The estimate

\[
 \sum_{\substack{n^{2/3}<p\le n\\b_{n\bmod p}\equiv0\pmod p}}
 \log p=o(n)
\]

is **not proved by the currently available Dwork--Katz, Sato--Tate or
Chebotarev results** in the form proposed in the question.

The specific claimed bridge

\[
 b_r\equiv0\pmod p
 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
 \text{Hasse--Witt matrix is zero}
\]

is incorrect. The correct statements are:

- \(H_p(t)=\sum_{r<p}b_rt^r\) is the Hasse/unit-root truncation;
- \(H_p(t_0)=0\) is a geometric nonordinary condition;
- \(b_r=0\) is a zero multiplicative Fourier coefficient of
  \(H_p(t)-t^{p-1}\).

The strongest elementary uniform information presently obtained here is

\[
 b_{p-1-r}=b_r,
 \qquad
 \text{no adjacent zeros},
 \qquad
 \nu_p\le(p-1)/2.
\]

A vertical estimate such as \(\nu_p\ll p^{1/2}\) would yield estimate
(11) for almost all \(n\), but not pointwise. To finish Problem 3.2 for
every \(n\), one needs either:

1. the horizontal large-sieve estimate (28), or an equivalent
   prime-aspect trace-function theorem; or
2. a deterministic divisibility/resultant identity forcing every
   selected prime to divide an auxiliary integer of logarithmic size
   \(o(n)\).

That horizontal theorem is the exact remaining research target.
