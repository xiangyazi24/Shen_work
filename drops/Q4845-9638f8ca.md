# Problem 2.5: the exact Padé/Nikishin theorem that can prove convergence

## Executive verdict

There **is** a theorem that can replace a full matrix-valued Almkvist–Zeilberger decay proof, but only after an exact approximation-theoretic identification has been supplied.

The cleanest theorem is the classical **Markov theorem for Padé approximants of a Stieltjes function**. Catalan's constant is the value at \(z=1\) of the Stieltjes function

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal C(z)
:=\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+zt^2}\,dt
=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-z)^m}{(2m+1)^2},
\qquad \mathcal C(1)=G.
}
\tag{1}
\]

Equivalently, with \(s=t^2\),

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal C(z)=\int_0^1\frac{d\mu(s)}{1+zs},
\qquad
d\mu(s)=\frac{-\log s}{4\sqrt s}\,ds.
}
\tag{2}
\]

The moments are rational:

\[
\boxed{
\int_0^1s^m\,d\mu(s)=\frac1{(2m+1)^2}.
}
\tag{3}
\]

If the CMF ratios are proved to be diagonal or fixed-near-diagonal Padé approximants to \(\mathcal C\), evaluated at \(z=1\), then Markov's theorem gives convergence immediately. Moreover, Stahl–Totik regularity gives the exact geometric rate

\[
\boxed{
17-12\sqrt2=(3+2\sqrt2)^{-2}=0.0294372515\ldots.
}
\tag{4}
\]

This is exactly the observed CMF rate.

However, the following implication is false:

\[
\text{matching Poincaré roots and three common column limits}
\Longrightarrow
\text{Padé or Hermite–Padé approximants}.
\]

The needed finite certificate is an **orthogonality/Padé identification lemma**. It can be substantially smaller than the full matrix AZ certificate, but it cannot be omitted.

There is also a genuine \(3\times3\) Hermite–Padé route. The natural two-function system is not the pair of constants \((1,G)\), but

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
f_0(z)&=\int_0^1\frac{dt}{1+zt^2}
      =\frac{\arctan\sqrt z}{\sqrt z},\\[1mm]
f_1(z)&=\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+zt^2}\,dt
      =\mathcal C(z).
\end{aligned}}
\tag{5}
\]

A step-line type-II Hermite–Padé system for \((f_0,f_1)\) has a four-term scalar recurrence and therefore a \(3\times3\) transfer matrix. This is structurally much closer to the challenge CMF. If the printed matrix is identified with that step-line transfer matrix, then the Markov/Stieltjes convergence theorem for Nikishin systems is the appropriate citation.

The short answer is therefore:

> **Yes, a standard theorem proves convergence once the CMF is identified as a Padé or Nikishin/Hermite–Padé table. No standard theorem proves that identification from the recurrence spectrum alone.**

# 1. The scalar Stieltjes formulation of Catalan's constant

Define

\[
\mathcal C(z)=\sum_{m\ge0}\frac{(-z)^m}{(2m+1)^2}.
\]

For \(|z|<1\), expand the denominator in (1):

\[
\begin{aligned}
\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+zt^2}\,dt
&=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}(-z)^m
  \int_0^1t^{2m}(-\log t)\,dt\\
&=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-z)^m}{(2m+1)^2}.
\end{aligned}
\]

At \(z=1\), monotone or dominated convergence gives

\[
\mathcal C(1)=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^m}{(2m+1)^2}=G.
\]

Let

\[
\widehat\mu(w)=\int_0^1\frac{d\mu(s)}{w-s},
\qquad w\in\mathbb C\setminus[0,1].
\tag{6}
\]

Since \(w=-1/z\),

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal C(z)=-\frac1z\widehat\mu(-1/z).
}
\tag{7}
\]

In particular,

\[
\boxed{G=-\widehat\mu(-1).}
\tag{8}
\]

This places the evaluation point strictly outside the support \([0,1]\). That is the setting in which the classical Markov theorem is strongest.

# 2. The precise Markov–Padé theorem

Let \(\mu\) be a finite positive measure with infinitely many support points in a compact real interval \([a,b]\), and let

\[
\widehat\mu(w)=\int_a^b\frac{d\mu(s)}{w-s}.
\]

Let \(Q_n\) be the monic degree-\(n\) orthogonal polynomial for \(\mu\):

\[
\int_a^b s^jQ_n(s)\,d\mu(s)=0,
\qquad 0\le j<n.
\tag{9}
\]

Define the associated polynomial

\[
P_{n-1}(w)
=\int_a^b\frac{Q_n(w)-Q_n(s)}{w-s}\,d\mu(s).
\tag{10}
\]

Then \(P_{n-1}/Q_n\) is the \([n-1/n]\) Padé approximant to \(\widehat\mu\) at infinity. Markov's theorem states that

\[
\boxed{
\frac{P_{n-1}(w)}{Q_n(w)}
\longrightarrow\widehat\mu(w)
}
\tag{11}
\]

locally uniformly on \(\mathbb C\setminus[a,b]\).

The decisive identity is the exact error formula

\[
\boxed{
\widehat\mu(w)-\frac{P_{n-1}(w)}{Q_n(w)}
=
\frac1{Q_n(w)^2}
\int_a^b\frac{Q_n(s)^2}{w-s}\,d\mu(s).
}
\tag{12}
\]

For the Catalan measure (2), put

\[
R_n:=-\frac{P_{n-1}(-1)}{Q_n(-1)}.
\tag{13}
\]

Using (8) and (12),

\[
\boxed{
G-R_n
=
\frac1{Q_n(-1)^2}
\int_0^1\frac{Q_n(s)^2}{1+s}\,d\mu(s)>0.
}
\tag{14}
\]

Thus convergence follows from a positive error integral. This is already a complete Padé-type proof once the CMF ratios have been identified with the \(R_n\).

## References for the theorem

The theorem is classical. Suitable references are:

- E. M. Nikishin and V. N. Sorokin, *Rational Approximations and Orthogonality*, Translations of Mathematical Monographs 92, AMS, 1991.
- G. A. Baker Jr. and P. Graves-Morris, *Padé Approximants*, second edition, Cambridge University Press; see the chapter on Stieltjes series and Markov functions.

# 3. Why the exact rate is \(17-12\sqrt2\)

The density

\[
\frac{-\log s}{4\sqrt s}
\]

is positive almost everywhere on \((0,1)\). Hence the measure is regular in the Stahl–Totik sense; this also follows from the classical Erdős–Turán criterion for interval measures with positive almost-everywhere density.

Let

\[
\Phi(w)=2w-1+2\sqrt{w(w-1)},
\tag{15}
\]

where the square root is chosen so that

\[
\sqrt{w(w-1)}\sim w
\qquad(w\to\infty).
\]

Then \(\Phi\) maps \(\overline{\mathbb C}\setminus[0,1]\) conformally onto the exterior of the unit disk. For a regular measure, the Padé error satisfies

\[
\boxed{
\lim_{n\to\infty}
\left|
\widehat\mu(w)-\frac{P_{n-1}(w)}{Q_n(w)}
\right|^{1/n}
=|\Phi(w)|^{-2}.
}
\tag{16}
\]

At \(w=-1\), the specified branch gives

\[
\Phi(-1)=-3-2\sqrt2,
\]

so

\[
|\Phi(-1)|=3+2\sqrt2.
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{
\lim_{n\to\infty}|G-R_n|^{1/n}
=(3+2\sqrt2)^{-2}
=17-12\sqrt2.
}
\tag{17}
\]

This explains the silver-ratio rate without appealing to the singularity of \(K\) at \(k=1\). The closest singularity of the Stieltjes function \(\mathcal C(z)\) is the cut \((-\infty,-1]\); the evaluation point is \(z=1\). Potential theory for that interval gives precisely (17).

A standard reference for regular orthogonal-polynomial root asymptotics is:

- H. Stahl and V. Totik, *General Orthogonal Polynomials*, Cambridge University Press, 1992.

# 4. The exact finite certificate needed from the CMF

Suppose the challenge produces rational numbers

\[
r_{N,j}=\frac{P_{N,j}}{Q_{N,j}},
\qquad j=1,2,3.
\]

The following would be sufficient.

## Certificate P: scalar Padé identification

For each column \(j\), exhibit an integer shift \(e_j\), a nonzero rational scale \(\kappa_{N,j}\), and orthogonal polynomials \(Q_{N+e_j}^{\rm OP}\) for the measure (2) such that

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
Q_{N,j}
&=\kappa_{N,j}Q_{N+e_j}^{\rm OP}(-1),\\
P_{N,j}
&=-\kappa_{N,j}P_{N+e_j-1}^{\rm assoc}(-1).
\end{aligned}}
\tag{18}
\]

Then every column ratio is an adjacent Padé approximant and (14)–(17) prove the desired limit and rate.

If

\[
Q_n^{\rm OP}(s)=\sum_{\ell=0}^nq_{n,\ell}s^\ell,
\]

orthogonality is the completely rational system

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{\ell=0}^n
\frac{q_{n,\ell}}{[2(k+\ell)+1]^2}=0,
\qquad 0\le k<n.
}
\tag{19}
\]

No elliptic functions occur in this verification. The numerator is then determined by (10), and is rational because the quotient

\[
\frac{Q_n(w)-Q_n(s)}{w-s}
\]

is polynomial in \(s,w\) and all moments are rational.

## What is not enough

The following do not imply (18):

- the common Poincaré roots;
- the determinant factorization of \(M(n)\);
- agreement of a few hundred digits;
- the fact that all three columns converge to one number;
- the Brafman identity alone.

The initial matrix and the full finite-\(n\) coefficients must enforce the moment conditions (19), or an equivalent recurrence-plus-initial-values statement.

## A practical screen

Compute the first several Padé approximants to the rational moment sequence

\[
m_k=\frac1{(2k+1)^2}
\]

and compare them exactly with the three CMF columns after allowing:

- a fixed index shift;
- a common rational scale;
- reversal or association of numerator/denominator polynomials;
- one or two Christoffel, Geronimus, or Radau modifications.

A persistent exact match would identify the correct orthogonality statement. A failure at low order rules out the simplest scalar Padé interpretation.

# 5. Why a \(3\times3\) Hermite–Padé structure is plausible

The challenge's order-three scalar recurrence is more naturally associated with two weights than with one ordinary orthogonality measure.

Define

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
d\mu_0(s)&=\frac{ds}{2\sqrt s},\\
d\mu_1(s)&=\frac{-\log s}{4\sqrt s}\,ds.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{20}
\]

Their Markov functions in the \(z\)-coordinate are exactly

\[
\begin{aligned}
f_0(z)&=\int_0^1\frac{d\mu_0(s)}{1+zs}
      =\frac{\arctan\sqrt z}{\sqrt z},\\
f_1(z)&=\int_0^1\frac{d\mu_1(s)}{1+zs}
      =\mathcal C(z).
\end{aligned}
\tag{21}
\]

Their moments are

\[
\boxed{
\int_0^1s^k\,d\mu_0(s)=\frac1{2k+1},
\qquad
\int_0^1s^k\,d\mu_1(s)=\frac1{(2k+1)^2}.
}
\tag{22}
\]

For a multi-index \((n_0,n_1)\), a type-II multiple orthogonal polynomial \(Q_{n_0,n_1}\) satisfies

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\int_0^1s^kQ_{n_0,n_1}(s)\,d\mu_0(s)&=0,
&&0\le k<n_0,\\
\int_0^1s^kQ_{n_0,n_1}(s)\,d\mu_1(s)&=0,
&&0\le k<n_1.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{23}
\]

On the step line

\[
(0,0),(1,0),(1,1),(2,1),(2,2),\ldots,
\]

multiple orthogonal polynomials for two measures satisfy a four-term recurrence. Its state transfer is \(3\times3\). This is exactly the dimension seen in Problem 2.5.

The two-weight moments in (22) are rational, so this interpretation is compatible with a rational CMF.

## 5.1 Relation to a Nikishin system

The logarithmic weight has an explicit Cauchy-transform representation. For \(0<s<1\), define

\[
\widehat\sigma(s)
:=\int_0^{\infty}
\frac{dx}{(s+x)(1+x)^2}.
\tag{24}
\]

A partial-fraction integration gives

\[
\boxed{
-\log s=(1-s)+(1-s)^2\widehat\sigma(s).
}
\tag{25}
\]

The measure \(dx/(1+x)^2\), transferred to the interval \((-\infty,0]\), is finite and positive. Therefore the pair in (20) is obtained by a triangular polynomial transformation of a Nikishin pair whose consecutive supports are \([0,1]\) and \((-\infty,0]\). The intervals touch only at \(0\), and neither measure has an atom there.

This is precisely the setting addressed by the theory of Nikishin systems with touching or unbounded supports, after the polynomial transformation and the corresponding shifts of multi-indices are tracked.

## 5.2 The theorem that can be cited

A standard diagonal/balanced-index version is:

> Let \((s_0,\ldots,s_m)=\mathcal N(\sigma_0,\ldots,\sigma_m)\) be a Nikishin system. Along balanced multi-indices tending to infinity, the type-II simultaneous Hermite–Padé approximants \(P_{\mathbf n,j}/Q_{\mathbf n}\) converge locally uniformly to the Cauchy transforms \(\widehat s_j\) outside the convex hull of the first support. The common denominators are normal, all their zeros lie in the first support, and associated simultaneous quadrature rules converge.

The principal references are:

- U. Fidalgo Prieto and G. López Lagomasino, *Nikishin systems are perfect*, arXiv:1001.0554; especially Corollaries 1.1 and 1.2.
- U. Fidalgo Prieto and G. López Lagomasino, *Nikishin systems are perfect. Case of unbounded and touching supports*, arXiv:1009.1922.
- E. M. Nikishin and V. N. Sorokin, *Rational Approximations and Orthogonality*, AMS, 1991.

For rational or polynomial perturbations of Nikishin systems, see also:

- U. Fidalgo Prieto, G. López Lagomasino, and S. Medina Peralta, *Hermite–Padé approximation for certain systems of meromorphic functions*, arXiv:1310.7010.
- L. G. González Ricardo, G. López Lagomasino, and S. Medina Peralta, works on multi-level Hermite–Padé convergence and logarithmic asymptotics, arXiv:2001.08276, arXiv:2002.06194, and arXiv:2106.11370.

## 5.3 What must still be shown

To invoke this theorem for the challenge one must prove that, after the known Pochhammer normalization and a finite basis change, the CMF is the step-line transfer of the polynomials in (23), or of a precisely stated Christoffel/Geronimus modification of them.

The exact finite conditions are

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\sum_{\ell}q_\ell\frac1{2(k+\ell)+1}&=0,\\
\sum_{\ell}q_\ell\frac1{[2(k+\ell)+1]^2}&=0,
\end{aligned}}
\tag{26}
\]

for the appropriate ranges of \(k\), together with the second-kind numerator formulas and the transformed initial rows.

This is likely the smallest rigorous substitute for the matrix AZ certificate.

# 6. What the Brafman identity proves—and what it does not

Let

\[
a=3-2\sqrt2,
\qquad b=3+2\sqrt2.
\]

The Legendre integral representation gives

\[
D_n
=\frac1\pi\int_a^b
\frac{x^n\,dx}{\sqrt{(b-x)(x-a)}}.
\tag{27}
\]

Let \(
u\) be the push-forward of the product of this arcsine measure with itself under \((x,y)\mapsto xy\). Then

\[
\operatorname{supp}\nu=[a^2,b^2]
=[\rho,\rho^{-1}],
\qquad
\rho=17-12\sqrt2,
\tag{28}
\]

and

\[
\boxed{
D_n^2=\int s^n\,d\nu(s).
}
\tag{29}
\]

Consequently

\[
\boxed{
F(z)=\sum_{n\ge0}D_n^2z^n
=\int_{\rho}^{\rho^{-1}}
\frac{d\nu(s)}{1-zs}.
}
\tag{30}
\]

So the Brafman function itself is a Markov function. Classical Padé approximants to \(F\) converge away from its cut.

However, the Catalan functional is

\[
G=\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\int_0^\rho
\frac{1+z}{\sqrt z(1-z)}F(z)\,dz.
\tag{31}
\]

The integration interval reaches the branch endpoint \(z=\rho\). Local uniform Padé convergence on compact subsets outside the cut does **not**, by itself, justify passing to this endpoint integral, nor does it identify the challenge CMF with those Padé approximants.

This is why the direct Stieltjes function \(\mathcal C(z)\) is the cleaner target: \(G=\mathcal C(1)\) is an exterior evaluation, and the Markov error formula applies immediately.

## 6.1 A Nikishin interpretation of the Brafman functional

For completeness, interchange the integrations in (31):

\[
G=\int_{\rho}^{\rho^{-1}}\Psi(s)\,d\nu(s),
\tag{32}
\]

where

\[
\Psi(s)=\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\int_0^\rho
\frac{1+z}{\sqrt z(1-z)(1-zs)}\,dz.
\tag{33}
\]

Set \(B=\rho^{-1}\), \(x=1/z\), and

\[
d\sigma(x)=\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\frac{dx}{\sqrt x(x-1)},
\qquad x\in[B,\infty).
\tag{34}
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{
\Psi(s)
=\sigma([B,\infty))
+(s+1)\int_B^\infty\frac{d\sigma(x)}{x-s}.
}
\tag{35}
\]

Thus the pair \((\nu,\Psi\nu)\) is a polynomial perturbation of a Nikishin pair with supports \([\rho,B]\) and \([B,\infty)\), touching at one endpoint. This explains why Nikishin theory is relevant to the elliptic formulation.

It still does not identify the printed CMF. Moreover, moments of \(\Psi\nu\) are not the simplest rational input data, whereas the moments in (22) are explicitly rational. Therefore the system (20)–(23) is the better computational target.

# 7. Why Stahl's theorem is not enough

Stahl's theorem proves convergence in capacity of diagonal Padé approximants to broad classes of multivalued analytic functions, in the maximal single-valued domain determined by a minimal-capacity cut.

It does not prove that an arbitrary \(3\times3\) P-recursive matrix product is a Padé table. It also does not automatically give:

- an exact value at a prescribed point;
- simultaneous convergence of all three CMF columns;
- a positive error integral;
- the normalization selected by the initial \(2\times3\) matrix;
- a Hermite–Padé interpretation.

For this problem, positivity of the measure makes Markov/Nikishin theory both stronger and simpler than a general Stahl argument.

# 8. Recommended proof architecture

The most economical rigorous route is now:

## Route A: scalar Padé

1. Define the Stieltjes function \(\mathcal C(z)\) by (1)–(3).
2. Compute its diagonal and neighboring Padé tables from the moments \(1/(2m+1)^2\).
3. Match the three CMF columns exactly to fixed neighboring table entries.
4. Apply (14) for convergence and (17) for the rate.

This route requires only one orthogonality family. Its weakness is that a generic ordinary orthogonal polynomial for the logarithmic weight need not have a low-degree rational recurrence in \(n\), so the exact CMF match may fail.

## Route B: two-function Hermite–Padé

1. Use the rational moment pair in (22).
2. Construct the step-line type-II multiple orthogonal polynomials satisfying (23).
3. Compute their four-term recurrence or nearest-neighbor transfer matrix.
4. Compare that \(3\times3\) transfer with the Pochhammer-normalized challenge matrix.
5. Match the initial \(2\times3\) rows to the relevant second-kind functions.
6. Cite the Nikishin Markov/Stieltjes theorem for convergence at \(w=-1\).

This route naturally explains the matrix size and is the more plausible interpretation after the homogeneous Sym\(^2\)(Delannoy) coboundary failed.

## What the citation buys

Once Steps 3–5 of Route B are established, the cited theorem supplies:

- normality and uniqueness of the approximants;
- location of denominator zeros on the real support;
- absence of spurious poles at the evaluation point;
- locally uniform convergence of the simultaneous approximants;
- simultaneous quadrature convergence;
- potential-theoretic asymptotics after the multi-index path is specified.

It removes the need for a separate AZ decay estimate.

## What the citation does not buy

It does not supply the finite identity

\[
M_{25}^{\rm norm}(n)
=U(n)^{-1}M_{\rm HP}(n)U(n+1)
\]

or an equivalent moment-orthogonality certificate. That remains the problem-specific algebraic step.

# Final answer

The theorem you can cite is the Markov/Stieltjes convergence theorem for Padé approximants—or, in the genuinely \(3\times3\) formulation, its Nikishin-system extension to type-II Hermite–Padé approximants.

The most concrete scalar target is

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal C(z)
=\sum_{m\ge0}\frac{(-z)^m}{(2m+1)^2}
=\int_0^1\frac{-\log s}{4\sqrt s(1+zs)}\,ds,
\qquad \mathcal C(1)=G.
}
\]

For its diagonal Padé approximants \(R_n\), Markov's theorem gives

\[
\boxed{
G-R_n
=\frac1{Q_n(-1)^2}
\int_0^1\frac{Q_n(s)^2}{1+s}
\frac{-\log s}{4\sqrt s}\,ds>0,
}
\]

and Stahl–Totik regularity gives

\[
\boxed{
\lim_{n\to\infty}|G-R_n|^{1/n}
=17-12\sqrt2.
}
\]

For the matrix-size explanation, use the two rational-moment weights

\[
\boxed{
\frac{ds}{2\sqrt s},
\qquad
\frac{-\log s}{4\sqrt s}\,ds,
}
\]

whose step-line type-II Hermite–Padé polynomials have a four-term recurrence and a \(3\times3\) transfer matrix.

Therefore the smallest honest missing lemma is no longer a multivariate AZ certificate. It is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{the normalized Problem 2.5 CMF is the step-line transfer matrix, or a finite rational gauge thereof, for the moment system (22).}
}
\]

If that lemma is proved, the convergence theorem is already in the literature and can be cited. Without it, no general Padé, Stahl, Totik, Aptekarev, or Nikishin theorem applies merely from the observed spectrum and numerical limit.
