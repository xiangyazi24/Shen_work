# Problem 2.5: the epsilon-regularized matrix AZ identity, boundary conditions, and the extension class

## Executive verdict

The matrix Almkvist–Zeilberger strategy can in principle identify the Catalan extension class, but the data currently available do **not** yet close the proof.

There are four precise conclusions.

1. **`d = 2` is not yet a proved minimum.** In the fixed normalization, denominator support, geometric monomial support, and symmetry sector of Q4838, it is the first serious search degree. The exact minimum is the first `d` for which the reconstructed rational certificate survives exact verification and all boundary tests. To prove that the minimum is two one must certify zero nullity for `d=0,1` and exhibit a valid certificate for `d=2`.

2. **An AZ certificate at `epsilon = 0` does not automatically survive the regularization.** If
   
   $$
   h_{n,\varepsilon}(u,v,t)
   =\frac{u^n v^n t^\varepsilon}
   {Q(u,v)^{n+1}(1+t^2)},
   \qquad Q=1-u^2v^2,
   $$
   
   then differentiating the twisted divergence introduces the explicit forcing term
   
   $$
   \boxed{(1+t^2)C^{(0)}.}
   $$
   
   One must solve the AZ identity to first order in `epsilon`, or else keep this term as a nonzero off-diagonal extension cocycle. Equation (22) of Q4838 is the formula that exposes the obstruction; by itself it is not a proof that the regularized identity holds.

3. **For closed `u`- and `v`-cycles, there are no endpoint conditions at saddle points.** The `u,v` boundary terms vanish because they are integrals of total derivatives over closed cycles, provided the certificate fluxes are single-valued and have no poles on the cycles. Critical points determine asymptotics, not Stokes boundaries. If open arcs are used instead, the exact conditions are weighted flux conditions printed in Section 6 below.

4. **The kernel written in Q4838 is not, by itself, the Delannoy saddle carrier.** Its exponential phase is
   
   $$
   \Psi(u,v)=\frac{uv}{1-u^2v^2},
   $$
   
   whose stationary locus is the non-isolated curve `u v = ± i`; it does not have the four isolated Delannoy saddles. The four saddles belong to
   
   $$
   \Phi_D(u,v)=
   \bigl(3+\sqrt2(u+u^{-1})\bigr)
   \bigl(3+\sqrt2(v+v^{-1})\bigr).
   $$
   
   Therefore the proposed boundary test at four “critical points” mixes two different carriers. Before a CRT search can close the proof, the kernel must either be replaced by the genuine Delannoy carrier or an explicit birational/contour equivalence must be proved.

Conditional on correcting that carrier issue, finding both the order-zero and order-one-in-`epsilon` certificates, checking the relative boundary conditions, and matching the initial matrix would give a complete recurrence-and-connection proof. The modular linear algebra alone proves only the algebraic divergence identity; it does not prove the analytic boundary or connection statements.

# 1. Exact twisted-divergence algebra

Put

$$
s=u^2v^2,
\qquad
Q=1-s,
$$

and define

$$
\boxed{
 h_{n,\varepsilon}(u,v,t)
 =\frac{u^n v^n t^\varepsilon}
 {Q^{n+1}(1+t^2)}.}
\tag{1.1}
$$

Its shift quotient is

$$
\boxed{
\frac{h_{n+1,\varepsilon}}{h_{n,\varepsilon}}
=\frac{uv}{Q}.}
\tag{1.2}
$$

The logarithmic derivatives are

$$
\boxed{
\ell_u
=\partial_u\log h_{n,\varepsilon}
=\frac{n+(n+2)s}{uQ},}
\tag{1.3}
$$

$$
\boxed{
\ell_v
=\frac{n+(n+2)s}{vQ},}
\tag{1.4}
$$

and

$$
\boxed{
\ell_t^{(\varepsilon)}
=\frac{\varepsilon}{t}-\frac{2t}{1+t^2}
=\frac{\varepsilon+(\varepsilon-2)t^2}
{t(1+t^2)}.}
\tag{1.5}
$$

Write

$$
\mathcal D_u=\partial_u+\ell_u,
\qquad
\mathcal D_v=\partial_v+\ell_v,
\qquad
\mathcal D_t^{(\varepsilon)}
=\partial_t+\ell_t^{(\varepsilon)}.
$$

The matrix identity sought in Q4838 is

$$
\boxed{
\frac{uv}{Q}\,\mathcal P_{n+1}(\varepsilon)
-\mathcal P_n(\varepsilon)M_{\rm norm}(n)
=
\mathcal D_uX_u(\varepsilon)
+\mathcal D_vX_v(\varepsilon)
+\mathcal D_t^{(\varepsilon)}X_t(\varepsilon).}
\tag{1.6}
$$

Use the denominator-clearing forms

$$
\boxed{
X_u=uQ A,
\qquad
X_v=vQ B,
\qquad
X_t=t(1+t^2)C.}
\tag{1.7}
$$

A direct calculation gives

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal D_u(uQA)
=uQ\,\partial_uA+igl(n+1+(n-1)s\bigr)A,}
\tag{1.8}
$$

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal D_v(vQB)
=vQ\,\partial_vB+igl(n+1+(n-1)s\bigr)B,}
\tag{1.9}
$$

and

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal D_t^{(\varepsilon)}
\bigl(t(1+t^2)C\bigr)
=(1+t^2)
\bigl(t\partial_tC+(1+\varepsilon)C\bigr).}
\tag{1.10}
$$

Equation (1.10) is the formula referred to as equation (22) in Q4838.

Multiplication of (1.6) by `h_{n,epsilon}` converts the twisted divergences into ordinary total derivatives:

$$
h_{n,\varepsilon}\mathcal D_uX_u
=\partial_u(h_{n,\varepsilon}X_u),
$$

and similarly for `v,t`. This is the exact algebraic basis of the proposed integration argument.

# 2. What is the minimum degree in `n`?

## 2.1 The exact answer is not yet known

The integer called `d` is not invariant under rational gauge changes. It depends on all of the following fixed choices:

- the Pochhammer normalization of the CMF;
- the balancing shear;
- the common denominator in `n` allowed for `A,B,C`;
- the monomial support in `u,v,t`;
- the `u <-> v` symmetry sector;
- whether the full `3 × 3` matrix or only the two challenge rows are solved;
- whether only `epsilon=0` or the first epsilon jet is included.

For a fixed ansatz, define

$$
\boxed{
 d_{\min}
 =\min\{d:\text{an exact rational certificate of degree }d
 \text{ exists and passes the boundary tests}\}.}
\tag{2.1}
$$

Q4838 establishes only that `d=2` is the first serious bound after normalization and balancing. It does not establish that the systems for `d=0` and `d=1` have zero kernel, and it does not exhibit a reconstructed `d=2` certificate. Thus the statement

$$
d_{\min}=2
$$

is presently a computational hypothesis, not a theorem.

## 2.2 Why degree two is the correct first target

In the balanced basis:

- `M_norm(n)` is bounded at infinity;
- the logarithmic derivatives (1.3)–(1.4) are affine in `n`;
- differentiating a geometric monomial does not raise its `n`-degree;
- the nontrivial formal shear has width three between the outer and neutral modes.

Consequently a certificate coefficient polynomial of degree `d` produces bulk equations of degree at most `d+1` before the fixed denominators are cleared. A constant ansatz is highly restrictive; degree one can implement only the most elementary common growth correction. Degree two is the first bound at which a nontrivial cancellation pattern among the three modes is realistically possible.

This is a heuristic degree estimate. The formal-index triple

$$
(0,-3,0)
$$

versus the homogeneous Delannoy-square triple

$$
(-1,-1,-1)
$$

forces a relative three-power shear, but it does **not** translate canonically into a numerator degree. Denominators and leading-symbol cancellations can move degree between numerator and denominator.

## 2.3 How to certify that the minimum is two

The required finite computation is:

1. Build the exact differentiated polynomial identity before specializing `u,v,t`.
2. Fix the denominator support from Q4838.
3. Form the modular linear systems for `d=0,1,2`.
4. For `d=0` and `d=1`, produce a full-rank maximal minor modulo at least one good prime. A nonzero modular minor proves the corresponding rational matrix has full column rank.
5. For `d=2`, reconstruct a nonzero kernel over `Q`, clear denominators, and verify (1.6) symbolically.
6. Verify the contour and endpoint conditions from Sections 5–6.

Only then is `d_min=2` proved.

With the total-geometric-degree-two, `u <-> v`-symmetric support counted in Q4838, the base system has

$$
153(d+1)
$$

scalar unknowns. Thus `d=2` means 459 unknowns. Solving simultaneously through first order in `epsilon` doubles the count to 918. If only the two rows selected by the challenge initial matrix are needed, the corresponding counts are 306 and 612.

The recommended jet search is

```text
base jet:       d0 = 0,1,2,3,4
first eps jet:  d1 = d0, then d0+1 if necessary
```

The pair `(d0,d1)=(2,2)` is the first serious extension search; `(2,3)` is the next one.

# 3. The epsilon derivative and the extension cocycle

Expand every unknown through first order:

$$
\mathcal P_n(\varepsilon)
=\mathcal P_n^{(0)}
+\varepsilon\mathcal P_n^{(1)}+O(\varepsilon^2),
$$

and similarly

$$
A=A^{(0)}+\varepsilon A^{(1)}+\cdots,
\quad
B=B^{(0)}+\varepsilon B^{(1)}+\cdots,
\quad
C=C^{(0)}+\varepsilon C^{(1)}+\cdots.
$$

Let

$$
\mathscr L_n(P)
:=\frac{uv}{Q}P_{n+1}-P_nM_{\rm norm}(n).
$$

The coefficient of `epsilon^0` in (1.6) is

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathscr L_n(\mathcal P^{(0)})={}&
\mathcal D_u(uQA^{(0)})
+\mathcal D_v(vQB^{(0)})\\
&+\mathcal D_t^{(0)}
\bigl(t(1+t^2)C^{(0)}\bigr).
\end{aligned}}
\tag{3.1}
$$

Since

$$
\left.
\frac{\partial\ell_t^{(\varepsilon)}}{\partial\varepsilon}
\right|_{\varepsilon=0}
=\frac1t,
$$

the coefficient of `epsilon^1` is

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathscr L_n(\mathcal P^{(1)})={}&
\mathcal D_u(uQA^{(1)})
+\mathcal D_v(vQB^{(1)})\\
&+\mathcal D_t^{(0)}
\bigl(t(1+t^2)C^{(1)}\bigr)
+\frac1tX_t^{(0)}.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{3.2}
$$

Because

$$
X_t^{(0)}=t(1+t^2)C^{(0)},
$$

the new forcing term is

$$
\boxed{
\frac1tX_t^{(0)}=(1+t^2)C^{(0)}.}
\tag{3.3}
$$

In the cleared polynomial form, (3.2) becomes

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathscr L_n(\mathcal P^{(1)})={}&
\mathcal D_u(uQA^{(1)})
+\mathcal D_v(vQB^{(1)})\\
&+(1+t^2)
\bigl(t\partial_tC^{(1)}+C^{(1)}+C^{(0)}\bigr).
\end{aligned}}
\tag{3.4}
$$

This is the decisive extension equation.

### Consequence

A certificate found only at `epsilon=0` survives unchanged for nonzero `epsilon` only if

$$
C^{(0)}=0
$$

or if the forcing term (3.3) is canceled by first-order corrections. Therefore equation (1.10) does **not** automatically imply survival of the AZ identity. It tells us exactly what additional linear system must be solved.

## 3.1 Split versus non-split behavior

The class of

$$
(1+t^2)C^{(0)}
$$

modulo the image of the three twisted-divergence operators is the candidate extension class.

- If it can be absorbed by `A^(1),B^(1),C^(1)` with `P^(1)=0` and zero boundary, the epsilon derivative stays in the same homogeneous period module. The extension splits at this level.
- If it cannot be absorbed but is represented by a nonzero `P^(1)` or a controlled `t=1` boundary state, the value and derivative form a block-triangular enlarged system. This is the desired nontrivial extension mechanism.

Thus a **zero** first-jet obstruction would not by itself identify Catalan's constant; the nonzero relative cohomology class is the object that can carry the connection constant.

# 4. Integration of the AZ identity

Define the matrix period

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal I_n(\varepsilon)
=\int_{\Gamma_u}\int_{\Gamma_v}\int_0^1
h_{n,\varepsilon}(u,v,t)
\mathcal P_n(u,v,t;\varepsilon)
\,dt\,dv\,du.}
\tag{4.1}
$$

Multiplying (1.6) by `h_{n,epsilon}` and integrating gives

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal I_{n+1}(\varepsilon)
-\mathcal I_n(\varepsilon)M_{\rm norm}(n)
=\mathcal B_{u,n}(\varepsilon)
+\mathcal B_{v,n}(\varepsilon)
+\mathcal B_{t,n}(\varepsilon).}
\tag{4.2}
$$

The fluxes are explicit. From (1.7),

$$
\boxed{
h_{n,\varepsilon}X_u
=\frac{u^{n+1}v^nt^\varepsilon}
{Q^n(1+t^2)}A,}
\tag{4.3}
$$

$$
\boxed{
h_{n,\varepsilon}X_v
=\frac{u^nv^{n+1}t^\varepsilon}
{Q^n(1+t^2)}B,}
\tag{4.4}
$$

and

$$
\boxed{
h_{n,\varepsilon}X_t
=\frac{u^nv^n}{Q^{n+1}}
 t^{\varepsilon+1}C.}
\tag{4.5}
$$

If `Gamma_u,Gamma_v` are closed cycles, the first two fluxes vanish under the analytic conditions of Section 5. For the interval in `t`,

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal B_{t,n}(\varepsilon)
=\int_{\Gamma_u}\int_{\Gamma_v}
\frac{u^nv^n}{Q^{n+1}}
\left[t^{\varepsilon+1}
C_n(u,v,t;\varepsilon)\right]_{t=0}^{t=1}
\,dv\,du.}
\tag{4.6}
$$

If `C` is bounded near zero and `Re epsilon > -1`, the `t=0` term vanishes. The upper boundary is

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal B_{t,n}(\varepsilon)
=\int_{\Gamma_u}\int_{\Gamma_v}
\frac{u^nv^n}{Q^{n+1}}
C_n(u,v,1;\varepsilon)
\,dv\,du.}
\tag{4.7}
$$

There are two valid closures.

### Homogeneous closure

Require

$$
\boxed{C_n(u,v,1;\varepsilon)=0}
\tag{4.8}
$$

for all `n` and through the required epsilon order. An even-in-`t` implementation can impose the stronger factor

$$
C_n=(1-t^2)\widetilde C_n.
$$

Then `I_n(epsilon)` satisfies the normalized CMF shift for every epsilon, and its derivative satisfies the same homogeneous shift.

### Relative or extension closure

Allow a nonzero boundary but prove that

$$
\mathcal B_{t,n}(\varepsilon)
$$

belongs to a finite-dimensional explicitly known boundary module. Augment `I_n` by that boundary state. The resulting recurrence is block triangular. This is the natural way for a non-split extension class to appear.

An arbitrary nonzero `C(u,v,1)` is not sufficient. Its moments must satisfy an independently verified recurrence and must couple to the neutral direction with the correct initial data.

# 5. Differentiation under the integral and the Catalan moment

For `|Re epsilon| < eta < 1`, compact `u,v` cycles avoiding the singular locus and polynomial certificates give a uniform integrable majorant in `t`. Hence differentiation under the integral is legitimate, and

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal I_n'(0)
=\int\!\int\!\int
h_{n,0}
\left(
\mathcal P_n^{(1)}
+\mathcal P_n^{(0)}\log t
\right).
\end{aligned}}
\tag{5.1}
$$

The elementary Catalan identity has the sign

$$
\boxed{
-\left.\frac{d}{d\varepsilon}
\int_0^1\frac{t^\varepsilon}{1+t^2}\,dt
\right|_{\varepsilon=0}
=G.}
\tag{5.2}
$$

Thus `-I_n'(0)`, rather than `I_n'(0)`, carries the Catalan moment when the remaining amplitude is independent of `t`.

Differentiating (4.2) gives

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal I_{n+1}'(0)
-\mathcal I_n'(0)M_{\rm norm}(n)
=\mathcal B_{t,n}'(0),}
\tag{5.3}
$$

provided the `u,v` fluxes vanish uniformly in epsilon. Since `log 1 = 0`, equation (4.7) yields

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal B_{t,n}'(0)
=\int_{\Gamma_u}\int_{\Gamma_v}
\frac{u^nv^n}{Q^{n+1}}
C_n^{(1)}(u,v,1)
\,dv\,du.}
\tag{5.4}
$$

The base coefficient `C^(0)(1)` drives the undifferentiated boundary recurrence; the first epsilon coefficient `C^(1)(1)` drives the differentiated boundary recurrence. The factor `t^epsilon` itself contributes no derivative at `t=1`.

## 5.1 Parity in `t` is an arithmetic condition

Let

$$
J_k(\varepsilon)
=\int_0^1\frac{t^{k+\varepsilon}}{1+t^2}\,dt.
$$

Then

$$
\boxed{
J_{k+2}(\varepsilon)
=\frac1{k+\varepsilon+1}-J_k(\varepsilon).}
\tag{5.5}
$$

The base values are

$$
J_0(0)=\frac\pi4,
\qquad
J_1(0)=\frac12\log2,
$$

and

$$
\boxed{-J_0'(0)=G,}
\qquad
\boxed{-J_1'(0)=\frac{\pi^2}{48}.}
\tag{5.6}
$$

Therefore:

- derivatives of even `t`-powers lie in `Q G + Q`;
- derivatives of odd `t`-powers lie in `Q pi^2 + Q`.

To isolate the Catalan extension without an unwanted `pi^2` component, impose `t -> -t` even parity on `P^(0)` and on the relevant first-jet amplitude, or explicitly prove cancellation of the odd sector. This parity check must be part of the exact verification pipeline.

# 6. Exact `u,v` boundary conditions

## 6.1 Closed contours

If `Gamma_u` and `Gamma_v` are closed, the exact requirements are:

1. `h X_u` and `h X_v` are single-valued meromorphic in neighborhoods of the cycles;
2. no pole lies on either cycle;
3. the cycles do not cross `Q=0` or a certificate denominator;
4. the chosen branches and orientations return to themselves after one circuit.

Under these conditions,

$$
\oint_{\Gamma_u}\partial_u(hX_u)\,du=0,
\qquad
\oint_{\Gamma_v}\partial_v(hX_v)\,dv=0.
$$

No condition such as

$$
A(u_\pm)=0,
\qquad B(v_\pm)=0
$$

is required at an interior saddle. Imposing it can erase the asymptotic mode one is trying to retain.

## 6.2 Open arcs

Suppose instead that the `u` contour is an oriented arc from `a_u` to `b_u`. The exact flux condition is

$$
\boxed{
\left[
\frac{u^{n+1}v^nt^\varepsilon}
{Q(u,v)^n(1+t^2)}
A_n(u,v,t;\varepsilon)
\right]_{u=a_u}^{u=b_u}=0.}
\tag{6.1}
$$

Similarly, for a `v` arc,

$$
\boxed{
\left[
\frac{u^nv^{n+1}t^\varepsilon}
{Q(u,v)^n(1+t^2)}
B_n(u,v,t;\varepsilon)
\right]_{v=a_v}^{v=b_v}=0.}
\tag{6.2}
$$

If the prefactors are finite and nonzero at the endpoints, sufficient pointwise conditions are

$$
A_n(a_u,v,t;\varepsilon)
=A_n(b_u,v,t;\varepsilon)=0,
$$

$$
B_n(u,a_v,t;\varepsilon)
=B_n(u,b_v,t;\varepsilon)=0.
$$

Pairwise cancellation rather than separate vanishing is also valid, but it must hold identically in `n`, the remaining variables, and epsilon.

If an endpoint lies on `Q=0`, then

$$
hX_u\sim Q^{-n}A,
\qquad
hX_v\sim Q^{-n}B.
$$

Uniform vanishing for all `n` requires at least

$$
\operatorname{ord}_{Q=0}A\ge n+1,
\qquad
\operatorname{ord}_{Q=0}B\ge n+1,
$$

or an equivalent rapid-decay mechanism. A fixed-total-degree polynomial certificate cannot satisfy this. Hence open contours ending on `Q=0` are incompatible with the proposed fixed-degree ansatz unless a separate relative-cycle cancellation is proved.

# 7. Saddles are not boundaries

For the genuine Delannoy constant-term carrier, put

$$
\phi(u)=3+\sqrt2(u+u^{-1}),
\qquad
\Phi_D(u,v)=\phi(u)\phi(v).
$$

The critical points are

$$
u_+=1,
\qquad
u_-=-1,
$$

with values

$$
\phi(u_+)=3+2\sqrt2,
\qquad
\phi(u_-)=3-2\sqrt2.
$$

The four product saddles are

$$
(u_+,v_+),
\quad
(u_+,v_-),
\quad
(u_-,v_+),
\quad
(u_-,v_-),
$$

and their three distinct critical values are

$$
17+12\sqrt2,
\qquad
1,
\qquad
17-12\sqrt2.
$$

These points control the three Poincaré modes. They are not endpoints of the standard closed constant-term contours. The certificate amplitudes need to be regular there, not zero there.

By contrast, the Q4838 phase is

$$
\Psi(u,v)=\frac{uv}{1-u^2v^2}.
$$

Its logarithmic critical equations are

$$
\partial_u\log\Psi
=\frac{1+u^2v^2}{u(1-u^2v^2)}=0,
$$

$$
\partial_v\log\Psi
=\frac{1+u^2v^2}{v(1-u^2v^2)}=0.
$$

Thus its stationary locus is

$$
\boxed{uv=\pm i,}
\tag{7.1}
$$

a one-dimensional locus, with critical values `± i/2`. It does not have the four Delannoy saddles. A fixed-degree polynomial numerator cannot change this exponential phase.

This is a necessary spectral screen before any large CRT computation. Either:

1. replace the carrier by `Phi_D(u,v)^n`;
2. exhibit a birational transform and a transformed integration cycle that maps the Delannoy saddles and critical values correctly; or
3. show that the desired modes arise from explicitly controlled singular/end-point contributions rather than stationary points.

Without one of these, a successful formal linear-algebra certificate would not yet explain the CMF Poincaré spectrum or its asymptotics.

## 7.1 The neutral index condition is a saddle-jet condition

A generic two-dimensional nondegenerate saddle contributes a factor `n^{-1}`. The normalized CMF requires outer indices `0` and neutral index `-3`. Therefore a period representation must provide:

- one net extra power of `n` at the outer saddles;
- cancellation of the `n^{-1}` and `n^{-2}` coefficients in the combined contribution of the two mixed saddles.

These are conditions on the period amplitude `P_n` and the cycle coefficients, not boundary conditions on `A,B`. A sufficient, though not necessary, local condition for the neutral mode is fourth-order vanishing of the effective amplitude at each mixed saddle. More generally, one may allow cancellation between the two mixed saddles and verify that the first two stationary-phase coefficients vanish.

This saddle-jet test is independent of the AZ divergence verification and must be performed afterward.

# 8. Does the epsilon-regularized identity close the proof?

It does so **conditionally**, after five separate certificates are supplied.

## Certificate A: the base AZ identity

Find and exactly verify (3.1).

## Certificate B: the first epsilon jet

Find and exactly verify (3.2), or equivalently (3.4). This identifies the candidate extension cocycle.

## Certificate C: relative boundary closure

Prove one of:

- all three boundary fluxes vanish through first epsilon order; or
- the `t=1` boundary lies in a known finite boundary module and yields a verified block-triangular recurrence.

## Certificate D: Catalan readout

Prove the `t`-parity and initial-value identities that isolate

$$
-G=\int_0^1\frac{\log t}{1+t^2}\,dt
$$

with no residual `pi^2`, `pi`, or `log 2` component in the required row functional.

## Certificate E: cycle and asymptotic matching

Match the integrated period matrix at one initial index to the normalized challenge matrix, decompose the cycle into the three saddle sectors, and verify the formal-index cancellations.

Only after A–E does the AZ framework prove that the CMF connection coefficient is `G` and that the error is exponentially small.

The final asymptotic is not expected to be the exact geometric expression

$$
S_n=c_+G+c_0+c_-\left(\frac{c_-}{c_+}\right)^n.
$$

Poincaré theory gives full formal series. For the actual ratio one expects the form established in the earlier asymptotic analysis,

$$
\boxed{
\frac{p_n}{q_n}-G
=\kappa n^{-3}(17-12\sqrt2)^n
\bigl(1+O(n^{-1})\bigr)
+\eta(17-12\sqrt2)^{2n}
\bigl(1+O(n^{-1})\bigr).}
\tag{8.1}
$$

An exact pure power occurs only after additional exceptional cancellations.

# 9. Recommended modular-screening pipeline

The correct finite computation is block lower triangular in the epsilon jet.

```text
1. Choose the genuine Delannoy phase or prove the carrier transformation.
2. Normalize and balance M(n).
3. Fix D_X(n), geometric support, t-parity, and u<->v symmetry.
4. Solve the epsilon^0 system for d=0,1,2,3,4.
5. Certify zero kernels at lower degrees by nonzero maximal minors.
6. Reconstruct and verify the first nonzero base certificate over Q.
7. With the base certificate fixed, solve the epsilon^1 forced system:
       L(P1,A1,B1,C1) = -(1+t^2) C0
   in the homogeneous-survival normalization,
   or retain P1 / the t=1 boundary as the extension state.
8. Interpolate in u,v,t before CRT reconstruction.
9. Verify the identities over Q after clearing denominators.
10. Check closed-cycle meromorphy or the exact open-arc flux equations.
11. Check t=0 and t=1 boundaries through epsilon order one.
12. Check even t-parity and the Catalan moment normalization.
13. Match the initial CMF rows.
14. Perform the saddle-jet calculation for indices (0,-3,0).
```

At modular stage 7 the first-jet system has the same coefficient matrix as the base system on the new unknowns, with an explicit forcing vector determined by `C^(0)`. This makes the extension computation substantially cheaper than a fresh nonlinear search.

# 10. Bottom line

1. **Minimum degree:** `d=2` is the first credible bound, not yet a proved minimum. The exact minimum requires zero-kernel certificates for `d<2` and a reconstructed valid certificate at `d=2`.
2. **Epsilon survival:** no, an `epsilon=0` certificate does not automatically survive. The first derivative introduces the forcing `(1+t^2)C^(0)`. Solving or retaining that forcing is the extension-class computation.
3. **Closed contours:** the `u,v` boundary terms vanish by closed-cycle total-derivative integration, with regularity and pole-avoidance conditions. No vanishing at saddles is required.
4. **Open contours:** the exact conditions are the weighted flux equations (6.1)–(6.2). If an endpoint lies on `Q=0`, fixed-degree certificates cannot normally make the flux vanish for all `n`.
5. **Carrier correction:** the phase `uv/(1-u^2v^2)` does not have the four Delannoy saddles. This must be repaired before the proposed screening can identify the CMF extension.
6. **Proof status:** a base AZ identity plus its first epsilon jet, relative boundary closure, Catalan parity/readout, initial matching, and saddle-jet asymptotics would close the convergence proof and identify the connection constant. None of these analytic conclusions follows from CRT reconstruction alone.
