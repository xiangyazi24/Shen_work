# Apéry zeros modulo p: what Dwork theory controls, what it does not control, and the modular-form comparison

## Executive verdict

Let

$$
F(t)=Σ_{n≥0} b_n t^n,
$$

$$
A_p(t)=Σ_{j=0}^{p-1} b_j t^j ∈ 𝔽_p[t],
$$

and

$$
S_p={0≤j<p : b_j≡0 mod p},
\qquad
Z(p)=|S_p|.
$$

The conclusions are as follows.

1. **Dwork unit-root theory controls A_p(t) as a function of the geometric parameter t, not the support of its coefficient vector.** Its roots describe nonordinary or supersingular fibers. Z(p) counts zero coefficients of A_p. Frobenius eigenvalues alone do not give a nontrivial bound for Z(p).

2. **No published theorem from Dwork theory, Coleman theory, Kedlaya algorithms, or overconvergent modular forms presently gives a uniform power-saving bound**

$$
Z(p)=O(p^{1-δ}),  δ>0,
$$

for the Apéry numbers. In particular, I do not know a proved O(√p) or O(p^{2/3}) bound.

3. Two deterministic bounds are available:

$$
Z(p)≤(p-1)/2
$$

from the no-consecutive-zeros property, and a gap-polynomial argument gives

$$
Z(p)≪p/log p.
$$

I include the proof below. I have not located the second estimate in the literature, so it should be treated as an elementary lemma proved here, not as a theorem of Dwork or Gessel.

4. Lucas factorization gives an exact digit-counting law:

$$
#{0≤n<p^k : b_n≠0 mod p}=(p-Z(p))^k.
$$

Thus the zero proportion is exactly

$$
1-(1-Z(p)/p)^k.
$$

This propagates whatever Z(p) is; it does not bound Z(p).

5. There is a tautological rational Lucas zeta function:

$$
exp(Σ_{k≥1} [p^k-(p-Z(p))^k]T^k/k)
=
[1-(p-Z(p))T]/[1-pT].
$$

This is finite-state combinatorics, not a Hasse-Weil zeta function, and it contains no square-root cancellation.

6. For the non-CM newform

$$
f=8.4.a.a=η(2z)^4η(4z)^4,
$$

the correct theorem for the distribution of a_ℓ(f) mod p, with fixed residual prime p and varying primes ℓ, is Chebotarev applied to the residual Galois representation. It is not Mazur-Tate-Teitelbaum or Coleman theory. For every sufficiently large nonexceptional p,

$$
dens{ℓ : a_ℓ(f)≡0 mod p}=p/(p^2-1).
$$

The density is therefore 1/p+O(p^{-3}). This horizontal modular-coefficient theorem has no known implication for Z(p), whose variable is the Apéry index j rather than a Frobenius prime ℓ.

The decisive distinction is

$$
zeros of the Hasse polynomial as a function of t

e
zero coefficients of the Hasse polynomial.
$$

## 1. What the Dwork congruence actually says

Gessel's Lucas theorem gives, for 0≤r<p,

$$
b_{mp+r}≡b_m b_r mod p.
$$

At the generating-function level this is

$$
F(t)≡A_p(t)F(t^p) mod p.
$$

Since F(t^p)=F(t)^p in 𝔽_p[[t]], one may also write

$$
A_p(t)F(t)^{p-1}=1
$$

in 𝔽_p[[t]].

Dwork theory lifts this identity. On an ordinary residue domain, the unit-root Frobenius is represented by a quotient of periods of the form

$$
F(t)/F(t^σ),
$$

and A_p(t) is its first reduction modulo p. Stronger Apéry supercongruences give higher-p-power approximations to this quotient.

For the modular motive attached to the weight-four form, the good-prime local polynomial is

$$
X^2-a_p(f)X+p^3.
$$

At an ordinary prime, a_p(f) is nonzero mod p. The polynomial has a p-adic unit root α_p and a slope-three root β_p, with

$$
α_p β_p=p^3,
$$

and

$$
α_p≡a_p(f) mod p^3.
$$

Thus, granting the normalization in the question, a congruence such as

$$
b_{np-1}≡γ(p)b_{n-1} mod p^3
$$

is exactly the type of one-block Frobenius congruence supplied by the unit root.

It controls a special arithmetic progression of indices, or more generally compatibility of period coefficients across a Frobenius block. It does not tell us how many of

$$
b_0,b_1,...,b_{p-1} mod p
$$

are zero. A rank-two or rank-three Frobenius matrix has only O(1) eigenvalues, whereas Z(p) is a support statistic of a vector of length p.

## 2. Roots in the parameter versus zero coefficients

For a geometric family, A_p(t) is a Hasse invariant. At a point t_0, the condition A_p(t_0)≠0 usually characterizes the ordinary locus on which the unit root exists analytically. The roots of A_p(t) in an algebraic closure of 𝔽_p therefore encode exceptional fibers.

By contrast,

$$
Z(p)=#{j : coefficient of t^j in A_p(t) is zero}.
$$

Root geometry and coefficient support are nearly independent. Over 𝔽_p,

$$
1+t+...+t^{p-1}=(t-1)^{p-1}
$$

has no zero coefficients and only one geometric root, while

$$
t^{p-1}-1
$$

has only two nonzero coefficients and p-1 distinct nonzero roots.

Consequently, even a complete description of the Newton slopes, the unit-root eigenvalue, and the supersingular locus does not give a coefficient-zero bound.

A recent theorem makes this distinction especially clear. Caruso, Fürnsinn, Vargas-Montoya, and Zudilin prove that the Apéry truncation has the form

$$
A_p(t)=B_p(t)^2
$$

when p≡1,5,7,11 mod 24, and

$$
A_p(t)=(t^2-34t+1)B_p(t)^2
$$

when p≡13,17,19,23 mod 24. This is strong information about the algebraic function and its Galois group. It still gives no useful bound for the number of zero coefficients, because polynomial squaring can create extensive coefficient cancellation.

There is also a useful no-go principle. Frobenius eigenvalues are invariants of the isocrystal. The Taylor coefficient vector of a chosen horizontal period is not: it changes under a change of local frame or multiplication by an analytic unit. Therefore no estimate for Z(p) can follow from the Frobenius eigenvalues alone. A successful theorem must also use the distinguished integral lattice and the distinguished holomorphic period at t=0.

## 3. What would turn p-adic geometry into an O(√p) bound?

A plausible route would be to realize the index function

$$
j ↦ b_j mod p,
\qquad j∈𝔽_p,
$$

as a bounded-conductor trace function of a sheaf on the index line. Additive-character orthogonality would then give

$$
Z(p)=p^{-1} Σ_{a∈𝔽_p} Σ_{j∈𝔽_p} ψ(a b_j).
$$

If one could prove uniformly for a≠0 that

$$
|Σ_{j∈𝔽_p} ψ(a b_j)|≪√p,
$$

then

$$
Z(p)=1+O(√p).
$$

This is the type of estimate supplied by Deligne's Riemann-hypothesis theorem for bounded-conductor trace functions.

The present Picard-Fuchs crystal is a sheaf in the geometric variable t. It does not provide a trace-function description of the coefficient index j↦b_j. Passing from a period in t to its j-th Taylor coefficient is coefficient extraction, not evaluation of the same sheaf at j.

Thus this argument identifies the missing structure precisely, but no bounded-conductor index sheaf is presently known for the Apéry numbers.

## 4. Unconditional deterministic bounds for Z(p)

Assume p≥5.

### 4.1 No consecutive zeros

If

$$
b_m≡b_{m+1}≡0 mod p
$$

with m+1<p, the recurrence propagates backwards and forces b_0≡0, contradicting b_0=1. Also

$$
b_{p-1}≡1 mod p.
$$

Hence zeros in 0,...,p-1 are nonconsecutive and avoid both endpoints. Therefore

$$
Z(p)≤floor((p-1)/2).
$$

### 4.2 A gap-polynomial bound

Put

$$
P(X)=34X^3+51X^2+27X+5.
$$

Define

$$
R_0(X)=0,
\qquad
R_1(X)=1,
$$

and, for h≥1,

$$
R_{h+1}(X)=P(X+h)R_h(X)-(X+h)^6R_{h-1}(X).
$$

Suppose j<j+h<p are consecutive zero positions. Since b_{j+1}≠0, divide the recurrence by b_{j+1} and clear the denominator

$$
Π_{r=2}^{h}(j+r)^3.
$$

This gives

$$
R_h(j)≡0 mod p.
$$

Moreover,

$$
deg R_h≤3(h-1).
$$

Let s_h be the coefficient of X^{3(h-1)}. Comparing leading terms gives

$$
s_0=0,
\qquad
s_1=1,
\qquad
s_{h+1}=34s_h-s_{h-1}.
$$

In particular,

$$
0<s_h≤34^{h-1}.
$$

Choose

$$
H=floor(log p/(2 log 34)).
$$

For 2≤h≤H, the leading coefficient s_h lies strictly between 0 and p, so R_h mod p is not the zero polynomial. Therefore the number of zero gaps of a fixed length h≤H is at most

$$
deg R_h≤3(h-1).
$$

The total number of short gaps is O(H^2). Every remaining gap has length greater than H, so there are at most p/H long gaps. Consequently

$$
Z(p)-1≤O(H^2)+p/H,
$$

and hence

$$
Z(p)≪p/log p.
$$

This is o(p), but it is not a power saving.

### Why h=2,3,4,5 do not prove O(p^{2/3})

To derive Z(p)=O(p^{2/3}) by this gap method, one would need nonvanishing of R_h mod p uniformly for all

$$
h≤c p^{1/3}
$$

or another comparably long range, while keeping deg R_h=O(h). Computations for four fixed values of h only exclude finitely many short gaps. Dwork unit-root theory currently gives no uniform nonvanishing theorem for the contents or leading coefficients of the moving family R_h up to h of order p^{1/3}.

## 5. Lucas factorization gives exact digit statistics

Write

$$
n=n_0+n_1p+...+n_{k-1}p^{k-1},
\qquad
0≤n_i<p.
$$

Gessel's theorem gives

$$
b_n≡Π_{i=0}^{k-1} b_{n_i} mod p.
$$

Therefore

$$
b_n≠0 mod p
$$

if and only if every digit n_i lies outside S_p. There are exactly p-Z(p) allowed digits, so

$$
#{0≤n<p^k : b_n≠0 mod p}=(p-Z(p))^k,
$$

and

$$
#{0≤n<p^k : b_n≡0 mod p}=p^k-(p-Z(p))^k.
$$

The zero proportion is

$$
1-(1-Z(p)/p)^k.
$$

This formula is exact. It also shows why Lucas theory cannot bound Z(p): the one-digit set S_p is the free input from which all higher-digit statistics are generated.

Malik and Straub prove the reflection symmetry

$$
b_j≡b_{p-1-j} mod p.
$$

Their random-residue model predicts paired zeros with an asymptotic Poisson law of mean 1/2, and therefore predicts

$$
Pr(Z(p)=0)→e^{-1/2}.
$$

This is a heuristic, not a theorem. Even the infinitude of primes with Z(p)=0 remains open.

## 6. Why Coleman and Kedlaya do not currently bound |S_p|

### Coleman zero-counting tools

Strassmann's theorem and Coleman rigid-analytic methods can bound the zeros of a single rigid analytic function on a p-adic residue disk when its Newton polygon or Weierstrass degree is controlled.

To apply this here, one would need a rigid analytic interpolation B_p(x), for x∈ℤ_p, such that

$$
B_p(j)≡b_j mod p,
\qquad
0≤j<p,
$$

with Weierstrass degree bounded independently of p. No such interpolation with controlled degree is known. The base-p digit behavior supplied by Lucas is finite-state and highly nonanalytic in the index. It is not the same as rigid-analytic dependence of a period on t.

### Kedlaya algorithms

Kedlaya-style algorithms compute Frobenius matrices, slopes, and zeta functions of varieties or overconvergent F-isocrystals. They can efficiently recover A_p(t) or local Frobenius data. They do not turn a bound on cohomological dimension into a sparsity theorem for the p Taylor coefficients of A_p(t).

Algorithmically one may compute S_p by generating b_0,...,b_{p-1} mod p. The missing ingredient is not computability but a structural estimate uniform in p.

## 7. A zeta function for the Lucas zero language

Let

$$
N_k=#{0≤n<p^k : b_n≡0 mod p}.
$$

By the exact digit formula,

$$
N_k=p^k-(p-Z(p))^k.
$$

Consequently

$$
Z_{p,Lucas}(T)
=
exp(Σ_{k≥1} N_k T^k/k)
=
[1-(p-Z(p))T]/[1-pT].
$$

This rationality is elementary. Its two eigenvalues are p and p-Z(p). There is no analogue of a Weil weight forcing an error of size p^{k/2}; the unknown Z(p) appears directly as the eigenvalue gap.

### Higher congruence moduli

For fixed r, Beukers's p-linear-scheme machinery gives a finite-state description of Apéry numbers modulo p^r. If

$$
N_{p,r}(k)=#{0≤n<p^k : b_n≡0 mod p^r},
$$

then accepted-word counts are obtained from a finite transition matrix M_{p,r}:

$$
N_{p,r}(k)=u^T M_{p,r}^k v.
$$

Hence

$$
Σ_{k≥0} N_{p,r}(k)T^k
=
u^T(I-TM_{p,r})^{-1}v
$$

is rational for each fixed p and r.

This is automata rationality. The transition matrix has row sums bounded by p, so its eigenvalues are only bounded trivially by p. There is no purity theorem or square-root spectral gap.

If one asks for the diagonal condition

$$
b_n≡0 mod p^k,
\qquad
0≤n<p^k,
$$

the modulus and the automaton both change with k. Fixed-r rationality does not imply rationality or a Hasse-Weil estimate for this diagonal sequence.

## 8. Why Dwork rationality is not the relevant theorem

For a fixed variety X over 𝔽_q, Dwork rationality concerns

$$
Z(X,T)=exp(Σ_{m≥1} #X(𝔽_{q^m}) T^m/m).
$$

The points over 𝔽_{q^m} are related functorially by Frobenius, and cohomology supplies eigenvalues with Weil weights.

The set

$$
{0≤n<p^k : b_n≡0 mod p^r}
$$

is instead a language of base-p digit words. There is no canonical identification of its words with 𝔽_{p^k}-points of a fixed variety compatible with field Frobenius. Its rationality, when available, comes from a finite automaton rather than a fixed cohomology group.

A related subtlety is Dwork's unit-root L-function conjecture. For ordinary families, the unit-root part need not be rational; the general theorem is p-adic meromorphy of the unit-root L-function. This still concerns Frobenius over closed points of a geometric base, not coefficient-index zeros.

Thus there is presently no Hasse-Weil analogue yielding a bound for Z(p).

## 9. The modular form 8.4.a.a

The LMFDB form 8.4.a.a is the rational, non-CM weight-four newform

$$
f(z)=η(2z)^4η(4z)^4
$$

with trivial character and no nontrivial inner twist. Its expansion begins

$$
f(q)=q-4q^3-2q^5+24q^7-11q^9+... .
$$

For a residual prime p>3, the associated representation is

$$
ρ̄_{f,p}: Gal(ℚbar/ℚ)→GL_2(𝔽_p).
$$

For primes ℓ not dividing 8p,

$$
tr ρ̄_{f,p}(Frob_ℓ)≡a_ℓ(f) mod p,
$$

and

$$
det ρ̄_{f,p}(Frob_ℓ)≡ℓ^3 mod p.
$$

Because f is non-CM with rational coefficients and no inner twists, the Ribet-Momose large-image theorem implies that for all but finitely many p, the image contains SL_2(𝔽_p). Its determinant group is

$$
D_p=(𝔽_p^×)^3.
$$

Thus for a nonexceptional p,

$$
image(ρ̄_{f,p})=G_p
=
{g∈GL_2(𝔽_p) : det(g)∈D_p}.
$$

### 9.1 Exact trace-zero density

Fix δ∈𝔽_p^×, and let χ_p be the quadratic character. A direct count gives

$$
#{g∈GL_2(𝔽_p) : tr(g)=0, det(g)=δ}
=
p[p+χ_p(-δ)].
$$

To see this, write a trace-zero matrix as

```text
[a   b]
[c  -a]
```

and use bc=-a^2-δ, counting separately the cases in which the right side is zero or nonzero.

Every nonzero determinant fiber has size

$$
|SL_2(𝔽_p)|=p(p^2-1).
$$

For p>3, the quadratic character is nontrivial on the cube subgroup, so

$$
Σ_{δ∈D_p} χ_p(-δ)=0.
$$

Summing over δ gives

$$
#{g∈G_p : tr(g)=0}/|G_p|
=
p/(p^2-1).
$$

Chebotarev therefore yields, for every fixed nonexceptional p,

$$
#{ℓ≤X : ℓ does not divide 8p, a_ℓ(f)≡0 mod p}
\sim
[p/(p^2-1)]Li(X).
$$

Under GRH for the number field cut out by ρ̄_{f,p}, effective Chebotarev gives the fixed-p estimate

$$
#{ℓ≤X : a_ℓ(f)≡0 mod p}
=
[p/(p^2-1)]Li(X)
+
O_p(X^{1/2} log X).
$$

The O_p constant hides the degree and discriminant of the residual extension. This is not useful uniformly when p grows with X without stronger uniform effective-Chebotarev input.

For a general residue c∈𝔽_p, the maximal-image density is

$$
δ_p(c)
=
[p+|D_p|^{-1}Σ_{δ∈D_p} χ_p(c^2-4δ)]/(p^2-1).
$$

Thus the residues are close to equidistributed, on the natural scale 1/p.

### 9.2 Mazur-Tate-Teitelbaum

Mazur-Tate-Teitelbaum concerns the p-adic L-function of f, its interpolation factors, and exceptional or trivial zeros. It studies a distinguished analytic function in the cyclotomic variable. It does not count primes ℓ for which a_ℓ(f) lies in a specified residue class modulo p.

### 9.3 Coleman and Coleman-Edixhoven

Coleman theory of overconvergent modular forms studies U_p slopes, classicality, and p-adic families in the weight direction. Coleman-Edixhoven gives semisimplicity and structural information for U_p. These are vertical or p-local statements. They do not provide horizontal equidistribution of a_ℓ(f) mod p as ℓ varies.

The horizontal theorem is Chebotarev.

### 9.4 Why this still does not bound Z(p)

The modular coefficient a_p(f), or the unit root α_p, is one Frobenius scalar at p. The set S_p contains up to p coefficient indices j. There is no known correspondence

$$
j↦Frob_{ℓ(j)}
$$

that turns b_j mod p into a_{ℓ(j)}(f) mod p. Moreover, the diagonal coefficient a_p(f) occurs at the residual characteristic and is ramified, so it is not covered by the fixed-p, varying-ℓ Chebotarev statement.

The exact density p/(p^2-1) is therefore a useful analogy for the random 1/p heuristic, but not a proof about Apéry coefficient zeros.

## 10. Direct answers

### Question 1

Dwork unit-root Frobenius does not presently give a nontrivial bound for Z(p). It controls the Hasse polynomial as a function of t, the ordinary locus, and Frobenius-block congruences such as the stated ASD relation. It does not control coefficient support. No O(√p), O(p^{2/3}), or other uniform power-saving estimate is known from this theory.

The best unconditional bounds identified here are

$$
Z(p)≤(p-1)/2
$$

and the elementary

$$
Z(p)≪p/log p.
$$

### Question 2

No known Coleman or Kedlaya application bounds |S_p|. Coleman would require a bounded-degree rigid analytic interpolation in the index j. Kedlaya computes geometric Frobenius in t. Lucas gives exact propagation from S_p but leaves the one-digit set itself undetermined.

### Question 3

There is an exact rational automaton or Lucas zeta function, and fixed-p^r finite-state counts have rational ordinary generating functions. These are not Hasse-Weil zeta functions and carry no purity or square-root error term. Dwork rationality for fixed varieties does not apply to the moving digit-word problem, and the diagonal modulus p^k is farther still from a fixed geometric object.

### Question 4

For fixed nonexceptional p and varying primes ℓ, Chebotarev gives the exact trace-zero density

$$
p/(p^2-1).
$$

Mazur-Tate-Teitelbaum and Coleman-Edixhoven do not give this distribution; they concern p-adic L-functions, U_p slopes, and variation in weight. The Chebotarev theorem concerns modular Fourier coefficients rather than Apéry digit coefficients and therefore does not bound Z(p).

## 11. What a genuine new proof would need

There are two concrete targets.

### Target A: an index trace function

Construct a bounded-conductor sheaf whose trace at j∈𝔽_p is b_j mod p, then prove square-root cancellation in the additive-character sums. This would yield Z(p)=1+O(√p).

### Target B: uniform gap-polynomial nonvanishing

Prove that the recurrence polynomials R_h remain nonzero modulo p, with controlled degree, uniformly for h≤p^θ. The elementary gap argument would then give a power saving. Control through h of order p^{1/3} would produce the desired O(p^{2/3}) scale.

Neither target follows from unit-root eigenvalues alone. Both require new arithmetic information about the distinguished coefficient lattice.
