# Apéry zeros modulo p and the limits of the Dwork approach

## Executive answer

Let

$$
F(t)=Σ_{n≥0} b_n t^n,
$$

$$
A_p(t)=Σ_{j=0}^{p-1} b_j t^j ∈ 𝔽_p[t],
$$

and

$$
S_p={0≤j<p : b_j≡0 mod p},
$$

$$
Z(p)=|S_p|.
$$

The main conclusions are these.

1. Dwork theory controls A_p(t) as a function of the geometric parameter t. Its zero set is related to the nonordinary or supersingular locus. Z(p), however, counts zero coefficients of A_p(t). These are fundamentally different statistics.

2. Frobenius eigenvalues, unit roots, overconvergent modular forms, Coleman theory, and Kedlaya algorithms do not currently give a uniform power-saving estimate for Z(p). In particular, no proved bound of the form O(√p) or O(p^{2/3}) is known from those methods.

3. The recurrence gives the elementary bound

$$
Z(p)≤(p-1)/2.
$$

A gap-polynomial argument improves this to

$$
Z(p)≪p/log p.
$$

I include the proof. I have not found this latter estimate stated in the literature, so it should be treated as a lemma proved here rather than attributed to Dwork or Gessel.

4. Lucas factorization gives an exact formula for higher digit lengths:

$$
#{0≤n<p^k : b_n≠0 mod p}=(p-Z(p))^k.
$$

It propagates the one-digit zero set S_p but does not bound it.

5. There is a rational finite-state zeta function for the Lucas zero language,

$$
exp(Σ_{k≥1}[p^k-(p-Z(p))^k]T^k/k)
=
[1-(p-Z(p))T]/[1-pT].
$$

This is automata rationality, not Hasse-Weil rationality, and it carries no purity or square-root estimate.

6. For the modular form 8.4.a.a, with fixed residual prime p and varying primes ℓ, Chebotarev gives the relevant distribution theorem. At every sufficiently large nonexceptional p,

$$
dens{ℓ : a_ℓ(f)≡0 mod p}=p/(p^2-1).
$$

Mazur-Tate-Teitelbaum and Coleman-Edixhoven do not give this horizontal distribution. The Chebotarev result does not imply a bound for Z(p), because the Apéry index j is not a Frobenius prime.

The decisive distinction is

$$
zeros of A_p(t) as a polynomial in t
≠
zero coefficients of A_p(t).
$$

## 1. The Dwork and Lucas identities

Gessel proved the Lucas congruence

$$
b_{mp+r}≡b_m b_r mod p,
$$

for 0≤r<p. In generating-function form,

$$
F(t)≡A_p(t)F(t^p) mod p.
$$

Since F(t^p)=F(t)^p over 𝔽_p,

$$
A_p(t)F(t)^{p-1}=1
$$

in 𝔽_p[[t]].

Dwork theory lifts this relation. On an ordinary residue domain, the unit-root Frobenius is represented by a period quotient of the form

$$
F(t)/F(t^σ),
$$

and A_p(t) is its first reduction modulo p. Higher Apéry supercongruences give higher-p-power approximations to this quotient.

For the weight-four modular motive, the good-prime local polynomial is

$$
X^2-a_p(f)X+p^3.
$$

At an ordinary prime, a_p(f) is nonzero modulo p. The roots α_p and β_p satisfy

$$
α_pβ_p=p^3,
$$

with α_p a p-adic unit and β_p of valuation 3. Hence

$$
α_p≡a_p(f) mod p^3.
$$

This is the mechanism behind an Atkin-Swinnerton-Dyer congruence of the stated type

$$
b_{np-1}≡γ(p)b_{n-1} mod p^3.
$$

It controls a Frobenius block or a distinguished arithmetic progression of coefficients. It does not constrain the support of the entire vector

$$
(b_0,b_1,...,b_{p-1}) mod p.
$$

A finite-rank crystal has only finitely many Frobenius eigenvalues, while Z(p) is a statistic involving p Taylor coefficients.

## 2. Why the Hasse invariant does not bound coefficient zeros

The polynomial A_p(t) is a Hasse invariant for the Picard-Fuchs family. Values t_0 for which A_p(t_0) is nonzero belong to the ordinary locus; roots of A_p(t) encode exceptional fibers.

That geometric zero set is unrelated to coefficient sparsity. Over 𝔽_p,

$$
1+t+...+t^{p-1}=(t-1)^{p-1}
$$

has no zero coefficients and only one geometric root. Conversely,

$$
t^{p-1}-1
$$

has only two nonzero coefficients and p-1 distinct nonzero roots.

Even the strong recent factorization of the Apéry truncation does not change this. Caruso, Fürnsinn, Vargas-Montoya, and Zudilin prove

$$
A_p(t)=B_p(t)^2
$$

for p≡1,5,7,11 mod 24, and

$$
A_p(t)=(t^2-34t+1)B_p(t)^2
$$

for p≡13,17,19,23 mod 24. This is powerful information about the algebraic function and its Galois group, but the coefficients of a square can undergo extensive cancellation. It gives no useful estimate for Z(p).

There is also an invariance obstruction. Frobenius eigenvalues are invariants of the isocrystal. The Taylor coefficients of a chosen period change under a local gauge or multiplication by an analytic unit. Therefore a coefficient-support estimate cannot follow from the Frobenius eigenvalues alone. It must use the distinguished integral lattice and the distinguished holomorphic solution at t=0.

## 3. What a successful p-adic argument would need

A route to O(√p) would be to realize

$$
j↦b_j mod p
$$

as a bounded-conductor trace function on the index line 𝔽_p. Additive-character orthogonality would give

$$
Z(p)=p^{-1}Σ_{a∈𝔽_p}Σ_{j∈𝔽_p}ψ(a b_j).
$$

If, for every a≠0,

$$
|Σ_{j∈𝔽_p}ψ(a b_j)|≪√p,
$$

then

$$
Z(p)=1+O(√p).
$$

This is exactly the sort of conclusion Deligne's theorem gives for bounded-conductor trace functions.

The existing Picard-Fuchs crystal is a sheaf in t, not in the coefficient index j. Extracting the j-th Taylor coefficient is not evaluation of the same sheaf at j. No bounded-conductor index sheaf with trace b_j mod p is currently known.

## 4. Unconditional bounds from the recurrence

Assume p≥5.

### 4.1 No consecutive zeros

If

$$
b_m≡b_{m+1}≡0 mod p
$$

with m+1<p, the recurrence propagates backward and forces b_0≡0, contradicting b_0=1. Also

$$
b_{p-1}≡1 mod p.
$$

Thus zeros in 0,...,p-1 are nonconsecutive and avoid both endpoints. Therefore

$$
Z(p)≤floor((p-1)/2).
$$

### 4.2 Gap polynomials

Set

$$
P(X)=34X^3+51X^2+27X+5.
$$

Define

$$
R_0(X)=0,
$$

$$
R_1(X)=1,
$$

and

$$
R_{h+1}(X)=P(X+h)R_h(X)-(X+h)^6R_{h-1}(X).
$$

Suppose j and j+h are consecutive zero positions, with j+h<p. Since b_{j+1} is nonzero, normalize by b_{j+1} and clear the denominator

$$
Π_{r=2}^{h}(j+r)^3.
$$

The recurrence gives

$$
R_h(j)≡0 mod p.
$$

Furthermore,

$$
deg R_h≤3(h-1).
$$

Let s_h be the coefficient of X^{3(h-1)} in R_h. Comparing leading terms gives

$$
s_0=0,
$$

$$
s_1=1,
$$

$$
s_{h+1}=34s_h-s_{h-1}.
$$

Hence

$$
0<s_h≤34^{h-1}.
$$

Take

$$
H=floor(log p/(2log 34)).
$$

For 2≤h≤H, the leading coefficient satisfies 0<s_h<p, so R_h is not the zero polynomial modulo p. The number of gaps of length h is therefore at most

$$
deg R_h≤3(h-1).
$$

There are O(H^2) short gaps in total. Every other gap has length greater than H, so there are at most p/H long gaps. Thus

$$
Z(p)-1≤O(H^2)+p/H,
$$

and consequently

$$
Z(p)≪p/log p.
$$

This is sublinear, but it is not a power saving.

### 4.3 Why the explicit h=2,3,4,5 calculations are insufficient

An O(p^{2/3}) conclusion from this method would require uniform nonvanishing of R_h modulo p through a range such as

$$
h≤c p^{1/3},
$$

while retaining deg R_h=O(h). Verifying four fixed values of h only excludes finitely many short gaps. No current Dwork theorem controls the content or leading coefficient of the moving family R_h throughout a range of length p^{1/3}.

## 5. Exact consequences of Lucas factorization

Write

$$
n=n_0+n_1p+...+n_{k-1}p^{k-1},
$$

with 0≤n_i<p. Then

$$
b_n≡Π_{i=0}^{k-1}b_{n_i} mod p.
$$

Therefore b_n is nonzero modulo p exactly when every digit n_i lies outside S_p. There are p-Z(p) permitted digits, so

$$
#{0≤n<p^k : b_n≠0 mod p}=(p-Z(p))^k,
$$

and

$$
#{0≤n<p^k : b_n≡0 mod p}=p^k-(p-Z(p))^k.
$$

The zero proportion is exactly

$$
1-(1-Z(p)/p)^k.
$$

Lucas theory therefore turns the unknown one-digit statistic into exact higher-digit statistics. It does not estimate the one-digit statistic itself.

Malik and Straub also prove the reflection symmetry

$$
b_j≡b_{p-1-j} mod p.
$$

Their random-residue model predicts paired zero positions with a Poisson law of mean 1/2, and hence predicts

$$
Pr(Z(p)=0)→e^{-1/2}.
$$

This remains conjectural. Even the infinitude of primes for which Z(p)=0 is open.

## 6. Coleman theory and Kedlaya algorithms

Strassmann and Coleman methods can bound zeros of a rigid analytic function when its Newton polygon or Weierstrass degree is controlled. To use them here one would need a rigid analytic interpolation B_p(x) on ℤ_p satisfying

$$
B_p(j)≡b_j mod p
$$

for 0≤j<p, with Weierstrass degree bounded independently of p. No such interpolation is known. Lucas digit behavior is finite-state in j and is not the same as rigid-analytic variation of a period in t.

Kedlaya-style algorithms compute Frobenius matrices, slopes, and zeta functions of varieties or overconvergent F-isocrystals. They can compute A_p(t) and its geometric Frobenius data. They do not turn the bounded dimension of cohomology into a sparsity theorem for the p Taylor coefficients of A_p(t).

Thus these methods make S_p computable, but do not provide a uniform a priori upper bound for |S_p| beyond recurrence-based estimates.

## 7. Zeta functions for the zero language

Let

$$
N_k=#{0≤n<p^k : b_n≡0 mod p}.
$$

The exact Lucas count gives

$$
N_k=p^k-(p-Z(p))^k.
$$

Hence

$$
Z_{p,Lucas}(T)
=
exp(Σ_{k≥1}N_kT^k/k)
=
[1-(p-Z(p))T]/[1-pT].
$$

This rational function has the two elementary eigenvalues p and p-Z(p). There is no Weil-weight constraint forcing a p^{k/2} error term. The unknown Z(p) is itself the eigenvalue gap.

For fixed r, Beukers's p-linear schemes give a finite-state description modulo p^r. If

$$
N_{p,r}(k)=#{0≤n<p^k : b_n≡0 mod p^r},
$$

then for a finite transition matrix M_{p,r},

$$
N_{p,r}(k)=u^T M_{p,r}^k v.
$$

Therefore

$$
Σ_{k≥0}N_{p,r}(k)T^k
=
u^T(I-TM_{p,r})^{-1}v
$$

is rational for each fixed p and r. This is automata rationality. The transition matrix has p outgoing digit transitions per state, so its eigenvalues have only the trivial size bound O(p); there is no purity theorem or square-root spectral gap.

If the modulus is diagonal, namely p^k at digit length k, the automaton itself changes with k. Fixed-r rationality gives no rationality or Hasse-Weil estimate for that diagonal sequence.

Dwork rationality concerns a fixed variety X over 𝔽_q and the function

$$
Z(X,T)=exp(Σ_{m≥1}#X(𝔽_{q^m})T^m/m).
$$

The fields 𝔽_{q^m} are related by Frobenius, and cohomology supplies eigenvalues with Weil weights. A language of base-p digit words is not canonically the set of extension-field points of a fixed variety. Its rationality comes from a finite automaton, not from crystalline or étale cohomology.

The unit-root L-function theorem is also different. In ordinary families, the general conclusion is p-adic meromorphy of the unit-root L-function, not a coefficient-zero estimate for a Taylor series.

## 8. The modular form 8.4.a.a

The LMFDB newform is

$$
f(z)=η(2z)^4η(4z)^4
$$

of level 8 and weight 4. It has rational coefficients, trivial character, no complex multiplication, and no nontrivial inner twist. Its q-expansion begins

$$
f(q)=q-4q^3-2q^5+24q^7-11q^9+... .
$$

For p>3, let

$$
ρbar_{f,p}:Gal(Qbar/Q)→GL_2(𝔽_p)
$$

be the residual representation. For primes ℓ not dividing 8p,

$$
tr ρbar_{f,p}(Frob_ℓ)≡a_ℓ(f) mod p,
$$

and

$$
det ρbar_{f,p}(Frob_ℓ)≡ℓ^3 mod p.
$$

For all but finitely many p, the Ribet-Momose large-image theorem gives an image containing SL_2(𝔽_p). Since the determinant is the third power of the cyclotomic character, the determinant group is

$$
D_p=(𝔽_p^×)^3.
$$

Thus at a nonexceptional p the image is

$$
G_p={g∈GL_2(𝔽_p) : det(g)∈D_p}.
$$

### 8.1 Exact density of trace zero

Fix δ∈𝔽_p^× and let χ_p be the quadratic character. Direct counting gives

$$
#{g∈GL_2(𝔽_p) : tr(g)=0, det(g)=δ}
=
p[p+χ_p(-δ)].
$$

Indeed, a trace-zero matrix has the form

```text
[a   b]
[c  -a]
```

and determinant δ exactly when

$$
bc=-a^2-δ.
$$

For each a, count the pairs (b,c) according as the right side is zero or nonzero.

Every nonzero determinant fiber has size

$$
|SL_2(𝔽_p)|=p(p^2-1).
$$

For p>3, χ_p is nontrivial on the cube subgroup, so

$$
Σ_{δ∈D_p}χ_p(-δ)=0.
$$

It follows that

$$
#{g∈G_p : tr(g)=0}/|G_p|
=
p/(p^2-1).
$$

Chebotarev now gives, for each fixed nonexceptional p,

$$
#{ℓ≤X : ℓ does not divide 8p and a_ℓ(f)≡0 mod p}
\sim
[p/(p^2-1)]Li(X).
$$

Under GRH for the residual extension, effective Chebotarev gives

$$
#{ℓ≤X : a_ℓ(f)≡0 mod p}
=
[p/(p^2-1)]Li(X)
+
O_p(X^{1/2}log X).
$$

The p-dependence in the error hides the degree and discriminant of the residual field, so this is not a useful bound when p grows with X without stronger uniform Chebotarev input.

For a general residue c∈𝔽_p, the maximal-image density is

$$
δ_p(c)
=
[p+|D_p|^{-1}Σ_{δ∈D_p}χ_p(c^2-4δ)]/(p^2-1).
$$

This is close to uniform on the natural scale 1/p.

### 8.2 What MTT and Coleman-Edixhoven say

Mazur-Tate-Teitelbaum concerns p-adic L-functions, interpolation factors, and exceptional zeros in the cyclotomic variable. It does not count primes ℓ with a specified value of a_ℓ(f) modulo p.

Coleman theory studies overconvergent modular forms, U_p slopes, classicality, and p-adic variation in weight. Coleman-Edixhoven gives semisimplicity and related local structure for U_p. These are vertical or p-local results, not horizontal equidistribution as ℓ varies.

The horizontal distribution is supplied by the residual Galois representation and Chebotarev.

### 8.3 Why the modular theorem does not bound Z(p)

The coefficient a_p(f), or the unit root α_p, is one Frobenius scalar. The set S_p contains p possible coefficient indices j. There is no known correspondence

$$
j↦Frob_{ℓ(j)}
$$

that turns b_j mod p into a_{ℓ(j)}(f) mod p. Moreover, a_p(f) is the coefficient at the residual characteristic and is ramified, so it is not covered by the fixed-p, varying-ℓ Chebotarev theorem.

The exact density p/(p^2-1) supports a random 1/p analogy, but it is not a proof about the Apéry coefficient vector.

## 9. Direct answers to the four questions

### Question 1

Dwork unit-root Frobenius does not currently give a nontrivial bound for Z(p). It controls A_p(t) as a function of t, the ordinary locus, and Frobenius-block congruences. It does not control the coefficient support of A_p(t). No O(√p), O(p^{2/3}), or other uniform power-saving estimate is known from this theory.

The unconditional bounds identified here are

$$
Z(p)≤(p-1)/2
$$

and

$$
Z(p)≪p/log p.
$$

### Question 2

No known Coleman or Kedlaya application gives an a priori power-saving bound for |S_p|. Coleman would require a bounded-degree rigid analytic interpolation in j. Kedlaya computes Frobenius in t. Lucas gives exact propagation from S_p but leaves S_p itself undetermined.

### Question 3

There is an exact rational Lucas zeta function, and fixed-modulus finite-state counts have rational ordinary generating functions. These are not Hasse-Weil zeta functions and carry no purity or square-root error term. Dwork rationality for a fixed variety does not apply to the moving digit-word problem. The diagonal modulus p^k is farther still from a fixed geometric object.

### Question 4

For fixed nonexceptional p and varying primes ℓ, Chebotarev gives

$$
dens{ℓ : a_ℓ(f)≡0 mod p}=p/(p^2-1).
$$

MTT and Coleman-Edixhoven do not give this distribution. They concern p-adic L-functions, U_p slopes, and variation in weight. The Chebotarev theorem concerns modular Fourier coefficients rather than Apéry digit coefficients, so it does not bound Z(p).

## 10. Two concrete targets for a new proof

### Target A: an index trace function

Construct a bounded-conductor sheaf whose trace at j∈𝔽_p equals b_j mod p, then prove square-root cancellation in the additive-character sums. This would give

$$
Z(p)=1+O(√p).
$$

### Target B: uniform gap-polynomial nonvanishing

Prove that R_h remains nonzero modulo p, with degree O(h), uniformly for h≤p^θ. Control through h of order p^{1/3} would yield the desired O(p^{2/3}) scale by the gap argument.

Neither target follows from unit-root eigenvalues alone. Both require new arithmetic information about the distinguished coefficient lattice.
