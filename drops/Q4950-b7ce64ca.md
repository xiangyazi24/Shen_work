# Apéry zeros modulo \(p\): what Dwork theory controls, what it does not control, and the modular-form comparison

## Executive verdict

Let

\[
F(t)=\sum_{n\ge 0}b_nt^n,\qquad
A_p(t)=\sum_{j=0}^{p-1}b_jt^j\in\mathbf F_p[t],
\]

and

\[

1. **Dwork’s unit-root theory controls \(A_p(t)\) as a polynomial in the geometric parameter \(t\), not the support of its coefficient vector.** Its roots describe nonordinary/supersingular fibers; \(Z(p)\) counts zero coefficients of \(A_p\). 2. **No published Dwork, Coleman, Kedlaya, or overconvergent-modular-form theorem presently gives a uniform power-saving bound**
   
   \[
   Z(p)=O(p^{1-\delta})\qquad(\delta>0)
   \]

   \[
   Z(p)\le rac{p-1}{2}

## 1. What the Dwork congruence actually says

Gessel’s Lucas theorem gives, for \(0\le r<p\),

\[
oxed{b_{mp+r}\equiv b_mb_r\pmod p.}
	ag{1.1}
\]

At the generating-function level this is

\[
oxed{F(t)\equiv A_p(t)F(t^p)\pmod p.}
	ag{1.2}
\]

Because \(F(t^p)=F(t)^p\) in \(\mathbf F_p[[t]]\), one may also write

\[
A_p(t)F(t)^{p-1}=1\quad	ext{in }\mathbf F_p[[t]].
	ag{1.3}
\]

Dwork’s theory lifts (1.2). On an ordinary residue domain, the unit-root Frobenius is represented by a quotient of periods of the form

\[
rac{F(t)}{F(t^\sigma)},
\]

and the first truncation \(A_p(t)\) is its reduction modulo \(p\). Stronger Apéry supercongruences give higher-\(p\)-power approximations to this quotient.

For the modular motive attached to the weight-four form, the local polynomial at a good prime is

\[
X^2-a_p(f)X+p^3.
	ag{1.4}
\]

At an ordinary prime, \(a_p(f)
ot\equiv0\pmod p\), this polynomial has a \(p\)-adic unit root \(\alpha_p\) and a slope-three root \(eta_p\), with

\[
\alpha_peta_p=p^3,\qquad
\alpha_p\equiv a_p(f)\pmod{p^3}.
	ag{1.5}
\]

Thus, granting the normalization in the question, a congruence such as

\[
b_{np-1}\equiv\gamma(p)b_{n-1}\pmod{p^3}
	ag{1.6}
\]

is exactly the sort of one-block Frobenius congruence supplied by the unit root.

### What (1.6) controls

It controls one special arithmetic progression of indices, or more generally the compatibility of period coefficients across a Frobenius block. It is very strong \(p\)-adically.

### What it does not control

It does not say how many of the \(p\) unrelated residues

\[
b_0,b_1,\ldots,b_{p-1}\pmod p
\]

are zero. A rank-two or rank-three Frobenius matrix has only \(O(1)\) eigenvalues; \(Z(p)\) is a support statistic of a vector of length \(p\).

## 2. Roots in the parameter versus zero coefficients

For a family of varieties, \(A_p(t)\) is a Hasse invariant. At a point \(t_0\), the condition

\[
A_p(t_0)
e0
\]

usually characterizes the ordinary locus on which the unit root exists analytically. The roots of \(A_p(t)\) in \(\overline{\mathbf F}_p\) therefore encode exceptional geometric fibers.

But

\[
Z(p)=\#\{j:[t^j]A_p(t)=0\}
\]

is a coefficient-support statistic. Root geometry and coefficient support are almost independent. Two elementary examples over \(\mathbf F_p\) make the distinction vivid:

\[
1+t+\cdots+t^{p-1}=(t-1)^{p-1}
\]

has no zero coefficients and only one geometric root, while

\[
t^{p-1}-1
\]

has only two nonzero coefficients and \(p-1\) distinct nonzero roots.

Accordingly, even a complete description of the Newton slopes, the unit-root eigenvalue, and the supersingular locus does not give a coefficient-zero bound.

A recent result makes the point especially clearly. Caruso–Fürnsinn–Vargas-Montoya–Zudilin prove that the Apéry truncation has the form

\[
A_p(t)=B_p(t)^2
\]

for \(p\equiv1,5,7,11\pmod{24}\), and

\[
A_p(t)=(t^2-34t+1)B_p(t)^2
\]

for \(p\equiv13,17,19,23\pmod{24}\). This is strong information about the algebraic function and its Galois group. It still gives no useful bound for the number of zero coefficients: squaring a polynomial can create extensive coefficient cancellation.

### A useful no-go principle

Frobenius eigenvalues are invariants of the isocrystal. The Taylor coefficient vector of a chosen horizontal period is not: it changes under a change of local frame or multiplication of the period by an analytic unit. Therefore no estimate for \(Z(p)\) can follow from the Frobenius eigenvalues **alone**. A successful theorem must also use the special integral lattice and the distinguished holomorphic period at \(t=0\).

## 3. What would turn Dwork theory into an \(O(\sqrt p)\) bound?

One possible route would be to realize the index function

\[
j\longmapsto b_j\pmod p,\qquad j\in\mathbf F_p,
\]

as a bounded-conductor trace function of an \(\ell\)-adic or \(p\)-adic sheaf on the **index line**. Then additive-character orthogonality would give

\[
Z(p)=rac1p\sum_{a\in\mathbf F_p}\sum_{j\in\mathbf F_p}\psi(ab_j).
	ag{3.1}
\]

If one could prove uniformly for \(a
e0\) that

\[
\left|\sum_{j\in\mathbf F_p}\psi(ab_j)ight|\ll\sqrt p,
	ag{3.2}
\]

then

\[
Z(p)=1+O(\sqrt p).
\]

This is exactly the sort of estimate that Deligne’s Riemann-hypothesis theorem supplies for bounded-conductor trace functions.

The present Picard–Fuchs crystal, however, is a sheaf in the geometric variable \(t\). It does not provide a trace-function description of the coefficient index \(j\mapsto b_j\). Passing from a period in \(t\) to its \(j\)-th Taylor coefficient is a coefficient-extraction operation, not evaluation of the same sheaf at \(j\).

Thus (3.2) identifies the missing structure precisely, but no such bounded-conductor index sheaf is presently known for the Apéry numbers.

## 4. Unconditional deterministic bounds for \(Z(p)\)

Assume \(p\ge5\).

### 4.1 No consecutive zeros

If

\[
b_m\equiv b_{m+1}\equiv0\pmod p
\]

with \(m+1<p\), the recurrence propagates backwards and forces \(b_0\equiv0\), contradicting \(b_0=1\). Also

\[
b_{p-1}\equiv1\pmod p.
\]

Hence zeros in \(0,\ldots,p-1\) are nonconsecutive and avoid both endpoints, giving

\[
oxed{Z(p)\le\left\lfloorrac{p-1}{2}ightfloor.}
	ag{4.1}
\]

### 4.2 A gap-polynomial bound \(Z(p)\ll p/\log p\)

Put

\[
P(X)=34X^3+51X^2+27X+5.
\]

Define

\[
R_0(X)=0,\qquad R_1(X)=1,
\]

and for \(h\ge1\),

\[
oxed{
R_{h+1}(X)=P(X+h)R_h(X)-(X+h)^6R_{h-1}(X).}
	ag{4.2}
\]

Suppose \(j<j+h<p\) are consecutive zero positions. Since \(b_{j+1}
e0\), divide the recurrence by \(b_{j+1}\) and clear the denominator

\[
\prod_{r=2}^{h}(j+r)^3.
\]

One obtains

\[
oxed{R_h(j)\equiv0\pmod p.}
	ag{4.3}
\]

Moreover,

\[
\deg R_h\le3(h-1).
	ag{4.4}
\]

Let \(s_h\) be the coefficient of \(X^{3(h-1)}\). Comparing leading terms in (4.2) gives

\[
s_0=0,\qquad s_1=1,\qquad
oxed{s_{h+1}=34s_h-s_{h-1}.}
	ag{4.5}
\]

In particular,

\[
0<s_h\le34^{h-1}.
	ag{4.6}
\]

Choose

\[
H=\left\lfloorrac{\log p}{2\log34}ightfloor.
	ag{4.7}
\]

For \(2\le h\le H\), the leading coefficient \(s_h\) lies strictly between \(0\) and \(p\), so \(R_hmod p\) is not the zero polynomial. Therefore the number of zero gaps of a fixed length \(h\le H\) is at most

\[
\deg R_h\le3(h-1).
\]

The total number of short gaps is \(O(H^2)\). Every remaining gap has length \(>H\), so the number of long gaps is at most \(p/H\). Consequently

\[
Z(p)-1\le O(H^2)+rac pH,
\]

and hence

\[
oxed{Z(p)\llrac p{\log p}.}
	ag{4.8}
\]

This is \(o(p)\), but it is not a power saving.

### Why the explicit \(h=2,3,4,5\) computations do not prove \(O(p^{2/3})\)

To derive \(Z(p)=O(p^{2/3})\) by this gap method, one would need to know that \(R_hmod p\) is nonzero uniformly for all

\[
h\le c\,p^{1/3}
\]

(or another comparably long range), while retaining \(\deg R_h=O(h)\). Computations for four fixed values of \(h\) only exclude finitely many short gaps. Dwork’s unit-root theory presently gives no uniform nonvanishing theorem for the contents or leading coefficients of this moving family \(R_h\) up to \(h\asymp p^{1/3}\).

## 5. Lucas factorization gives exact digit statistics

Write

\[
n=n_0+n_1p+\cdots+n_{k-1}p^{k-1},\qquad0\le n_i<p.
\]

Gessel’s theorem gives

\[
b_n\equiv\prod_{i=0}^{k-1}b_{n_i}\pmod p.
	ag{5.1}
\]

Therefore

\[
b_n
ot\equiv0\pmod p
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
n_i
otin S_p\ 	ext{for every }i.
\]

There are exactly \(p-Z(p)\) allowed digits, so

\[
oxed{\#\{0\le n<p^k:b_n
ot\equiv0\pmod p\}=(p-Z(p))^k,}
	ag{5.2}
\]

and

\[
oxed{\#\{0\le n<p^k:b_n\equiv0\pmod p\}=p^k-(p-Z(p))^k.}
	ag{5.3}
\]

The zero proportion is thus

\[
1-\left(1-rac{Z(p)}pight)^k.
	ag{5.4}
\]

This formula is exact. It also shows why Lucas theory cannot bound \(Z(p)\): the one-digit set \(S_p\) is the free input from which all higher-digit statistics are generated.

Malik–Straub prove the additional reflection symmetry

\[
b_j\equiv b_{p-1-j}\pmod p.
	ag{5.5}
\]

Their random-residue model predicts paired zeros with an asymptotic Poisson law of mean \(1/2\), and hence

\[
\Pr(Z(p)=0)\longrightarrow e^{-1/2}.
\]

This is a compelling heuristic, not a theorem. Even the infinitude of primes with \(Z(p)=0\) remains open.

## 6. Why Coleman and Kedlaya do not currently bound \(|S_p|\)

### Coleman’s zero-counting tools

Strassmann’s theorem and Coleman’s rigid-analytic methods can bound the number of zeros of a **single rigid analytic function** on a \(p\)-adic residue disk when its Newton polygon or Weierstrass degree is controlled.

To apply this here one would need a rigid analytic interpolation

\[
B_p(x)\quad (x\in\mathbf Z_p)
\]

with

\[
B_p(j)\equiv b_j\pmod p,\qquad0\le j<p,
\]

and a Weierstrass degree bounded independently of \(p\). No such interpolation with controlled degree is known. The base-\(p\) digit behavior supplied by Lucas is finite-state and highly nonanalytic in the index; it is not the same as the rigid-analytic dependence of a period on the geometric parameter \(t\).

### Kedlaya’s algorithms

Kedlaya-style algorithms compute Frobenius matrices, slopes, and zeta functions of varieties or overconvergent \(F\)-isocrystals. They can efficiently recover \(A_p(t)\) or local Frobenius data. They do not turn a bound on cohomological dimension into a sparsity theorem for the \(p\) Taylor coefficients of \(A_p(t)\).

Algorithmically one may compute \(S_p\) by generating \(b_0,\ldots,b_{p-1}mod p\). What is missing is not computability but a structural estimate uniform in \(p\).

## 7. A “zeta function” for the Lucas zero language

Let

\[
N_k=\#\{0\le n<p^k:b_n\equiv0\pmod p\}.
\]

By (5.3),

\[
N_k=p^k-(p-Z(p))^k.
\]

Consequently the exponential generating zeta is

\[
egin{aligned}
\mathcal Z_{p,\mathrm{Lucas}}(T)
&=\exp\left(\sum_{k\ge1}rac{N_k}{k}T^kight)\\
&=\exp\left(\sum_{k\ge1}rac{p^k-(p-Z(p))^k}{k}T^kight)\\
&=oxed{rac{1-(p-Z(p))T}{1-pT}}.
\end{aligned}
	ag{7.1}
\]

This rationality is completely elementary. Its two “eigenvalues” are \(p\) and \(p-Z(p)\). There is no analogue of Weil’s weight constraint forcing an error of size \(p^{k/2}\); indeed the unknown \(Z(p)\) appears directly as an eigenvalue gap.

### Higher congruence moduli

For fixed \(r\), Beukers’s \(p\)-linear-scheme machinery gives a finite-state description of Apéry numbers modulo \(p^r\). If

\[
N_{p,r}(k)=\#\{0\le n<p^k:b_n\equiv0\pmod{p^r}\},
\]

then accepted-word counts are obtained from a finite transition matrix \(M_{p,r}\):

\[
N_{p,r}(k)=u^{\mathsf T}M_{p,r}^{\,k}v.
	ag{7.2}
\]

Hence the ordinary generating function

\[
\sum_{k\ge0}N_{p,r}(k)T^k
=u^{\mathsf T}(I-TM_{p,r})^{-1}v
	ag{7.3}
\]

is rational for each fixed \(p,r\).

Again, this is automata rationality. The transition matrix has row sums bounded by \(p\), so its eigenvalues are only bounded trivially by \(p\); there is no purity theorem or square-root spectral gap.

If one asks for the diagonal condition

\[
b_n\equiv0\pmod{p^k}\quad	ext{with }0\le n<p^k,
\]

the modulus and the automaton both change with \(k\). Fixed-\(r\) rationality does not give rationality or a Hasse–Weil estimate for this diagonal sequence.

## 8. Why Dwork’s rationality theorem is not the relevant theorem

For a fixed variety \(X/\mathbf F_q\), Dwork’s rationality theorem concerns

\[
Z(X,T)=\exp\left(\sum_{m\ge1}rac{\#X(\mathbf F_{q^m})}{m}T^might).
\]

The points over \(\mathbf F_{q^m}\) are related functorially by Frobenius, and étale/crystalline cohomology supplies eigenvalues with Weil weights.

The set

\[
\{0\le n<p^k:b_n\equiv0\pmod{p^r}\}
\]

is instead a language of base-\(p\) digit words. There is no canonical identification of its words with \(\mathbf F_{p^k}\)-points of a fixed variety compatible with field Frobenius. Its rationality, when available, comes from a finite automaton, not from a fixed cohomology group.

A related subtlety is Dwork’s unit-root \(L\)-function conjecture. For ordinary families, the unit-root part need not be a rational function; the theorem proved in this direction is generally **\(p\)-adic meromorphy** of the unit-root \(L\)-function. This still concerns Frobenius over closed points of a geometric base, not coefficient-index zeros.

Thus there is presently no Hasse–Weil analogue that yields a bound for \(Z(p)\).

Thus the exact density \(p/(p^2-1)\) is a useful analogy for the random \(1/p\) heuristic, but it is not a proof about Apéry coefficient zeros.

## 10. Direct answers to the four questions

### (1) Can unit-root Frobenius bound \(Z(p)\)?

**Not with currently known theory.** It controls the Hasse polynomial as a function of \(t\), the ordinary locus, and Frobenius-block congruences such as the stated ASD relation. It does not control how many coefficients of \(A_p(t)\) vanish. No uniform \(O(\sqrt p)\), \(O(p^{2/3})\), or other power-saving estimate for \(Z(p)\) is presently known from Dwork theory.

The best unconditional statements identified here are

\[
Z(p)\lerac{p-1}{2}
\]

and the elementary

\[
Z(p)\ll p/\log p.
\]

### (2) Can Coleman or Kedlaya bound \(|S_p|\)?

**No known application does so.** Coleman would require a bounded-degree rigid analytic interpolation in the index \(j\); Kedlaya computes the geometric Frobenius in \(t\). Lucas gives exact propagation from \(S_p\), but leaves the one-digit set itself undetermined.

### (3) Is there a Hasse–Weil-like zeta function?

There is an exact rational **automaton/Lucas** zeta, equation (7.1), and fixed-\(p^r\) finite-state counts have rational ordinary generating functions. These are not Hasse–Weil zeta functions and carry no purity or square-root error term. Dwork’s rationality theorem for fixed varieties does not apply to the moving digit-word problem; the diagonal modulus \(p^k\) is even farther from a fixed geometric object.

### (4) What is known for \(f=8.4.a.a\)?

For fixed nonexceptional \(p\) and varying primes \(\ell\), Chebotarev gives the exact trace-zero density

\[
rac{p}{p^2-1}.
\]

MTT and Coleman–Edixhoven do not give this distribution; their subject is \(p\)-adic \(L\)-functions, \(U_p\)-slopes, and variation in weight. The Chebotarev theorem concerns modular Fourier coefficients, not the Apéry digit coefficients, and therefore does not bound \(Z(p)\).

## 11. What a genuine new proof would need

There are two plausible concrete targets.

### Target A: an index trace function

Construct a bounded-conductor sheaf whose trace at \(j\in\mathbf F_p\) is \(b_jmod p\), then prove the character-sum estimate (3.2). This would yield \(Z(p)=1+O(\sqrt p)\).

### Target B: uniform gap-polynomial nonvanishing

Prove that the recurrence polynomials \(R_h\) in (4.2) remain nonzero modulo \(p\), with controlled degree, uniformly for \(h\le p^	heta\). The elementary gap argument would then give a power saving. For example, control through \(h\asymp p^{1/3}\) would produce the desired \(O(p^{2/3})\)-scale bound.

Neither target follows from the unit-root eigenvalues alone. Both require new arithmetic information about the distinguished coefficient lattice.
