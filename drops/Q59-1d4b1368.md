# Universal-pair budget after the exact converse fails

## Executive verdict

Let

\[
Q_n=(a_n,b_n)\in\mathbf F_p^2,
\qquad 0\le n<p,
\]

and let

\[
U_p=\#\bigl\{\{n,m\}:0\le n<m<p,\ Q_n=Q_m\bigr\}.
\]

Palindromy gives the forced contribution \((p-1)/2\), but the equality

\[
U_p=(p-1)/2
\]

is false. The corrected weak target

\[
\boxed{U_p=O(p)}
\]

remains open.

What is rigorously available now is the following.

1. **Exact per-gap resultant bound.** After removing the forced reflection factor, if
   \[
   R_h=\operatorname{Res}(F_h,G_h)\ne0,
   \]
   then the number of nonforced common roots modulo \(p\) is at most
   \[
   \deg\gcd(\overline F_h,\overline G_h)
   \le \min\{3h+O(1),v_p(R_h)\}.
   \]
   This is a Smith-normal-form/subresultant statement, stronger than the indicator \(1_{p\mid R_h}\).

2. **Best clean pointwise bound currently obtainable from the established gap machinery:**
   \[
   \boxed{U_p\ll p^{3/2}.}
   \]
   The proof uses block compression of equal-state fibers together with the degree bound for the single continuant \(N_h\). The second equation does not presently improve the exponent because no uniform bound for the modular gcd degree is known.

3. **Resultants do give bounded mean for a truncated short-gap range, not for all gaps.** If \(P<p\le2P\), \(H\le P/2\), and
   \[
   \log|R_h|\ll h^2\log(2h),
   \]
   then
   \[
   \frac{1}{\pi(2P)-\pi(P)}
   \sum_{P<p\le2P} E_p(H)
   \ll \frac{H^3\log(2H)}{P}.
   \]
   Thus the mean is \(O(1)\) for
   \[
   H\ll (P/\log P)^{1/3}.
   \]
   This does **not** prove \(E_p=O(1)\) on average for the full range \(h\le p/2\).

4. Combining the short-gap resultant mean with a block argument gives the unconditional dyadic average
   \[
   \boxed{
   \operatorname*{Avg}_{P<p\le2P} E_p
   \ll P^{4/3}(\log P)^{1/3}.
   }
   \]
   This is an improvement over the pointwise \(p^{3/2}\) scale, but is still far from the observed constant mean \(1/8\).

5. **The persistence \(73\mid R_3,R_4,R_5,\ldots\) is now explained.** It is not propagation of the in-window pair \(Q_1=Q_4\) to every later gap. It comes primarily from boundary/wrap roots. Since \(73\mid b_2\), every \(h\ge4\) has a common boundary root
   \[
   x=-(h-2)\pmod{73},
   \]
   corresponding to a segment that wraps to index \(2\), outside the admissible interval \(0\le x<x+h<p\).

6. The clean load-bearing lemma is therefore not a statement about how often \(p\mid R_h\). It is the **boundary-saturated total gcd-degree estimate**
   \[
   \boxed{
   \sum_{1\le h\le(p-1)/2}
   \deg D^{\circ}_{p,h}\ll p,
   }
   \]
   where \(D^{\circ}_{p,h}\) is the common factor of \(N_h,L_h\) after removing reflection and boundary factors. The stronger observed statement
   \[
   \deg D^{\circ}_{p,h}=O(1)
   \]
   uniformly in \(p,h\) would immediately prove \(U_p=O(p)\).

7. A pointwise bound \(E_p=O(1)\) for every prime is not predicted by the Poisson\((1/8)\) model. The model predicts an unbounded, very slowly growing maximum, of order roughly
   \[
   \frac{\log\pi(X)}{\log\log\pi(X)}
   \]
   among primes \(p\le X\). The theorem needed by Proposition A is only \(E_p=O(p)\), which is vastly weaker.

The rest of this note gives complete statements and proofs.

# 1. Orbit bookkeeping: forced pairs versus base collisions

Put

\[
M=\frac{p-1}{2}.
\]

Palindromy says

\[
Q_{p-1-n}=Q_n.
\]

Use the \(M+1\) representatives

\[
0,1,\ldots,M.
\]

For a value \(v\in\mathbf F_p^2\), define

\[
\mu_v=\#\{0\le r\le M:Q_r=v\}.
\]

The number of **base collisions** is

\[
B_p:=\sum_v\binom{\mu_v}{2}
=\#\{0\le r<s\le M:Q_r=Q_s\}.
\tag{1.1}
\]

Let

\[
B_p^{\mathrm{mid}}
=\#\{0\le r<M:Q_r=Q_M\}
\]

count collisions involving the fixed midpoint orbit. Every collision between two nonmidpoint orbits creates four unordered pairs among the full \(p\) indices; a collision with the midpoint creates two. Therefore

\[
\boxed{
U_p=M+4B_p-2B_p^{\mathrm{mid}}.
}
\tag{1.2}
\]

In particular,

\[
M+2B_p\le U_p\le M+4B_p.
\tag{1.3}
\]

Thus

\[
U_p=O(p)\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad B_p=O(p).
\tag{1.4}
\]

For \(1\le h\le M\), put

\[
c_p(h)
=\#\{0\le x\le M-h:Q_{x+h}=Q_x\}.
\tag{1.5}
\]

Then every base pair has one gap, so

\[
\boxed{B_p=\sum_{h=1}^{M}c_p(h).}
\tag{1.6}
\]

This is the exact quantity to bound. It avoids the fourfold palindromic bookkeeping and automatically excludes the forced reflection pair.

# 2. The continuant pair and the primitive resultant

The established continuants satisfy

\[
N_{h+1}(x)
=P(x+h)N_h(x)-(x+h)^6N_{h-1}(x),
\qquad N_0=0,\quad N_1=1,
\tag{2.1}
\]

with

\[
\deg N_h=3(h-1).
\]

Write

\[
\mathcal Q_h(x)=\prod_{j=2}^{h}(x+j)^3
\tag{2.2}
\]

and

\[
L_h(x)
=\mathcal Q_h(x)+(x+1)^3N_{h-1}(x+1).
\tag{2.3}
\]

The project’s transfer-matrix calculation gives

\[
Q_{x+h}=Q_x
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
N_h(x)=L_h(x)=0
\tag{2.4}
\]

whenever the segment is nonsingular.

Reflection gives

\[
N_h(-x-h-1)=(-1)^{h-1}N_h(x),
\]

and the analogous relation for \(L_h\). For even \(h\), both polynomials therefore contain the forced factor

\[
C_h(x)=2x+h+1.
\]

Put

\[
C_h(x)=
\begin{cases}
2x+h+1,&h\text{ even},\\
1,&h\text{ odd},
\end{cases}
\tag{2.5}
\]

and let \(F_h,G_h\in\mathbf Z[x]\) be the primitive parts of

\[
N_h/C_h,\qquad L_h/C_h.
\]

Define

\[
R_h=\operatorname{Res}(F_h,G_h)\in\mathbf Z\setminus\{0\}.
\tag{2.6}
\]

The factor \(C_h\) removes the universal palindrome root, but it does **not** remove the boundary/wrap roots discussed below. Thus “primitive resultant” here means reflection-primitive, not collision-primitive.

# 3. Exact resultant/subresultant bound

For a prime \(p\), let

\[
d_{p,h}
=\deg\gcd_{\mathbf F_p[x]}(\overline F_h,\overline G_h).
\tag{3.1}
\]

## Proposition 3.1 — modular gcd degree is bounded by the resultant valuation

For every prime \(p\),

\[
\boxed{
 d_{p,h}\le v_p(R_h).
}
\tag{3.2}
\]

Consequently,

\[
\boxed{
 c_p(h)\le d_{p,h}\le
 \min\{\deg F_h,\deg G_h,v_p(R_h)\}
 \le \min\{3h+O(1),v_p(R_h)\}.
}
\tag{3.3}
\]

### Proof

Let \(S_h\) be the integer Sylvester matrix of \(F_h,G_h\), padded using their degrees over \(\mathbf Z\). Then

\[
\det S_h=\pm R_h.
\]

Over a field, the degree of the polynomial gcd is at most the nullity of the corresponding padded Sylvester matrix. Put \(S_h\) in Smith normal form over \(\mathbf Z\):

\[
US_hV=\operatorname{diag}(s_1,\ldots,s_D),
\qquad s_i\mid s_{i+1}.
\]

The nullity modulo \(p\) is the number of invariant factors divisible by \(p\). Every such invariant factor contributes at least one to

\[
v_p(\det S_h)=\sum_i v_p(s_i)=v_p(R_h).
\]

Therefore

\[
\deg\gcd(\overline F_h,\overline G_h)
\le\operatorname{nullity}(S_h\bmod p)
\le v_p(R_h).
\]

Every admissible common root contributes to this gcd, giving (3.3). ∎

This is the cleanest rigorous form of the resultant method. It is stronger than

\[
c_p(h)\le 3h\,\mathbf1_{p\mid R_h},
\]

because it accounts for the actual \(p\)-adic rank defect.

## Corollary 3.2 — explicit pointwise resultant bound

One has

\[
\boxed{
B_p
\le
\sum_{h=1}^{M}
\min\{3h+O(1),v_p(R_h)\}.
}
\tag{3.4}
\]

Together with (1.2), this gives an explicit unconditional upper bound for \(U_p\).

However, its worst-case size is still \(O(p^2)\). A fixed prime may divide many \(R_h\), and the raw family \(R_h\) is in fact designed to have such persistent divisibility because of boundary roots.

## Gap \(h=2\)

For \(h=2\), the reduced pair has a Bézout identity with constant \(12\). Hence for every \(p\ge5\),

\[
\gcd(\overline F_2,\overline G_2)=1,
\qquad c_p(2)=0.
\tag{3.5}
\]

This is a genuine exceptionless theorem. It does not presently extend uniformly to all \(h\).

A standard reference for the relation between Sylvester rank, subresultants, and modular gcd degree is the subresultant formalism; for a \(p\)-adic treatment see Xavier Caruso, [*Resultants and subresultants of p-adic polynomials*](https://arxiv.org/abs/1507.06502).

# 4. Why Cauchy--Schwarz does not automatically improve the bound

The two-equation information is exactly the sequence

\[
d_{p,h}=\deg\gcd(\overline F_h,\overline G_h).
\]

One has

\[
B_p\le\sum_{h\le M}d_{p,h}.
\tag{4.1}
\]

Cauchy--Schwarz gives

\[
B_p^2
\le M\sum_{h\le M}d_{p,h}^2.
\tag{4.2}
\]

This is useful only after proving a genuinely new second-moment estimate for the modular gcd degrees. The inequality itself does not save anything: the worst-case bounds \(d_{p,h}\ll h\) give a right side of order \(p^4\).

Likewise, replacing \(d_{p,h}\) by \(v_p(R_h)\) merely turns the problem into

\[
\sum_{h\le M}v_p(R_h),
\]

which can be large because of the cumulative boundary factors. The two-equation structure has therefore isolated the correct quantity, but has not yet supplied the needed cancellation.

# 5. Best clean pointwise theorem currently provable: \(U_p\ll p^{3/2}\)

The following bound uses only the already established single-continuant root estimate. It is stronger than the raw \(O(p^2)\) count and appears to be the best clean pointwise theorem available without a new gcd lemma.

## Theorem 5.1

For every prime \(p\ge5\),

\[
\boxed{B_p\ll p^{3/2},\qquad U_p\ll p^{3/2}.}
\tag{5.1}
\]

### Proof

Fix \(1\le H\le M+1\), and partition the representative interval

\[
\{0,1,\ldots,M\}
\]

into

\[
J=\left\lceil\frac{M+1}{H}\right\rceil
\]

consecutive blocks of length at most \(H\).

For each state value \(v\), let \(t_{v,j}\) be its multiplicity in block \(j\). The number \(B_p(H)\) of base collisions with gap \(<H\) contains every pair lying in one block, so

\[
B_p(H)
\ge\sum_{v,j}\binom{t_{v,j}}2
=\frac12\left(\sum_{v,j}t_{v,j}^2-(M+1)\right).
\tag{5.2}
\]

For each \(v\), Cauchy--Schwarz across the \(J\) blocks gives

\[
\sum_j t_{v,j}^2\ge\frac{\mu_v^2}{J}.
\]

Since

\[
\sum_v\mu_v^2=(M+1)+2B_p,
\]

we obtain

\[
B_p(H)
\ge
\frac{M+1+2B_p}{2J}-\frac{M+1}{2}.
\tag{5.3}
\]

Equivalently,

\[
B_p
\le
J B_p(H)+\frac{J-1}{2}(M+1).
\tag{5.4}
\]

If \(Q_{x+h}=Q_x\), then in particular \(N_h(x)=0\). For \(h<H\ll\sqrt p\), the nonzero polynomial \(N_h\bmod p\) has degree \(3(h-1)\), hence

\[
B_p(H)
\le\sum_{h=1}^{H-1}3(h-1)
\ll H^2.
\tag{5.5}
\]

Using

\[
J\ll p/H+1,
\]

(5.4) gives

\[
B_p\ll pH+\frac{p^2}{H}+p.
\tag{5.6}
\]

Choose \(H\asymp\sqrt p\). Then

\[
B_p\ll p^{3/2}.
\]

Finally use (1.2). ∎

This proof also explains precisely what a uniform two-equation theorem would buy. If instead one knew

\[
B_p(H)\ll H
\]

uniformly up to \(H\asymp p\), then (5.4) would give \(B_p\ll p\).

# 6. What the resultant height proves on average

Assume the project’s coefficient-height estimate

\[
\log|R_h|\ll h^2\log(2h).
\tag{6.1}
\]

For \(P<p\le2P\), define the truncated base collision count

\[
B_p(H)=\sum_{1\le h\le H}c_p(h),
\qquad H\le P/2.
\tag{6.2}
\]

## Theorem 6.1 — weighted short-gap average

One has

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{P<p\le2P}B_p(H)\log p
\le
\sum_{h\le H}\log|R_h|
\ll H^3\log(2H).
}
\tag{6.3}
\]

### Proof

By Proposition 3.1,

\[
c_p(h)\le v_p(R_h).
\]

Therefore

\[
\sum_p c_p(h)\log p
\le\sum_p v_p(R_h)\log p
\le\log|R_h|.
\]

Sum over \(h\le H\). ∎

Consequently,

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{P<p\le2P}B_p(H)
\ll
\frac{H^3\log(2H)}{\log P}.
}
\tag{6.4}
\]

Using

\[
\pi(2P)-\pi(P)\asymp P/\log P,
\]

we get

\[
\boxed{
\operatorname*{Avg}_{P<p\le2P} B_p(H)
\ll
\frac{H^3\log(2H)}{P}.
}
\tag{6.5}
\]

Thus the truncated mean is \(O(1)\) when

\[
H^3\log H\ll P,
\]

for example

\[
H\ll(P/\log P)^{1/3}.
\tag{6.6}
\]

More generally, Markov gives

\[
\#\{P<p\le2P:B_p(H)\ge A\}
\ll
\frac{H^3\log(2H)}{A\log P}.
\tag{6.7}
\]

## Important correction

Setting \(H\asymp P\) in (6.4) gives a total bound of order \(P^3\), hence no bounded full-range mean. The fact that each fixed \(R_h\) has finitely many prime divisors is not uniform in \(h\), and \(h\) grows with \(p\).

Therefore the height estimate does **not** prove

\[
\operatorname*{Avg}_{p\le X}E_p=O(1)
\]

for the complete universal-pair statistic. It proves this only for the short-gap truncation (6.6).

# 7. A full-range average consequence: \(P^{4/3}(\log P)^{1/3}\)

The short-gap average can be combined with the block inequality (5.4).

For \(p\asymp P\),

\[
B_p
\ll
\frac{P}{H}B_p(H)+\frac{P^2}{H}+P.
\tag{7.1}
\]

Summing over \(P<p\le2P\), applying (6.4), and dividing by the number of primes gives

\[
\operatorname*{Avg}_{P<p\le2P}B_p
\ll
H^2\log(2H)+\frac{P^2}{H}+P.
\tag{7.2}
\]

Choose

\[
H\asymp(P^2/\log P)^{1/3}.
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{
\operatorname*{Avg}_{P<p\le2P}B_p
\ll
P^{4/3}(\log P)^{1/3}.
}
\tag{7.3}
\]

By (1.2), the same bound, with the forced \(O(P)\) term added, holds for \(U_p\).

This is a genuine unconditional average improvement over the pointwise \(p^{3/2}\) estimate, but it is still many orders of magnitude above the observed Poisson\((1/8)\) mean.

# 8. Long gaps: what reflection does and does not do

A base collision uses representatives

\[
0\le r<s\le M.
\]

Its primary gap is

\[
h=s-r\le M.
\tag{8.1}
\]

The full palindromic quartet is

\[
\{r,s,p-1-s,p-1-r\}.
\]

Besides the two pairs of gap \(h\), the cross pairs have gap

\[
g=p-1-r-s.
\tag{8.2}
\]

Thus an extra orbit collision appears with two natural gap parameters \(h,g\), and

\[
\min(h,g)\le M.
\]

However, both can lie in

\[
(p/3,p/2].
\]

For example, taking \(r\) near \(0.05p\) and \(s\) near \(0.45p\) makes both gaps approximately \(0.4p\)--\(0.5p\). Therefore reflection does not reduce every collision to \(h\le p/3\).

The resultant criterion itself remains valid for all

\[
h\le M<p.
\]

There is no algebraic breakdown when

\[
\deg N_h=3(h-1)>p.
\]

A high-degree polynomial over \(\mathbf F_p\) may vanish at every field element without being the zero polynomial, but the statements

\[
\text{common root}\Rightarrow p\mid R_h
\]

and

\[
\#\text{ common roots}\le\deg\gcd(\overline F_h,\overline G_h)
\le v_p(R_h)
\]

still hold. What fails is only the usefulness of the bare degree bound. The honest long-gap estimate is

\[
 c_p(h)
 \le
 \min\{M-h+1,\deg\gcd(\overline F_h,\overline G_h),v_p(R_h)\}.
\tag{8.3}
\]

The range

\[
p/3<h\le p/2
\]

is therefore a genuine part of the open problem.

# 9. Why \(73\) divides many consecutive \(R_h\)

The raw resultant family has a systematic boundary contamination that makes repeated divisibility unavoidable.

For \(2\le a\le h\), splitting the continuant at the singular point \(x=-a\) gives

\[
\boxed{
N_h(-a)
=(-1)^{a-1}((a-1)!)^3 b_{a-1}\,((h-a)!)^3b_{h-a}.
}
\tag{9.1}
\]

Since

\[
L_h(x)=\mathcal Q_h(x)+(x+1)^3N_{h-1}(x+1)
\]

and \(\mathcal Q_h(-a)=0\), the same formula yields

\[
\boxed{
L_h(-a)
=(-1)^{a-1}((a-1)!)^3 b_{a-2}\,((h-a)!)^3b_{h-a}.
}
\tag{9.2}
\]

Assume \(p>h\), as in the relevant range \(h\le(p-1)/2\). The factorials are units modulo \(p\). Consecutive Apéry values cannot both vanish modulo \(p\). Therefore

\[
N_h(-a)\equiv L_h(-a)\equiv0\pmod p
\]

occurs precisely through the common factor

\[
b_{h-a}\equiv0\pmod p
\]

apart from the impossible consecutive-zero alternative.

Equivalently, every zero

\[
j\in Z_p,\qquad 1\le j\le h-2,
\]

creates the common boundary root

\[
\boxed{x=-(h-j).}
\tag{9.3}
\]

In its standard representative modulo \(p\), this is

\[
x=p-h+j,
\]

and

\[
x+h=p+j>p-1.
\]

So it is outside the admissible nonwrapping interval.

## The prime \(73\)

Since

\[
b_2=73,
\]

for every \(h\ge4\), choose \(a=h-2\). Then

\[
N_h(-(h-2))\equiv L_h(-(h-2))\equiv0\pmod{73}.
\tag{9.4}
\]

Thus

\[
73\mid R_h\qquad(h\ge4)
\]

through a moving boundary root. At \(h=4\) the root is \(x=-2\equiv71\); at \(h=5\) it is \(x=-3\equiv70\); and so on. Each segment wraps to the fixed bottom index \(2\).

The factor \(73^2\mid R_3\) reflects the actual in-window pair \(Q_1=Q_4\) and its reflection. The later divisibilities do **not** mean that there is a gap-\(4\), gap-\(5\), etc. universal pair in the admissible interval. The increasing valuations record local intersection multiplicities and accumulated boundary defects.

This explains the apparent “propagation”:

\[
\boxed{
\text{one zero }b_j\equiv0
\text{ contaminates every raw }R_h\text{ with }h\ge j+2.
}
\tag{9.5}
\]

Therefore

\[
\#\{h\le p/2:p\mid R_h\}
\]

can be of order \(p\) even when there is only one primitive in-window collision. Bounding this cardinality is the wrong target.

# 10. Boundary saturation: the correct resultant object

Let

\[
S_h(x)=\prod_{a=2}^{h}(x+a).
\tag{10.1}
\]

After the reflection factor has been removed, put

\[
D_{p,h}=\gcd(\overline F_h,\overline G_h).
\]

Define its boundary-saturated part by removing every factor supported on \(S_h=0\):

\[
D^{\circ}_{p,h}
=
\frac{D_{p,h}}
{\gcd(D_{p,h},S_h^\infty)}.
\tag{10.2}
\]

Every base collision lies away from the boundary, so

\[
\boxed{c_p(h)\le\deg D^{\circ}_{p,h}.}
\tag{10.3}
\]

The exact theorem sufficient for Lemma U is

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{h=1}^{M}\deg D^{\circ}_{p,h}\ll p.
}
\tag{U-sat}
\]

A stronger but very concrete sufficient theorem is

\[
\boxed{
\deg D^{\circ}_{p,h}\le C
\quad\text{for all }p,h.
}
\tag{U-unif}
\]

The experiments reported in the project give degrees at most \(2\) at the contaminated primes tested.

One can define an integral saturated eliminant by the Rabinowitsch trick. Consider

\[
I_h=(F_h(x),G_h(x),1-yS_h(x))\subset\mathbf Z[x,y].
\tag{10.4}
\]

Any nonzero integer in

\[
I_h\cap\mathbf Z
\]

is divisible by every prime for which \(F_h,G_h\) have a nonboundary common root over \(\overline{\mathbf F}_p\). Computing these saturated eliminants is more faithful than factoring the raw \(R_h\). Their height alone will probably still not prove the full theorem, but their gcd and primitive-divisor structure is the correct experiment.

# 11. The contiguous Bézout identity: genuine progress toward the uniform gcd bound

The proved contiguous identity is

\[
\boxed{
N_h(x+1)N_h(x)
-N_{h-1}(x+1)N_{h+1}(x)
=\mathcal Q_h(x)^2.
}
\tag{11.1}
\]

Consequently, away from the boundary \(\mathcal Q_h(x)=0\), the polynomials

\[
N_h(x),\qquad N_{h+1}(x)
\]

have no common root. This is the finite-field analogue of consecutive coprimality for an orthogonal-polynomial system.

More can be said at an in-window universal root \(\alpha\). If

\[
N_h(\alpha)=L_h(\alpha)=0,
\qquad \mathcal Q_h(\alpha)\ne0,
\]

then from the definition of \(L_h\),

\[
N_{h-1}(\alpha+1)
=-\frac{\mathcal Q_h(\alpha)}{(\alpha+1)^3}
e0.
\tag{11.2}
\]

Substituting into (11.1) gives

\[
\boxed{
N_{h+1}(\alpha)
=(\alpha+1)^3\mathcal Q_h(\alpha)
e0.
}
\tag{11.3}
\]

Thus an admissible common root of \(N_h,L_h\) cannot remain a root of the neighboring continuant at the same base point. This rules out the most naive propagation mechanism.

It does not yet prove (U-unif), because roots can move with \(h\), and the boundary root (9.3) does move. The next algebraic target is a localized subresultant or Euclidean identity of the form

\[
A_h(x)N_h(x)+B_h(x)L_h(x)=D_h(x)
\quad\text{in }\mathbf Z[x,S_h^{-1}],
\tag{11.4}
\]

where

\[
\deg D_h=O(1)
\]

uniformly in \(h\). Such an identity would immediately imply (U-unif).

A weaker and possibly more realistic target is a telescoping bound for the localized subresultant nullities:

\[
\sum_{h\le M}
\operatorname{nullity}_{\mathbf F_p}
S_h^{\circ}
\ll p,
\tag{11.5}
\]

where \(S_h^{\circ}\) is a Sylvester or Bézout matrix after boundary saturation.

# 12. Is \(E_p=O(1)\) for every prime plausible?

Probably not.

The palindromic-orbit random model has approximately

\[
\frac{p+1}{2}
\]

independent points in a space of size \(p^2\). Its birthday count has mean

\[
\binom{(p+1)/2}{2}\frac1{p^2}\longrightarrow\frac18.
\]

Thus a Poisson\((1/8)\) law is exactly the expected model. A Poisson distribution has unbounded support. Across the infinitely many primes, the expected maximum up to \(X\) grows slowly, at the familiar scale

\[
\frac{\log\pi(X)}{\log\log\pi(X)}
\]

up to lower-order corrections.

So a universal constant bound

\[
E_p\le C\quad\text{for every }p
\]

would be arithmetically surprising and is not suggested by the null model, despite the current small data maximum.

The theorem required by the pair-variance argument is only

\[
E_p=O(p),
\]

or equivalently (U-sat). This leaves enormous room for the predicted rare birthday collisions.

# 13. Paper-ready statements

The following propositions can be inserted now without conjectural claims.

## Proposition 13.1 — universal-pair decomposition

Let \(p\ge5\) be prime and \(M=(p-1)/2\). Define

\[
B_p=\#\{0\le r<s\le M:Q_r=Q_s\}
\]

and

\[
B_p^{\mathrm{mid}}
=\#\{0\le r<M:Q_r=Q_M\}.
\]

Then

\[
U_p=M+4B_p-2B_p^{\mathrm{mid}}.
\]

In particular,

\[
M+2B_p\le U_p\le M+4B_p.
\]

### Proof

Each nonmidpoint palindromic orbit has two elements and contributes one forced pair. A collision of two such orbits contributes four additional cross pairs. A collision with the midpoint orbit contributes two. Summing over fibers gives the formula. ∎

## Proposition 13.2 — resultant certificate

Let \(F_h,G_h\) be the primitive reflection-reduced continuant pair and \(R_h=\operatorname{Res}(F_h,G_h)\ne0\). Then

\[
c_p(h)\le
\deg\gcd(\overline F_h,\overline G_h)
\le v_p(R_h).
\]

Therefore

\[
B_p\le\sum_{h\le M}\min\{3h+O(1),v_p(R_h)\}.
\]

### Proof

Use the Sylvester matrix and Smith normal form as in Proposition 3.1. ∎

## Theorem 13.3 — unconditional pointwise bound

For every prime \(p\ge5\),

\[
U_p\ll p^{3/2}.
\]

### Proof

Partition the palindromic representatives into blocks of length \(H\), apply Cauchy--Schwarz to the fiber multiplicities, and bound close equal pairs by

\[
\sum_{h<H}\deg N_h\ll H^2.
\]

This gives

\[
B_p\ll pH+p^2/H.
\]

Set \(H\asymp\sqrt p\) and use Proposition 13.1. ∎

## Theorem 13.4 — truncated average over primes

Suppose

\[
\log|R_h|\ll h^2\log(2h).
\]

For \(H\le P/2\),

\[
\sum_{P<p\le2P}B_p(H)\log p
\ll H^3\log(2H).
\]

Hence

\[
\operatorname*{Avg}_{P<p\le2P}B_p(H)
\ll H^3\log(2H)/P.
\]

In particular, the short-gap base-collision count has bounded mean for

\[
H\ll(P/\log P)^{1/3}.
\]

## Corollary 13.5 — full-range dyadic average

Under the same height assumption,

\[
\operatorname*{Avg}_{P<p\le2P}B_p
\ll P^{4/3}(\log P)^{1/3}.
\]

### Proof

Combine Theorem 13.4 with the block inequality

\[
B_p\ll(P/H)B_p(H)+P^2/H+P
\]

and choose \(H\asymp(P^2/\log P)^{1/3}\). ∎

# 14. Recommended next computations

The raw factorization of \(R_h\) is no longer the right experiment. The highest-value computations are:

1. **Boundary-saturated gcd degrees.** For each \(p,h\), compute
   \[
   \deg D^{\circ}_{p,h}
   \]
   and its sum over \(h\le(p-1)/2\).

2. **Localized subresultant sequence.** Compute the first nonzero subresultant of \(F_h,G_h\) after saturation by \(S_h\), and test whether its index is uniformly at most \(2\).

3. **Saturated eliminants.** Compute generators of
   \[
   (F_h,G_h,1-yS_h)\cap\mathbf Z
   \]
   for small \(h\), then factor their gcds across consecutive \(h\).

4. **Boundary factor audit.** Verify the exact formulas (9.1)--(9.2) symbolically and divide all predictable Apéry-zero contributions out of the resultant data before looking for primitive primes.

5. **Euclidean transition in \(h\).** Search for an identity connecting
   \[
   D^{\circ}_{p,h-1},D^{\circ}_{p,h},D^{\circ}_{p,h+1}
   \]
   in the localized ring. The contiguous identity (11.1) strongly suggests that such an identity should exist, but it has not yet been derived.

6. **Test the exact theorem needed, not only the maximum.** Record
   \[
   \sum_{h\le(p-1)/2}\deg D^{\circ}_{p,h}.
   \]
   Even if an individual gap occasionally has gcd degree larger than \(2\), a linear total bound is sufficient.

# Final answer to the four questions

## 1. Best unconditional bound and average theorem

The exact resultant statement is

\[
c_p(h)\le\deg\gcd(\overline F_h,\overline G_h)\le v_p(R_h).
\]

It yields the explicit pointwise sum (3.4). Together with the block argument, the best clean pointwise theorem now is

\[
\boxed{U_p\ll p^{3/2}.}
\]

The height of \(R_h\) proves \(O(1)\) average only for gaps

\[
h\ll(P/\log P)^{1/3}
\]

among primes \(p\asymp P\). It does **not** prove \(E_p=O(1)\) on average for the full range. The full-range consequence obtainable this way is

\[
\operatorname*{Avg}_{p\asymp P}E_p
\ll P^{4/3}(\log P)^{1/3}.
\]

## 2. Long gaps

Reflection reduces to representatives and hence to

\[
h\le(p-1)/2,
\]

but not to \(h\le p/3\). The resultant argument remains algebraically valid when the degree exceeds \(p\); only the raw degree root bound becomes ineffective. The range

\[
p/3<h\le p/2
\]

is genuinely open.

## 3. Strong pointwise bound and repeated divisibility of \(R_h\)

A uniform \(O(1)\) bound for every prime is model-implausible. More importantly, repeated divisibility of the raw \(R_h\) does not count repeated primitive collisions. The formulas (9.1)--(9.3) show that one bottom zero \(b_j\equiv0\) forces boundary common roots for every \(h\ge j+2\). For \(p=73\), \(b_2=73\) explains all later \(73\mid R_h\).

The correct target is the boundary-saturated gcd family. A uniform bound

\[
\deg D^{\circ}_{p,h}=O(1)
\]

would prove \(U_p=O(p)\); the weaker total bound (U-sat) is already sufficient.

## 4. What is paper-ready now

The paper can state unconditionally:

- the exact decomposition \(U_p=M+4B_p-2B_p^{\mathrm{mid}}\);
- the Smith-normal-form resultant bound \(c_p(h)\le v_p(R_h)\);
- the exceptionless gap-\(2\) theorem;
- the pointwise bound \(U_p\ll p^{3/2}\);
- the short-gap average theorem (6.3)--(6.6);
- the full-range dyadic average \(P^{4/3}(\log P)^{1/3}\);
- the boundary contamination formulas and the corrected saturated-gcd conjecture.

It should **not** claim an unconditional bounded mean for the full \(E_p\), nor interpret \(p\mid R_h\) as an in-window collision.
