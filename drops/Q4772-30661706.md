# Problem 2.5: the explicit Delannoy symmetric square and why no scalar factorial gauge can be the full answer

## Executive conclusion

The spectral observation is correct:

\[
-16,\qquad -16(17+12\sqrt2),\qquad -16(17-12\sqrt2)
\]

is the \((-16)\)-twist of the symmetric-square spectrum

\[
1,\qquad 17+12\sqrt2,\qquad 17-12\sqrt2
\]

of the rank-two roots \(3\pm2\sqrt2\). Equivalently, the rank-two roots can be written

\[
\rho_\pm=4i(3\pm2\sqrt2)=4i(1\pm\sqrt2)^2,
\]

and then

\[
\rho_+^2=-16(17+12\sqrt2),\qquad
\rho_+\rho_-=-16,\qquad
\rho_-^2=-16(17-12\sqrt2).
\]

However, three corrections are decisive.

1. The recurrence written in the question is **not** the central-Delannoy recurrence. The actual central-Delannoy recurrence is
   \[
   \boxed{
   (n+1)D_{n+1}=3(2n+1)D_n-nD_{n-1},
   \qquad D_0=1,\ D_1=3.}
   \tag{0.1}
   \]
   It gives \(D_n=1,3,13,63,321,\ldots\).

2. The symmetric square of (0.1) is explicit:
   \[
   \boxed{
   \begin{aligned}
   &(n+3)^2(2n+3)U_{n+3}\\
   &\quad-(2n+5)(35n^2+140n+131)U_{n+2}\\
   &\quad+(2n+3)(35n^2+140n+131)U_{n+1}\\
   &\quad-(2n+5)(n+1)^2U_n=0.
   \end{aligned}}
   \tag{0.2}
   \]
   Every quadratic product of two solutions of (0.1), including \(U_n=D_n^2\), satisfies (0.2).

3. There is **no scalar gauge**
   \[
   Q_n=g_nU_n
   \tag{0.3}
   \]
   —with factorials, Pochhammer factors, powers of \(4i\), or any other regular hypergeometric scalar—taking the full Problem 2.5 scalar operator to (0.2). This can be disproved without knowing the enormous degree-28 coefficients. The first subleading formal exponents of the three challenge modes are not equal:
   \[
   \boxed{
   \begin{aligned}
   \frac{Q^{(0)}_{n+1}}{Q^{(0)}_n}
   &=-16n^7\left(1+\frac{33}{2n}+O(n^{-2})\right),\\
   \frac{Q^{(\pm)}_{n+1}}{Q^{(\pm)}_n}
   &=-16n^7(17\pm12\sqrt2)
     \left(1+\frac{39}{2n}+O(n^{-2})\right).
   \end{aligned}}
   \tag{0.4}
   \]
   By contrast, all three modes of the Delannoy symmetric square have the same power correction
   \[
   \frac{U^{(j)}_{n+1}}{U^{(j)}_n}
   =\lambda_j\left(1-\frac1n+O(n^{-2})\right).
   \tag{0.5}
   \]
   Multiplication by one scalar \(g_n\) adds the same correction to every mode, so it cannot turn the common coefficient \(-1\) in (0.5) into both \(33/2\) and \(39/2\).

Thus the \(4i\)-twisted symmetric-square relation is an exact **Poincaré-level** statement, but the finite operator comparison, if it exists, must use a non-scalar rational coboundary or an Ore intertwiner. The correct target is

\[
\boxed{
M(n)\,V(n+1)=V(n)\,\rho(n)\operatorname{Sym}^2 A(n),}
\tag{0.6}
\]

or, after scalarization,

\[
\boxed{
L_{25}\,T=R\,L_{\mathrm{Del}}^{\langle2\rangle},
\qquad
T=t_0(n)+t_1(n)S+t_2(n)S^2,}
\tag{0.7}
\]

not \(Q_n=g_nD_n^2\).

# 1. The correct central-Delannoy recurrence

The central Delannoy numbers have the finite-sum and generating-function formulas

\[
\boxed{
D_n=\sum_{k=0}^n\binom nk\binom{n+k}{k}=P_n(3),}
\tag{1.1}
\]

and

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{n\ge0}D_nz^n=\frac1{\sqrt{1-6z+z^2}}.}
\tag{1.2}
\]

The Legendre recurrence at \(x=3\) is exactly

\[
(n+1)D_{n+1}=3(2n+1)D_n-nD_{n-1}.
\tag{1.3}
\]

Its Poincaré polynomial is

\[
\alpha^2-6\alpha+1,
\]

with roots

\[
\alpha_\pm=3\pm2\sqrt2=(1\pm\sqrt2)^2.
\tag{1.4}
\]

The recurrence stated in the question,

\[
(n+1)^2D_{n+1}=(6n^2+6n+2)D_n-n^2D_{n-1},
\tag{1.5}
\]

has the same leading Poincaré roots but is not (1.3). For example, \(D_0=1\) in (1.5) forces

\[
D_1=2,\qquad D_2=\frac{27}{4},
\]

so it does not produce \(1,3,13,63,\ldots\).

I compute the symmetric square of both recurrences below, so the normalization issue is completely explicit.

# 2. General discrete symmetric-square formula

Write a rank-two recurrence in normalized form

\[
y_{n+2}=a_ny_{n+1}+b_ny_n.
\tag{2.1}
\]

Let \(y,z\) be any two solutions and put

\[
u_n=y_nz_n.
\]

Eliminating the mixed term \(y_{n+1}z_n+y_nz_{n+1}\) gives

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
u_{n+3}={}&
\left(a_{n+1}^2+\frac{a_{n+1}b_{n+1}}{a_n}\right)u_{n+2}\\
&+\left(a_na_{n+1}b_{n+1}+b_{n+1}^2\right)u_{n+1}\\
&-\frac{a_{n+1}b_{n+1}b_n^2}{a_n}u_n.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{2.2}
\]

This recurrence has solution space

\[
\operatorname{span}\{y_1^2,y_1y_2,y_2^2\},
\]

where \(y_1,y_2\) is a basis of the rank-two solution space.

## 2.1 Applying (2.2) to the true Delannoy recurrence

From (1.3), shifted by one index,

\[
y_{n+2}=\frac{3(2n+3)}{n+2}y_{n+1}
          -\frac{n+1}{n+2}y_n.
\]

Thus

\[
a_n=\frac{3(2n+3)}{n+2},
\qquad
b_n=-\frac{n+1}{n+2}.
\]

Substitution into (2.2), followed by clearing denominators, yields

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&(n+3)^2(2n+3)U_{n+3}\\
&\quad-(2n+5)(35n^2+140n+131)U_{n+2}\\
&\quad+(2n+3)(35n^2+140n+131)U_{n+1}\\
&\quad-(2n+5)(n+1)^2U_n=0.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{2.3}
\]

In fully expanded form,

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&(2n^3+15n^2+36n+27)U_{n+3}\\
&-(70n^3+455n^2+962n+655)U_{n+2}\\
&+(70n^3+385n^2+682n+393)U_{n+1}\\
&-(2n^3+9n^2+12n+5)U_n=0.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{2.4}
\]

The leading polynomial is

\[
t^3-35t^2+35t-1
=(t-1)(t^2-34t+1),
\tag{2.5}
\]

with roots

\[
1,\qquad17+12\sqrt2,\qquad17-12\sqrt2.
\]

The square solution starts

\[
D_n^2=1,9,169,3969,103041,\ldots,
\]

and direct substitution verifies (2.3).

# 3. What the \(4i\) twist really proves

Let

\[
\widetilde D_n=(4i)^nD_n.
\]

Equation (1.3) becomes

\[
\boxed{
(n+1)\widetilde D_{n+1}
=12i(2n+1)\widetilde D_n+16n\widetilde D_{n-1}.}
\tag{3.1}
\]

Its Poincaré roots are

\[
4i(3\pm2\sqrt2).
\]

Every quadratic product is multiplied by

\[
(4i)^{2n}=(-16)^n.
\]

Therefore the symmetric-square roots become exactly

\[
-16(17+12\sqrt2),\qquad -16,\qquad -16(17-12\sqrt2).
\tag{3.2}
\]

This explains the complete challenge Poincaré cubic. It does **not** explain the factorial scale \(n^7\). The natural zeroth-order scalar that adds that scale is

\[
g_n^{\mathrm{asym}}=C(-16)^n\Gamma(n)^7,
\qquad
\frac{g_{n+1}^{\mathrm{asym}}}{g_n^{\mathrm{asym}}}
=-16n^7.
\tag{3.3}
\]

Starting at \(n=0\), one may instead use \((-16)^n(n!)^7\), whose quotient is \(-16(n+1)^7\). Either version only matches the leading Newton slope. It does not match the finite recurrence.

## 4.1 Comparison with the Delannoy formal powers

Insert

\[
D_n\sim \alpha^n n^\beta
\]

into (1.3). The leading equation is

\[
\alpha^2-6\alpha+1=0,
\]

and the coefficient of \(1/n\) gives

\[
\boxed{\beta=-\frac12}
\]

for both roots. Therefore every quadratic product has power \(-1\), and every mode of (2.3) has

\[
\frac{U_{n+1}}{U_n}
=\lambda\left(1-\frac1n+O(n^{-2})\right).
\tag{4.7}
\]

Suppose a scalar gauge existed, with

\[
\frac{g_{n+1}}{g_n}
=-16n^7\left(1+\frac{\tau}{n}+O(n^{-2})\right).
\tag{4.8}
\]

Then every transformed mode would have

\[
\frac{Q_{n+1}}{Q_n}
=-16n^7\lambda
\left(1+\frac{\tau-1}{n}+O(n^{-2})\right).
\tag{4.9}
\]

The coefficient \(\tau-1\) is necessarily the same for all three modes. Equations (4.5) and (4.6) demand simultaneously

\[
\tau-1=\frac{33}{2}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\tau-1=\frac{39}{2},
\]

which is impossible.

Thus

\[
\boxed{
\text{there is no scalar }g_n\text{ taking the challenge operator to }
\operatorname{Sym}^2(\text{Delannoy}).}
\tag{4.10}
\]

The same conclusion holds if one uses the literal recurrence (1.5): its two rank-two modes have power \(-1\), so all symmetric products have the common power \(-2\). A scalar multiplication still cannot create two different challenge corrections.

This is stronger than a high-precision numerical failure. It excludes every regular scalar factorial/Pochhammer gauge at the level of formal difference modules.

# 5. Endpoint equation that any scalar gauge would have to satisfy

Let

\[
L_{25}=c_0(n)+c_1(n)S+c_2(n)S^2+c_3(n)S^3
\]

be the primitive challenge operator, and let

\[
L_D=d_0(n)+d_1(n)S+d_2(n)S^2+d_3(n)S^3
\]

be (2.3), with

\[
d_0=-(2n+5)(n+1)^2,
\qquad
d_3=(n+3)^2(2n+3).
\]

If \(Q_n=g_nU_n\), \(s(n)=g_{n+1}/g_n\), and the two operators agreed up to multiplication by a rational function, then necessarily

\[
\boxed{
s(n)s(n+1)s(n+2)
=\frac{c_0(n)d_3(n)}{c_3(n)d_0(n)}.}
\tag{5.1}
\]

Using the endpoint factorizations reported in the challenge repository,

\[
\begin{aligned}
c_0(n)={}&\frac{8192}{K}
(n+1)(n+\tfrac32)(n+2)(n+\tfrac52)^2(n+3)^5\\
&\times(n+\tfrac72)^4(n+4)^3(n+\tfrac92)^3(n+5)^2P_6(n),\\
c_3(n)={}&\frac2K(n+\tfrac72)P_6'(n),
\end{aligned}
\]

one obtains the necessary equation

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
s(n)s(n+1)s(n+2)={}&-4096
\frac{(n+\tfrac32)^2(n+2)(n+\tfrac52)(n+3)^7}
     {n+1}\\

\frac{P_6(n)}{P_6'(n)}.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{5.2}
\]

Equation (5.2) is only a necessary endpoint condition. Even if it had a rational solution, the two middle coefficient identities would still have to hold. The formal-exponent contradiction in Section 4 shows that the full scalar comparison cannot hold.

# 7. What the exact finite certificate should be

The difference of three between the two challenge corrections,

\[
\frac{39}{2}-\frac{33}{2}=3,
\]

is characteristic of a nontrivial shearing or an Ore intertwiner whose leading symbol vanishes on one spectral branch. This is compatible with a hidden symmetric square, but not with scalar multiplication.

There are two equivalent finite targets.

## 7.1 Invariant-conic / matrix certificate

After a determinant-normalizing coboundary, search for a nonsingular rational symmetric matrix \(J(n)\) satisfying

\[
\boxed{
M(n)^TJ(n+1)M(n)=\delta(n)^2J(n).}
\tag{7.1}
\]

The projective zero set of \(v^TJ(n)v\) is the moving Veronese conic. If it splits over the working field, parameterizing it produces a rank-two matrix \(A(n)\) and a rational matrix \(V(n)\) satisfying

\[
M(n)V(n+1)=V(n)\rho(n)\operatorname{Sym}^2A(n).
\tag{7.2}
\]

This is product-compatible:

\[
M(0)\cdots M(N-1)V(N)
=V(0)\left(\prod_{n=0}^{N-1}\rho(n)\right)
\operatorname{Sym}^2\!\left(A(0)\cdots A(N-1)\right).
\]

## 7.2 Ore-intertwiner certificate

Let \(L_D^{\langle2\rangle}\) denote (2.3). Search for

\[
T=t_0(n)+t_1(n)S+t_2(n)S^2
\]

and another Ore operator \(R\) such that

\[
\boxed{L_{25}T=R L_D^{\langle2\rangle}.}
\tag{7.3}
\]

This is linear in the unknown coefficients after degree and denominator bounds are fixed. The leading symbol of \(T\) must account for the mode-dependent shift seen in (4.5)–(4.6).

Either (7.2) or (7.3), followed by initial-value matching, would be a genuine proof of a Delannoy symmetric-square origin.

# 9. Answers to the three requests

## (1) Explicit symmetric square

For the actual central-Delannoy recurrence, the answer is (2.3), equivalently (2.4).

For the literal recurrence written in the question, the answer is (2.7), equivalently (2.8).

## (2) Explicit gauge

The \(4i\) twist and factorial scale give only the leading candidate

\[
\frac{g_{n+1}}{g_n}\sim-16n^7.
\]

There is no exact scalar \(g_n\) mapping the full challenge operator to either Delannoy symmetric square. The mode corrections (4.5) and (4.6) prove this.

The required finite transformation must instead be a non-scalar matrix coboundary (7.2) or an Ore intertwiner (7.3).

## (3) Operator and solution comparison

The operators do **not** match after any scalar gauge, so there is no simple closed-form scalar ratio to verify numerically. The exact formal-exponent contradiction is stronger than a high-precision comparison.

A hidden symmetric-square identification remains possible only in the non-scalar sense. Its proof requires solving the invariant-conic equation or the Ore-intertwiner equation and then matching three initial values.

Finally, the Catalan target

\[
G=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^k}{(2k+1)^2}
=\int_0^1\frac{\arctan x}{x}\,dx
=-\int_0^1\frac{\log x}{1+x^2}\,dx
\]

would enter only after the finite module equivalence and connection-coefficient calculation are established. The Poincaré spectrum alone does not identify that period.
