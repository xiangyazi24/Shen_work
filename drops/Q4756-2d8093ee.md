# 2. The two regulator one-forms

## 2.1 The form in Problem 3.1

On a simply connected open subset on which logarithms are chosen, put

\[

\]

Then

\[

\[

\]

Since the \(A\)-polynomial component is a curve, the pullback of \(du\wedge dv\) to its smooth locus is zero. Hence \(\omega\) is closed on that locus. It is locally exact, but its global periods can be nonzero because the logarithms and the Chern–Simons line have monodromy.

A useful elementary rewrite is

\[

	ag{2.3}

## 2.2 Bloch–Wigner is the volume regulator

\[

\[
oxed{dD(z)=\eta(z,1-z).}
	ag{2.7}
\]

\[

	ag{2.8}

On the path in Problem 3.1, \(x>0\) and \(y>0\), with continuously chosen real logarithms. Hence

## 2.3 Rogers is the holomorphic/Chern–Simons regulator

Define, on chosen branches,

\[
\mathcal R(z)
 :=\operatorname{Li}_2(z)
   +rac12\log z\,\log(1-z)
   -rac{\pi^2}{6}.
	ag{2.10}
\]

\[
oxed{

\[

\[

Walter Neumann's extended Bloch group makes this precise: the extended Rogers dilogarithm gives the universal Cheeger–Chern–Simons class, while its Bloch–Wigner component gives volume. See

- Walter D. Neumann, [*Extended Bloch group and the Cheeger–Chern–Simons class*](https://arxiv.org/abs/math/0307092), Geom. Topol. 8 (2004), 413–474;

# 4. The meaning of \(85=5\cdot17\)

## 4.1 No direct volume relation

## 4.3 Endpoint filling test

If \(x=M\) and \(y=L\) are unsquared meridian and longitude eigenvalues, then the endpoint relations read

\[
P_eta:\quad LM^{-1}=1,
\qquad
P_\alpha:\quad L^2M^{-1}=1.
	ag{4.7}
\]

These are peripheral filling relations of slopes \((-1,1)\) and \((-1,2)\) in the convention \(M^pL^q=1\). If \(x,y\) are squared eigenvalues, or if the longitude is reversed, central signs and slope labels change. The exact normalization must be checked before assigning topological fillings.

If the endpoint representations extend over those two filled manifolds, then (4.6) should be computable as the difference of their \(\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb C)\) Chern–Simons invariants. This is a concrete way to test the origin of \(85\):

1. identify the two filling slopes in the challenge convention;
2. certify that each endpoint representation extends;
3. compute an extended Bloch class for each filling;
4. subtract the two classes;
5. determine the order of the resulting torsion class.

If the order is \(85\), then \(5\) and \(17\) are the primary torsion factors of a relative Chern–Simons class. Without this calculation, assigning separate geometric meanings to \(5\) and \(17\) would be numerology.

## 4.4 Other exact places where \(85\) could arise

The same denominator can appear in equivalent forms:

- the order of the relative class in the kernel of the extended-to-ordinary Bloch map;
- the denominator of the Kirk–Klassen Chern–Simons line holonomy;
- the determinant/index of a flattening lattice from the Neumann–Zagier gluing matrix;
- the least common multiple of denominators of the two endpoint Chern–Simons invariants;
- the order of the peripheral \(K_2\) symbol after specialization;
- a tame-symbol index at ideal points of the normalized \(A\)-curve.

All of these are calculable. The prime factorization alone does not distinguish them.

# 6. Goncharov regulators versus Zagier's conjecture for \(\zeta_F(2)\)

## 6.1 What Zagier's conjecture controls

For a number field \(F\) with \(r_2\) conjugate pairs of complex embeddings, the weight-two Borel regulator is built from matrices of Bloch–Wigner values

\[
D(\sigma_i(z_j)).
	ag{6.1}
\]

Zagier's conjectural formula for \(\zeta_F(2)\) expresses the zeta value, up to explicit powers of \(\pi\), the discriminant, and rational factors, through a determinant of such regulator values for a basis of the relevant Bloch group.

This is exactly the right architecture for hyperbolic volume and for the non-torsion ordinary Bloch class of the complete \(7_2\) representation. Neumann and Yang show that the Bloch invariant of a finite-volume hyperbolic \(3\)-manifold lies in a subgroup determined by its invariant trace field:

- Walter D. Neumann and Jun Yang, [*Bloch invariants of hyperbolic \(3\)-manifolds*](https://arxiv.org/abs/math/9712224).

Goncharov constructs the corresponding \(K_3\)/mixed-Tate classes whose Borel regulator is volume:

- Alexander B. Goncharov, [*Volumes of hyperbolic manifolds and mixed Tate motives*](https://arxiv.org/abs/alg-geom/9601021).

## 6.2 Why it is not the shortest proof of \(4\pi^2/85\)

The ordinary Borel regulator kills torsion. A rational multiple of \(\pi^2\) in the extended regulator is precisely the type of information lost after projecting to Bloch–Wigner \(D\). Thus a route based only on

\[
\zeta_F(2)\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad
\det(D(\sigma_i(z_j)))
\]

is aimed at the wrong component.

The likely structure is

\[
	ext{non-torsion ordinary Bloch parts cancel}
\quad+\quad
	ext{a residual extended torsion class of order }85.
	ag{6.2}
\]

Zagier's conjecture may be useful as a consistency check on the complete-volume part, or if the endpoint class unexpectedly has a nonzero ordinary Bloch component. It should not be the first tool for proving a pure rational \(\pi^2\) value.

## 6.3 The correct motivic formulation

Let \(X\) be the smooth locus of the augmented canonical curve. The relative path from \(P_\alpha\) to \(P_eta\) defines a motivic/Hodge path-torsor period. Its regulator has two coupled components:

\[
egin{array}{ccl}
	ext{ordinary Bloch/Borel component}
&\longrightarrow&	ext{Bloch–Wigner }D	ext{ and volume},\\[2mm]
	ext{extended Cheeger–Chern–Simons component}
&\longrightarrow&	ext{Rogers dilogarithm and rational }\pi^2	ext{ periods}.
\end{array}
	ag{6.3}
\]

Problem 3.1 is located in the second line. Goncharov's general regulator philosophy is therefore applicable, but the computational object should be the **extended Bloch class of the path**, not merely an element of \(B(F)\) whose Borel regulator is a volume.

# 7. Modular units: possible in principle, unsupported at present

A modular-unit proof would require much more than recognizing a knot or a small denominator. One would need an explicit diagram

\[
X_\Gamma\stackrel{\phi}{\longrightarrow}C
	ag{7.1}
\]

from a modular curve such that

\[
x\circ\phi,\ y\circ\phi
\]

are modular units. Then the divisors of both functions would be supported at cusps, and the regulator could be evaluated by modular-symbol or Siegel-unit identities.

There are several immediate tests.

1. **Normalize the \(A\)-component and compute its genus and gonality.** A singular plane model can be misleading.
2. **Compute the divisors of \(x\) and \(y\).** If they cannot be supported over a plausible cusp set under any low-degree map, a modular-unit description is ruled out.
3. **If the normalization has genus one, compute its Jacobian, conductor, and the images of \(x,y\).** Modularity of an elliptic curve over \(\mathbb Q\) does not by itself imply that given functions are modular units.
4. **If the genus is higher, find an explicit congruence subgroup and map.** Similarity of numerical periods is not evidence.
5. **Check the endpoint points.** A useful modular proof would normally send them to cusps, torsion points, or CM points with explicitly controlled regulators.

At present no such map is part of the data. The fact that \(7_2\) is a twist knot supplies a Riley parametrization and a layered ideal triangulation, not automatically a modular curve.

A reduction to **known Bloch–Wigner values** is also not the most natural target. Known \(D\)-values at roots of unity produce Clausen/Dirichlet-\(L\) values and volumes. Rational multiples of \(\pi^2\) arise naturally from the real/extended Rogers functional equations and their branch corrections. Thus the more realistic simplification is

\[
	ext{endpoint extended Bloch class}
\ \xrightarrow{	ext{five-term relations}}
	ext{torsion flattening class},
	ag{7.2}
\]

not

\[
I=\sum_i q_iD(\zeta_i).
\]

# 9. A second route through the peripheral \(K_2\) symbol

Let \(K=\mathbb C(C)\). The peripheral functions define a Steinberg symbol

\[
\{x,y\}\in K_2(K).
	ag{9.1}
\]

For an eigenvalue component of a knot character variety, this symbol is expected—and in the standard \(A\)-polynomial framework is known in the relevant sense—to be torsion/tempered. The local obstruction at an ideal point \(v\) is its tame symbol

\[
\partial_v\{x,y\}
=(-1)^{v(x)v(y)}
\left.rac{x^{v(y)}}{y^{v(x)}}ight|_v.
	ag{9.2}
\]

A direct arithmetic route is:

1. normalize the curve and list all zeros and poles of \(x,y\);
2. compute (9.2) at every point;
3. prove all tame symbols are roots of unity;
4. determine the minimal torsion exponent \(N\);
5. construct an explicit pre-Bloch element \(\xi\) with
   
   \[
   \delta(\xi)=N\,x\wedge y;
   	ag{9.3}
   \]
6. evaluate the extended regulator of \(\xi(P_eta)-\xi(P_\alpha)\).

This route and the triangulation route should produce the same class. The triangulation has the advantage that it supplies \(\xi=\sumarepsilon_j[z_j]\) automatically; the tame-symbol route may reveal the arithmetic source of \(85\) more transparently.

# 10. Direct answers to the four questions

## (1) Relationship with \(D(z)\)

The precise differential formulas are

\[
dD(z)=\log|z|\,d\arg(1-z)-\log|1-z|\,d\arg z,
\]

and

\[
2d\mathcal R(z)=\log z\,d\log(1-z)-\log(1-z)\,d\log z.
\]

An ideal triangulation should give a finite identity (3.6) with algebraic endpoint shapes. The integral is therefore a rational linear combination of **extended Rogers** values. Its volume companion is a rational linear combination of Bloch–Wigner values, but on this real peripheral path that companion should cancel. A formula using \(D(z_i)\) alone would miss the Chern–Simons/flattening component responsible for the rational \(\pi^2\).

## (2) Meaning of \(85\), volume, and Chern–Simons

There is no indicated direct relation to the complete volume. The integral is naturally a relative Chern–Simons period:

\[
I/(4\pi^2)=1/85\pmod{\mathbb Z}.
\]

The strongest concrete conjecture is that the relative extended Bloch class has order \(85\), up to standard \(2\)-torsion normalizations. To prove a meaning for the factors \(5\) and \(17\), compute endpoint filling classes, the flattening lattice, or tame-symbol indices. The complete complement's Chern–Simons invariant is a different value unless one endpoint is shown to be the complete representation, which it is not in the displayed positive-real coordinates.

## (3) Goncharov/Zagier and the number field

Goncharov's regulator and the Neumann–Zickert extended Bloch group are exactly the right framework. The natural complete trace/Riley field is the quintic

\[
\mathbb Q[Z]/(Z^5+Z^4+4Z^3+3Z^2+3Z+1),
\]

with the geometric complex embedding. The endpoint fields should be computed separately by (5.5)–(5.9). Zagier's conjecture for \(\zeta_F(2)\) controls the ordinary Borel/Bloch–Wigner regulator and is therefore more relevant to the hyperbolic volume than to the rational Chern–Simons period \(4\pi^2/85\).

## (4) Modular parametrization or known \(D\)-values

No modular-unit parametrization is currently justified by the available data. It would require an explicit map from a modular curve and proofs that \(x,y\) are modular units. The knot-native route through a geometric triangulation and lifted five-term relations is more direct and already supplies the needed algebraic dilogarithm arguments. The expected final simplification is to a torsion **extended Rogers** class, not to a nonzero sum of ordinary Bloch–Wigner values.
