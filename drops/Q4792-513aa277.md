\[
L_{25}\circ T=R\circ L_{\mathrm{Sym}^2},
\qquad T,R\in\mathbf Q(n)[S],
\tag{1}

\frac{u_{n+1}}{u_n}\sim c\,n^7,

infinity.

\[
L_{25}^{\sharp}\circ T=R\circ L_{\mathrm{Sym}^2},
\tag{2}
\]

* both operators are regular at infinity;

  \[
  (0,-3,0)\quad\text{for }L_{25}^{\sharp},
  \qquad(-1,-1,-1)\quad\text{for }L_{\mathrm{Sym}^2};
  \]
* an order-zero rational gauge is impossible, but an order-one or order-two

* the degree at infinity of a minimally normalized intertwiner is forced to
  be \(1\).

There is a second important correction. In the direction stated in (2), the
factor \(P_6'(n)\) is **not forced into the denominators of \(t_0,t_1,t_2\)**.
It appears in the numerator of the determinant of the companion-matrix map and

the inverse direction.

# 1. Why the raw Ore identity is impossible

Let

\[
L_{25}=\sum_{i=0}^3c_i(n)S^i,
\qquad
A:=L_{\mathrm{Sym}^2}=\sum_{i=0}^3a_i(n)S^i.
\]

The degrees of the \(c_i\) are

\[
28,21,14,7.
\]

Hence the Newton polygon at infinity has one slope \(7\), with multiplicity
\(3\). Its formal solutions have ratios

\[
\frac{y_{n+1}}{y_n}
=-16\lambda\,n^7\left(1+O(n^{-1})\right),
\qquad
\lambda\in\{1,17+12\sqrt2,17-12\sqrt2\}.
\tag{4}
\]

By contrast, \(A\) is regular at infinity. If \(v\) is any of its three formal
solutions, then

\[
\frac{v_{n+1}}{v_n}
=\lambda\left(1+O(n^{-1})\right).
\]

Now let

\[
T=\sum_{j=0}^m t_j(n)S^j,
\qquad t_j\in\mathbf Q(n).
\]

Then

\[
(Tv)_n=\sum_{j=0}^m t_j(n)v_{n+j}
=v_n\sum_{j=0}^m t_j(n)\lambda^j(1+O(n^{-1})).
\]

Cancellations can change only the power of \(n\). The exponential ratio remains
\(\lambda\), and the Newton slope remains \(0\). Therefore \(Tv\) cannot be a
nonzero solution of the raw \(L_{25}\).

If (1) held, \(T\) would annihilate all three solutions of \(A\). Thus \(T\)
would be right-divisible by \(A\). For \(\operatorname{ord}T<3\), this forces
\(T=0\).

So the missing common hypergeometric twist is logically prior to the rational
Ore-intertwiner search.

# 2. The optimal hypergeometric twist

Put

\[
B(n):=P_6'(n).
\]

The supplied shift identity is

\[
P_6(n)=B(n+1).
\tag{5}
\]

Define

\[
\boxed{
\delta(n)
=-2(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(2n+5)(2n+7)^2.
}
\tag{6}
\]

Equivalently,

\[
\delta(n)
=-16(n+2)^2(n+3)^2
\left(n+\frac52\right)
\left(n+\frac72\right)^2.
\]

Let \(H_n\) be the hypergeometric sequence with

\[
\frac{H_{n+1}}{H_n}=\delta(n).
\]

One convenient primitive is

\[
\boxed{
H_n=(-16)^n(2)_n^2(3)_n^2
\left(\frac52\right)_n
\left(\frac72\right)_n^2.
}
\tag{7}
\]

Let \(\mathsf H\) denote multiplication by \(H_n\). The rationally twisted
operator is

\[
\widehat L_{25}:=\mathsf H^{-1}L_{25}\mathsf H
=\sum_{j=0}^3c_j(n)
\prod_{q=0}^{j-1}\delta(n+q)\,S^j.
\tag{8}
\]

This twist is especially efficient because

\[
\delta(n)
=-16n^7\left(1+\frac{39}{2n}+O(n^{-2})\right).
\]

The two outer modes of the raw operator have correction \(39/2\), while the
middle mode has correction \(33/2\). Hence the exponents after (8) are

\[
\boxed{0,-3,0.}
\tag{9}
\]

All three Delannoy-square exponents are \(-1\). Their differences are therefore

\[
\boxed{1,-2,1,}
\tag{10}
\]

which are integral and can be produced by a rational shearing.

## 2.1 Relation to the alternative half-integer gauge

Another useful gauge is

\[
G_n=(-16)^n(1)_n^4(3/2)_n^3.
\]

The two gauges differ by the rational function

\[
H_n=C\,q(n)G_n,
\]

where

\[
q(n)=(n+1)^4(n+2)^2
\left(n+\frac32\right)^3
\left(n+\frac52\right)^2
\tag{11}
\]

and \(C\ne0\) is constant. Thus an intertwiner whose entries have growth
\(n^{12}\) in the \(G\)-normalization becomes an intertwiner of growth \(n\)
in the \(H\)-normalization. This illustrates why a bare statement such as
“the intertwiner has degree \(12\)” is not invariant under rational
redistribution of the scalar twist.

# 4. The six Ore coefficient equations

Write

\[
T=t_0+t_1S+t_2S^2,
\qquad
R=r_0+r_1S+r_2S^2,
\]

and use the shorthand

\[
f^{[k]}(n):=f(n+k).
\]

The Ore multiplication rule is

\[
(fS^i)(gS^j)=f(n)g(n+i)S^{i+j}.
\]

Equating the coefficients of \(S^0,\ldots,S^5\) in

\[
L_{25}^{\sharp}T=RA
\tag{16}
\]

gives

\[
\boxed{\ell_0t_0=r_0a_0,}
\tag{E0}
\]

\[
\boxed{\ell_0t_1+\ell_1t_0^{[1]}
=r_0a_1+r_1a_0^{[1]},}
\tag{E1}
\]

\[
\boxed{\ell_0t_2+\ell_1t_1^{[1]}+\ell_2t_0^{[2]}
=r_0a_2+r_1a_1^{[1]}+r_2a_0^{[2]},}
\tag{E2}
\]

\[
\boxed{\ell_1t_2^{[1]}+\ell_2t_1^{[2]}+\ell_3t_0^{[3]}
=r_0a_3+r_1a_2^{[1]}+r_2a_1^{[2]},}
\tag{E3}
\]

\[
\boxed{\ell_2t_2^{[2]}+\ell_3t_1^{[3]}
=r_1a_3^{[1]}+r_2a_2^{[2]},}
\tag{E4}
\]

\[
\boxed{\ell_3t_2^{[3]}=r_2a_3^{[2]}.}
\tag{E5}
\]

There is a minor correction to the proposed plan: \(S^5\) expresses \(t_2\)
in terms of \(r_2\), not \(t_1\).

A convenient elimination order is

\[
\boxed{
r_0=\frac{\ell_0t_0}{a_0},
}
\tag{17}
\]

\[
\boxed{
r_2=\frac{\ell_3t_2^{[3]}}{a_3^{[2]}},
}
\tag{18}
\]

and then

\[
\boxed{
r_1=
\frac{\ell_0t_1+\ell_1t_0^{[1]}-r_0a_1}
{a_0^{[1]}}.
}
\tag{19}
\]

Substitute (17)--(19) into (E2), (E3), and (E4). The result is a system of
three homogeneous **linear** difference equations for \(t_0,t_1,t_2\).
This is smaller and better conditioned than solving simultaneously for all six
unknown rational functions.

# 5. Where the apparent sextic goes

Set \(B(n)=P_6'(n)\), so \(P_6(n)=B(n+1)\).

Suppose \(t_0,t_2\) are regular at the sextic orbit. Equation (17) gives

\[
\begin{aligned}
r_0={}&-4\,
\frac{(n+3)^2(n+4)(2n+3)(2n+7)B(n+1)}{n+1}
\,t_0.
\end{aligned}
\tag{20}
\]

Equation (18) gives

\[
\begin{aligned}
r_2={}&-4\,
\frac{(n+2)(n+3)(n+4)^2(2n+11)^2B(n)}{(n+5)^2}
\,t_2^{[3]}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{21}
\]

Thus in the forward direction

\[
\operatorname{Sol}(A)\longrightarrow\operatorname{Sol}(L_{25}^{\sharp}),
\]

the two apparent sextic factors are naturally carried by \(r_0\) and \(r_2\).
They need not occur as poles of \(t_j\).

In the reverse direction, an inverse matrix or reverse Ore map necessarily
divides by \(B(n)\). This explains why a computation performed with the
opposite orientation reports \(P_6'\) in the denominator.

So the statement

> “\(P_6'\) is the obstruction to a polynomial forward intertwiner”

is not justified by the endpoint factors alone. The full middle equations must
be solved before that conclusion can be drawn.

# 6. Exact determinant of any companion-matrix gauge

Use the standard companion matrix

\[
C_L(n)=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&1&0\\
0&0&1\\
-\ell_0/\ell_3&-\ell_1/\ell_3&-\ell_2/\ell_3
\end{pmatrix}
\]

and define \(C_A\) similarly from \(a_0,a_1,a_2,a_3\).

An Ore intertwiner \(T\) induces a rational matrix \(X(n)\) satisfying

\[
X(n+1)C_A(n)=C_L(n)X(n).
\tag{22}
\]

Taking determinants gives

\[
\frac{\det X(n+1)}{\det X(n)}
=\frac{\ell_0(n)a_3(n)}{\ell_3(n)a_0(n)}.
\tag{23}
\]

Using (14) and the Delannoy-square coefficients, the quotient simplifies to

\[
\boxed{
\frac{\det X(n+1)}{\det X(n)}
=
\frac{B(n+1)}{B(n)}
\frac{(n+3)^3(2n+3)^2}
{(n+1)(n+2)(n+4)(2n+11)^2}.
}
\tag{24}
\]

This is an exact rational multiplicative coboundary. One primitive is

\[
\boxed{
\mathscr D(n)=
\frac{B(n)(n+1)(n+2)^2}
{(n+3)(2n+3)^2(2n+5)^2(2n+7)^2(2n+9)^2}.
}
\tag{25}
\]

Indeed,

\[
\frac{\mathscr D(n+1)}{\mathscr D(n)}
\]

is exactly the right side of (24). Therefore any invertible rational companion
gauge has

\[
\boxed{\det X(n)=C\mathscr D(n)}
\tag{26}
\]

for a nonzero constant \(C\in\mathbf Q\).

This gives several useful conclusions.

1. The numerator and denominator of the determinant both have degree \(9\),
   in agreement with the zero total exponent difference at infinity.
2. The forward determinant has \(B(n)\) in its **numerator**.
3. The inverse determinant has \(B(n)\) in its denominator.
4. Any proposed \(X\), \(T\), or \(R\) can be rejected immediately if its
   induced determinant does not satisfy (24).

# 7. What “minimal degree” means here

For a rational function \(f=p/q\) in reduced form, define

\[
\deg_\infty f=\deg p-\deg q.
\]

This is the degree relevant to formal exponents.

After the twist (7), the source exponents are all \(-1\), while the target
exponents are \(0,-3,0\). Therefore a nonzero intertwiner mapping the two outer
modes must have

\[
\boxed{\max_j\deg_\infty t_j=1.}
\tag{27}
\]

Its leading symbol must be nonzero at \(17\pm12\sqrt2\). On the middle mode it
must cancel three successive asymptotic orders in order to change the generic
output exponent \(0\) to \(-3\).

This proves that order zero is impossible. It does **not** prove that order one
is impossible: a first-order symbol can vanish at the middle root \(1\), and
subleading rational coefficients can in principle create the additional two
cancellations. Thus the actual minimal Ore order is

\[
1\quad\text{or}\quad2,
\]

and must be decided by the exact linear system.

## 7.1 Three useful degree normalizations

### Apparent-sextic denominator

If one imposes

\[
t_j(n)=\frac{T_j(n)}{B(n)},
\]

then (27) gives the first compatible bound

\[
\boxed{\deg T_j\le7.}
\tag{28}
\]

This is the natural first ansatz for the **reverse** map. It is a diagnostic,
not a theorem that \(B\) is required in the forward map.

### Determinant-normal-form denominator

The denominator in (25) suggests the linear-factor budget

\[
q_{\det}(n)=
(n+3)(2n+3)^2(2n+5)^2(2n+7)^2(2n+9)^2,
\tag{29}
\]

which has degree \(9\). If a common denominator of this size is used, (27)
gives

\[
\boxed{\deg T_j\le10.}
\tag{30}
\]

This is a good first complete search box, but the determinant alone does not
prove that every entry has this common denominator.

### The nonoptimal \(G_n\) gauge

In the \(G_n=(-16)^n(1)_n^4(3/2)_n^3\) normalization, the target-source exponent
differences are \(12,9,12\). A polynomial ansatz then naturally starts around
degree \(12\). Multiplication by the rational factor (11) converts it to the
degree-at-infinity-one normalization above.

Hence the numerical value called “the minimal degree” changes by \(11\) under
a harmless rational redistribution of the scalar twist. The invariant answer
is (27), not a single raw polynomial degree.

# 8. Finite exact solve

The exact computation should be performed in the following order.

## Step A: insert the full \(P_9\) and \(P_{10}\)

Their complete coefficients are indispensable. Different degree-\(9\) and
degree-\(10\) polynomials with the same endpoint factorization lead to different
linear systems and different answers.

## Step B: build \(L_{25}^{\sharp}\)

Use (13)--(14) and verify the identity by exact polynomial arithmetic.

## Step C: test Ore order one before order two

Set \(t_2=0\), eliminate \(r_0,r_1,r_2\) by (17)--(19), and solve the three
remaining identities. If the nullspace is zero, repeat with \(t_2\ne0\).

## Step D: staged denominator search

A sensible order is:

1. polynomial \(t_j\) with degree at infinity at most \(1\);
2. denominator \(B(n)\), numerator degree at most \(7\);
3. denominator \(q_{\det}(n)\), numerator degree at most \(10\);
4. an Abramov universal denominator obtained from the full first-order system;
5. only then add shifts \(B(n+s)\) if local Smith data require them.

## Step E: verify, do not infer

For every candidate, expand both sides of (16) and check all six coefficients
identically. Then construct the companion matrix \(X\) and check both (22) and
(26).

The following are **not** determined by Poincaré roots, formal exponents, or the

* whether the minimal Ore order is \(1\) or \(2\);
* whether \(P_6'\) occurs in the forward \(T\)-denominator;
* the reduced numerator degrees of the three \(t_j\);

system requires the full \(P_9\) and \(P_{10}\). The first serious degree boxes

\(10\) over the determinant-normal-form denominator (29). A claim of a larger
“minimal degree” before making the twist (7) is mainly measuring a nonoptimal
scalar normalization rather than the intrinsic complexity of the
intertwiner.
