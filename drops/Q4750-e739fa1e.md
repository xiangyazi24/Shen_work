# Problem 2.5: the missing factor is not a constant factor, and it may not exist at all

Let the primitive scalar operator be written in the forward-shift convention

$$
L=a_0(n)+a_1(n)S+a_2(n)S^2+a_3(n)S^3,
\qquad (Su)_n=u_{n+1},
$$

so that

$$
a_0(n)u_n+a_1(n)u_{n+1}+a_2(n)u_{n+2}+a_3(n)u_{n+3}=0.
$$

In this convention the degree pattern is

$$
(\deg a_0,\deg a_1,\deg a_2,\deg a_3)=(28,21,14,7).
$$

The main conclusions are:

1. **A degree-three Poincaré polynomial does not imply an order-one Ore factor.** The three Poincaré roots describe three asymptotic modes. They do not say that any one of those modes is an exact hypergeometric solution.
2. **The reciprocal-root argument must be separated into raw and projective normalizations.** For the projective roots, the reciprocal of the smallest root is indeed the largest root. For the raw roots, the reciprocal of the smallest-modulus root is not another root.
3. **If an order-one right factor over $\mathbb Q(n)$ exists, it must be of the form $S-r(n)$ with**
   $$
   r(n)\sim -16n^7.
   $$
   It cannot asymptotically correspond to either irrational Poincaré root over $\mathbb Q(n)$.
4. **A scalar gauge produces an $(S-1)$ factor if and only if the gauge sequence is already an exact hypergeometric solution of $L$.** A common polynomial GCD of the four recurrence coefficients does not determine such a gauge.
5. **The A052795 factorial ratio is not the required gauge.** Its successive ratio has degree one in $n$, whereas the recurrence requires a degree-seven ratio.
6. The most striking structural fact is not a first-order factor but an exact **Poincaré-level symmetric-square identity** with Cooper–Shvets level 8. The Problem 2.5 cubic is the negative symmetric square of the level-8 quadratic. This points toward a rational coboundary to a twisted $\operatorname{Sym}^2$ module, not toward a summation factor.
7. The two cited Shvets papers arXiv:2604.14219 and arXiv:2604.06239 prove **$\zeta(3)$** Apéry limits at levels 8 and 6. They do not prove Catalan limits. The Catalan-relevant Shvets paper is arXiv:2604.09723, and its Catalan recurrence is a different Appendix-B.6 recurrence whose Poincaré spectrum is incompatible with Problem 2.5.

# 1. The three Poincaré roots and what they actually mean

The raw Poincaré polynomial from the primitive operator is

$$
\chi_{25}(c)=c^3+560c^2+8960c+4096
=(c+16)(c^2+544c+256).
$$

Its roots, ordered by modulus, are

$$
\begin{aligned}
c_D&=-272-192\sqrt2=-16(17+12\sqrt2),\\
c_M&=-16,\\
c_R&=-272+192\sqrt2=-16(17-12\sqrt2).
\end{aligned}
$$

Numerically,

$$
|c_D|\approx543.529,
\qquad |c_M|=16,
\qquad |c_R|\approx0.471.
$$

A Poincaré solution has the form, up to powers of $n$,

$$
u_n\asymp (n!)^7c^n.
$$

After division by the common scale $-16$, the projective roots are

$$
\lambda_D=17+12\sqrt2=(1+\sqrt2)^4,
\qquad
\lambda_M=1,
\qquad
\lambda_R=17-12\sqrt2=(\sqrt2-1)^4.
$$

They satisfy

$$
\lambda_D\lambda_R=1
$$

and are the roots of

$$
\phi(\lambda)=\lambda^3-35\lambda^2+35\lambda-1
=(\lambda-1)(\lambda^2-34\lambda+1).
$$

## 1.1 The reciprocal question

If by $r_3$ one means the smallest **projective** root,

$$
r_3=17-12\sqrt2,
$$

then

$$
\boxed{\frac1{r_3}=17+12\sqrt2,}
$$

which is the dominant projective root.

If by $r_3$ one means the smallest-modulus **raw** root

$$
c_R=-16(17-12\sqrt2),
$$

then

$$
\frac1{c_R}=-\frac{17+12\sqrt2}{16}=\frac{c_D}{256},
$$

which is **not** one of the raw Poincaré roots.

Thus reciprocal closure appears only after removal of the common scalar $-16$.

There is also a coefficient-order issue in the proposed test. If

$$
\chi(x)=\alpha_3x^3+\alpha_2x^2+\alpha_1x+\alpha_0,
$$

then

$$
\alpha_3+\frac{\alpha_2}{r}
+\frac{\alpha_1}{r^2}+\frac{\alpha_0}{r^3}
=\frac{\chi(r)}{r^3}.
$$

This evaluates $\chi$ at $r$, not at $1/r$. By contrast,

$$
\chi(1/r)
=\frac{\alpha_3}{r^3}+\frac{\alpha_2}{r^2}
+\frac{\alpha_1}{r}+\alpha_0.
$$

## 1.2 The smallest root is not automatically the convergence mode

Suppose

$$
q_n=Q_Du_n^{(D)}+Q_Mu_n^{(M)}+Q_Ru_n^{(R)},
$$

and similarly for $p_n$. Convergence of $p_n/q_n$ to $G$ normally means that the dominant connection coefficients satisfy

$$
P_D=GQ_D.
$$

Therefore the error

$$
e_n:=p_n-Gq_n
$$

has no $c_D$ mode. Generically its leading remaining mode is $c_M$, not $c_R$. Hence the generic convergence ratio is

$$
\left|\frac{c_M}{c_D}\right|
=\frac1{17+12\sqrt2}
=17-12\sqrt2
=(\sqrt2-1)^4.
$$

Only if a second independent connection coefficient also vanishes does the smallest mode control the error, in which case the rate becomes

$$
\left|\frac{c_R}{c_D}\right|
=(17-12\sqrt2)^2.
$$

So there is no rule saying that “one root must be the convergence mode” in the sense of an order-one suboperator. Convergence is a cancellation of a dominant connection coefficient.

## 1.3 Why $v_n=q_{n+1}-q_n$ grows

For a dominant denominator,

$$
\frac{q_{n+1}}{q_n}\sim c_Dn^7.
$$

Consequently

$$
\frac{q_{n+1}-q_n}{q_n}
\sim c_Dn^7-1,
$$

which grows in modulus. This is expected.

It is not an independent test for right divisibility by $S-1$. Even when

$$
L=Q(S-1),
$$

the difference $(S-1)q$ is a solution of the quotient operator $Q$ and may itself be dominant. The decisive test for an ungauged $(S-1)$ factor is the exact coefficient-sum identity

$$
a_0(n)+a_1(n)+a_2(n)+a_3(n)\equiv0.
$$

Your symbolic failure of this identity is the relevant result.

# 2. Exact order-one right-factor criterion

Let

$$
L=a_0+a_1S+a_2S^2+a_3S^3.
$$

A right factor

$$
S-r(n)
$$

exists if and only if there is a nonzero hypergeometric solution $h_n$ with

$$
\frac{h_{n+1}}{h_n}=r(n).
$$

Equivalently, $r$ must satisfy the exact nonlinear Riccati identity

$$
\boxed{
a_0(n)
+a_1(n)r(n)
+a_2(n)r(n)r(n+1)
+a_3(n)r(n)r(n+1)r(n+2)=0.
}
\tag{2.1}
$$

This is the finite-$n$ test. Poincaré factorization is only the leading equation obtained from (2.1) at infinity.

Indeed, if

$$
L=(b_0+b_1S+b_2S^2)(S-r),
$$

then the Ore relation $Sf(n)=f(n+1)S$ gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
a_0&=-b_0r,\\
a_1&=b_0-b_1r(n+1),\\
a_2&=b_1-b_2r(n+2),\\
a_3&=b_2.
\end{aligned}
$$

Once $r$ is known, the quotient is recovered by

$$
\begin{aligned}
b_2(n)&=a_3(n),\\
b_1(n)&=a_2(n)+a_3(n)r(n+2),\\
b_0(n)&=a_1(n)+b_1(n)r(n+1)
       =-\frac{a_0(n)}{r(n)}.
\end{aligned}
$$

The equality of the two formulas for $b_0$ is exactly (2.1).

## 2.1 What the Poincaré polynomial permits over $\mathbb Q(n)$

Because the coefficient degrees step by seven, any rational hypergeometric ratio must have

$$
r(n)\sim \kappa n^7,
$$

where $\kappa$ is a raw Poincaré root.

If $r(n)\in\mathbb Q(n)$, then its leading coefficient $\kappa$ is rational. Among the three roots of $\chi_{25}$, only

$$
\kappa=-16
$$

is rational. Therefore:

$$
\boxed{
\text{Any order-one right factor over }\mathbb Q(n)
\text{ must satisfy }r(n)\sim-16n^7.
}
$$

The factors associated with $c_D$ and $c_R$, if they existed individually, would live over $\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)(n)$. Over $\mathbb Q(n)$ they would be expected to occur, if at all, as a conjugate order-two object.

This sharply answers part (a):

> The candidate is not a constant factor $S-1/r_3$. The only possible rational first-order factor is $S-r(n)$ with $r(n)\sim-16n^7$. There is no theorem saying that such a factor exists.

## 2.2 The simplest candidate and the exact test

The simplest degree-seven gauge corresponding to the rational asymptotic root is

$$
h_n^{(0)}=(-16)^n(n!)^7,
$$

whose ratio is

$$
r_0(n)=-16(n+1)^7.
$$

With your exact $a_j(n)$, compute

$$
\begin{aligned}
R_0(n):={}&a_0(n)
+a_1(n)r_0(n)\\
&+a_2(n)r_0(n)r_0(n+1)\\
&+a_3(n)r_0(n)r_0(n+1)r_0(n+2).
\end{aligned}
$$

If $R_0(n)\not\equiv0$, this simple factorial gauge fails. One must then run a complete Petkovšek/van-Hoeij hypergeometric-solution search for rational $r(n)$ with leading behavior $-16n^7$.

A software-independent certificate of a positive result is just the factored rational function $r(n)$ together with the identity (2.1). A certificate of nonexistence is the exhaustive candidate list from the hypergeometric-solution algorithm and the nonzero residual for every candidate.

The public recurrence report currently prints only the linear factors and unnamed irreducible factors $P_6,P_9,P_{10},P'_6$, not their full coefficients. Consequently an exact Petkovšek test cannot be reconstructed from that report alone. Your full SymPy output is precisely the missing input.

A direct SymPy check for a proposed ratio is:

```python
# a0, a1, a2, a3 are the exact reduced coefficient polynomials.
n = sp.symbols('n', integer=True)

r = -16*(n + 1)**7
r1 = sp.expand(r.subs(n, n + 1))
r2 = sp.expand(r.subs(n, n + 2))

residual = sp.factor(
    a0 + a1*r + a2*r*r1 + a3*r*r1*r2
)
print(residual)
```

For a general candidate $r(n)$, the same one-line residual is a complete verification.

# 3. What a scalar gauge can and cannot do

Let

$$
q_n=h_n\widetilde q_n,
\qquad
r_h(n)=\frac{h_{n+1}}{h_n}.
$$

Conjugation by $h$ transforms the operator to

$$
\widetilde L=h^{-1}Lh
=\sum_{j=0}^3a_j(n)\frac{h_{n+j}}{h_n}S^j.
$$

Thus its four coefficients are

$$
\begin{aligned}
\widetilde a_0&=a_0,\\
\widetilde a_1&=a_1r_h(n),\\
\widetilde a_2&=a_2r_h(n)r_h(n+1),\\
\widetilde a_3&=a_3r_h(n)r_h(n+1)r_h(n+2).
\end{aligned}
$$

The transformed operator has $S-1$ as a right factor exactly when

$$
\widetilde a_0+\widetilde a_1+\widetilde a_2+\widetilde a_3=0.
$$

But this identity is exactly

$$
\frac{Lh}{h_n}=0.
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{
\text{A gauge makes }S-1\text{ a right factor}
\iff h_n\text{ is already an exact hypergeometric solution of }L.
}
$$

A gauge is not a way around the factor problem. It is another formulation of the same factor problem.

## 3.1 Why the removed GCD does not determine $h_n$

The common factor

$$
4(n+2)^5(n+3)^5(n+4)^2(2n+3)(2n+5)^2(2n+7)^2
$$

is polynomial content of the eliminated scalar operator. It is not gauge data.

Multiplying an operator by any nonzero polynomial $g(n)$ changes its common GCD but leaves its solution space unchanged:

$$
L u=0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
g(n)L u=0
$$

away from the finitely many zeros of $g$. By contrast, a gauge multiplies the $S^j$ coefficient by the shift-dependent quotient $h_{n+j}/h_n$, which is different for every $j$.

Thus a common GCD is neither invariant nor sufficient to recover a hypergeometric solution.

## 3.2 The A052795 candidate fails the degree test

The shifted A052795 interpolation discussed previously is

$$
H_N=\frac{(6N+6)!}{(5N+6)!}.
$$

Its exact ratio is

$$
\frac{H_{N+1}}{H_N}
=
\frac{(6N+7)(6N+8)(6N+9)(6N+10)(6N+11)(6N+12)}
{(5N+7)(5N+8)(5N+9)(5N+10)(5N+11)}.
$$

Hence

$$
\frac{H_{N+1}}{H_N}
\sim \frac{6^6}{5^5}N.
$$

This ratio has degree one, whereas every Poincaré mode of Problem 2.5 has a degree-seven ratio. Therefore A052795 cannot be the scalar gauge that neutralizes a Problem 2.5 mode. At most it could be one factor inside a much larger degree-seven gamma product, but the three initial values $1,12,306$ do not establish that.

The necessary leading gauges are instead

$$
\begin{aligned}
h_n^{(D)}&=c_D^n(n!)^7,\\
h_n^{(M)}&=(-16)^n(n!)^7,\\
h_n^{(R)}&=c_R^n(n!)^7,
\end{aligned}
$$

possibly multiplied by powers of shifted gamma functions and a rational correction. These are asymptotic normalizations, not exact solutions.

After the rational middle gauge $h_n^{(M)}$, the transformed Poincaré roots are

$$
17+12\sqrt2,\qquad1,\qquad17-12\sqrt2.
$$

This explains why a neutral root appears at infinity. It still does not imply an exact $(S-1)$ factor.

## 3.3 Scalar gauge versus the noncube determinant

For a scalar matrix gauge

$$
\widetilde M(n)=h_n^{-1}M(n)h_{n+1}
=\frac{h_{n+1}}{h_n}M(n),
$$

one has

$$
\det\widetilde M(n)
=\left(\frac{h_{n+1}}{h_n}\right)^3\det M(n).
$$

Thus a scalar gauge changes the determinant only by a cube. It cannot change the class of $\det M(n)$ in

$$
\mathbb Q(n)^\times/\mathbb Q(n)^{\times3}.
$$

Since the printed determinant is not a cube, no scalar factorial gauge can turn the printed matrix into a literal symmetric square. A genuinely non-scalar rational coboundary is required:

$$
\widetilde M(n)=U(n)^{-1}M(n)U(n+1),
$$

for which

$$
\det\widetilde M(n)
=\det M(n)\frac{\det U(n+1)}{\det U(n)}.
$$

This distinction is essential: the scalar gauge sought in part (b) and the matrix coboundary needed for a hidden symmetric square are different problems.

# 4. The exact level-8 Poincaré identity

Shvets's level-8 sequence satisfies

$$
(n+1)^3s_{n+1}
=(2n+1)(12n^2+12n+4)s_n-16n^3s_{n-1}.
$$

Its limiting characteristic polynomial is

$$
P_8(x)=x^2-24x+16.
$$

Let its roots be $\alpha,\beta$. Then

$$
\alpha+\beta=24,
\qquad
\alpha\beta=16,
$$

and explicitly

$$
\alpha=12+8\sqrt2=4(3+2\sqrt2),
\qquad
\beta=12-8\sqrt2=4(3-2\sqrt2).
$$

The symmetric-square eigenvalues are

$$
\alpha^2,\qquad\alpha\beta=16,\qquad\beta^2.
$$

Their characteristic polynomial is

$$
\begin{aligned}
P_8^{\operatorname{Sym}^2}(y)
&=(y-\alpha^2)(y-16)(y-\beta^2)\\
&=y^3-560y^2+8960y-4096.
\end{aligned}
$$

Indeed,

$$
\alpha^2+\alpha\beta+\beta^2
=(\alpha+\beta)^2-\alpha\beta
=24^2-16=560,
$$

and the second elementary symmetric sum is $16\cdot560=8960$.

Now compare with Problem 2.5:

$$
\boxed{
\chi_{25}(c)=-P_8^{\operatorname{Sym}^2}(-c).
}
\tag{4.1}
$$

Thus the three raw Problem 2.5 roots are precisely

$$
-\alpha^2,\qquad-\alpha\beta=-16,\qquad-\beta^2.
$$

After common normalization, this is

$$
(3+2\sqrt2)^2,\qquad1,\qquad(3-2\sqrt2)^2,
$$

or

$$
(1+\sqrt2)^4,\qquad1,\qquad(1-\sqrt2)^4.
$$

This is a much sharper explanation of the cubic than “there should be a one-dimensional convergence factor.”

## 4.1 Why the neutral asymptotic eigenvalue need not define a line submodule

For a constant $2\times2$ matrix with eigenvalues $\mu,\nu$, its symmetric square has eigenvalues

$$
\mu^2,\quad\mu\nu,\quad\nu^2.
$$

The middle eigenvalue is the determinant of the limiting rank-two matrix. But for a varying rank-two cocycle, the determinant is naturally a one-dimensional representation in

$$
\bigwedge^2V,
$$

not an invariant line inside

$$
\operatorname{Sym}^2V.
$$

A limiting matrix may therefore have a neutral eigenvalue while the full nonautonomous symmetric-square module is irreducible and has no hypergeometric line. This is exactly the situation in which the Poincaré polynomial factors but the Ore operator does not.

## 4.2 The concrete operator-level test suggested by (4.1)

Write the level-8 companion matrix as

$$
T_8(n)=
\begin{pmatrix}
A_n&B_n\\
1&0
\end{pmatrix},
$$

where

$$
A_n=\frac{(2n+1)(12n^2+12n+4)}{(n+1)^3},
\qquad
B_n=-\frac{16n^3}{(n+1)^3}.
$$

In the quadratic basis $(x^2,xy,y^2)$,

$$
\operatorname{Sym}^2T_8(n)=
\begin{pmatrix}
A_n^2&2A_nB_n&B_n^2\\
A_n&B_n&0\\
1&0&0
\end{pmatrix},
$$

up to transpose or conjugation according to row/column convention.

The natural exact search is therefore not

$$
L_{25}=L_2(S-r),
$$

but rather a twisted coboundary equation

$$
\boxed{
M_{25}(n)U(n+1)
=\rho(n)U(n)\operatorname{Sym}^2T_8(n+s),
}
\tag{4.2}
$$

for a small integer shift $s$, a rational scalar twist $\rho(n)$, and

$$
U(n)\in\operatorname{GL}_3(\mathbb Q(n)).
$$

The determinant condition forced by (4.2) is

$$
\det M_{25}(n)\frac{\det U(n+1)}{\det U(n)}
=\rho(n)^3\det(T_8(n+s))^3.
$$

After determinant normalization, the decisive symmetric-square test is the existence of a rational moving conic:

$$
\boxed{
M_{25}(n)^TJ(n+1)M_{25}(n)=\delta(n)^2J(n),
}
\tag{4.3}
$$

with $J(n)$ nonsingular and symmetric. Equation (4.3), followed by straightening the conic to $Y^2-XZ=0$, is the exact certificate for a hidden rank-two system.

The Poincaré identity (4.1) makes this test substantially more motivated than a search for an $(S-1)$ factor.

# 5. Correction concerning the Shvets papers

The premises in part (c) combine three different papers and two different constants.

## 5.1 arXiv:2604.14219 is a level-8 $\zeta(3)$ result

The paper [*Eta-products, Eichler integrals, and the level-8 Apéry limit*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14219) proves

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{B_n^{(8)}}{s_n}
=\frac7{32}\zeta(3).
$$

Its modular parametrization is

$$
\boxed{
t(\tau)=
\frac{\eta(\tau)^8\eta(8\tau)^8}
{\eta(2\tau)^8\eta(4\tau)^8},
}
$$

and

$$
\boxed{
Y(\tau)=
\frac{\eta(2\tau)^6\eta(4\tau)^6}
{\eta(\tau)^4\eta(8\tau)^4}.
}
$$

Here $t$ is a weight-zero Hauptmodul for the Fricke quotient $\Gamma_0(8)^+$ and $Y$ has weight two.

The Eichler-integral form is the weight-four Eisenstein combination

$$
\boxed{
g_8(\tau)=
\frac{E_4(\tau)-21E_4(2\tau)+84E_4(4\tau)-64E_4(8\tau)}{240}.
}
$$

Its $L$-series is

$$
L(g_8,s)
=\zeta(s)\zeta(s-3)
(1-2^{-s})(1-2^{2-s})(1-2^{4-s}),
$$

and

$$
L(g_8,3)=\frac7{32}\zeta(3).
$$

This is a weight-four, trivial-character $\zeta(3)$ mechanism.

The product mentioned in the question,

$$
\eta(\tau)^8\eta(2\tau)^8,
$$

has weight eight because the total eta exponent is sixteen. It is not the level-8 Hauptmodul, the weight-two parametrizing form, or the weight-four Eichler form used in arXiv:2604.14219.

## 5.2 arXiv:2604.06239 is a level-6 $\zeta(3)$ result

The paper [*The Domb Apéry-limit and a proof of the Ramanujan Machine conjecture Z2*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.06239) proves

$$
\frac{B_n}{D_n}\longrightarrow\frac7{24}\zeta(3)
$$

using level-6 eta products, Atkin–Lehner involutions, and weight-four Eichler integrals. It is also not a Catalan result.

## 5.3 The Catalan-relevant Shvets paper treats a different recurrence

The relevant paper is [*Order-3 pi-formulas, Apéry-like kernels, and Clausen functoriality for Conservative Matrix Fields*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.09723).

It proves that one explicit Catalan recurrence printed in Appendix B.6 of *From Euler to AI* has a right factor $S-1$ and an order-two kernel

$$
(2n+5)^2g_{n+2}
-4(n+2)^2g_{n+1}
+(n+1)(n+2)g_n=0.
$$

That kernel is a hypergeometric twist of the coefficient sequence of

$$
{}_2F_1\!\left(\frac12,1;\frac32;z\right)^2.
$$

Its limiting characteristic polynomial is

$$
4x^2-4x+1=(2x-1)^2,
$$

which has a double root. Problem 2.5 has three distinct projective roots

$$
17+12\sqrt2,\quad1,\quad17-12\sqrt2.
$$

No scalar gauge can change root ratios or turn a double root into three distinct roots. Therefore the Appendix-B.6 Catalan factorization in arXiv:2604.09723 is not a factorization of the Ramanujan Challenge Problem 2.5 operator.

# 6. Symmetric square is not the same operation as a 2-isogeny

The passage

$$
(1\pm\sqrt2)^2
\longmapsto
(1\pm\sqrt2)^4
$$

can arise in at least three different ways:

1. taking a symmetric square;
2. sampling a transfer matrix every two steps;
3. a genuine degree-two modular correspondence.

Only the third is a 2-isogeny. The Poincaré calculation proves the first interpretation at the level of limiting spectra; it does not prove the third.

A genuine modular 2-isogeny statement requires explicit modular coordinates $x(\tau)$ and $z(\tau)$ satisfying a degree-two modular equation, together with an operator pullback showing that the relevant Picard–Fuchs or Ore modules correspond. Squared characteristic roots alone are insufficient.

At the level of modular forms, the basic degeneracy map is

$$
(V_2f)(\tau)=f(2\tau).
$$

If

$$
L(f,s)=\sum_{n\ge1}\frac{a_n}{n^s},
$$

then

$$
L(V_2f,s)=2^{-s}L(f,s).
$$

Thus a 2-isogeny or oldform lift changes the local factor at $2$ and rescales critical $L$-values, but it does not normally change a $\zeta(3)$ period into a Catalan period.

# 7. The correct modular mechanism for Catalan's constant

Catalan's constant is

$$
G=L(\chi_{-4},2),
$$

where

$$
\chi_{-4}(n)=
\begin{cases}
0,&2\mid n,\\
1,&n\equiv1\pmod4,\\
-1,&n\equiv3\pmod4.
\end{cases}
$$

The natural modular object is therefore a weight-three Eisenstein series with nebentypus $\chi_{-4}$, not the weight-four trivial-character form $g_8$ above.

For example, define the generalized Eisenstein series, up to the conventional constant term, by

$$
E_{3,\chi_{-4},1}(\tau)
=\sum_{n\ge1}
\left(\sum_{d\mid n}\chi_{-4}(n/d)d^2\right)q^n.
$$

Its Dirichlet series factors directly:

$$
\begin{aligned}
L(E_{3,\chi_{-4},1},s)
&=\sum_{n\ge1}\frac1{n^s}
  \sum_{d\mid n}\chi_{-4}(n/d)d^2\\
&=L(\chi_{-4},s)\zeta(s-2).
\end{aligned}
$$

By analytic continuation at the critical point $s=2$,

$$
\boxed{
L(E_{3,\chi_{-4},1},2)
=L(\chi_{-4},2)\zeta(0)
=-\frac12G.
}
\tag{7.1}
$$

This is the precise weight-three Eisenstein mechanism for $G$.

Under the level-raising map $V_2$,

$$
L(V_2E_{3,\chi_{-4},1},2)
=\frac14L(E_{3,\chi_{-4},1},2)
=-\frac18G.
$$

Hence a level-8 or level-16 oldform combination can still carry $G$; the effect of the 2-isogeny is a rational modification of the Euler factor at $2$. The character $\chi_{-4}$ and the critical value $s=2$ remain the arithmetic source.

This gives the correct conditional statement for Problem 2.5:

> If an explicit modular parametrization identifies the Problem 2.5 companion with an Eichler integral of a weight-three form in the $\chi_{-4}$ Eisenstein sector, then Catalan's constant persists under a 2-isogeny through a rational change of the local factor at $2$.

But this is a different mechanism from the weight-four $\zeta(3)$ argument in arXiv:2604.14219 and arXiv:2604.06239.

# 8. Recommended exact computations

The following sequence of tests will settle the structure without relying on asymptotic inference.

## Test 1: complete hypergeometric-factor search

Using the full reduced $a_j(n)$, solve (2.1) for $r(n)\in\mathbb Q(n)$. The leading behavior restricts the search to

$$
r(n)\sim-16n^7.
$$

- If a solution is found, print $r(n)$ and verify (2.1) exactly. Then the correct factor is $S-r(n)$, and the gauge is obtained from $h_{n+1}=r(n)h_n$.
- If no solution is found by a complete Petkovšek/van-Hoeij search, then there is no order-one right factor over $\mathbb Q(n)$ and no hypergeometric scalar gauge can create $S-1$.

## Test 2: factor over $\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)(n)$

Repeat over the quadratic extension. A conjugate pair of first-order factors would indicate splitting of an order-two block over $\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)$. Failure there further supports an irreducible symmetric-square module.

## Test 3: invariant conic

Search for low-degree rational entries of a symmetric matrix $J(n)$ satisfying

$$
M(n)^TJ(n+1)M(n)=\delta(n)^2J(n).
$$

This is linear in the entries of $J(n+1)$ once the degree ansatz and $\delta$ are fixed. A nonsingular solution is the exact hidden-$\operatorname{Sym}^2$ certificate.

## Test 4: compare with the level-8 symmetric square

Use (4.2) with the explicit $T_8(n)$. Compare determinants first, then solve the resulting linear equations for a rational matrix $U(n)$ of increasing degree. The exact Poincaré identity makes this a finite, well-targeted coboundary search.

## Test 5: modular identification only after the operator test

To prove a modular Catalan formula, one needs all of the following:

1. an explicit Hauptmodul or algebraic modular coordinate for the Problem 2.5 generating function;
2. an exact pullback of the scalar differential/Ore operator;
3. a weight-three form with character $\chi_{-4}$ governing the companion/Eichler integral;
4. a Fricke or modular-symbol calculation reducing its critical period to a rational multiple of $G$.

The silver-ratio roots and the level-8 symmetric-square identity are strong search clues, but they are not substitutes for these four certificates.

# Final answers to (a), (b), and (c)

## (a)

There is no reason an order-one right factor must exist. The reciprocal of the smallest **projective** root is the dominant projective root, but the reciprocal of the smallest **raw** root is not another raw root. A right factor is governed by the exact Riccati equation (2.1), not by reciprocal-root numerology.

If a right factor over $\mathbb Q(n)$ exists, it must be

$$
S-r(n),
\qquad r(n)\sim-16n^7.
$$

It cannot be a constant $S-1/r_3$. The most plausible structural alternative is that no order-one factor exists and the operator is a twisted irreducible symmetric square.

## (b)

The coefficient GCD does not determine a gauge. A scalar gauge has an $(S-1)$ factor exactly when its gauge sequence is an exact hypergeometric solution of the original operator. The A052795 candidate has a degree-one ratio and therefore cannot be the required degree-seven gauge.

The simplest asymptotic rational gauge is

$$
h_n=(-16)^n(n!)^7,
$$

but it must be tested by the exact residual (2.1). If it fails and the complete hypergeometric search returns no rational $r(n)$, then no scalar gauge of the requested type exists.

## (c)

The exact spectral relation is

$$
\chi_{25}(c)=-P_8^{\operatorname{Sym}^2}(-c),
$$

so the fourth powers arise naturally from a symmetric square of the level-8 quadratic spectrum. This is not, by itself, a 2-isogeny theorem.

The cited level-8 and level-6 Shvets papers prove $\zeta(3)$ limits through weight-four forms. Catalan's constant instead arises naturally from a weight-three Eisenstein series with character $\chi_{-4}$, as in (7.1). A genuine 2-isogeny would alter only the local factor at $2$ and would preserve $G$ up to a rational scaling once that weight-three character-valued modular structure is established.
