# Audit of the direct transformed-signal proof of Paper 2, Proposition 2.3

## Executive verdict

The proposed simplification is mathematically valid on the one-dimensional interval, and it is cleaner and sharper than the paper's displayed Ehrling calculation.

Let

\[
q:=P+1,
\qquad a:=\frac{\beta}{q},
\qquad z:=\frac{v}{(1+v)^a}.
\]

Under

\[
\beta\ge0,
\qquad P>\max\{1,\beta\},
\]

one has

\[
q>2,\qquad 0\le a<1,\qquad P-\beta>0.
\]

The exact derivative factorization is

\[
z_x
=(1+v)^{-a}
\left(1-a\frac{v}{1+v}\right)v_x
=(1+v)^{-a}
\frac{1+(1-a)v}{1+v}\,v_x.
\]

Since \(v\ge0\),

\[
0<\frac{1+(1-a)v}{1+v}\le1,
\]

and therefore

\[
|z_x|\le \frac{|v_x|}{(1+v)^a}.
\]

Consequently,

\[
z^{q-2}|z_x|^2
\le
\frac{v^{P-1}|v_x|^2}{(1+v)^\beta}.
\]

Testing the elliptic equation by

\[
W(v)=\frac{v^P}{(1+v)^\beta}
\]

gives

\[
(P-\beta)G+\mu A\le \nu I,
\]

where

\[
A:=\int_0^1\frac{v^{P+1}}{(1+v)^\beta},
\quad
G:=\int_0^1\frac{v^{P-1}|v_x|^2}{(1+v)^\beta},
\quad
I:=\int_0^1\frac{u^\gamma v^P}{(1+v)^\beta}.
\]

A pointwise conjugate-exponent Young inequality yields

\[
\nu I\le \frac\mu2 A+C_Y B,
\qquad
B:=\int_0^1\frac{u^{\gamma(P+1)}}{(1+v)^\beta},
\]

with the explicit positive constant

\[
\boxed{
C_Y(P,\mu,\nu)
=
\frac{\nu^{P+1}}{P+1}
\left(\frac{2P}{\mu(P+1)}\right)^P.
}
\]

Hence

\[
G\le \frac{C_Y}{P-\beta}B.
\]

Applying the already-proved uniform positive interval Agmon/GN inequality to \(z\) with exponent \(q=P+1\), and choosing

\[
\boxed{
\delta:=\varepsilon\frac{P-\beta}{C_Y}>0,
}
\]

gives exactly

\[
\int_0^1\frac{v^{P+1}}{(1+v)^\beta}
\le
\varepsilon\int_0^1\frac{u^{\gamma(P+1)}}{(1+v)^\beta}
+C_{\rm Ag}(P+1,\delta)
\left(
\int_0^1\frac{v}{(1+v)^{\beta/(P+1)}}
\right)^{P+1}.
\]

Thus the final choice can be

\[
C_\varepsilon=C_{\rm Ag}
\left(P+1,\varepsilon\frac{P-\beta}{C_Y(P,\mu,\nu)}\right).
\]

This constant is uniform in \(t\), the horizon \(T\), and the particular solution. It depends only on the fixed interval, \(P,\beta,\mu,\nu\), and \(arepsilon\). It does not need a quantitative lower bound for \(v\).

There are two proof-engineering cautions:

1. Strict positivity of \(v\) must be produced before invoking the existing `UnitIntervalPositiveAgmonInterpolation`, because the base solution structure records only `v ≥ 0`.
2. The derivative comparison must be proved on the open physical interval and transported through the integral while discarding the two endpoints. One must **not** claim the ordinary derivative chain rule for the zero extensions at \(0\) and \(1\).

Neither caution invalidates the argument, and both can be discharged with existing repo patterns and no axioms or `sorry`.

```lean
def prop23Transform
```

# 2. Exact power identities for the transformed signal

Because \(V>0\) and \(1+V>0\), all real-power identities are legitimate.

## 2.1 Left side

Pointwise on \([0,1]\),

\[
\begin{aligned}
z^q
&=\left(\frac{V}{(1+V)^a}\right)^q\\
&=\frac{V^q}{(1+V)^{aq}}\\
&=\frac{V^{P+1}}{(1+V)^\beta}.
\end{aligned}
\]

The exact algebra used in the last line is

\[
a q=\frac\beta q q=\beta.
\]

Recommended lemma:

```lean
theorem prop23Transform_rpow_q
    {V beta q : ℝ}
    (hV : 0 ≤ V) (hbase : 0 < 1 + V)
    (hq : q ≠ 0) :
    (V / (1 + V) ^ (beta / q)) ^ q =
      V ^ q / (1 + V) ^ beta := by
  rw [Real.div_rpow hV
    (Real.rpow_nonneg hbase.le (beta / q))]
  rw [← Real.rpow_mul hbase.le]
  have : (beta / q) * q = beta := by field_simp [hq]
  rw [this]
```

Depending on the current Mathlib orientation of `Real.rpow_mul`, the middle rewrite may need `.symm`.

## 2.2 Mass term

Definitionally,

\[
\int_0^1 z
=
\int_0^1\frac{v}{(1+v)^{\beta/(P+1)}}.
\]

Therefore the Agmon mass remainder is already the exact remainder required by Proposition 2.3:

\[
\left(\int_0^1z\right)^q
=
\left(
\int_0^1\frac{v}{(1+v)^{\beta/(P+1)}}
\right)^{P+1}.
\]

No Hölder interpolation is needed after Agmon. This removes equations (2.44) and its extra Young constant from the paper proof.

# 3. Derivative formula and gradient comparison

## 3.1 Exact factorized derivative

On \(x\in(0,1)\), write

\[
Z(x)=V(x)(1+V(x))^{-a}.
\]

Then

\[
\begin{aligned}
Z'(x)
&=V'(x)(1+V)^{-a}
-aV(1+V)^{-a-1}V'\\
&=V'(x)(1+V)^{-a}
\left(1-a\frac V{1+V}\right)\\
&=V'(x)(1+V)^{-a}
\frac{1+(1-a)V}{1+V}.
\end{aligned}
\]

The final factorization is the best Lean form because it isolates a manifestly positive factor:

\[
R(V):=\frac{1+(1-a)V}{1+V}.
\]

For \(V\ge0\) and \(0\le a<1\),

\[
0<R(V)\le1.
\]

Indeed,

\[
1+(1-a)V>0
\]

and

\[
1+(1-a)V\le1+V
\]

because \(aV\ge0\).

Recommended derivative lemma:

```lean
theorem prop23Transform_hasDerivAt
    {V : ℝ → ℝ} {x beta q : ℝ}
    (hV : HasDerivAt V (deriv V x) x)
    (hbase : 0 < 1 + V x)
    (hq : q ≠ 0) :
    HasDerivAt
      (fun y => V y * (1 + V y) ^ (-(beta / q)))
      (deriv V x * (1 + V x) ^ (-(beta / q)) *
        ((1 + (1 - beta / q) * V x) / (1 + V x))) x := by
  have hB : HasDerivAt (fun y => 1 + V y) (deriv V x) x := by
    simpa using (hasDerivAt_const x (1 : ℝ)).add hV
  have hpow := hB.rpow_const
    (p := -(beta / q)) (Or.inl hbase.ne')
  convert hV.mul hpow using 1
  field_simp [hbase.ne']
  ring
```

The exact syntax around the derivative coefficient of `rpow_const` may require unfolding the returned expression before the final `field_simp`.

## 3.2 Absolute derivative bound

The scalar factor estimate gives

\[
|Z'|
=(1+V)^{-a}R(V)|V'|
\le(1+V)^{-a}|V'|.
\]

Equivalently,

\[
|Z'|\le\frac{|V'|}{(1+V)^a}.
\]

Recommended scalar lemma:

```lean
theorem prop23_ratio_mem_Ioc
    {V a : ℝ} (hV : 0 ≤ V) (ha0 : 0 ≤ a) (ha1 : a < 1) :
    0 < (1 + (1 - a) * V) / (1 + V) ∧
      (1 + (1 - a) * V) / (1 + V) ≤ 1 := by
  have hden : 0 < 1 + V := by linarith
  constructor
  · exact div_pos (by nlinarith) hden
  · rw [div_le_one hden]
    nlinarith
```

Then use `abs_of_pos` on the ratio and positivity of `(1+V)^(-a)`.

## 3.3 Exact weighted-gradient power

Since \(q-2=P-1\),

\[
\begin{aligned}
z^{q-2}|z_x|^2
&\le
\left(\frac{V}{(1+V)^a}\right)^{q-2}
\frac{|V_x|^2}{(1+V)^{2a}}\\
&=
\frac{V^{q-2}|V_x|^2}
{(1+V)^{a(q-2)+2a}}\\
&=
\frac{V^{P-1}|V_x|^2}{(1+V)^\beta}.
\end{aligned}
\]

The exponent identity is

\[
a(q-2)+2a=aq=\beta.
\]

Thus, writing

\[
G_z:=\int_0^1z^{q-2}|z_x|^2,
\]

one obtains

\[
\boxed{G_z\le G.}
\]

## 3.4 Endpoint issue: use an almost-everywhere comparison

This is the main hidden formalization issue.

`intervalDomainLift` is a zero extension. If the physical boundary value is positive, that zero extension is discontinuous across \(0\) and \(1\). Hence the ordinary derivative chain rule for the zero extension must not be asserted at the endpoints.

The correct proof is:

1. Prove the derivative formula for every \(x\in(0,1)\), where a neighborhood of \(x\) stays in the physical interval.
2. Prove continuity/integrability of both weighted-gradient integrands using closed-interval `derivWithin` representatives, exactly as done throughout `IntervalAgmonInterpolation.lean` and `IntervalDomainMFiniteDescent.lean`.
3. Compare the ordinary-derivative representatives almost everywhere on `(0,1)`, discarding the endpoint set `{0,1}` of measure zero.
4. Apply integral monotonicity.

Recommended theorem shape:

```lean
theorem prop23Transform_weightedGradient_integral_le
    {p : CM2Params} {T t P : ℝ}
    {u v : ℝ → intervalDomainPoint → ℝ}
    (hsol : IsPaper2ClassicalSolution intervalDomainM p T u v)
    (ht0 : 0 < t) (htT : t < T)
    (hP1 : 1 < P) (hPbeta : p.β < P) :
    intervalDomainM.integral (fun x =>
      (prop23Transform p.β (P + 1) (v t) x) ^ (P - 1) *
        (intervalDomainM.gradNorm
          (prop23Transform p.β (P + 1) (v t)) x) ^ 2)
    ≤
    intervalDomainM.integral (fun x =>
      (v t x) ^ (P - 1) *
        (intervalDomainM.gradNorm (v t) x) ^ 2 /
          (1 + v t x) ^ p.β)
```

The left exponent may instead be written `(P + 1) - 2`; normalize it to `P - 1` early with `ring_nf`.

# 4. Positivity and closed-interval regularity of `z`

## 4.1 Strict positivity

The base `IsPaper2ClassicalSolution` structure stores only \(v\ge0\). The existing uniform Agmon theorem requires strict positivity of its input.

For the interval elliptic equation, strict positivity is genuine and already has a producer in the legacy domain:

```lean
intervalDomain_solution_lift_v_pos_Icc
```

The proof uses the positive lower bound of \(u(t,\cdot)\) on the compact interval and elliptic positivity. For the paper-faithful `intervalDomainM`, add the identical theorem:

```lean
theorem intervalDomainM_solution_lift_v_pos_Icc
    {p : CM2Params} {T t : ℝ}
    {u v : ℝ → intervalDomainPoint → ℝ}
    (hsol : IsPaper2ClassicalSolution intervalDomainM p T u v)
    (ht0 : 0 < t) (htT : t < T) :
    ∀ x ∈ Set.Icc (0 : ℝ) 1,
      0 < intervalDomainLift (v t) x
```

This is not a new analytic assumption: the elliptic equation is unchanged when passing from `intervalDomain` to `intervalDomainM`. The proof can be ported literally or factored through a domain-independent interval elliptic positivity lemma.

Then \(z>0\) follows immediately because its denominator is positive.

Importantly, the Agmon constant does not depend on the quantitative lower bound for \(v\). Strict positivity is used only to justify real-power calculus and the existing theorem interface.

## 4.2 `C²` on the closed physical interval

The correct target is

```lean
ContDiffOn ℝ 2 (intervalDomainLift z) (Set.Icc (0 : ℝ) 1)
```

—not global `ContDiff ℝ 2`.

On `Icc 0 1`, the lift of `z` agrees with

\[
V(1+V)^{-a}.
\]

Since \(V\) is `C²` within the closed interval and \(1+V>0\), the product is `C²` within the interval by `ContDiffOn.rpow_const` and multiplication.

Recommended proof structure:

```lean
have hV2 : ContDiffOn ℝ 2 V (Set.Icc (0 : ℝ) 1) := ...
have hbase : ContDiffOn ℝ 2 (fun x => 1 + V x) (Set.Icc (0 : ℝ) 1) :=
  contDiffOn_const.add hV2
have hpow : ContDiffOn ℝ 2
    (fun x => (1 + V x) ^ (-a)) (Set.Icc (0 : ℝ) 1) :=
  hbase.rpow_const (fun x hx => Or.inl (ne_of_gt (hbase_pos x hx)))
have hexpr : ContDiffOn ℝ 2
    (fun x => V x * (1 + V x) ^ (-a)) (Set.Icc (0 : ℝ) 1) :=
  hV2.mul hpow
exact hexpr.congr (fun x hx => by
  simp [z, V, intervalDomainLift, hx, Real.rpow_neg (hbase_pos x hx).le])
```

The exact `congr` orientation may need reversing.

No endpoint ordinary derivative formula is needed for the Agmon theorem. Its implementation is endpoint-safe: it uses `ContDiffOn` on `Icc`, one-sided/within derivatives in the Agmon proof, and ordinary derivatives only in the open interior.

# 5. Exact weighted elliptic test

Let

\[
W(V):=V^P(1+V)^{-\beta}.
\]

For \(V>0\),

\[
W'(V)
=V^{P-1}(1+V)^{-\beta}
\left(P-\beta\frac{V}{1+V}\right).
\]

Because

\[
0\le\frac{V}{1+V}\le1
\]

and \(eta\ge0\),

\[
P-\beta\frac{V}{1+V}\ge P-\beta>0.
\]

Multiplying

\[
V_{xx}-\mu V+\nu U^\gamma=0
\]

by \(W(V)\), integrating, and using \(V_x(0)=V_x(1)=0\) gives the exact identity

\[
\int_0^1
\left(P-\beta\frac{V}{1+V}\right)
\frac{V^{P-1}|V_x|^2}{(1+V)^\beta}
+\mu A
=\nu I.
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{(P-\beta)G+\mu A\le\nu I.}
\]

## 5.1 Lean producer, not a frontier hypothesis

This should be proved with the existing genuine integration-by-parts pattern from `IntervalDomainMFiniteDescent.lean`:

- define the multiplier and its interior derivative;
- prove closed-interval continuity of the multiplier and derivative representative;
- use `intervalFluxByParts_open`;
- discharge the endpoint term from the actual Neumann values;
- substitute the elliptic equation pointwise on `(0,1)`;
- convert by integral congruence.

Recommended exact identity theorem:

```lean
theorem prop23_elliptic_multiplier_identity
    {p : CM2Params} {T t P : ℝ}
    {u v : ℝ → intervalDomainPoint → ℝ}
    (hsol : IsPaper2ClassicalSolution intervalDomainM p T u v)
    (ht0 : 0 < t) (htT : t < T)
    (hP1 : 1 < P) :
    let U := intervalDomainLift (u t)
    let V := intervalDomainLift (v t)
    (∫ x in (0 : ℝ)..1,
      (P - p.β * V x / (1 + V x)) *
        V x ^ (P - 1) * (deriv V x) ^ 2 /
          (1 + V x) ^ p.β) +
      p.μ * (∫ x in (0 : ℝ)..1,
        V x ^ (P + 1) / (1 + V x) ^ p.β)
    =
      p.ν * (∫ x in (0 : ℝ)..1,
        U x ^ p.γ * V x ^ P / (1 + V x) ^ p.β)
```

Then a short monotonicity lemma derives the `(P-beta)G` inequality.

Do not use the abstract `intervalDomain_lp_energy_balance_of_frontiers`; this is a static elliptic identity and the repo already contains the concrete `intervalFluxByParts_open` infrastructure needed to prove it directly.

# 6. Sharp pointwise Young inequality

Let

\[
X:=\frac{V^{P+1}}{(1+V)^\beta},
\qquad
Y:=\frac{U^{\gamma(P+1)}}{(1+V)^\beta}.
\]

Both are nonnegative, and positivity gives the exact factorization

\[
\frac{U^\gamma V^P}{(1+V)^\beta}
=X^{P/(P+1)}Y^{1/(P+1)}.
\]

Set

\[
r:=\frac{P}{P+1}\in(0,1).
\]

Apply the repo's proved scalar lemma

```lean
scalar_rpow_young_absorb
```

with

```text
small exponent  r = P/(P+1),
large exponent  s = 1,
coefficient     A = nu * Y^(1/(P+1)),
Young epsilon   mu/2,
base            x = X.
```

This yields

\[
\nu X^{P/(P+1)}Y^{1/(P+1)}
\le\frac\mu2 X+C_Y Y,
\]

where

\[
C_Y
=
\frac{\nu^{P+1}}
{(P+1)\left(\frac\mu2\frac{P+1}{P}\right)^P}
=
\frac{\nu^{P+1}}{P+1}
\left(\frac{2P}{\mu(P+1)}\right)^P.
\]

Recommended scalar lemma:

```lean
def prop23YoungConstant (mu nu P : ℝ) : ℝ :=
  (nu ^ (P + 1) / (P + 1)) *
    (2 * P / (mu * (P + 1))) ^ P

theorem prop23_cross_young
    {mu nu gamma beta P U V : ℝ}
    (hmu : 0 < mu) (hnu : 0 < nu)
    (hgamma : 0 < gamma) (hbeta : 0 ≤ beta)
    (hP : 1 < P) (hU : 0 ≤ U) (hV : 0 ≤ V) :
    nu * (U ^ gamma * V ^ P / (1 + V) ^ beta) ≤
      (mu / 2) * (V ^ (P + 1) / (1 + V) ^ beta) +
      prop23YoungConstant mu nu P *
        (U ^ (gamma * (P + 1)) / (1 + V) ^ beta)
```

Prove the density factorization in a separate lemma. Keeping the factorization separate prevents one giant `field_simp`/`ring_nf` block from becoming brittle.

After integration,

\[
\nu I\le\frac\mu2 A+C_YB.
\]

Combining with the elliptic test gives

\[
(P-\beta)G+\frac\mu2 A\le C_YB.
\]

Since \(A\ge0\),

\[
\boxed{G\le\frac{C_Y}{P-\beta}B.}
\]

Recommended theorem:

```lean
theorem prop23_weighted_v_gradient_le_source
    {p : CM2Params} {T t P : ℝ}
    {u v : ℝ → intervalDomainPoint → ℝ}
    (hsol : IsPaper2ClassicalSolution intervalDomainM p T u v)

          (u t x) ^ (p.γ * (P + 1)) /
            (1 + v t x) ^ p.β)
```

All denominator signs are now explicit:


# 7. Final Agmon composition

The existing theorem is

```lean
unitIntervalPositiveAgmonInterpolation :
  ∀ q > 1, ∀ delta > 0,
    ∃ Cdelta > 0, ∀ f,
      (∀ x, 0 < f x) →
      ContDiffOn ℝ 2 (intervalDomainLift f) (Set.Icc 0 1) →
      ∫ f^q ≤ delta * ∫ f^(q-2) |f_x|²
        + Cdelta * (∫ f)^q
```

Choose

\[
\delta:=\varepsilon\frac{P-\beta}{C_Y}.
\]

The sign proof is immediate from

\[
\varepsilon>0,
\quad P-\beta>0,
\quad C_Y>0.
\]

Apply the theorem to

```lean
z := prop23Transform p.β (P + 1) (v t).
```

Using the identities and estimates already proved:

\[
\begin{aligned}
A
&=\int z^q\\
&\le \delta G_z+C_{\rm Ag}(q,\delta)M^q\\
&\le \delta G+C_{\rm Ag}(q,\delta)M^q\\
&\le \delta\frac{C_Y}{P-\beta}B+C_{\rm Ag}(q,\delta)M^q\\
&=\varepsilon B+C_{\rm Ag}(q,\delta)M^q.
\end{aligned}
\]

The last coefficient identity is

\[
\delta\frac{C_Y}{P-\beta}
=\varepsilon.
\]

It should be discharged by `field_simp` using the two nonzero proofs for `C_Y` and `P-beta`.

# 9. The case `beta = 0`

There is no exceptional analytic branch.

When \(eta=0\),

\[
a=0,\qquad z=v,\qquad z_x=v_x.
\]

The derivative ratio becomes exactly one:

\[
\frac{1+(1-a)v}{1+v}=1.
\]

The elliptic multiplier is \(W=v^P\), and the exact identity becomes

\[
P\int v^{P-1}|v_x|^2+\mu\int v^{P+1}
=\nu\int u^\gamma v^P.
\]

The chosen Agmon coefficient is

\[
\delta=\varepsilon\frac{P}{C_Y}>0.
\]

All real-power denominators reduce to one because the base \(1+v\) is strictly positive. There is no `0^0` issue.

For Lean robustness, allow the general proof to simplify `beta = 0` automatically rather than creating a separate theorem, but include small scalar simp lemmas if `Real.rpow_zero` does not fire through the chosen normal form.

# 10. Comparison with the paper proof

The paper applies Ehrling to

\[
g=\frac{v^{(P+1)/2}}{(1+v)^{\beta/2}}
=z^{(P+1)/2}.
\]

It then differentiates the two displayed factors separately and uses

\[
|A-B|^2\le2|A|^2+2|B|^2,
\]

which leads to the coarse coefficient

\[
\frac{(P+1)^2}{2}+\frac{\beta^2}{2}
\]

in front of \(G\); see equations (2.42)--(2.43).

The transformed-variable factorization keeps the cancellation:

\[
g_x
=\frac{P+1}{2}z^{(P-1)/2}z_x,
\]

and

\[
z_x=(1+v)^{-a}
\left(1-a\frac v{1+v}\right)v_x.
\]

Therefore

\[
|g_x|^2
\le\frac{(P+1)^2}{4}
\frac{v^{P-1}|v_x|^2}{(1+v)^\beta}.
\]

The already-proved `unitIntervalPositiveAgmonInterpolation` has this chain-rule coefficient incorporated internally, so applying it to `z` directly is exactly the clean abstraction. It is not a different mathematical argument; it is the factorized, sharper form of the paper's Ehrling step.

The paper also performs a subsequent Hölder/Young estimate to replace an \(L^{(P+1)/2}\)-type remainder by

\[
\left(\int z\right)^{P+1}.
\]

The current uniform Agmon theorem already returns that mass remainder directly, so this second paper step disappears entirely.
