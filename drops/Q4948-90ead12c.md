# Gap-polynomial coprimality and the zero count for Apéry numbers

Let

\[
P(n)=34n^3+51n^2+27n+5,
\]

and normalize the gap polynomials by

\[
N_1(m)=1,\qquad N_2(m)=P(m+1),
\]

\[
N_{h+1}(m)=P(m+h)N_h(m)-(m+h)^6N_{h-1}(m).
\]

Then \(\deg N_h=3(h-1)\), and whenever

\[
b_m\equiv b_{m+h}\equiv 0\pmod p,
\]

with \(0\le m<m+h<p\), one has

\[
N_h(m)\equiv0\pmod p.
\]

The main conclusion is:

> **The proposed consecutive-gap double count cannot improve the exponent \(2/3\).** In fact, the exact extremal problem defined only by the degree bounds has order \(p^{2/3}\), with leading constant \(3/2\).
>
> Genuine pairwise disjointness of the reductions \(N_h\bmod p\), if it held for all relevant \(h\), would be vastly stronger: using all pairs of zeros, it would imply \(Z(p)\le2\). But characteristic-zero coprimality, or nonzero integer resultants, does not imply such modular disjointness. For the actual Apéry gap polynomials, adjacent resultants factor explicitly through the earlier Apéry numbers, so modular collisions are forced as soon as an Apéry zero exists.

## 1. The exact consecutive-gap optimization

Write the zeros in increasing order as

\[
0\le m_1<m_2<\cdots<m_Z<p,
\]

and put

\[
h_i=m_{i+1}-m_i\ge2,
\qquad 1\le i\le Z-1.
\]

For each \(h\ge2\), let

\[
r_h=\#\{i:h_i=h\}.
\]

Every left endpoint of a gap of length \(h\) is a root of \(N_h\bmod p\). Therefore

\[
r_h\le \#\{x\in\mathbf F_p:N_h(x)=0\}\le 3(h-1).
\]

Also

\[
\sum_{h\ge2}hr_h=m_Z-m_1\le p-1.
\]

Thus the degree-only problem is the integer program

\[
M(P)=\max\left\{
\sum_{h\ge2}r_h:
0\le r_h\le3(h-1),\quad
\sum_{h\ge2}hr_h\le P
\right\},
\]

where \(P=p-1\).

Since the value-to-cost ratio of a gap of length \(h\) is \(1/h\), an optimizer fills the capacities in increasing order of \(h\). Indeed, if \(r_k>0\), \(r_h<3(h-1)\), and \(h<k\), replacing one gap of length \(k\) by one of length \(h\) preserves the number of gaps and decreases the total length.

The cumulative capacity through \(H\) is

\[
\sum_{h=2}^{H}3(h-1)=\frac{3H(H-1)}2,
\]

while its total length is

\[
\sum_{h=2}^{H}3h(h-1)
=3\sum_{h=2}^{H}(h^2-h)
=H^3-H.
\]

Let \(H\) be the unique integer satisfying

\[
H^3-H\le P<(H+1)^3-(H+1).
\]

Then the exact optimum is

\[
\boxed{
M(P)=\frac{3H(H-1)}2+
\left\lfloor
\frac{P-(H^3-H)}{H+1}
\right\rfloor.}
\]

The last term is less than \(3H\), because

\[
\bigl((H+1)^3-(H+1)\bigr)-(H^3-H)=3H(H+1).
\]

Consequently

\[
Z(p)-1\le M(p-1)
=\frac32p^{2/3}+O(p^{1/3}).
\]

For example, using \(H<(p-1)^{1/3}+1\), one obtains the completely explicit estimate

\[
Z(p)
<\frac32(p-1)^{2/3}
+\frac92(p-1)^{1/3}+4.
\]

More importantly, the exponent and the leading constant are sharp for this information set. If

\[
P=H^3-H,
\]

take exactly \(3(h-1)\) gaps of every length \(2\le h\le H\). Then

\[
Z-1=\frac{3H(H-1)}2
\asymp \frac32P^{2/3}.
\]

Arrange those gaps in any order and take cumulative sums to obtain distinct endpoints. For each \(h\), declare the left endpoints of the gaps of length \(h\) to be roots of an auxiliary polynomial \(F_h\) of degree at most \(3(h-1)\). Since every endpoint has only one outgoing consecutive gap, these root sets can even be chosen disjoint for different \(h\).

Therefore:

\[
\boxed{\text{Disjoint root sets do not improve the consecutive-gap count.}}
\]

The reason is simple: the equations

\[
N_{h_i}(m_i)=0
\]

are imposed at distinct points \(m_i\). Ordinary coprimality of \(N_h\) and \(N_k\) concerns a common root at the same point and does not interact with this one-equation-per-endpoint bookkeeping.

## 2. Degrees alone remain compatible with \(\Theta(p^{2/3})\), even using all pairs

One might try to improve the preceding argument by using not merely consecutive pairs, but every pair of zeros. Degrees alone still do not force an exponent below \(2/3\).

Here is an explicit abstract model.

For a large prime \(p\), put

\[
M=\left\lfloor\left(\frac{p-1}{10}\right)^{2/3}\right\rfloor,
\qquad
S=\{a_j:0\le j\le M\},
\]

where

\[
a_j=\left\lfloor10j^{3/2}\right\rfloor.
\]

Then \(a_M\le p-1\), and

\[
|S|=M+1\asymp 10^{-2/3}p^{2/3}.
\]

For \(h\ge1\), let

\[
A_h=\#\{(i,j):0\le i<j\le M,\ a_j-a_i=h\}.
\]

We claim that

\[
A_h\le3(h-1),\qquad h\ge2.
\]

Indeed, write \(j=i+r\) and

\[
f_r(x)=(x+r)^{3/2}-x^{3/2}.
\]

If \(a_{i+r}-a_i=h\), then

\[
|10f_r(i)-h|<1.
\]

Set

\[
y=\frac{h+1}{10}.
\]

Since \(f_r(i)\ge r^{3/2}\), one has

\[
r\le y^{2/3}.
\]

For a fixed \(r\), the function \(f_r\) is increasing, and

\[
f_r'(x)
=\frac{3r}{2(\sqrt{x+r}+\sqrt x)}.
\]

On the relevant range \(f_r(x)\le y\), we have

\[
\sqrt x\le\frac{2y}{3r},
\qquad
\sqrt r\le\frac yr,
\]

and hence

\[
\sqrt{x+r}+\sqrt x
\le2\sqrt x+\sqrt r
\le\frac{7y}{3r}.
\]

Therefore

\[
f_r'(x)\ge\frac{9r^2}{14y}.
\]

The allowed interval for \(f_r(i)\) has length \(2/10\), so for each fixed \(r\) the number of possible integers \(i\) is at most

\[
1+\frac{2}{10}\frac{14y}{9r^2}
=1+\frac{28(h+1)}{900r^2}.
\]

Summing over \(r\le y^{2/3}\) gives

\[
A_h
\le y^{2/3}
+\frac{28(h+1)}{900}\sum_{r\ge1}\frac1{r^2}
<\left(\frac{h+1}{10}\right)^{2/3}
+\frac{28\pi^2}{5400}(h+1).
\]

For \(h\ge10\), since \(y^{2/3}\le y\), the right side is less than

\[
0.152(h+1)<3(h-1).
\]

For \(h\le9\), there are no pairs at all, because every nonzero difference between two elements of \(S\) is at least \(10\).

Now define, over \(\mathbf F_p\),

\[
F_h(x)=
\prod_{\substack{i:\ \exists j>i\\a_j-a_i=h}}
(x-a_i).
\]

Then

\[
\deg F_h=A_h\le3(h-1),
\]

and for every pair \(a_i<a_j\) in \(S\),

\[
F_{a_j-a_i}(a_i)=0.
\]

Thus even the full system of pair incidences

\[
\text{“if }x,x+h\in S,\text{ then }F_h(x)=0\text{”}
\]

is compatible with \(|S|\asymp p^{2/3}\).

This proves an information-theoretic limitation:

\[
\boxed{
\text{Degree bounds alone cannot yield }O(p^{1/2}).}
\]

The construction necessarily has many modular intersections among different \(F_h\): a fixed point \(a_i\) is a root of one polynomial for every later point. Preventing those multiple memberships is exactly where genuine modular coprimality would enter.

Characteristic-zero coprimality by itself does not prevent this phenomenon. Distinct polynomials can be coprime over \(\mathbf Q\) and nevertheless have identical reductions modulo a prescribed prime. The elementary example

\[
x-ph,\qquad h=1,2,3,\ldots,
\]

consists of pairwise coprime polynomials over \(\mathbf Q\), while every one reduces to \(x\) modulo \(p\). Thus nonzero integer resultants are not enough; one needs uniform control of which of those resultants are divisible by the prime under consideration.

## 3. What genuine modular disjointness would imply

Now use the full pair implication for the actual zero set.

Fix a zero \(m_i\). For every later zero \(m_j\), put

\[
d_{ij}=m_j-m_i.
\]

Then

\[
N_{d_{ij}}(m_i)=0.
\]

If there are two later zeros \(m_j<m_k\), then the same point \(m_i\) is a common root of

\[
N_{m_j-m_i}
\quad\text{and}\quad
N_{m_k-m_i},
\]

and the two indices are distinct.

Therefore, if the reductions \(N_h\bmod p\) had pairwise disjoint root sets for all \(2\le h\le p-1\), then no zero could have two later zeros. Taking the first zero gives

\[
\boxed{Z(p)\le2.}
\]

So there are two very different statements:

1. **Disjointness used only on consecutive-gap equations:** no improvement over \(p^{2/3}\), because the evaluations occur at different points.
2. **Disjointness combined with all pair equations from a common anchor:** the bound becomes \(Z(p)\le2\).

The second statement is correct but far stronger than what follows from small characteristic-zero resultant computations.

## 4. A collision graph that measures exactly what resultants provide

Define the resultant collision graph

\[
\Gamma_p(H)
\]

with vertex set

\[
\{2,3,\ldots,H\},
\]

and an edge between \(r\ne s\) when

\[
p\mid\operatorname{Res}_m(N_r,N_s).
\]

A common root in \(\mathbf F_p\) implies resultant divisibility, so if \(m\) is a zero of \(b\), the set

\[
D_m(H)=\{h\le H:m+h<p,\ b_{m+h}\equiv0\pmod p\}
\]

is a clique in \(\Gamma_p(H)\).

Let \(\omega_p(H)\) be the clique number. Then every interval of the form

\[
[m,m+H]
\]

whose left endpoint is a zero contains at most

\[
1+\omega_p(H)
\]

zeros. Greedily grouping the ordered zeros into such intervals gives

\[
\boxed{
Z(p)\le
\bigl(1+\omega_p(H)\bigr)
\left\lceil\frac{p}{H+1}\right\rceil.}
\]

In particular, if

\[
p\nmid
\Delta_H,
\qquad
\Delta_H=
\prod_{2\le r<s\le H}
\operatorname{Res}(N_r,N_s),
\]

then \(\Gamma_p(H)\) has no edges, \(\omega_p(H)=1\), and

\[
Z(p)\le2\left\lceil\frac{p}{H+1}\right\rceil.
\]

For each fixed \(H\), if every pair \(N_r,N_s\) with \(r,s\le H\) is coprime over \(\mathbf Q\), only finitely many primes divide \(\Delta_H\). This gives an eventual bound for that fixed \(H\), but no quantitative power saving unless \(H\) is allowed to grow with \(p\) and the prime divisors of \(\Delta_H\) are controlled uniformly.

Let \(E_p(H)\) be the number of edges of \(\Gamma_p(H)\). A clique of size \(q\) contains \(q(q-1)/2\) edges, hence

\[
\omega_p(H)
\le\frac{1+\sqrt{1+8E_p(H)}}2.
\]

Using the first zero as anchor and taking \(H=p-1\), one gets

\[
Z(p)-1\le\omega_p(p-1),
\]

and therefore

\[
\boxed{
Z(p)
\le
\frac{3+\sqrt{1+8E_p(p-1)}}2.}
\]

Consequently:

- \(E_p(p-1)=O(p)\) would imply \(Z(p)=O(\sqrt p)\).
- More generally, \(E_p(p-1)=o(p^{4/3})\) would improve on the exponent \(2/3\).
- Degree information alone gives no such bound on \(E_p\).

This graph formulation isolates the exact missing arithmetic input: one must control, for a fixed prime \(p\), how often \(p\) divides the pairwise resultants.

## 5. The adjacent resultants can be computed explicitly

For the actual Apéry gap polynomials there is a useful exact identity, and it shows that resultant divisibility is not independent of the zero problem.

First,

\[
N_2(m)=P(m+1),
\]

and

\[
N_3(m)=P(m+2)P(m+1)-(m+2)^6.
\]

Modulo \(N_2\),

\[
N_3(m)\equiv-(m+2)^6.
\]

Hence

\[
\operatorname{Res}(N_2,N_3)
=\operatorname{Res}(N_2,-(m+2)^6)
=-N_2(-2)^6.
\]

Since

\[
N_2(-2)=P(-1)=-5,
\]

we recover

\[
\boxed{\operatorname{Res}(N_2,N_3)=-5^6.}
\]

The same calculation iterates. Put

\[
R_h=\operatorname{Res}_m(N_h,N_{h+1}).
\]

Using

\[
N_{h+1}\equiv-(m+h)^6N_{h-1}\pmod{N_h},
\]

multiplicativity of the resultant gives, up to sign,

\[
R_h
= N_h(-h)^6R_{h-1}.
\]

There are also explicit endpoint evaluations. The continuant determinant for \(N_h\), together with

\[
P(-n-1)=-P(n),
\]

gives the reflection identity

\[
N_h(-h)=(-1)^{h-1}N_h(-1).
\]

At \(m=-1\), the recurrence for \(N_h\) is exactly the Apéry recurrence after factorial scaling, so

\[
N_h(-1)=((h-1)!)^3b_{h-1}.
\]

Therefore

\[
N_h(-h)
=(-1)^{h-1}((h-1)!)^3b_{h-1}.
\]

Iterating the resultant recursion yields

\[
\boxed{
\left|\operatorname{Res}(N_h,N_{h+1})\right|
=
\prod_{j=1}^{h-1}
\bigl(j!^3b_j\bigr)^6.}
\]

This identity has an important consequence. For \(p>h\), the factorials are nonzero modulo \(p\), and

\[
p\mid\operatorname{Res}(N_h,N_{h+1})
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
p\mid b_j
\text{ for some }1\le j\le h-1.
\]

Moreover, if \(p\mid b_j\) and \(h=j+1\), then

\[
N_h(-h)=0\pmod p,
\]

and the defining recurrence gives

\[
N_{h+1}(-h)=0\pmod p.
\]

Thus \(N_h\) and \(N_{h+1}\) have the explicit common \(\mathbf F_p\)-root \(-h\).

So a universal modular-disjointness theorem for the \(N_h\) cannot hold at any prime with \(Z(p)>0\). In this precise sense the adjacent resultants are partly circular: their bad prime divisors already record earlier Apéry zeros.

## 6. What Bézout and shifted resultants say about a chain

For consecutive gaps \(h,k\), a three-zero chain

\[
x,\quad x+h,\quad x+h+k
\]

implies

\[
N_h(x)=0,
\qquad
N_k(x+h)=0.
\]

These are equations at different points. To apply a resultant, put them in the same variable:

\[
\mathcal S_{h,k}
=\operatorname{Res}_x
\bigl(N_h(x),N_k(x+h)\bigr).
\]

Then existence of such a chain implies

\[
p\mid\mathcal S_{h,k}.
\]

For the actual Apéry zero chain there is an additional equation from the first and third zero:

\[
N_{h+k}(x)=0.
\]

Hence also

\[
p\mid\operatorname{Res}(N_h,N_{h+k}).
\]

For a longer gap word

\[
\mathbf h=(h_1,\ldots,h_r),
\qquad
s_j=h_1+\cdots+h_j,
\]

the consecutive-edge equations are

\[
N_{h_j}(x+s_{j-1})=0,
\qquad 1\le j\le r,
\]

while the equations obtained by anchoring at the first zero are

\[
N_{s_j}(x)=0,
\qquad 1\le j\le r.
\]

For a fixed gap word, the number of possible starting points \(x\in\mathbf F_p\) is at most the number of roots of

\[
\gcd_{\mathbf F_p[x]}
\bigl(N_{s_1}(x),\ldots,N_{s_r}(x)\bigr),
\]

or, using only consecutive equations, the gcd of the shifted polynomials

\[
N_{h_j}(x+s_{j-1}).
\]

One nonvanishing pairwise resultant is enough to rule out the word. Conversely, for three or more polynomials, vanishing of every pairwise resultant is only necessary, not sufficient, for a common root: different pairs can meet at different algebraic points.

This is all that one-variable Bézout theory supplies. There is no product-of-degrees estimate that globally counts all possible chains, because:

1. the equations for each fixed word are univariate and their intersection is governed by a gcd;
2. different gap words produce different shifted polynomial systems;
3. the relevant resultants are large integers whose divisibility by the same prime may be highly correlated.

Thus resultant theory gives a precise obstruction for each prescribed gap pattern, but a global \(O(\sqrt p)\) estimate requires a new uniform statement about the resultant-divisibility graph or about the shifted resultants.

## 7. Direct answers

### (a) Can the consecutive-gap double count give \(O(\sqrt p)\)?

No. The exact optimization under

\[
r_h\le3(h-1),
\qquad
\sum hr_h\le p-1
\]

has size

\[
\frac32p^{2/3}+O(p^{1/3}),
\]

and this is sharp. Disjointness of root sets for different \(h\) does not affect this local count because the evaluations occur at different endpoints.

If, however, one uses every pair of zeros and assumes genuine pairwise disjointness of all reductions \(N_h\bmod p\), then the much stronger conclusion \(Z(p)\le2\) follows.

### (b) What is the best bound from degrees and coprimality alone?

- From degrees alone, even including all pair incidences, the exponent \(2/3\) is optimal in an abstract polynomial model.
- Pairwise coprimality over \(\mathbf Q\) gives no uniform modular improvement, because a fixed prime may divide many nonzero resultants.
- Pairwise coprimality after reduction modulo the particular prime \(p\) would give \(Z(p)\le2\).
- An intermediate quantitative theorem is expressed by the collision graph. For example, \(E_p=O(p)\) exceptional resultant pairs would imply \(Z(p)=O(\sqrt p)\).

For the actual Apéry polynomials, the formula

\[
\left|\operatorname{Res}(N_h,N_{h+1})\right|
=
\prod_{j=1}^{h-1}(j!^3b_j)^6
\]

shows that modular non-coprimality is itself tied to the zero set one is trying to bound.

### (c) Does Bézout or resultant theory constrain chains?

Yes, but only after shifting all equations into a common variable. A two-gap chain requires

\[
p\mid
\operatorname{Res}_x(N_h(x),N_k(x+h)),
\]

and, using the first and third zeros, also

\[
p\mid\operatorname{Res}(N_h,N_{h+k}).
\]

A longer chain forces all cumulative distances from one anchor to form a clique in the resultant collision graph. This is a useful exact reformulation, but it does not by itself bound the clique number. Establishing \(O(\sqrt p)\) by this route would require a uniform theorem showing that only \(O(p)\) pairs \((h,k)\) have resultants divisible by \(p\), or another comparably strong restriction on the shifted-resultant graph.

So the route is viable only after adding genuinely arithmetic information about the family \(N_h\); degrees and characteristic-zero coprimality alone stop at the \(p^{2/3}\) barrier.
