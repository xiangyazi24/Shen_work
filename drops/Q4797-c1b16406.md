# Problem 2.7: the actual companion matrix, the challenge sequences, and what the level-11 transform does not yet prove

## Executive verdict

The official Ramanujan Challenge statement gives **a scalar four-term recurrence**, not a separately specified \(3\times3\) conservative matrix field. The canonical \(3\times3\) matrix attached to it is the ordinary companion transfer matrix written explicitly in Section 2 below.

The two challenge solutions are completely determined by the published initial triples

\[
\begin{aligned}
p_0&=-612218384750,\\
p_1&=-\frac{9525021973931919}{18100},\\
p_2&=-\frac{29561828382772029}{65380},
\end{aligned}
\qquad
\begin{aligned}
q_0&=-215040420000,\\
q_1&=-\frac{167282265043404}{905},\\
q_2&=-\frac{964185327658080}{6071}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{1}
\]

They satisfy

\[
u_{n+1}=\frac{B_n}{A_n}u_n-
\frac{C_{n-1}}{A_{n-1}}u_{n-1}+
\frac{D_{n-2}}{A_{n-2}}u_{n-2},\qquad n\ge2.
\tag{2}
\]

The centered-binomial/even-section transform of Cooper's level-11 sequence,

\[
W_n=\frac1{256^n}\sum_{k=0}^{2n}
\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}T_k,
\tag{3}
\]

**does not equal \(q_n\)**. Already

\[
W_0=1,\qquad W_1=\frac1{16},
\]

whereas

\[
\frac{q_1}{q_0}=0.859569944180336\ldots\ne\frac1{16}.
\]

Thus (3) is, at present, an exact **spectral transform**, not an explicit formula for the challenge denominator. A nontrivial Ore intertwiner, a rank-one twist, and possibly an even/odd two-component transform would still be required.

Likewise, no public formula is currently known for a transformed Cooper companion producing \(p_n\), and no public "second numerator" has been identified whose quotient gives \(\zeta(2)\) or \(\zeta(3)\) separately.

The official challenge places Problem 2.7 in its section of problems whose proofs are known to the authors but were not public at release. Consequently, the public recurrence and the level-11 spectral identity do not by themselves yield the requested complete convergence proof. They do yield the following sharp conditional asymptotic statement. Let \(\mu_0\) be the unique real root of

\[
4\mu^3-220\mu^2+8\mu-1=0,
\qquad
\mu_0=54.963695098\ldots .
\]

Then the raw dominant recurrence root is

\[
r_0=\frac{\mu_0}{64}=0.8588077359\ldots,
\]

and the two conjugate roots have modulus

\[
|r_\pm|=0.00105378514\ldots .
\]

Hence, if the connection identity

\[
p_n-(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))q_n
\]

has zero coefficient on the dominant mode, Poincaré--Perron theory gives

\[
\limsup_{n\to\infty}
\left|\frac{p_n}{q_n}-(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))\right|^{1/n}
\le
\boxed{\rho=rac1{2\mu_0^{3/2}}
=0.0012270326576\ldots}.
\tag{4}
\]

This is about \(2.911\) decimal digits per step. What remains nonpublic is the arithmetic or period argument proving that the dominant coefficient cancels at precisely
\(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\).

# 2. The explicit \(3\times3\) transfer matrix

Put the row state

\[
X_n(u)=(u_n,u_{n-1},u_{n-2}).
\]

Then

\[
X_{n+1}(u)=X_n(u)M_{27}(n),
\tag{9}
\]

with

\[
\boxed{
M_{27}(n)=
\begin{pmatrix}
\dfrac{B_n}{A_n}&1&0\\[2mm]
-\dfrac{C_{n-1}}{A_{n-1}}&0&1\\[2mm]
\dfrac{D_{n-2}}{A_{n-2}}&0&0
\end{pmatrix},\qquad n\ge2.
}
\tag{10}
\]

Indeed, the first component of the row product in (9) is exactly the right side of (2), while the other two components shift the state.

Let

\[
\mathcal A_2=
\begin{pmatrix}
p_2&p_1&p_0\\
q_2&q_1&q_0
\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{11}
\]

Then, for \(N\ge2\),

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal A_2M_{27}(2)M_{27}(3)\cdots M_{27}(N-1)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
p_N&p_{N-1}&p_{N-2}\\
q_N&q_{N-1}&q_{N-2}
\end{pmatrix}.
}
\tag{12}
\]

If column states are preferred, transpose (10):

\[
\begin{pmatrix}u_{n+1}\\u_n\\u_{n-1}\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
B_n/A_n&-C_{n-1}/A_{n-1}&D_{n-2}/A_{n-2}\\
1&0&0\\
0&1&0
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}u_n\\u_{n-1}\\u_{n-2}\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{13}
\]

This is a companion cocycle. It is not, merely by being \(3\times3\), a conservative matrix field in the stronger multivariate path-independence sense used in the CMF literature. No distinct polynomial \(3\times3\) matrix for Problem 2.7 appears in the official challenge statement.

# 4. Cooper's level-11 sequence and the transform \(K\)

Cooper's actual level-11 recurrence is

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
(n+1)^3T_{n+1}={}&2(2n+1)(5n^2+5n+2)T_n\\
&-8n(7n^2+1)T_{n-1}\\
&+22n(2n-1)(n-1)T_{n-2},
\end{aligned}}
\tag{15}
\]

with

\[
T_0,T_1,T_2,T_3,\ldots
=1,4,28,268,3004,36784,476476,6418192,\ldots .
\]

Its characteristic polynomial is

\[
C_{11}(t)=t^3-20t^2+56t-44.
\tag{16}
\]

Define \(K\) by (3). For a pure mode \(T_k=t^k\), the binomial theorem gives

\[
(KT)_n=\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}\right)^n.
\tag{17}
\]

This realizes the exact characteristic-polynomial relation

\[
16F\!\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{4}\right)
=-C_{11}(t)C_{11}(4-t),
\qquad
F(\mu)=4\mu^3-220\mu^2+8\mu-1.
\tag{18}
\]

For Cooper's distinguished integer solution, the transformed sequence begins

\[
\boxed{
W_0=1,\qquad
W_1=\frac1{16},\qquad
W_2=\frac{355}{16384},\qquad
W_3=\frac{44611}{4194304}.
}
\tag{19}
\]

These values are plainly not a constant multiple of the challenge \(q_n\). If one normalizes at \(n=0\), the proposed equality would predict

\[
q_1=q_0W_1=-13440026250,
\]

whereas the actual value is

\[
q_1=-\frac{167282265043404}{905}
=-184842281815.916\ldots .
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{q_n\ne(KT)_n}
\tag{20}
\]

under the published normalizations.

A possible finite relation would have to be substantially more general, for example

\[
q_n=h_n\bigl(r_0(n)W_n+r_1(n)W_{n+1}+r_2(n)W_{n+2}\bigr),
\tag{21}
\]

with a hypergeometric rank-one twist \(h_n\) and rational Ore-intertwiner coefficients \(r_j(n)\). Neither (21) nor the minimal scalar recurrence of \(W_n\) has yet been publicly identified with the full challenge operator.

There is also a parity issue: the even-section transform loses the odd part of the ordinary centered binomial transform. Its exact operator image is naturally a two-component even/odd system, not a scalar conjugation \(KSK^{-1}\).

# 5. Why \(K\) does not currently give the numerator \(p_n\)

The operation \(K\) transports homogeneous solutions of Cooper's recurrence. It does not manufacture a new connection constant. To obtain a challenge numerator from Cooper data, one would need:

1. a second Cooper solution \(T^{\mathrm{comp}}_n\) carrying the relevant Eichler or period connection coefficient;
2. the same holonomic sum transform applied to that companion;
3. an exact intertwiner of the form (21), valid for the complete rank-three module;
4. initial-value matching with the challenge triple \(p_0,p_1,p_2\).

No such formula is present in Cooper's classification or in the public challenge materials. Thus there is presently no justified identity of the form

\[
p_n=\frac1{256^n}\sum_{k=0}^{2n}
\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}P_k^{(11)}
\tag{22}
\]

with a known explicit \(P_k^{(11)}\).

The target constant itself has the elementary mixed-period representation

\[
\boxed{
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)=
\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{1-\tfrac12\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
}
\tag{23}
\]

A complete proof would naturally produce an \(n\)-dependent deformation of (23), or an equivalent level-11 Eichler integral, whose creative telescoping recurrence is precisely (2).

# 6. Is there a second numerator for ζ(2) or ζ(3) separately?

An order-three recurrence has a three-dimensional solution space, but this fact alone does not imply that its connection field has the distinguished basis

\[
1,\quad\zeta(2),\quad\zeta(3).
\]

The challenge supplies only two rational solutions \(p,q\) and one asserted connection ratio

\[
\frac{p_n}{q_n}\longrightarrow \zeta(2)+\zeta(3).
\]

From this alone one cannot reconstruct rational solutions \(p^{(2)},p^{(3)}\) satisfying

\[
\frac{p_n^{(2)}}{q_n}\to\zeta(2),
\qquad
\frac{p_n^{(3)}}{q_n}\to\zeta(3).
\]

Such a splitting would require two separately normalized extension classes or period functionals. In modular language, the ζ(3) part is expected to arise from a weight-four Eichler/Eisenstein extension, while ζ(2) is a different Tate period. The public scalar recurrence does not specify a canonical splitting of those two extensions.

Consequently:

\[
\boxed{
\text{no explicit rational "second numerator" for }ζ(2)
\text{ or }ζ(3)\text{ separately is presently established.}
}
\tag{24}
\]

The third independent scalar solution may be generated by any independent initial triple, but an arbitrary such solution has no reason to possess either special limit.

# 7. What Poincaré theory proves, and what it does not

From the leading terms of (5),

\[
\frac{B_n}{A_n}\to\frac{55}{64},
\qquad
\frac{C_n}{A_n}\to\frac1{2048},
\qquad
\frac{D_n}{A_n}\to\frac1{2^{20}}.
\]

The characteristic polynomial of the raw recurrence is

\[
\chi(r)=r^3-\frac{55}{64}r^2+rac1{2048}r-rac1{2^{20}}.
\tag{25}
\]

Putting \(\mu=64r\) yields \(F(\mu)=0\). Let \(\mu_0\) be the real root. Since the other roots are conjugate and

\[
\mu_0\mu_+\mu_-=\frac14,
\]

one obtains

\[
|\mu_\pm|=\frac1{2\sqrt{\mu_0}}.
\]

Therefore

\[
\frac{|r_\pm|}{r_0}
=\frac{|\mu_\pm|}{\mu_0}
=\frac1{2\mu_0^{3/2}}=\rho.
\tag{26}
\]

Assume that \(q_n\) has nonzero coefficient on the \(r_0\)-branch and that

\[
e_n:=p_n-Lq_n,\qquad L=\zeta(2)+\zeta(3),
\]

has zero coefficient on that branch. Since all three characteristic roots are simple, the Poincaré--Perron/Birkhoff theory then gives, for every \(arepsilon>0\),

\[
\boxed{
\frac{e_n}{q_n}=O\bigl((\rho+\varepsilon)^n\bigr).
}
\tag{27}
\]

More sharply, after computing the formal powers of \(n\), one gets a polynomial factor times \(ho^n\). In particular,

\[
\limsup_{n\to\infty}
\left|\frac{p_n}{q_n}-L\right|^{1/n}\le\rho.
\]

Since

\[
\rho^{50}=2.77\times10^{-146},
\]

one expects roughly \(145\) correct digits at \(n=50\), up to the connection prefactor and polynomial factors.

However, Poincaré theory does **not** identify \(L\). The missing arithmetic statement is precisely the dominant-mode cancellation

\[
\operatorname{Dom}(p)-L\operatorname{Dom}(q)=0.
\tag{28}
\]

A period integral, modular Eichler integral, CMF boundary map, or certified Ore/integral transform is needed to prove (28). The spectral identity (18) alone cannot do so.

# 8. Status of the requested complete proof

The official challenge explicitly says that the problems in Section 2 have proofs or algorithmic proof procedures known to the authors, while those proofs were initially withheld. The publicly available statement supplies the recurrence and initial values, but not the period certificate proving (28).

Therefore the strongest presently checkable public result is:

- the recurrence and its companion matrix are explicit;
- the numerical limit is extremely well supported;
- the level-11 characteristic-polynomial transform is exact;
- the exponential rate (26) follows conditionally from dominant-mode cancellation;
- the actual cancellation at \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\), and hence a complete public proof, still requires the hidden or independently reconstructed connection formula.

It would be mathematically incorrect to claim a complete proof merely from the match of Poincaré polynomials or from the transform (3).

# 9. Exact reproducible computation

The existing repository script uses expressions such as `B(n)/A(n)` with Python integers. Under Python 3 this division first produces a binary floating-point number, which is then multiplied by an `mpmath` number. Raising `mp.dps` therefore does **not** make that computation an exact 50-digit verification.

The following replacement uses exact rational arithmetic throughout and only converts to `mpmath` at the end.

```python
from fractions import Fraction as Q
import mpmath as mp

mp.mp.dps = 220


def A(n: int) -> int:
    return (
        1024 * (2*n + 5)**4 * (2*n + 7)**3 * (2*n + 9)**3
        * (946*n*n + 6407*n + 10860)
    )


def B(n: int) -> int:
    p6 = (
        104060*n**6 + 1745370*n**5 + 12145238*n**4
        + 44886481*n**3 + 92943995*n**2
        + 102256019*n + 46709052
    )
    return 128 * (2*n + 7)**3 * (2*n + 9)**3 * p6


def C(n: int) -> int:
    p5 = (
        3784*n**5 + 57792*n**4 + 351019*n**3
        + 1059230*n**2 + 1587211*n + 944620
    )
    return 16 * (n + 3)**4 * (2*n + 9)**3 * p5


def D(n: int) -> int:
    return (n + 3)**4 * (n + 4)**6 * (946*n*n + 4515*n + 5399)


def extend(seq: list[Q], N: int) -> list[Q]:
    for n in range(2, N):
        seq.append(
            Q(B(n), A(n)) * seq[n]
            - Q(C(n - 1), A(n - 1)) * seq[n - 1]
            + Q(D(n - 2), A(n - 2)) * seq[n - 2]
        )
    return seq


p = extend([
    Q(-612218384750),
    Q(-9525021973931919, 18100),
    Q(-29561828382772029, 65380),
], 51)

q = extend([
    Q(-215040420000),
    Q(-167282265043404, 905),
    Q(-964185327658080, 6071),
], 51)


def mpq(x: Q) -> mp.mpf:
    return mp.mpf(x.numerator) / x.denominator

for n in range(10):
    print(n, p[n], q[n], mp.nstr(mpq(p[n]) / mpq(q[n]), 80))

ratio50 = mpq(p[50]) / mpq(q[50])
target = mp.zeta(2) + mp.zeta(3)

print("p50/q50 =", mp.nstr(ratio50, 210))
print("target  =", mp.nstr(target, 210))
print("error   =", mp.nstr(ratio50 - target, 30))
```

The target prefix to compare against is (14). An exact run of this program, rather than the floating-coefficient repository script, is the appropriate numerical certificate.

# Final answers

## (a) Matrix

The official problem does not publish a separate CMF matrix. Its canonical \(3\times3\) companion transfer matrix is (10), and its product identity is (12).

## (b) \(p_n,q_n\)

They are the two rational solutions with initial values (1). Their first ten rounded values are listed in Section 3; exact fractions are generated by the code in Section 9.

## (c) Does \(K\) give \(q_n\)?

No, not directly. The equality fails already at \(n=1\). The transform gives the exact spectral map but not the challenge normalization or full finite-\(n\) module. A relation such as (21) remains to be proved.

There is likewise no known explicit transformed formula for \(p_n\).

## (d) Separate numerators for ζ(2), ζ(3)

None is currently identified from the public recurrence. Order three alone does not imply that the connection constants split canonically as \(1,\zeta(2),\zeta(3)\).

## (e) Complete convergence proof

The public data do not contain the period certificate that identifies the dominant coefficient ratio with \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\). Conditional on that single connection identity, the ratio error has spectral rate (4), namely approximately

\[
0.0012270326576^n.
\]

## (f) \(n=50\)

The target is

\[
2.846990970007820721872153328157475179983936193547297319527829\ldots .
\]

The corrected exact-rational program above is the proper way to compute \(p_{50}/q_{50}\). The currently committed `mpmath` script should not be cited as a 50-digit verification because its recurrence coefficients are first rounded to binary floats.
