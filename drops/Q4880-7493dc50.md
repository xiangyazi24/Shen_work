# Problem 2.5: is the Catalan CMF a symmetric square?

## Executive conclusion

The spectral observation is exact and important, but it does **not** by itself prove that the printed \(3\times3\) matrix is a symmetric square.

The rigorous status is:

1. The recurrence quoted in the question is not the central-Delannoy recurrence. The correct recurrence for
   
   \[
   D_n=P_n(3)=1,3,13,63,321,\ldots
   \]
   
   is
   
   \[
   \boxed{(n+1)D_{n+1}=3(2n+1)D_n-nD_{n-1}.}
   \tag{0.1}
   \]

2. An explicit \(2\times2\) Delannoy transfer matrix is therefore known, and its symmetric square has exactly the normalized characteristic polynomial
   
   \[
   (\xi-1)(\xi^2-34\xi+1).
   \]

3. The printed matrix \(M(n)\) is **not literally** a symmetric square over \(\mathbf Q(n)\). Its determinant is not a cube, and its columns fail the Veronese rank-one identities.

4. After the stated Pochhammer twist
   
   \[
   H_n=(-16)^n(2)_n^2(3)_n^2
   \left(\frac52\right)_n
   \left(\frac72\right)_n^2,
   \]
   
   the determinant obstruction to an \(n\)-dependent rational gauge disappears. The normalized system has the correct formal spectrum, the correct determinant shift class, and an explicit symmetric-square conjugacy at \(n=\infty\).

5. What is **not** currently certified is an invertible rational matrix
   
   \[
   U(n)\in \mathrm{GL}_3(\mathbf Q(n))
   \]
   
   satisfying the exact product-compatible identity
   
   \[
   \boxed{
   M_H(n)U(n+1)=U(n)\operatorname{Sym}^2 B_D(n),
   }
   \tag{0.2}
   \]
   
   where \(M_H(n)=M(n)/(H_{n+1}/H_n)\). Matching Poincaré roots is only a necessary condition.

6. The decisive finite test is the existence of a rational moving Veronese conic. In the row convention used below, one must find a nonsingular symmetric matrix \(J(n)\) satisfying
   
   \[
   \boxed{
   \overline M(n)J(n+1)\overline M(n)^T
   =\left(\frac{n+1}{n+2}\right)^2J(n),
   }
   \tag{0.3}
   \]
   
   where \(\overline M\) is the balanced, \(H\)-normalized matrix. This equation is linear in the six entries of \(J\).

7. Even a positive symmetric-square certificate would **not immediately prove the Catalan limit**. A symmetric square identifies the homogeneous rank-three carrier, not the connection coefficient selected by the two initial rows. The plain Delannoy/Legendre module naturally carries the period \(\log 2\); Catalan's constant enters through an integrated or parameter-derivative extension. Moreover, the irrationality of \(G\) is not known, so there is no available “Delannoy irrationality measure for \(G\)” that can close the argument automatically.

The complete Sage program below performs all exact tests, searches both for \(U(n)\) and for the invariant conic, and verifies any candidate symbolically.

# 1. Correct Delannoy rank-two system

Put

\[
\alpha_n=\frac{n+1}{n+2},
\qquad
\beta_n=\frac{3(2n+3)}{n+2}.
\tag{1.1}
\]

For the row state

\[
r_n=(D_n,D_{n+1}),
\]

the recurrence (0.1), shifted once, gives

\[
D_{n+2}=-\alpha_nD_n+\beta_nD_{n+1}.
\]

Thus

\[
\boxed{
r_{n+1}=r_nB_D(n),
\qquad
B_D(n)=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&-\alpha_n\\
1&\beta_n
\end{pmatrix}.}
\tag{1.2}
\]

Its determinant and limiting characteristic polynomial are

\[
\det B_D(n)=\alpha_n=\frac{n+1}{n+2},
\]

\[
B_D(\infty)=
\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&6\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\chi_{B_D(\infty)}(t)=t^2-6t+1.
\]

The roots are

\[
3\pm2\sqrt2.
\]

## 1.1 Row-convention symmetric square

For a row action

\[
(x,y)\longmapsto(x,y)
\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix},
\]

the induced action on the quadratic row

\[
(x^2,xy,y^2)
\]

is

\[
\boxed{
\operatorname{Sym}^2_{\rm row}
\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
a^2&ab&b^2\\
2ac&ad+bc&2bd\\
c^2&cd&d^2
\end{pmatrix}.}
\tag{1.3}
\]

Therefore the explicit Delannoy-square transfer matrix is

\[
\boxed{
S_D(n)=\operatorname{Sym}^2_{\rm row}B_D(n)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&0&\alpha_n^2\\
0&-\alpha_n&-2\alpha_n\beta_n\\
1&\beta_n&\beta_n^2
\end{pmatrix}.}
\tag{1.4}
\]

Indeed,

\[
(D_n^2,D_nD_{n+1},D_{n+1}^2)S_D(n)
=(D_{n+1}^2,D_{n+1}D_{n+2},D_{n+2}^2).
\]

Its determinant is the required cube:

\[
\boxed{\det S_D(n)=\left(\frac{n+1}{n+2}\right)^3.}
\tag{1.5}
\]

Eliminating the mixed product gives

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&(2n+3)(n+3)^2u_{n+3}\\
&\quad-(2n+5)(35n^2+140n+131)u_{n+2}\\
&\quad+(2n+3)(35n^2+140n+131)u_{n+1}\\
&\quad-(2n+5)(n+1)^2u_n=0.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{1.6}
\]

Its Poincaré polynomial is

\[
\boxed{
\xi^3-35\xi^2+35\xi-1
=(\xi-1)(\xi^2-34\xi+1).}
\tag{1.7}
\]

Thus the spectral match is exact.

# 2. Why the printed matrix is not literally a symmetric square

For every \(2\times2\) matrix \(B\),

\[
\det\operatorname{Sym}^2(B)=(\det B)^3.
\tag{2.1}
\]

But

\[
\det M(n)
=-8(n+1)(n+2)^6(n+3)^5
(2n+3)^2(2n+5)^3(2n+7)^4.
\tag{2.2}
\]

Modulo cubes in \(\mathbf Q(n)^\times\), the factors

\[
(n+1)^1,
\quad(n+3)^2,
\quad(2n+3)^2,
\quad(2n+7)^1
\]

remain. Hence

\[
\boxed{
\det M(n)\notin \mathbf Q(n)^{\times3}.}
\tag{2.3}
\]

Therefore no \(B(n)\in\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbf Q(n))\) can satisfy

\[
M(n)=\operatorname{Sym}^2 B(n).
\]

The same conclusion holds after a constant conjugation, because determinant is a similarity invariant.

There are also direct Veronese identities. For a row symmetric square \(S=(s_{ij})\), one has

\[
s_{12}^2=s_{11}s_{13},
\qquad
s_{32}^2=s_{31}s_{33},
\]

\[
s_{21}^2=4s_{11}s_{31},
\qquad
s_{23}^2=4s_{13}s_{33}.
\tag{2.4}
\]

The printed entries do not satisfy these identities.

This rules out only a **literal** symmetric square. An \(n\)-dependent gauge changes the determinant by a multiplicative coboundary:

\[
\det\bigl(U(n)^{-1}M(n)U(n+1)\bigr)
=\det M(n)\frac{\det U(n+1)}{\det U(n)}.
\tag{2.5}
\]

That is why a hidden difference-module symmetric square remains possible.

# 3. The necessary rank-one twist

The one-step ratio of the stated Pochhammer product is

\[
\boxed{
\delta_H(n):=\frac{H_{n+1}}{H_n}
=-2(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(2n+5)(2n+7)^2.}
\tag{3.1}
\]

For a row trajectory

\[
X_{n+1}=X_nM(n),
\qquad X_n=H_n\widehat X_n,
\]

we obtain

\[
\widehat X_{n+1}=\widehat X_nM_H(n),
\qquad
\boxed{M_H(n)=\frac{M(n)}{\delta_H(n)}.}
\tag{3.2}
\]

Its determinant simplifies to

\[
\boxed{
\det M_H(n)
=\frac{(n+1)(2n+3)^2}
{(n+3)(2n+7)^2}.}
\tag{3.3}
\]

The raw matrix should therefore be viewed, at best, as a **rank-one twist of a symmetric square**:

\[
M(n)U(n+1)
=\delta_H(n)U(n)S_D(n).
\tag{3.4}
\]

Since \(\delta_H(n)\) is not a square in \(\mathbf Q(n)\), it cannot in general be absorbed into a rational \(2\times2\) matrix: multiplying a rank-two matrix by \(c(n)\) multiplies its symmetric square by \(c(n)^2\). Thus the untwisted object to test is \(M_H\), not \(M\).

# 4. Balance the degree staircase

Put

\[
D(n)=\operatorname{diag}(1,n+1,(n+1)^2)
\]

and define

\[
\boxed{
\overline M(n)=D(n)^{-1}M_H(n)D(n+1).}
\tag{4.1}
\]

Then \(\overline M(n)\) is bounded at infinity and

\[
\boxed{
A_\infty:=\lim_{n\to\infty}\overline M(n)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
17&-24&0\\
-12&17&0\\
8&-12&1
\end{pmatrix}.}
\tag{4.2}
\]

The limiting Delannoy square is

\[
\boxed{
S_\infty:=\lim_{n\to\infty}S_D(n)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&0&1\\
0&-1&-12\\
1&6&36
\end{pmatrix}.}
\tag{4.3}
\]

There is an explicit rational conjugacy:

\[
\boxed{
V_\infty=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&3&17\\
0&-2&-12\\
0&0&8
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
A_\infty V_\infty=V_\infty S_\infty.}
\tag{4.4}
\]

Thus the symmetric-square interpretation at infinity is not merely an eigenvalue observation: the limiting matrices are explicitly conjugate over \(\mathbf Q\).

The exact finite-\(n\) equation to solve is now

\[
\boxed{
\overline M(n)V(n+1)=V(n)S_D(n),
\qquad
V(n)\longrightarrow V_\infty.}
\tag{4.5}
\]

The gauge in the original normalized basis is

\[
U(n)=D(n)V(n).
\tag{4.6}
\]

# 5. Exact determinant constraint for the unknown gauge

From (4.5),

\[
\frac{\det V(n+1)}{\det V(n)}
=\frac{\det S_D(n)}{\det\overline M(n)}.
\tag{5.1}
\]

Since

\[
\det\overline M(n)
=\frac{(n+2)^3(2n+3)^2}
{(n+1)^2(n+3)(2n+7)^2},
\tag{5.2}
\]

we get

\[
\boxed{
\frac{\det V(n+1)}{\det V(n)}
=
\frac{(n+1)^5(n+3)(2n+7)^2}
{(n+2)^6(2n+3)^2}.}
\tag{5.3}
\]

A rational primitive is

\[
\boxed{
\det V(n)
=-\frac{(n+2)(2n+3)^2(2n+5)^2}{(n+1)^5}.}
\tag{5.4}
\]

The constant is chosen so that

\[
\lim_{n\to\infty}\det V(n)=-16=\det V_\infty.
\]

Consequently any valid \(V(n)\) with the normalization (4.4) must satisfy (5.4) exactly. For the unbalanced gauge \(U=D V\),

\[
\boxed{
\det U(n)
=-\frac{(n+2)(2n+3)^2(2n+5)^2}{(n+1)^2}.}
\tag{5.5}
\]

These formulas remove the determinant ambiguity from the search.

They prove that a rational gauge is **not excluded** by determinants. They do not prove that one exists.

# 6. The invariant-conic criterion

In the row quadratic basis \((X,Y,Z)=(x^2,xy,y^2)\), the Veronese cone is

\[
Y^2-XZ=0.
\]

Use the integral symmetric matrix

\[
\boxed{
J_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&0&-1\\
0&2&0\\
-1&0&0
\end{pmatrix},}
\tag{6.1}
\]

so that

\[
(X,Y,Z)J_0(X,Y,Z)^T=2(Y^2-XZ).
\]

For every \(2\times2\) matrix \(B\),

\[
\operatorname{Sym}^2_{\rm row}(B)
J_0
\operatorname{Sym}^2_{\rm row}(B)^T
=(\det B)^2J_0.
\tag{6.2}
\]

If (4.5) holds, define

\[
J(n)=V(n)J_0V(n)^T.
\tag{6.3}
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{
\overline M(n)J(n+1)\overline M(n)^T
=\alpha_n^2J(n),
\qquad
\alpha_n=\frac{n+1}{n+2}.}
\tag{6.4}
\]

This is a six-dimensional **linear** rational difference equation. A nonsingular rational solution is necessary for a symmetric-square lift. If its conic is split over \(\mathbf Q(n)\), it is also sufficient projectively: a projective transformation preserving a nonsingular split conic comes from \(\mathrm{PGL}_2\).

The limiting conic is explicit. From (4.4),

\[
J_\infty=V_\infty J_0V_\infty^T.
\]

Up to a scalar,

\[
\boxed{
J_\infty=
\begin{pmatrix}
-2&0&-1\\
0&1&0\\
-1&0&0
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
A_\infty J_\infty A_\infty^T=J_\infty.}
\tag{6.5}
\]

Thus the most efficient exact computation is:

1. solve (6.4) with \(J(n)\to J_\infty\);
2. check \(\det J(n)\ne0\);
3. parameterize its conic over \(\mathbf Q(n)\);
4. straighten it to \(J_0\);
5. recover the induced \(2\times2\) Möbius action;
6. compare that action with \(B_D(n)\).

A zero rational-solution space for (6.4) proves that the CMF is not a symmetric square. A nondegenerate split solution gives the desired projective lift.

# 7. Why a symmetric-square result would not by itself prove the Catalan limit

Suppose an exact gauge \(V(n)\) is found. Then the two CMF rows become rational quadratic combinations of two Delannoy solutions. This identifies the homogeneous carrier, but the limit is still a global connection ratio determined by the transformed initial rows.

The dominant coefficient would have the form

\[
L=
\frac{p_0U(0)w^{(2)}_+(0)}
     {q_0U(0)w^{(2)}_+(0)},
\tag{7.1}
\]

where \(w^{(2)}_+\) is the dominant adjoint functional of the symmetric square. Nothing in the symmetric-square identity forces (7.1) to equal \(G\).

There are three specific reasons.

## 7.1 The plain Delannoy period is not Catalan's normal function

A standard second independent Legendre solution is \(\mathsf Q_n(3)\), with

\[
\mathsf Q_0(3)=\frac12\log2.
\]

Thus the homogeneous Delannoy square naturally produces the period algebra generated by \(1\) and \(\log2\) (and quadratic products thereof). Catalan's constant enters through

\[
G=\frac12\int_0^1K(k)\,dk,
\]

which is an integrated or parameter-derivative extension, not merely the homogeneous symmetric square.

## 7.2 Initial data remain load-bearing

Replacing the numerator sequence by

\[
P_n\longmapsto P_n+cQ_n
\]

preserves the same matrix module, Poincaré roots, and symmetric-square structure, but changes the limit by \(c\). Therefore the module alone cannot determine the constant.

## 7.3 There is no known irrationality measure for \(G\)

The irrationality of Catalan's constant is still unknown. Hence no argument may invoke a known “two-dimensional Delannoy irrationality measure” to force the pairing

\[
(p_0-Gq_0)w_+(0)=0.
\]

Even after finding \(U\), one still needs one exact period or initial-connection certificate proving that pairing.

So a symmetric-square decomposition would be a major structural result, but it would not by itself close Problem 2.5.

def affine_matrix_equations(F0, Fbasis):
    """
    Solve F0(n) + sum c_j Fbasis[j](n) == 0 entrywise.

    Returns (particular_solution, kernel_basis, coefficient_matrix, rhs),
    or None if inconsistent.
    """
    m = len(Fbasis)
    rows = []
    rhs = []

    for i in range(F0.nrows()):
        for j in range(F0.ncols()):
            rats = [K(F0[i,j])] + [K(F[i,j]) for F in Fbasis]

            common = R(1)
            for f in rats:
                common = lcm(common, R(f.denominator()))

            polys = [rational_to_polynomial(K(common)*f) for f in rats]
            maxdeg = max([-1] + [p.degree() for p in polys if p != 0])

            for d in range(maxdeg + 1):
                row = [coefficient_or_zero(p, d) for p in polys[1:]]
                b = -coefficient_or_zero(polys[0], d)

                if any(x != 0 for x in row):
                    rows.append(row)
                    rhs.append(b)
                elif b != 0:
                    return None

    if m == 0:
        return None if any(x != 0 for x in F0.list()) else (vector(QQ, []), [], None, None)

    A = matrix(QQ, rows)
    b = vector(QQ, rhs)

    Aug = A.augment(matrix(QQ, len(rhs), 1, list(rhs)))
    if A.rank() != Aug.rank():
        return None

    particular = A.solve_right(b)
    kernel = A.right_kernel().basis()
    return particular, kernel, A, b

def proper_matrix_basis(den, symmetric=False):
    """
    Basis for matrices whose entries are proper rational functions p(n)/den(n).
    For symmetric=True, use the six symmetric matrix positions.
    """
    den = R(den)
    max_degree = den.degree() - 1
    if max_degree < 0:
        return []

    basis = []

    if not symmetric:
        positions = [(i,j) for i in range(3) for j in range(3)]
        for i, j in positions:
            for k in range(max_degree + 1):
                E = matrix(K, 3, 3, 0)
                E[i,j] = n^k/den
                basis.append(E)
    else:
        positions = [(0,0),(0,1),(0,2),(1,1),(1,2),(2,2)]
        for i, j in positions:
            for k in range(max_degree + 1):
                E = matrix(K, 3, 3, 0)
                E[i,j] = n^k/den
                E[j,i] = n^k/den
                basis.append(E)

    return basis


def combine_affine(base, basis, coeffs):
    A = matrix(K, base)
    for c, B in zip(coeffs, basis):
        A += QQ(c)*B
    return A


def verify_V(V, verbose=True):
    residual = Mbar*shmat(V) - V*SD
    ok = is_zero_matrix(residual)

    if verbose:
        print("exact gauge residual zero:", ok)
        print("det(V) / target =", factor(V.det()/detV_target) if V.det() != 0 else 0)
        print("limit(V) =")
        print(mat_at_infinity(V))

    return ok and V.det() != 0


def search_V(den):
    """
    Search V(n)=Vinf+proper_part with the prescribed common denominator.
    This is an exact linear problem.
    """
    den = R(den)
    basis = proper_matrix_basis(den, symmetric=False)

    F0 = Mbar*Vinf - Vinf*SD
    Fbasis = [Mbar*shmat(B) - B*SD for B in basis]

    result = affine_matrix_equations(F0, Fbasis)
    if result is None:
        print("No V for denominator", factor(den))
        return None

    particular, kernel, A, b = result
    print("V search denominator:", factor(den))
    print("unknowns:", len(basis), "equations:", A.nrows(),
          "affine kernel dimension:", len(kernel))

    # First try the particular solution.
    coefficient_vectors = [particular]

    # Then try small perturbations in kernel directions.
    for h in kernel:
        for c in [-2, -1, 1, 2]:
            coefficient_vectors.append(particular + c*h)

    # A few deterministic two-direction combinations.
    if len(kernel) >= 2:
        for c1 in [-1, 1]:
            for c2 in [-1, 1]:
                coefficient_vectors.append(particular + c1*kernel[0] + c2*kernel[1])

    for coeffs in coefficient_vectors:
        V = combine_affine(Vinf, basis, coeffs)
        if is_zero_matrix(Mbar*shmat(V) - V*SD) and V.det() != 0:
            print("FOUND invertible rational V")
            verify_V(V)
            return V

    print("Linear solutions exist, but no invertible candidate was found in the tested affine combinations.")
    return None

def recover_projective_B(H):
    """
    Given H = scalar * Sym^2_row(B), recover a projective B
    on the chart H[0,0] != 0.

    We normalize the upper-left entry of B to 1.
    """
    H = matrix(K, H)
    if H[0,0] == 0:
        raise ValueError("change chart: H[0,0]=0")

    scale = H[0,0]
    b = H[0,1]/scale
    c = H[1,0]/(2*scale)
    d = H[1,1]/scale - b*c

    B = matrix(K, [[1, b], [c, d]])
    residual = H - scale*sym2_row(B)

    if not is_zero_matrix(residual):
        raise ValueError("H is not a projective row symmetric square on this chart")

    return scale, B

# 9. How to interpret the computation

## Outcome A: an invertible \(V(n)\) is found

Then the script verifies the exact identity

\[
\overline M(n)V(n+1)=V(n)S_D(n),
\]

not merely finitely many values. The original normalized gauge is

\[
U(n)=D(n)V(n),
\]

and the raw matrix satisfies

\[
M(n)U(n+1)
=\delta_H(n)U(n)\operatorname{Sym}^2 B_D(n).
\]

This is a complete twisted-symmetric-square certificate.

## Outcome B: a conic \(J(n)\) is found but no \(V(n)\)

Then the system is projectively a symmetric square, provided the conic is split over \(\mathbf Q(n)\). One must parameterize the conic, straighten it to \(J_0\), recover the induced Möbius matrix, and compare that matrix with \(B_D(n)\) up to a rational \(2\times2\) gauge.

## Outcome C: no conic is found for the tested denominators

This disproves only those denominator classes. A universal nonexistence result requires either:

- the complete rational-solution algorithm for the induced six-dimensional conic system; or
- an absolute-irreducibility/symmetric-product algorithm for the scalar order-three operator.

Certified algorithms for third-order difference equations in terms of second-order equations are described in:

- Yongjae Cha, *Closed form solutions of linear difference equations in terms of symmetric products*, arXiv:1301.4983;
- H. Bou KaedBey, M. van Hoeij, M. C. Tsui, *Solving Order 3 Difference Equations*, arXiv:2402.03868;
- H. Bou KaedBey, M. van Hoeij, M. C. Tsui, *Solving Third Order Linear Difference Equations in Terms of Second Order Equations*, arXiv:2402.11121.

Those algorithms are the appropriate route if the elementary denominator ladder fails.

# Final answer to the four tasks

## 1. Explicit rank-two Delannoy matrix

The exact natural choice is

\[
\boxed{
B_D(n)=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&-\dfrac{n+1}{n+2}\\[2mm]
1&\dfrac{3(2n+3)}{n+2}
\end{pmatrix}.}
\]

Its row symmetric square is (1.4), and its scalar cyclic recurrence is (1.6).

## 2. Relation to \(P_n(3)\)

The first solution is

\[
D_n=P_n(3)
={}_2F_1\!\left(\begin{matrix}-n,n+1\\1\end{matrix};-1\right).
\]

The full rank-two space is generated by \(P_n(3)\) and a second Legendre solution. The symmetric-square space is generated by

\[
P_n(3)^2,
\qquad P_n(3)\mathsf Q_n(3),
\qquad \mathsf Q_n(3)^2.
\]

## 3. The rational gauge \(U(n)\)

No explicit invertible rational \(U(n)\) is currently certified. What is known exactly is:

\[
U(n)=D(n)V(n),
\qquad
V(n)\to
\begin{pmatrix}
1&3&17\\0&-2&-12\\0&0&8
\end{pmatrix},
\]

and

\[
\det V(n)
=-\frac{(n+2)(2n+3)^2(2n+5)^2}{(n+1)^5}.
\]

The displayed Sage program performs the exact finite search and verifies any candidate.

## 4. Effect on the Catalan proof

A positive symmetric-square result would explain the silver-ratio spectrum and reduce the homogeneous rank-three dynamics to a rank-two Legendre system. It would **not** by itself prove

\[
\frac{P_n}{Q_n}\to G.
\]

The remaining load-bearing statement would still be

\[
(p_0-Gq_0)w_+(0)=0,
\]

or an equivalent period/integral identity. Catalan's constant belongs to the integrated elliptic extension, not automatically to the homogeneous symmetric square.
