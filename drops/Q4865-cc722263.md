# Problem 2.5: exact integrated-\(K\) pullback and the order-5 Ore-factor test

## Executive conclusion

The proposed factorization

\[
L_5=L_2\circ L_3,
\qquad \operatorname{ord}L_2=2,
\qquad \operatorname{ord}L_3=3,
\]

is an exact, decidable Ore-algebra question, but it is **not determined** by the data

\[
\operatorname{ord}L_5=5,\qquad \deg_zL_5=11,\qquad
\chi(\xi)=(\xi-1)(\xi^2-34\xi+1).
\]

The coefficients of the order-5 operator were not included in the question, so an honest yes/no verdict cannot be inferred from its order and Poincaré polynomial. The script below reconstructs that operator directly from the printed CMF, proves the guessed operator against held-out coefficients, and performs exact right Ore division.

There are two important corrections before running the test.

1. The integrated elliptic equation is third order:

   \[
   k(1-k^2)Y''' +(1-3k^2)Y''-kY'=0.
   \]

   Equivalently,

   \[
   L_{\rm int,k}
   =\bigl(k(1-k^2)D_k^2+(1-3k^2)D_k-k\bigr)D_k.
   \]

2. The raw pullback \(Y(k(z))\) is ramified at \(z=0\). Its local exponents are

   \[
   0,\frac12,\frac12.
   \]

   The more natural analytic Catalan section is

   \[
   U(z)=\frac{Y(k(z))}{k(z)},
   \qquad U(\rho)=Y(1)=G,
   \qquad \rho=17-12\sqrt2.
   \]

   Its operator is a rational gauge conjugate of the raw pullback. The code tests **both** candidates, as well as the homogeneous Brafman operator.

Even if the exact division succeeds, it does **not by itself prove** that the CMF limit is \(G\). If \(L_5\) is genuinely the minimal annihilator of \(\widehat F\) and has order five, then \(\widehat F\) is not itself killed by the order-three right factor. The equality

\[
L_5=L_2L_3
\]

would only say that \(\ker L_3\) is a differential submodule of \(\ker L_5\), while

\[
L_3\widehat F\in\ker L_2.
\]

One still needs an initial-value or connection-matrix certificate identifying the dominant singular coefficient of the CMF numerator and denominator with the distinguished section \(U(\rho)=G\).

# 1. Exact pullback of the integrated-\(K\) equation

Put

\[
k(z)=\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z},
\qquad
\Delta(z)=z^2-34z+1.
\]

For

\[
y(z)=Y(k(z)),
\]

the chain rule gives the exact equation

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&4z^2(1-z^2)^2\Delta(z)y'''\\
&\quad+8z(1-z^2)
 \bigl(1-54z+103z^2+48z^3-2z^4\bigr)y''\\
&\quad+\bigl(1-158z+1127z^2-852z^3-529z^4
             -110z^5+9z^6\bigr)y'=0.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{1}
\]

Let

\[
\theta=zD_z,
\]

and define

\[
A(z)=(1-z^2)^2\Delta(z),
\]

\[
B(z)=(1-z^2)
\bigl(1-54z+103z^2+48z^3-2z^4\bigr),
\]

\[
C(z)=1-158z+1127z^2-852z^3-529z^4-110z^5+9z^6.
\]

An Euler form of the same operator is

\[
\boxed{
L_{3,Y}
=\bigl[4A(z)(\theta-1)(\theta-2)
       +8B(z)(\theta-1)+C(z)\bigr]\theta.}
\tag{2}
\]

The right factor \(\theta\) is the constant solution of the integrated system.

## 1.1 Singularities

The displayed cyclic operator has singularities at

\[
z=0,\quad z=-1,\quad z=1,\quad z=\rho,
\quad z=\rho^{-1},\quad z=\infty.
\]

The point \(z=-1\) is an apparent ramification singularity: \(k'(-1)=0\), while \(k(-1)=2i\sqrt2\) is an ordinary point of the original equation. Its exponents are \(0,2,4\).

The point \(z=1\) is not optional; it lies over \(k=\infty\).

At \(z=0\), the indicial polynomial is

\[
\boxed{r(2r-1)^2.}
\tag{3}
\]

This gives the exponents \(0,1/2,1/2\).

## 1.2 The analytic gauge \(U=Y/k\)

Write

\[
y(z)=k(z)U(z).
\]

Since

\[
\frac{k'(z)}{k(z)}
=\frac{1+z}{2z(1-z)},
\]

the operator annihilating \(U\) is

\[
\boxed{
L_{3,U}=k(z)^{-1}L_{3,Y}k(z),}
\tag{4}
\]

which is obtained in an ordinary differential Ore algebra by the substitution

\[
D_z\longmapsto D_z+rac{1+z}{2z(1-z)}.
\]

This operator has rational coefficients despite the square root in \(k(z)\). Its local exponents at zero are

\[
-\frac12,0,0.
\]

The distinguished branch is analytic and satisfies

\[
U(0)=\frac\pi4,
\qquad U(\rho)=G.
\]

Up to a harmless constant, one can equivalently use

\[
U(z)=\frac{1-z}{\sqrt z}\,Y(k(z)).
\]

# 2. The homogeneous Brafman comparison operator

The homogeneous period

\[
F_D(z)=\sum_{n\ge0}D_n^2z^n
=\frac{2}{\pi(1-z)}K(k(z))
\]

is annihilated by

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
L_D={}&(2\theta-3)\theta^2
-z(2\theta+1)(35\theta^2-9)\\
&+z^2(2\theta+1)(35\theta^2+70\theta+26)
-z^3(2\theta+5)(\theta+1)^2.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{5}
\]

Its coefficient recurrence has exactly the normalized characteristic polynomial

\[
(\lambda-1)(\lambda^2-34\lambda+1).
\]

Thus three distinct order-three candidates should be tested:

\[
L_{3,Y},\qquad L_{3,U},\qquad L_D.
\]

A factorization by \(L_D\) would identify a homogeneous Delannoy-square submodule. A factorization by \(L_{3,U}\) would be more directly relevant to the Catalan endpoint, although neither result alone determines the CMF connection constant.

# 3. Exact Sage/`ore_algebra` computation

The following script is self-contained. It:

1. generates \(\widehat Q_n=Q_{n,0}/H_n\) directly from the printed CMF;
2. reconstructs the claimed Euler operator of order \(5\) and \(z\)-degree \(11\) by exact linear algebra;
3. validates it on unused coefficients;
4. constructs the three order-three candidates;
5. performs exact right and left Ore division;
6. optionally verifies that the order-five guess is a right factor of the known order-thirteen operator.

```python
from sage.all import *
from ore_algebra import OreAlgebra
from functools import reduce

# ================================================================
# 0. Differential Ore algebra Q(z)<Dz>
# ================================================================

Rz.<z> = PolynomialRing(QQ)
Kz = Rz.fraction_field()
Oz.<Dz> = OreAlgebra(Kz)
z = Kz(z)
theta = z * Dz


def op_order(P):
    """Version-independent order helper."""
    if P == 0:
        return -1
    try:
        return P.order()
    except AttributeError:
        return P.degree()


def op_lc(P):
    return P[op_order(P)]


def right_quo_rem(A, B):
    """
    Exact right division: A = Q*B + R, ord(R) < ord(B).

    Coefficients are stored on the left.  If r=ord(A)-ord(B), the
    leading coefficient of (c*Dz^r)*B is c*lc(B), so the usual
    Euclidean cancellation works over the coefficient field Q(z).
    """
    if B == 0:
        raise ZeroDivisionError("right division by zero operator")

    Q = Oz.zero()
    R = A
    b = op_order(B)
    b_lc = op_lc(B)

    while R != 0 and op_order(R) >= b:
        r = op_order(R) - b
        c = op_lc(R) / b_lc
        T = c * Dz**r
        Q += T
        R -= T * B

    return Q, R


def formal_adjoint(P):
    """Formal adjoint: (a(z) Dz^j)^* = (-Dz)^j a(z)."""
    return sum(((-Dz)**j) * Kz(P[j])
               for j in range(op_order(P) + 1))


def left_factor_test(A, B):
    """
    Test whether A=B*Q.  Since (B*Q)^*=Q^*B^*, this is right
    division of A^* by B^*.
    """
    Qstar, Rstar = right_quo_rem(formal_adjoint(A),
                                 formal_adjoint(B))
    if Rstar != 0:
        return False, None, Rstar
    Q = formal_adjoint(Qstar)
    return A == B * Q, Q, Oz.zero()


# ================================================================
# 1. Printed 3x3 CMF and the H_n normalization
# ================================================================

def Mstep(k):
    n = QQ(k)

    m11 = (-2*n-5)*(n+3)^2 * (
        136*n^4+1424*n^3+5548*n^2+9551*n+6141)
    m12 = (
        384*n^6+6384*n^5+44168*n^4+162698*n^3
        +336377*n^2+369933*n+169011)
    m13 = -(
        480*n^4+4980*n^3+19210*n^2+32690*n+20730)

    m21 = (n+2)^2*(n+3)^2*(4*n+10) * (
        48*n^3+386*n^2+1017*n+879)
    m22 = (n+2)^2 * (-272*n^5-3848*n^4-21732*n^3
                       -61184*n^2-85761*n-47808)
    m23 = (n+2)^2 * (
        320*n^3+2540*n^2+6610*n+5640)

    m31 = (-4*n-10)*(n+2)^2*(n+3)^2 * (
        32*n^4+302*n^3+1037*n^2+1530*n+813)
    m32 = (n+2)^2 * (
        192*n^6+2984*n^5+19116*n^4+64452*n^3
        +120256*n^2+117279*n+46476)
    m33 = (n+2)^2 * (-16*n^5-408*n^4-2912*n^3
                       -8884*n^2-12254*n-6240)

    return matrix(QQ, [
        [m11, m12, m13],
        [m21, m22, m23],
        [m31, m32, m33],
    ])


def delta(k):
    """H_{k+1}/H_k."""
    n = QQ(k)
    return -2*(n+2)^2*(n+3)^2*(2*n+5)*(2*n+7)^2


def qhat_terms(Nterms, column=0):
    """
    Return [Q_{n,column}/H_n] for n=0,...,Nterms-1.
    """
    q = vector(QQ, [33750, -36000, 9000])
    H = QQ(1)
    out = []

    for n in range(Nterms):
        out.append(q[column] / H)
        q = q * Mstep(n)
        H *= delta(n)

    return out


# ================================================================
# 2. Exact Euler-operator reconstruction
# ================================================================

def primitive_integer_vector(v):
    den = lcm([QQ(c).denominator() for c in v])
    w = [ZZ(QQ(c) * den) for c in v]
    nz = [abs(a) for a in w if a != 0]
    g = reduce(gcd, nz) if nz else ZZ(1)
    w = [a // g for a in w]
    first = next((a for a in w if a != 0), ZZ(1))
    if first < 0:
        w = [-a for a in w]
    return vector(ZZ, w)


def euler_residual(seq, coeff_vector, labels, n):
    """
    Coefficient of z^n in
        sum_{j,m} c[j,m] z^j theta^m sum a_n z^n.
    """
    ans = QQ(0)
    for c, (j, m) in zip(coeff_vector, labels):
        if n >= j:
            ans += QQ(c) * (n-j)^m * seq[n-j]
    return ans


def guess_euler_operator(seq, order=5, z_degree=11,
                         training_terms=None):
    """
    Guess L=sum_{j=0}^d z^j P_j(theta), deg P_j<=order.
    The claimed dimensions are order=5, d=11, hence 72 slots.
    """
    labels = [(j, m)
              for j in range(z_degree + 1)
              for m in range(order + 1)]

    if training_terms is None:
        training_terms = len(seq) - 20

    rows = []
    for n in range(training_terms):
        row = []
        for j, m in labels:
            row.append(QQ(0) if n < j
                       else QQ((n-j)^m) * seq[n-j])
        rows.append(row)

    A = matrix(QQ, rows)
    ker = A.right_kernel()
    print("Euler guess matrix:", A.nrows(), "x", A.ncols())
    print("kernel dimension:", ker.dimension())

    if ker.dimension() != 1:
        raise RuntimeError(
            "Expected a one-dimensional kernel.  Increase Nterms or "
            "change the claimed order/degree.")

    c = primitive_integer_vector(ker.basis()[0])

    # Held-out exact verification.
    bad = []
    for n in range(training_terms, len(seq)):
        r = euler_residual(seq, c, labels, n)
        if r != 0:
            bad.append((n, r))
    if bad:
        raise ArithmeticError(
            "The guessed operator failed held-out terms: %s" % (bad[:3],))

    L = Oz.zero()
    for cc, (j, m) in zip(c, labels):
        if cc:
            L += Kz(cc) * z**j * theta**m

    # Also return the commutative Euler polynomials P_j(T).
    RT.<T> = PolynomialRing(QQ)
    P = []
    for j in range(z_degree + 1):
        pj = RT.zero()
        for m in range(order + 1):
            idx = labels.index((j, m))
            pj += QQ(c[idx]) * T**m
        P.append(pj)

    return L, P, c, labels


# Generate substantially more terms than the 72 unknown slots.
seq = qhat_terms(150, column=0)
L5, Pj, coeff_vector, labels = guess_euler_operator(
    seq, order=5, z_degree=11, training_terms=120)

print("\nGuessed operator order:", op_order(L5))
print("Euler coefficient polynomials P_j(T):")
for j, pj in enumerate(Pj):
    if pj:
        print("j =", j, ":", pj.factor())

assert op_order(L5) == 5

# Fast local diagnostic at z=0.
print("indicial polynomial at z=0:", Pj[0].factor())


# ================================================================
# 3. The three exact order-3 candidates
# ================================================================

Delta = z^2 - 34*z + 1
A0 = (1-z^2)^2 * Delta
B0 = (1-z^2) * (1 - 54*z + 103*z^2 + 48*z^3 - 2*z^4)
C0 = 1 - 158*z + 1127*z^2 - 852*z^3 - 529*z^4 - 110*z^5 + 9*z^6

# Raw y(z)=Y(k(z)) pullback.
L3_Y = (4*A0*(theta-1)*(theta-2)
        + 8*B0*(theta-1)
        + C0) * theta


def conjugate_by_g(L, gprime_over_g):
    """
    If y=g*u and L(y)=0, return g^{-1} L g, annihilating u.
    In a differential Ore algebra this is D -> D+g'/g.
    """
    S = Dz + Kz(gprime_over_g)
    return sum(Kz(L[j]) * S**j for j in range(op_order(L)+1))


# Analytic section U=Y/k.  Constants in k are irrelevant.
hk = (1+z) / (2*z*(1-z))
L3_U = conjugate_by_g(L3_Y, hk)

# Homogeneous Brafman/Delannoy-square operator.
L3_D = (
    (2*theta-3)*theta^2
    - z*(2*theta+1)*(35*theta^2-9)
    + z^2*(2*theta+1)*(35*theta^2+70*theta+26)
    - z^3*(2*theta+5)*(theta+1)^2
)

assert op_order(L3_Y) == 3
assert op_order(L3_U) == 3
assert op_order(L3_D) == 3


# ================================================================
# 4. Exact factor tests
# ================================================================

candidates = {
    "raw_Y_pullback": L3_Y,
    "analytic_Y_over_k": L3_U,
    "Brafman_Delannoy_square": L3_D,
}

for name, L3 in candidates.items():
    print("\n===", name, "===")

    Q2, Rright = right_quo_rem(L5, L3)
    print("right factor?", Rright == 0)
    if Rright == 0:
        print("right quotient order:", op_order(Q2))
        print("verification L5 == Q2*L3:", L5 == Q2*L3)
        print("Q2 =", Q2)
    else:
        print("right remainder order:", op_order(Rright))
        print("right remainder:", Rright)

    is_left, Qleft, Rleft = left_factor_test(L5, L3)
    print("left factor?", is_left)
    if is_left:
        print("left quotient order:", op_order(Qleft))
        print("verification L5 == L3*Qleft:", L5 == L3*Qleft)
        print("Qleft =", Qleft)

def eval_poly_at_operator(p, X):

    )
```

# 4. Automatic pullback check on the rational square-root cover

For an independent check of the chain-rule computation, set \(z=x^2\). Then

\[
k(x)=\frac{4\sqrt2x}{1-x^2}
\]

is rational over \(\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)(x)\). The differential operator \(D_k\) pulls back to

\[
D_k=\frac1{k'(x)}D_x.
\]

The following short block constructs the pullback without using the pre-expanded formula (1).

```python
K2.<rt2> = QuadraticField(2)
Rx.<x> = PolynomialRing(K2)
Fx = Rx.fraction_field()
Ox.<Dx> = OreAlgebra(Fx)
x = Fx(x)

kx = 4*rt2*x/(1-x^2)
Dk_pull = (1/kx.derivative()) * Dx

L3_x = (
    kx*(1-kx^2) * Dk_pull^3
    + (1-3*kx^2) * Dk_pull^2
    - kx * Dk_pull
)

print("rational-cover pullback order:",
      L3_x.order() if hasattr(L3_x, 'order') else L3_x.degree())
print("rational-cover pullback:", L3_x)
```

This calculation is often more reliable than asking a general composition routine to handle the algebraic substitution \(\sqrt z\).

# 5. How to interpret the exact output

## Case A: `analytic_Y_over_k right factor? True`

Then

\[
L_5=Q_2L_{3,U}
\]

with \(\operatorname{ord}Q_2=2\). This proves that the analytic integrated-elliptic module is a differential submodule of the order-five module.

It still does not prove

\[
\lim P_n/Q_n=G.
\]

Because \(L_5\) is assumed minimal of order five, \(\widehat F\notin\ker L_{3,U}\). Instead,

\[
L_{3,U}\widehat F\in\ker Q_2.
\]

The remaining finite calculation is to construct the connection matrix and show that the CMF numerator-denominator functional selects the branch satisfying

\[
U(\rho)=G.
\]

One practical certificate is an Ore intertwiner \(T\) and rational functions \(a,b\) for which

\[
T\widehat F=a+bU,
\]

followed by exact matching of the Frobenius jet at \(z=0\).

## Case B: only `raw_Y_pullback right factor? True`

Then the order-five module contains the ramified integrated system, but the comparison with an ordinary Taylor series still requires the gauge \(Y\mapsto Y/k\) or a shearing on the quadratic cover.

## Case C: only `Brafman_Delannoy_square right factor? True`

Then the order-five operator contains the homogeneous symmetric-square period, not yet the Catalan Abel extension. One must separately construct the extension class carrying

\[
Y(1)=G.
\]

## Case D: all exact right remainders are nonzero

Then the relation is not a literal factorization. This does **not** rule out an Ore-module equivalence. The correct next test is a finite intertwiner equation

\[
L_5T=RL_{3,U},
\qquad
T=t_0(z)+t_1(z)D_z+t_2(z)D_z^2,
\]

with \(R\) of order at most four. Literal factorization is the special case \(T=1\).

The denominator support for \(t_i\) should be built from

\[
z(1-z)(1+z)(z^2-34z+1)
\]

and from the singular factors of the leading coefficient of \(L_5\).

# 6. Why factorization alone cannot identify the limit

Suppose the exact output is

\[
L_5=Q_2L_{3,U}.
\]

The solution-space sequence is then of the form

\[
0\longrightarrow\ker L_{3,U}
\longrightarrow\ker L_5
\xrightarrow{L_{3,U}}\ker Q_2
\longrightarrow0
\]

locally away from singular points. This is structural information, not a connection coefficient.

The CMF limit is the ratio of the dominant singular amplitudes of two specific Taylor solutions at \(z=\rho\). Replacing the numerator solution by

\[
P(z)+cQ(z)
\]

preserves the same differential operator and all local singular data, while shifting the quotient limit by \(c\). Therefore no factorization, Poincaré polynomial, or local monodromy table can determine the constant without the initial-row matching.

A complete proof after a successful factorization needs:

1. exact reconstruction and certification of \(L_5\);
2. exact factor or intertwiner certificate with \(L_{3,U}\);
3. a rational connection map from the CMF numerator and denominator solutions to a Frobenius basis of \(L_{3,U}\);
4. proof that the selected section is \(U=Y/k\), rather than another linear combination;
5. continuation to \(z=\rho\), where \(k(\rho)=1\) and \(U(\rho)=G\).

Items 2–4 are the load-bearing algebraic data.

---

# Final answer

The order-five factorization is plausible—especially as a desingularizing left multiple of the ramified order-three pullback—but it is **not implied** by the stated spectrum and cannot be certified without the actual order-five coefficients. The code above reconstructs those coefficients from the CMF and performs the exact test.

The factor that is most relevant to Catalan's constant is not merely the raw operator for \(Y(k(z))\), but its rational gauge conjugate for

\[
U(z)=\frac{Y(k(z))}{k(z)},
\qquad U(\rho)=G.
\]

Finally, even a successful equality \(L_5=L_2L_{3,U}\) would not on its own prove the CMF limit. It must be supplemented by an exact connection/initial-value certificate identifying the two printed CMF rows with the distinguished Catalan section.
