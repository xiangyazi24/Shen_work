# Catalan CMF: how the dominant connection coefficient can actually be proved

## Executive verdict

The most tractable route is **Approach 1, but in a sharpened form**:

> Do not try to identify the CMF with the homogeneous symmetric square of the Delannoy equation.  Identify it with the **integrated elliptic-period extension** and produce an exact Ore intertwiner or first-order gauge.  Then use the endpoint identity
> \[
> Y(1)=\frac12\int_0^1K(s)\,ds=G
> \]
> to evaluate the dominant connection coefficient.

The formal powers
\[
(\lambda_+,\tau_+)=(17+12\sqrt2,0),\qquad
(\lambda_0,\tau_0)=(1,-3),\qquad
(\lambda_-,\tau_-)=(17-12\sqrt2,0)
\]
are not those of the naive Delannoy symmetric square.  That is not an obstruction to the integrated-period route.  The integrated equation is a nontrivial rank-three extension of the rank-two elliptic equation by a constant solution, not a symmetric square.

There are two explicit integral/Padé formulas worth separating.

1. **An exact integral identity for the actual printed CMF** exists:
   \[
   G\mathbf q_N-\mathbf p_N
   =\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}\,\mathbf Q_N(t^2)\,dt,
   \qquad
   \mathbf Q_N(X)=\bigl(\mathbf q_0-(1+X)\mathbf p_0\bigr)M_0\cdots M_{N-1}.
   \]
   This identity is completely rigorous and CMF-specific, but it is a moment lift of the same matrix product; by itself it does not prove subdominant growth.

2. **A genuine positive Stieltjes–Padé identity for Catalan's constant** also exists:
   \[
   d\sigma(x)=\frac{-\log x}{4\sqrt x}\,dx,
   \qquad
   F(z)=\int_0^1\frac{d\sigma(x)}{z-x},
   \qquad F(-1)=-G.
   \]
   The usual orthogonal-polynomial Padé approximants converge to \(-G\), with an exact positive-measure error formula.  They are, however, not the printed CMF approximants under a direct same-index rational or hypergeometric scalar gauge.

Thus Approach 2 proves a different approximation theorem unless an additional, presently missing, finite identification is supplied.  Approach 3 is excellent for discovering the missing map, but arbitrary-precision zero detection is not an exact proof.

The exact missing artifact is one of the following equivalent certificates:

- an Ore intertwiner between the normalized CMF differential module and the pulled-back integrated elliptic module;
- a rational first-order gauge between their companion systems, allowing Frobenius shearing at the singular base point;
- or a matrix-valued creative-telescoping identity producing the CMF error row from an independently defined logarithmic period.

Once any one of these is found, the equality
\[
(\mathbf p_0-G\mathbf q_0)\,w_+(0)=0
\]
follows by a short rigorous argument.  I give that argument and the precise certificate below.

## 1. Normalize the CMF and isolate the exact statement

Let
\[
\mathbf p_0=(30921,-32972,8240),
\qquad
\mathbf q_0=(33750,-36000,9000),
\]
and let
\[
T_N=M(0)M(1)\cdots M(N-1).
\]
The raw matrix has a common hypergeometric scale.  A convenient normalization is
\[
H_N=(-16)^N(2)_N^2(3)_N^2
\left(\frac52\right)_N
\left(\frac72\right)_N^2,
\]
whose one-step quotient is
\[
\delta(N):=\frac{H_{N+1}}{H_N}
=-2(N+2)^2(N+3)^2(2N+5)(2N+7)^2.
\]
Put
\[
M_H(N)=\frac{M(N)}{\delta(N)}.
\]
Multiplication of every row by the common scalar \(H_N\) does not change any quotient \(P_{N,j}/Q_{N,j}\).

For a regular system at infinity it is useful to balance the coordinates by
\[
D_N=\operatorname{diag}(1,N+1,(N+1)^2),
\qquad
A_N=D_N^{-1}M_H(N)D_{N+1}.
\]
Then
\[
A_N=C+O(N^{-1}),
\qquad
C=
\begin{pmatrix}
17&-24&0\\
-12&17&0\\
8&-12&1
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
The eigenvalues of \(C\) are
\[
\lambda_+=17+12\sqrt2,\qquad 1,\qquad
\lambda_-=17-12\sqrt2.
\]

For the row convention \(x_{N+1}=x_NA_N\), the dominant coefficient functional is a column solution of the adjoint transport
\[
A_Nw_+(N+1)=w_+(N)
\]
with the Birkhoff normalization at infinity.  The coefficient of the dominant mode in the trajectory starting from a row \(r\) is, up to the fixed common normalization,
\[
c_+(r)=r\,w_+(0).
\]
Consequently
\[
\frac{\mathbf p_N}{\mathbf q_N}\longrightarrow G
\]
in every nondegenerate output column if and only if
\[
\boxed{(\mathbf p_0-G\mathbf q_0)w_+(0)=0,}
\]
provided \(\mathbf q_0w_+(0)\ne0\).

### A useful criterion

Let
\[
\mathbf e_N=(\mathbf p_0-G\mathbf q_0)A_0A_1\cdots A_{N-1}.
\]
Birkhoff–Trjitzinsky theory gives
\[
\mathbf e_N
=c_+\lambda_+^N(\ell_++O(N^{-1}))
+c_0N^{-3}(\ell_0+O(N^{-1}))
+c_-\lambda_-^N(\ell_-+O(N^{-1})).
\]
Therefore:

> **Dominant-mode exclusion lemma.**  Any independent proof that
> \[
> \|\mathbf e_N\|=o(\lambda_+^N)
> \]
> proves \(c_+=0\), hence proves
> \((\mathbf p_0-G\mathbf q_0)w_+(0)=0\).

This is the analytic target for an integral or Padé construction.  One does not need the full asymptotic constant; any strict improvement over the dominant scale is enough.

## 2. An exact integral identity for the actual CMF

Define the row-vector polynomial
\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf Q_0(X)
&=\mathbf q_0-(1+X)\mathbf p_0\\
&=(2829-30921X,\,-3028+32972X,\,760-8240X),
\end{aligned}
\]
and set
\[
\mathbf Q_N(X)=\mathbf Q_0(X)T_N.
\]
Since \(T_N\) is independent of \(X\),
\[
\boxed{\mathbf Q_N(X)=\mathbf q_N-(1+X)\mathbf p_N.}
\]
Let
\[
W(t)=\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}.
\]
The elementary moments are
\[
\int_0^1W(t)\,dt=G,
\qquad
\int_0^1W(t)(1+t^2)\,dt
=\int_0^1(-\log t)\,dt=1.
\]
It follows immediately, componentwise, that
\[
\boxed{
G\mathbf q_N-\mathbf p_N
=\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}\,\mathbf Q_N(t^2)\,dt.
}
\tag{2.1}
\]
Using
\[
\int_0^1\!\int_0^1 f(xy)\,dx\,dy
=\int_0^1f(t)(-\log t)\,dt,
\]
one also gets
\[
\boxed{
G\mathbf q_N-\mathbf p_N
=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{\mathbf Q_N((xy)^2)}{1+(xy)^2}\,dx\,dy.
}
\tag{2.2}
\]
These are exact identities for the printed initial rows and the printed matrix product.

There are also purely rational moment formulas.  Since every component of \(\mathbf Q_N\) is linear in \(X\),
\[
\boxed{
\mathbf q_N=\int_0^1(10-18x)\mathbf Q_N(x)\,dx,
}
\]
\[
\boxed{
\mathbf p_N=\int_0^1(6-12x)\mathbf Q_N(x)\,dx.
}
\]

### Why (2.1) is not yet the missing proof

Formula (2.1) is induced from the identity
\(\mathbf Q_N=\mathbf q_N-(1+X)\mathbf p_N\).  The polynomial inside the integral is still generated by the same matrix product whose dominant connection coefficient we are trying to understand.  No sign, orthogonality, or subdominant pointwise estimate for \(\mathbf Q_N(t^2)\) is currently visible.

Thus (2.1) is an exact **moment lift**, not yet a Beukers or Padé remainder formula.  To finish the proof one must replace \(\mathbf Q_N\) by an independently defined hypergeometric, elliptic, or multiple-orthogonal kernel and prove equality by recurrence plus initial data.

This distinction is load-bearing: merely writing a correct integral containing the CMF product does not exclude its dominant mode.

## 3. The correct analytic target is the integrated elliptic module

Let
\[
Y(k)=\frac12\int_0^kK(s)\,ds.
\]
Then
\[
Y(1)=G.
\]
The complete elliptic integral satisfies
\[
k(1-k^2)K''+(1-3k^2)K'-kK=0.
\]
Since \(K=2Y'\), the primitive \(Y\) satisfies
\[
\boxed{
k(1-k^2)Y'''+(1-3k^2)Y''-kY'=0.}
\tag{3.1}
\]
This order-three equation has a constant solution.  Its solution space is an extension
\[
0\longrightarrow \mathbf 1
\longrightarrow \mathcal M_{\rm int}
\longrightarrow \mathcal M_K
\longrightarrow0
\]
of the rank-two elliptic module by the trivial rank-one module.

That is exactly the structure suggested by the CMF spectrum:

- two outer modes corresponding to the two elliptic branches;
- one neutral mode arising from the primitive/extension direction.

It is not the homogeneous symmetric square \(\operatorname{Sym}^2(\mathcal M_D)\).  Therefore failure of Ore equivalence with the Delannoy symmetric square does not invalidate the integrated-period approach; it tells us which module should replace it.

### Brafman pullback

Set
\[
k(z)=\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z},
\qquad
\rho=17-12\sqrt2.
\]
Then \(k(0)=0\), \(k(\rho)=1\), and Brafman's identity gives
\[
\sum_{n\ge0}P_n(3)^2z^n
=\frac{2}{\pi(1-z)}K(k(z)).
\]
Equivalently,
\[
\boxed{
G=\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\int_0^\rho
\frac{1+z}{\sqrt z(1-z)}
\left(\sum_{n\ge0}P_n(3)^2z^n\right)dz.
}
\tag{3.2}
\]
This explains both the elliptic period and the singular point \(\rho\), but the truncations of (3.2) are not the printed rational CMF approximants.

### A rational first-order system on the ramified cover

Put \(z=x^2\),
\[
k(x)=\frac{4\sqrt2x}{1-x^2},
\qquad
\Delta_x=x^4-34x^2+1.
\]
Use the state
\[
X_{\rm int}=
\begin{pmatrix}
Y\\Y_k/\sqrt2\\Y_{kk}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
A direct calculation from (3.1) gives the rational connection
\[
\frac{dX_{\rm int}}{dx}=A_{\rm int}(x)X_{\rm int},
\]
where
\[
\boxed{
A_{\rm int}(x)=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&\dfrac{8(1+x^2)}{(1-x^2)^2}&0\\[3mm]
0&0&\dfrac{4(1+x^2)}{(1-x^2)^2}\\[3mm]
0&\dfrac{8(1+x^2)}{\Delta_x}
&-\dfrac{(1+x^2)(x^4-98x^2+1)}
{x(1-x^2)\Delta_x}
\end{pmatrix}.}
\tag{3.3}
\]
This is the concrete comparison system to use.  It is substantially better targeted than the Delannoy symmetric-square companion.

## 4. The finite gauge/Ore certificate that proves the scalar identity

Let \(\mathcal L_{\rm CMF}\) be the exact order-three differential factor satisfied by the generating functions of the **hypergeometrically normalized** CMF sequences.  It is essential to use the full operator, not only its Poincaré polynomial.

Write its companion system on the same \(x\)-cover as
\[
\frac{dX_{\rm CMF}}{dx}=A_{\rm CMF}(x)X_{\rm CMF}.
\]
The desired exact certificate is a rational or algebraic matrix \(U(x)\) satisfying
\[
\boxed{
U'(x)=A_{\rm CMF}(x)U(x)-U(x)A_{\rm int}(x).
}
\tag{4.1}
\]
Then \(X_{\rm CMF}=U X_{\rm int}\) maps every integrated elliptic solution to a CMF solution.

Equivalently, at scalar Ore-operator level, seek operators \(T,R\) such that
\[
\boxed{
\mathcal L_{\rm CMF}\,T=R\,\mathcal L_{\rm int},
\qquad
T=t_0(x)+t_1(x)D_x+t_2(x)D_x^2.
}
\tag{4.2}
\]
Equation (4.2) says that \(T\) maps solutions of the integrated elliptic equation into solutions of the CMF equation.

### The local-exponent issue

The exponent mismatch means one should not impose the false condition that \(U(0)\) be an ordinary invertible constant matrix.  The base point is singular and resonant.  One must first choose Frobenius shears
\[
S_{\rm CMF}(x),\qquad S_{\rm int}(x)
\]
that account for integral exponent shifts, and search in the form
\[
U(x)=S_{\rm CMF}(x)V(x)S_{\rm int}(x)^{-1},
\]
with \(V\) regular at \(0\).  Initial data are then matched through the leading sheared coefficient \(V(0)\), not by naively substituting \(x=0\) into a singular gauge.

This is why the formal powers \((0,-3,0)\) are useful rather than fatal: they determine the required shearing of the extension direction.

### Exact initial matching

Once \(U\) or \(T\) is known, compute the images of a Frobenius basis of (3.1).  There are three independent solutions:

1. the constant solution;
2. a primitive of the analytic elliptic solution \(K(k)\);
3. a primitive of the second elliptic solution.

Match the first three Taylor/Frobenius coefficients of the CMF numerator and denominator generating functions.  Since the differential equation has order three, three exact coefficients determine the solution.

The initial-row calculation must show that the dominant elliptic branch is selected with coefficients \(c_p,c_q\) satisfying
\[
\frac{c_p}{c_q}=Y(1)=G.
\]
Equivalently, the transformed error is a rational/algebraic multiple of the endpoint tail
\[
\boxed{
G-Y(k)=\frac12\int_k^1K(s)\,ds.
}
\tag{4.3}
\]

### Why this proves dominant cancellation

As \(k\to1^-\),
\[
K(k)=\log\frac{4}{\sqrt{1-k^2}}
+O\!\left((1-k^2)\log\frac1{1-k}\right).
\]
Hence
\[
G-Y(k)
=O\!\left((1-k)\log\frac1{1-k}\right).
\tag{4.4}
\]
Since \(k(\rho)=1\) and \(k'(\rho)\ne0\), the leading singular term belonging to the dominant elliptic branch is absent in (4.3).  After applying the explicitly known rational gauge, one reads off a coefficient bound strictly smaller than the dominant Birkhoff scale.  The dominant-mode exclusion lemma from Section 1 then gives
\[
(\mathbf p_0-G\mathbf q_0)w_+(0)=0.
\]

Thus, after the finite certificate (4.1) or (4.2), the analytic part of the proof is short.  The global connection coefficient is no longer guessed numerically; it is the endpoint value of a specified period.

## 5. A scalar creative-telescoping version of the same proof

A full matrix gauge is not the only possible certificate.  For a fixed output column, it is enough to construct an independently defined integral \(E_N\) such that:

1. \(E_N\) satisfies the exact normalized order-three CMF recurrence;
2. its first three values equal those of
   \[
   GQ_N-P_N;
   \]
3. the integral gives
   \[
   E_N=o(\lambda_+^N)
   \]
   in the balanced normalization.

Then recurrence uniqueness gives
\[
E_N=GQ_N-P_N
\]
for every \(N\), and the growth estimate kills the dominant coefficient.

This is often computationally easier than finding an invertible gauge because creative telescoping permits a noninvertible morphism and absorbs exact derivatives automatically.

### An explicit matrix-valued AZ search kernel

The Delannoy-square phase with the correct three critical values is
\[
\phi(u)=3+\sqrt2(u+u^{-1}),
\qquad
\Phi(u,v)=\phi(u)\phi(v).
\]
Introduce the Catalan parameter
\[
I(\varepsilon)=\int_0^1\frac{t^\varepsilon}{1+t^2}\,dt,
\qquad
-I'(0)=G,
\]
and the carrier
\[
\boxed{
h_N(u,v,t;\varepsilon)
=\Phi(u,v)^N\frac{t^\varepsilon}{uv(1+t^2)}.
}
\tag{5.1}
\]
Seek a \(3\times3\) rational matrix numerator \(\mathcal P_N\) and certificates \(\mathcal A_N,\mathcal B_N,\mathcal C_N\) satisfying
\[
\boxed{
h_{N+1}\mathcal P_{N+1}
-h_N\mathcal P_NM_H(N)
=\partial_u\mathcal A_N
+\partial_v\mathcal B_N
+\partial_t\mathcal C_N.
}
\tag{5.2}
\]
Integrate in \(u,v\) on constant-term contours and in \(t\) over \([0,1]\).  Differentiate at \(\varepsilon=0\).  The \(u,v\) divergences vanish; the logarithmic derivative produces \(-\log t/(1+t^2)\).  The \(t=1\) boundary should not automatically be killed: it is a natural carrier of the neutral extension class.

A previously found homogeneous Delannoy certificate in which the factor \(1+t^2\) cancels produces only the auxiliary row
\[
\frac{P_N(3)^2}{4}\bigl((N+2)^2,2(N+2),1\bigr),
\]
whose initial row is \((1,1,1/4)\), not either printed CMF row.  Therefore a valid Catalan certificate must retain the parameter derivative or a nonzero endpoint term.  The homogeneous certificate alone does not prove the challenge.

Equation (5.2) is a finite linear-algebra search after an ansatz for the numerator degrees.  It is the most concrete integral-kernel implementation of Approach 1.

## 6. The Rivoal–Zudilin and \({}_3F_2\) kernels: explicit but not yet matched

Catalan's constant has the elementary hypergeometric representation
\[
\boxed{
G={}_3F_2\!\left(
\begin{matrix}\frac12,\frac12,1\\[1mm]\frac32,\frac32\end{matrix};-1
\right).
}
\tag{6.1}
\]
Indeed,
\[
\frac{(1/2)_k}{(3/2)_k}=\frac1{2k+1}.
\]
An Euler double integral is
\[
\boxed{
G=\frac14\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{du\,dt}
{\sqrt{u(1-t)(1+ut)}}.
}
\tag{6.2}
\]
Integer shifts of the five hypergeometric parameters give a natural rank-three contiguous system.  Public `ramanujantools` code contains an explicit generic rank-three CMF for \({}_3F_2\) parameter shifts.

This makes a \({}_3F_2\) parent a credible candidate, but the following finite data are still required before it becomes a proof of the printed matrix:

- a base parameter point;
- an integral trajectory vector in the five-dimensional parameter lattice;
- a rational scalar Pochhammer factor;
- a rational \(3\times3\) gauge;
- and exact matching of the two printed initial rows.

In formulas, if \(T_\alpha(\lambda)\) denotes the generic \({}_3F_2\) shift along a lattice vector \(\alpha\), one must exhibit
\[
\boxed{
M_H(N)=r(N)S(N)^{-1}
T_\alpha(\lambda_0+N\alpha;-1)S(N+1)
}
\tag{6.3}
\]
with explicit \(r,S,\lambda_0,\alpha\).  Without (6.3), the fact that both objects have rank three and involve Catalan's constant is not an identification.

Zudilin's classical very-well-poised construction similarly gives explicit hypergeometric linear forms in \(1\) and \(G\), for example the family built from
\[
n!\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}(-1)^k
\left(k+\frac{n-1}{2}\right)
\frac{(k-n)_n(k+n)_n}{(k-\frac12)_{n+1}^3}.
\]
Creative telescoping gives an Apéry-like recurrence for those forms.  But this neighboring construction does not by itself identify the much larger printed \(3\times3\) matrix.  A contiguity/gauge certificate is still necessary.

Thus the Rivoal–Zudilin framework supplies excellent candidate kernels and explains why a logarithmic parameter should occur; it does not remove the connection-matrix problem.

## 7. What Approach 2 can prove exactly

Set
\[
\boxed{
d\sigma(x)=\frac{-\log x}{4\sqrt x}\,dx,
\qquad 0<x<1.}
\tag{7.1}
\]
Its moments are
\[
\int_0^1x^m\,d\sigma(x)=\frac1{(2m+1)^2}.
\]
Define the Markov function
\[
F(z)=\int_0^1\frac{d\sigma(x)}{z-x}.
\]
With \(x=t^2\),
\[
F(-1)
=-\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}\,dt
=-G.
\]

Let \(\pi_N\) be the monic degree-\(N\) orthogonal polynomial for \(\sigma\), and define its second-kind polynomial
\[
p_{N-1}(z)=\int_0^1
\frac{\pi_N(z)-\pi_N(x)}{z-x}\,d\sigma(x).
\]
Orthogonality gives the exact Padé error identity
\[
\boxed{
F(z)-\frac{p_{N-1}(z)}{\pi_N(z)}
=\frac1{\pi_N(z)^2}
\int_0^1\frac{\pi_N(x)^2}{z-x}\,d\sigma(x).
}
\tag{7.2}
\]
At \(z=-1\), positivity of \(\sigma\) proves
\[
-\frac{p_{N-1}(-1)}{\pi_N(-1)}\longrightarrow G.
\]
This is a complete Padé/Stieltjes proof of a rational approximation to \(G\).

Moreover, its geometric error base is also
\[
17-12\sqrt2.
\]
That agreement is real: it comes from the conformal geometry of the interval \([0,1]\) viewed from \(-1\).

### Why it is not yet the CMF Padé identity

The exterior ratio asymptotic for the monic orthogonal polynomials is
\[
\frac{\pi_{N+1}(-1)}{\pi_N(-1)}
\longrightarrow-\frac{3+2\sqrt2}{4}.
\]
The normalized CMF denominator has dominant ratio
\[
17+12\sqrt2=(3+2\sqrt2)^2.
\]
Consequently, a direct same-index identity
\[
Q_N^{\rm CMF}=R(N)\pi_N(-1)
\]
with a rational or ordinary hypergeometric scalar gauge \(R(N)\) is impossible: the successive quotient of \(R(N)\) would have to tend to
\[
-4(3+2\sqrt2),
\]
an irrational finite limit, whereas a rational function over \(\mathbb Q\) with a finite limit has a rational limit.

This does not rule out every possible contraction, quadratic transform, or multiple-orthogonal construction.  It does rule out the simplest identification.  The natural confluent two-weight system
\[
d\mu_0(x)=\frac{dx}{2\sqrt x},
\qquad
d\mu_1(x)=d\sigma(x)
\]
produces a four-term step-line recurrence, but its computed asymptotic coefficients do not match the printed CMF either.

Therefore Approach 2 is best viewed as:

- a rigorous independent approximation theorem for \(G\);
- a source of candidate kernels and positivity estimates;
- but not yet a proof of the specific CMF initial rows.

To make it a CMF proof one must still prove an exact Hermite–Padé identification, for example by showing that the CMF denominator polynomial satisfies a stated set of multiple orthogonality relations.  Spectrum and convergence rate alone are insufficient.

## 8. Why direct numerical identification cannot certify zero

The numerical computation of the projective dominant functional is extremely convincing:
\[
\frac{\mathbf p_0w_+(0)}{\mathbf q_0w_+(0)}
=0.915965594177219015\ldots
\]
agrees with \(G\) to hundreds of digits.

But a numerical residual
\[
(\mathbf p_0-G\mathbf q_0)w_+(0)
\]
smaller than \(10^{-N}\) is not an exact zero certificate.  The entries of \(w_+(0)\) are global connection constants, and there is no known general lower bound for a nonzero linear form in those constants and \(G\).  Increasing precision only increases confidence.

Numerics become rigorous when they are used to discover one of the following exact objects:

- a rational gauge \(U(x)\);
- an Ore intertwiner \(T\);
- a finite hypergeometric identity;
- a rational recurrence for a candidate integral plus exact initial matching;
- or exact multiple-orthogonality conditions.

After such an object is guessed, coefficient comparison in \(\mathbb Q(x)\), creative telescoping, or recurrence uniqueness proves it exactly.  Without that algebraic object, Approach 3 remains diagnostic rather than deductive.

## 9. Recommended proof program, in priority order

### Step 1: work with the integrated equation, not the symmetric square

Use (3.3) as the target first-order connection.  Derive the exact order-three factor \(\mathcal L_{\rm CMF}\) from the full normalized recurrence and put it on the same \(x\)-cover.

### Step 2: solve the finite Ore-Hom problem

Search first for
\[
\mathcal L_{\rm CMF}T=R\mathcal L_{\rm int},
\qquad \operatorname{ord}T\le2.
\]
This is weaker than demanding an isomorphism and is therefore more likely to exist.  Use the local exponents to bound the poles and degrees of \(t_0,t_1,t_2\).

If an invertible map is expected, solve the matrix equation (4.1) after Frobenius shearing.

### Step 3: match the printed initial rows exactly

Compute three exact startup coefficients of each mapped period.  Solve the resulting rational linear system.  The result must reproduce
\[
(30921,-32972,8240),
\qquad
(33750,-36000,9000)
\]
without numerical fitting.

### Step 4: identify the error with an endpoint tail

Show that the transformed error is the image of
\[
G-Y(k)=\frac12\int_k^1K(s)\,ds
\]
or of an equivalent logarithmic parameter derivative.  This is the variation-of-parameters identity that inserts the special value \(G\).

### Step 5: exclude the dominant mode

Use the local expansion at \(k=1\), together with the explicit gauge, to prove an \(o(\lambda_+^N)\) coefficient bound.  The dominant-mode exclusion lemma then proves
\[
(\mathbf p_0-G\mathbf q_0)w_+(0)=0.
\]

### Parallel computational route

If the Ore-Hom search fails, run the matrix-valued AZ search (5.2).  Its output would simultaneously provide the period representation, the recurrence, and the extension boundary term.

The Stieltjes Padé construction should be used as a source of ansätze and bounds, not invoked as though its denominators were already the CMF denominators.

## 10. Direct answers to the three proposed approaches

### Approach 1: same integral kernel at two parameter values

**Most tractable, after correction.**  The right object is an integrated elliptic or parameter-differentiated \({}_3F_2\) family.  One must exhibit its exact contiguous/gauge map to the printed matrix.  Once that map and initial matching are known, variation of parameters gives the endpoint tail (4.3), and the scalar identity follows rigorously.

An explicit base period is (6.2), and an explicit logarithmic deformation is
\[
I(\varepsilon)=\int_0^1\frac{t^\varepsilon}{1+t^2}\,dt,
\qquad -I'(0)=G.
\]
An explicit matrix-AZ carrier is (5.1).  What is not yet known is the numerator/certificate that reproduces the printed initial rows.

### Approach 2: Padé/Stieltjes

**A complete Padé identity exists, namely (7.2), but it currently proves a different sequence of approximants.**  To use it for the CMF one must find exact multiple orthogonality or a nontrivial contraction/gauge.  The simple same-index identification is excluded by ratio asymptotics.

### Approach 3: numerical identification

**Not a proof by itself.**  It is highly effective for guessing the connection matrix, pole divisor, degree bounds, or a short contiguous combination.  Exact zero requires a symbolic certificate or a theorem giving a nonzero lower bound, and no suitable lower bound is presently available.

## Bottom line

The scalar identity is not a consequence of the Poincaré polynomial, the formal powers, or the numerical limit.  It is a global period-connection statement.

The strongest explicit CMF-specific formula currently available is
\[
G\mathbf q_N-\mathbf p_N
=\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}
\bigl(\mathbf q_0-(1+t^2)\mathbf p_0\bigr)
M(0)\cdots M(N-1)\,dt.
\]
It is exact but not independently decaying.

A complete proof will result from one finite new certificate:
\[
\boxed{
\text{CMF normalized module}
\longleftarrow
\text{integrated elliptic/parameter-derivative period module}.
}
\]
After this map is written down and the initial rows are matched, the identity
\[
(\mathbf p_0-G\mathbf q_0)w_+(0)=0
\]
is an immediate consequence of
\[
G-Y(k)=\frac12\int_k^1K(s)\,ds
\]
and the absence of the leading elliptic singularity at \(k=1\).

At present I do not find a published or repository certificate carrying out that final map for the exact printed \(3\times3\) matrix.  Thus the honest status is: the proof has been reduced to a concrete finite Ore/gauge or matrix-AZ computation, but the required CMF-specific independent kernel has not yet been certified.
