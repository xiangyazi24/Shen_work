# Problem 3.2: the exact horizontal-prime obstruction for Apéry’s \(\zeta(3)\) approximants

## Executive verdict

There is an important normalization correction at the outset. The published Problem 3.2 in the challenge repository is the **classical Apéry \(\zeta(3)\) recurrence**, not the Problem 2.1 CMF. Write

\[
P(n)=34n^3+51n^2+27n+5,
\]

and let \(A_n,B_n\) be the two solutions

\[
(n+1)^3u_{n+1}-P(n)u_n+n^3u_{n-1}=0,
\tag{0.1}
\]

with

\[
(A_0,A_1)=(1,5),\qquad (B_0,B_1)=(0,6).
\tag{0.2}
\]

Thus \(A_n\) is the integral Apéry number

\[
A_n=\sum_{k=0}^n\binom nk^2\binom{n+k}{k}^2,
\tag{0.3}
\]

whereas \(B_n\) is the rational companion and \(B_n/A_n\to\zeta(3)\).

Put

\[
\ell_n=\operatorname{lcm}(1,2,\ldots,n),
\qquad D_n=\ell_n^3.
\tag{0.4}
\]

The challenge uses the clearing factor \(D_n\). If one writes \(d_n=\ell_n\), then the conjecture is

\[
\boxed{
G_n:=\gcd\bigl(d_n^3B_n,d_n^3A_n\bigr)=e^{o(n)}.
}
\tag{0.5}
\]

The current state of the art is:

1. **The conjecture is not presently a consequence of any known “horizontal large sieve for Apéry sequences.”** There is no general theorem of that name strong enough to prove (0.5), and there cannot be one for arbitrary CMFs without a canonical primitive integral normalization.
2. **The classical integral sequence \(A_n\) has exceptionally strong digitwise arithmetic:** Gessel’s Lucas congruences, Coster–Beukers supercongruences, Dwork congruences, constant-term realizations, and lower bounds for valuations due to Delaygue. Comparable results now cover the 15 classical sporadic Apéry-like sequences. These results concern one integral period solution. They do not control the common factors of that solution and the cleared rational companion.
3. **The reflection symmetry is rigorous.** For every prime \(p>3\), both solutions satisfy
   \[
   A_{p-1-r}\equiv A_r\pmod p,
   \qquad
   B_{p-1-r}\equiv B_r\pmod p
   \quad(0\le r<p),
   \tag{0.6}
   \]
   where \(B_r\) is \(p\)-integral because \(r<p\). This follows from the anti-invariance \(P(-1-n)=-P(n)\), the binomial formula for \(A_n\), the exact Casoratian, and the fact that reflection is an involution.
4. **There is an exact reduction to large primes.** The Casoratian gives
   \[
   B_nA_{n-1}-B_{n-1}A_n=\frac6{n^3}.
   \tag{0.7}
   \]
   Consequently all primes \(p\le\sqrt n\) contribute only \(e^{O(\sqrt n)}\) to \(G_n\), and every prime \(p>\sqrt n\) occurs in \(G_n\) with uniformly bounded exponent. Therefore
   \[
   \boxed{
   \log G_n=o(n)
   \iff
   \sum_{\substack{\sqrt n<p\le n\\p\mid G_n}}\log p=o(n).
   }
   \tag{0.8}
   \]
   This is the precise horizontal theorem still needed.
5. A promising proof should not begin with a probabilistic large sieve. It should first prove a **matrix Lucas/Dwork law for the full period–companion extension**, then compress the bad primes in each block \(a=\lfloor n/p\rfloor\) into a low-height auxiliary integer. Such a block-compression theorem would imply (0.8) deterministically by a Dirichlet-hyperbola estimate.

The remainder gives the exact reductions, the reflection proof, the known literature, and a concrete theorem whose proof would settle the challenge.

# 1. The exact Casoratian reduction

For two solutions \(U,V\) of (0.1), define

\[
W_n(U,V)=U_nV_{n-1}-U_{n-1}V_n.
\]

Substitution from the recurrence gives

\[
W_{n+1}(U,V)=\left(\frac{n}{n+1}\right)^3W_n(U,V).
\tag{1.1}
\]

For \((U,V)=(B,A)\), the initial value is

\[
W_1(B,A)=B_1A_0-B_0A_1=6.
\]

Hence

\[
\boxed{
B_nA_{n-1}-B_{n-1}A_n=\frac6{n^3}.
}
\tag{1.2}
\]

This elementary identity is the strongest unconditional starting point for the gcd problem.

## 1.1 Clearing denominators

Set

\[
X_n=D_nB_n\in\mathbb Z,
\qquad
Y_n=D_nA_n\in\mathbb Z.
\]

Multiplying (1.2) by \(D_nD_{n-1}\) gives

\[
\boxed{
X_nY_{n-1}-X_{n-1}Y_n
=\frac{6D_nD_{n-1}}{n^3}.
}
\tag{1.3}
\]

Since \(G_n=\gcd(X_n,Y_n)\) divides the left side, for every prime \(p\),

\[
\boxed{
\nu_p(G_n)
\le \nu_p(6)
+3\lfloor\log_p n\rfloor
+3\lfloor\log_p(n-1)\rfloor
-3\nu_p(n).
}
\tag{1.4}
\]

Several useful consequences are immediate.

- If \(p>n\) and \(p>3\), then \(p\nmid G_n\).
- If \(p=n>3\) is prime, then \(p\nmid G_n\).
- If \(p>\sqrt n\), \(p>3\), and \(p\nmid n\), then
  \[
  \nu_p(G_n)\le6.
  \tag{1.5}
  \]
- If \(p>\sqrt n\), \(p>3\), and \(p\mid n\), then the bound improves to at most \(3\), except for the already excluded case \(n=p\).

The empirical set \(\{0,1,2,4\}\) mentioned in the question is therefore not needed for the basic reduction. It is also not literally the set of all \(\nu_p(G_n)\) in the repository normalization: the existing computation reports a \(3\)-adic valuation equal to \(3\) at some indices. If \(\{0,1,2,4\}\) refers to another normalized local invariant, that invariant should be defined separately.

## 1.2 Small primes already give \(e^{o(n)}\)

For \(p\le\sqrt n\), (1.4) gives

\[
\nu_p(G_n)\log p\ll \log n.
\]

Using the elementary Chebyshev bound \(\pi(x)\ll x/\log x\),

\[
\sum_{p\le\sqrt n}\nu_p(G_n)\log p
=O(\sqrt n).
\tag{1.6}
\]

For \(p>\sqrt n\), the exponent is bounded by (1.5). Therefore, if

\[
\mathcal P_n=\{p:\sqrt n<p\le n,\ p\mid G_n\},
\]

then

\[
\sum_{p\in\mathcal P_n}\log p
\le \log G_n
\le O(\sqrt n)+6\sum_{p\in\mathcal P_n}\log p.
\tag{1.7}
\]

This proves the equivalence (0.8). The challenge is not primarily a problem of bounding high powers of one prime. It is a problem of preventing **linearly many logarithmic weights from distinct large primes**.

## 1.3 For large primes, only the cleared companion matters

For every prime \(p\le n\),

\[
\nu_p(D_nA_n)\ge3,
\]

because \(A_n\in\mathbb Z\) and \(p^3\mid D_n\). Thus, at the level of prime support,

\[
p\mid G_n
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
p\mid D_nB_n
\qquad(p\le n).
\tag{1.8}
\]

So the horizontal question can be phrased as follows:

> For how many large primes \(p\le n\) does the denominator clearing of the rational companion fail to be a \(p\)-adic unit?

This is substantially different from asking when \(p\mid A_n\).

# 2. The forced factorial divisor and the Smith-normal-form viewpoint

Set

\[
\widehat U_n=(n!)^3U_n.
\]

Then (0.1) becomes the integral continuant recurrence

\[
\boxed{
\widehat U_{n+1}=P(n)\widehat U_n-n^6\widehat U_{n-1}.
}
\tag{2.1}
\]

Both fundamental solutions of (2.1) are integral. Since

\[
(n!)^3U_n
=\left(\frac{n!}{\ell_n}\right)^3D_nU_n,
\]

put

\[
c_n=\left(\frac{n!}{\ell_n}\right)^3\in\mathbb Z.
\tag{2.2}
\]

Then

\[
\gcd\bigl((n!)^3B_n,(n!)^3A_n\bigr)=c_nG_n.
\tag{2.3}
\]

Thus Problem 3.2 says that the common divisor of the two integral continuants is the **forced divisor** \(c_n\), up to a subexponential excess.

The determinant is also exact:

\[
\boxed{
\det
\begin{pmatrix}
(n!)^3B_n&(n!)^3A_n\\
((n-1)!)^3B_{n-1}&((n-1)!)^3A_{n-1}
\end{pmatrix}
=6((n-1)!)^6.
}
\tag{2.4}
\]

Locally over \(\mathbb Z_p\), the gcd in (2.3) is the first Smith invariant of the row at time \(n\), while (2.4) controls the sum of the two Smith exponents of the fundamental matrix. This is the natural language for a generalization to rank-\(r\) CMFs: a Casoratian controls the product of Smith invariants, but an additional primitivity theorem is needed to control the first one.

# 3. What digit theorems actually prove for the classical Apéry numbers

## 3.1 Lucas congruences

Gessel proved that for every prime \(p\), every \(a\ge0\), and \(0\le r<p\),

\[
\boxed{
A_{ap+r}\equiv A_aA_r\pmod p.
}
\tag{3.1}
\]

If \(n=n_0+n_1p+\cdots+n_sp^s\), then

\[
\boxed{
A_n\equiv\prod_{j=0}^s A_{n_j}\pmod p.
}
\tag{3.2}
\]

Consequently \(p\mid A_n\) if and only if at least one base-\(p\) digit \(n_j\) belongs to

\[
Z_p=\{r\in\{0,\ldots,p-1\}:A_r\equiv0\pmod p\}.
\tag{3.3}
\]

This is an exact digit theorem for divisibility by \(p\). It is not, in general, an exact formula for \(\nu_p(A_n)\), because different summands in (0.3) can undergo additional \(p\)-adic cancellation.

## 3.2 Delaygue’s valuation theorem

Delaygue proved Beukers’ conjectural digit-count lower bounds. For example:

- for \(p=5\), every base-\(5\) digit equal to \(1\) or \(3\) contributes at least one factor of \(5\);
- for \(p=11\), every base-\(11\) digit equal to \(5\) contributes at least one factor of \(11\).

More generally, his theorems cover broad classes of multisums of factorial ratios and constant terms of Laurent-polynomial powers. The conclusion is a lower bound

\[
\nu_p(A_n)\ge \#\{\text{zero digits in the base-}p\text{ expansion of }n\},
\tag{3.4}
\]

under explicit hypotheses. This is a genuine Granville/Lucas-type phenomenon, but it is not a universal exact valuation formula.

## 3.3 Dwork and supercongruences

The Apéry numbers satisfy strong vertical congruences, including

\[
A_{mp^r}\equiv A_{mp^{r-1}}\pmod{p^{3r}}
\qquad(p\ge5).
\tag{3.5}
\]

Coster proved the full tower form for generalized Apéry sums; Mellit–Vlasenko proved Dwork congruences for constant terms of Laurent-polynomial powers; Straub gave a multivariate rational-function realization with corresponding supercongruences.

These results are powerful, but they address a **vertical tower**

\[
m,mp,mp^2,\ldots
\]

for one fixed prime. Problem 3.2 fixes \(n\) and varies all primes \(p\le n\). A vertical congruence does not by itself show that the horizontal support \(\mathcal P_n\) has small logarithmic weight.

## 3.4 Beyond the classical sequence

The modern picture for the 15 classical sporadic Apéry-like sequences is strong:

- Malik–Straub proved Lucas congruences for all 15;
- Gorodetsky produced constant-term representations for all 15 and strong Lucas congruences for 14, together with valuation lower bounds;
- Straub proved Gessel–Lucas congruences modulo \(p^2\) and two-term supercongruences for all 15;
- Rowland–Yassawi–Krattenthaler proved Lucas congruences modulo \(p^2\) for classical Apéry numbers under explicit restrictions on the digits;
- Straub’s 2025 partial-Lucas theorem extends digit-restricted congruences to arbitrary constant-term sequences;
- Caruso–Fürnsinn–Vargas-Montoya–Zudilin computed Galois groups of several Apéry-like generating series after reduction modulo primes.

None of these results currently gives the required horizontal theorem for the **rational companion**.

This answers Question 1:

> There is a broad Lucas–Dwork theory for geometrically or combinatorially normalized integral Apéry-like period sequences. There is no comparable general theorem for arbitrary CMFs, and the known digit results do not automatically control the cleared companion or its common gcd with the period solution.

# 4. Reflection symmetry is rigorous

The recurrence polynomial has the exact anti-symmetry

\[
\boxed{P(-1-n)=-P(n).}
\tag{4.1}
\]

Fix a prime \(p>3\). For a sequence \(U_0,\ldots,U_{p-1}\) satisfying (0.1) modulo \(p\) at the nonsingular indices \(1\le n\le p-2\), define

\[
(RU)_r=U_{p-1-r}.
\]

Substituting \(n\mapsto p-1-n\) and using (4.1) shows that \(RU\) satisfies the same recurrence. Thus \(R\) is an involution of the two-dimensional solution space over \(\mathbb F_p\).

## 4.1 Reflection of the integral Apéry solution

From (0.3),

\[
A_{p-1}\equiv1\pmod p,
\tag{4.2}
\]

because every term with \(k\ge1\) contains \(p\) in \(\binom{p-1+k}{k}\). Similarly,

\[
A_{p-2}\equiv1+4=5\pmod p,
\tag{4.3}
\]

because only \(k=0,1\) survive modulo \(p\). These are the initial values \(A_0,A_1\), so uniqueness for the recurrence gives

\[
\boxed{A_{p-1-r}\equiv A_r\pmod p.}
\tag{4.4}
\]

## 4.2 Reflection of the rational companion

For \(r<p\), the denominator of \(B_r\) divides \(\ell_r^3\), which is prime to \(p\); hence \(B_r\) has a well-defined reduction modulo \(p\).

Since \(A,B\) form a basis modulo \(p\) by the nonzero Casoratian, write

\[
RB=cA+dB.
\tag{4.5}
\]

Using (1.2) and (4.4), one checks for \(1\le r\le p-2\) that

\[
W_r(A,RB)=-W_{p-r}(A,B)=W_r(A,B)\pmod p.
\tag{4.6}
\]

Therefore \(d=1\). Since \(R^2=1\), equation (4.5) gives

\[
B=R^2B=B+2cA.
\]

As \(p>3\), this forces \(c=0\). Hence

\[
\boxed{B_{p-1-r}\equiv B_r\pmod p.}
\tag{4.7}
\]

This proves the reflection symmetry for both distinguished solutions on the first \(p\)-block.

## 4.3 Functional equations modulo \(p\)

Define the Hasse/Lucas polynomial

\[
H_p(x)=\sum_{r=0}^{p-1}A_rx^r.
\]

Equation (4.4) gives

\[
\boxed{x^{p-1}H_p(x^{-1})=H_p(x)\quad\text{in }\mathbb F_p[x].}
\tag{4.8}
\]

The Lucas congruence gives the standard Frobenius equation

\[
\boxed{
F_A(x)\equiv H_p(x)F_A(x^p)\pmod p,
}
\tag{4.9}
\]

where \(F_A(x)=\sum_{n\ge0}A_nx^n\).

Likewise, the truncated companion polynomial

\[
K_p(x)=\sum_{r=0}^{p-1}B_rx^r
\]

is palindromic:

\[
\boxed{x^{p-1}K_p(x^{-1})=K_p(x)\pmod p.}
\tag{4.10}
\]

What is not yet available is a scalar Lucas law for all \(B_{ap+r}\). The reason is structural. Let

\[
\mathcal L=	heta^3-x(2\theta+1)(17\theta^2+17\theta+5)+x^2(\theta+1)^3.
\tag{4.11}
\]

Then

\[
\mathcal L F_A=0,
\qquad
\mathcal L F_B=6x.
\tag{4.12}
\]

Thus the companion is an **inhomogeneous extension/normal function**, not another integral period at the origin. The correct missing statement is a matrix Frobenius or matrix Lucas law for the full extension, not a second copy of (4.9).

This answers Question 2:

> The reflection symmetry is exact and gives palindromic first-block polynomials. It does not by itself prove the gcd theorem; it must be combined with a Frobenius law describing how the cleared companion passes from one \(p\)-block to the next.

# 5. Why denominator lemmas do not control the gcd

A statement such as

\[
\ell_n^3B_n\in\mathbb Z
\tag{5.1}
\]

bounds the negative valuations of \(B_n\):

\[
\nu_p(B_n)\ge-3\lfloor\log_p n\rfloor.
\]

The gcd conjecture concerns the **positive excess** after these negative valuations have been canceled:

\[
\nu_p(G_n)
=\min\left(
3\lfloor\log_p n\rfloor+\nu_p(B_n),
3\lfloor\log_p n\rfloor+\nu_p(A_n)
\right).
\tag{5.2}
\]

These are logically different problems. A “prime-free denominator” estimate used in an irrationality proof does not rule out a large common numerator factor.

There also cannot be a gauge-invariant theorem for all CMFs. If a pair of CMF solutions is multiplied by the hypergeometric scalar \(2^n\), it remains P-recursive and is obtained by a rank-one CMF twist, but its common gcd acquires the exponential factor \(2^n\). Any general theorem must therefore specify:

- a primitive integral lattice;
- a normalization modulo scalar rank-one twists;
- bounded bad reduction;
- a Frobenius-compatible integral structure;
- and a nondegenerate period–extension class.

For rank two, the exact Casoratian (1.2) supplies a canonical determinant normalization. For a higher-rank CMF, the analogue is a primitive lattice together with all local Smith invariants of a fundamental matrix.

This answers Question 3:

> Integrality can be treated uniformly by factorial ratios, diagonals, constant terms, or geometric lattices. Subexponential excess gcd cannot. It requires an additional horizontal primitivity theorem for the full solution module.

# 6. Why a standard large sieve is not yet the missing theorem

A classical or Frobenius large sieve is designed to bound the number of members of a **set of indices** satisfying many local congruence restrictions. Problem 3.2 asks for a pointwise statement for every single \(n\). A singleton can occupy any allowed residue class modulo every prime, so local density information alone cannot prove (0.8).

Large-sieve methods could prove an average result such as

\[
\#\{n\le N:\log G_n>\varepsilon n\}=o(N),
\]

provided suitable equidistribution estimates were available. That would not prove the challenge’s all-\(n\) assertion.

The required extra input is an algebraic relation coupling the prime \(p\) to the quotient

\[
a=\left\lfloor\frac np\right\rfloor.
\]

## 6.1 A deterministic block-compression theorem that would suffice

Here is a concrete sufficient theorem.

> **Block-compression target.** There exist a fixed integer \(J\) and nonzero integers \(C_{a,j}\), \(1\le j\le J\), such that
> \[
> \log|C_{a,j}|=O(a)
> \tag{6.1}
> \]
> and, whenever \(p>\sqrt n\), \(a=\lfloor n/p\rfloor\), and \(p\mid G_n\), one has
> \[
> p\mid\prod_{j=1}^JC_{a,j}.
> \tag{6.2}
> \]

Group the bad primes by \(a=\lfloor n/p\rfloor\). They lie in

\[
\frac{n}{a+1}<p\le\frac na.
\]

For each block, (6.2) and the elementary Chebyshev bound give

\[
\sum_{\substack{p\in\mathcal P_n\\\lfloor n/p\rfloor=a}}\log p
\ll \min\left(a,\frac{n}{a^2}\right).
\tag{6.3}
\]

Therefore

\[
\begin{aligned}
\sum_{p\in\mathcal P_n}\log p
&\ll\sum_{a\le\sqrt n}\min\left(a,\frac n{a^2}\right)\\
&=O(n^{2/3}).
\end{aligned}
\tag{6.4}
\]

Together with (1.7), this would prove

\[
\log G_n=O(n^{2/3})=o(n).
\]

A bound \(\log|C_{a,j}|=O(a\log^C a)\) would still give \(o(n)\) with harmless logarithmic losses.

This is a sharper and more actionable formulation than “apply a horizontal large sieve.” The main arithmetic task is to derive the low-height obstruction integers \(C_{a,j}\) from a matrix Lucas/Dwork law and reflection.

# 7. What modularity contributes

The generating series \(F_A\) has a modular parametrization for \(\Gamma_1(6)\), and its differential equation is the Picard–Fuchs equation of a K3 family. Stienstra–Beukers interpreted its congruences through the formal Brauer group. Modern constant-term and Dwork formulations recover the same type of Frobenius structure algebraically.

This gives important local information:

- a Hasse invariant \(H_p\);
- ordinary versus supersingular primes/fibers;
- a unit-root Frobenius at ordinary primes;
- Dwork congruences and supercongruences;
- modular-form congruences at special indices.

But the challenge involves the mixed object \((F_A,F_B)\), with \(F_B\) satisfying the inhomogeneous equation (4.12). To control \(G_n\), one needs an integral Frobenius matrix for the extension

\[
0\longrightarrow M_{\mathrm{period}}
\longrightarrow M_{\mathrm{mixed}}
\longrightarrow\mathbf 1(-3)
\longrightarrow0,
\tag{7.1}
\]

or an equivalent explicit matrix-Dwork congruence for the recurrence. The pure K3 period alone does not see the numerator companion.

Nor does modularity imply complex multiplication. The Apéry family is modular, while CM occurs only at special fibers/points. When genuine CM is present, splitting and inertness can simplify the Frobenius matrix and may sharpen local valuations, but it still does not automatically yield a pointwise horizontal support theorem.

Chebotarev and Frobenius large-sieve theorems control primes satisfying a fixed conjugacy-class condition. Here the condition also contains the moving index \(n\), or equivalently \(a=\lfloor n/p\rfloor\). One must first convert \(p\mid G_n\) into a fixed algebraic condition of controlled height before those tools become applicable.

This answers Question 4:

> Modularity supplies the local Frobenius mechanism from which a matrix Lucas law may be derived. It is an essential structural advantage, but it does not by itself control the horizontal common-prime support.

# 8. A concrete proof program

A realistic route to Problem 3.2 is the following.

## Certificate A: integral transfer matrices

Use the factorial-scaled recurrence (2.1) and construct a primitive \(2\times2\) transfer matrix over \(\mathbb Z\). Compute its local Smith normal form and identify exactly the forced factor \(c_n\).

## Certificate B: first-block reflection

Use (4.4) and (4.7), including their lifts modulo \(p^2\) or \(p^3\) where possible. This part is already rigorous modulo \(p\).

## Certificate C: matrix Lucas/Dwork law

Prove a congruence for the full period–companion system, schematically

```text
normalized transfer at ap+r
    = first-block matrix at r
      × Frobenius block at a
      + controlled p-adic error.
```

The precise normalization must include the factor \(p^{3\lfloor n/p\rfloor}\) arising from the companion denominators. A scalar Lucas formula for \(A_n\) is insufficient.

## Certificate D: low-height obstruction

For \(p>\sqrt n\), write \(n=ap+r\). Combine Certificate C with reflection to eliminate \(r\) and prove

\[
p\mid G_n\Longrightarrow p\mid C_{a,j}
\]

for one of finitely many explicitly defined resultants/determinants \(C_{a,j}\) of height \(e^{O(a\operatorname{polylog}a)}\).

## Certificate E: hyperbola summation

Apply (6.3)–(6.4), then add the already-controlled small-prime contribution (1.6).

This program cleanly separates the local geometric input from the final elementary analytic estimate.

# 9. Direct answers to the four questions

## (1) Does a Granville-type digit theorem hold for general CMF sequences?

For the classical integral Apéry numbers and many constant-term/factorial-ratio Apéry-like sequences, yes in the form of Lucas congruences, Dwork congruences, and digit-count **lower bounds** for valuations. No theorem gives exact valuations for arbitrary CMFs. Arbitrary CMFs are too broad, and rational/hypergeometric gauges can change gcd growth exponentially.

## (2) Can reflection be made rigorous?

Yes. Equations (4.4) and (4.7) prove it for both classical solutions on the first \(p\)-block. It yields reciprocal/palindromic truncated generating polynomials. The missing extension is a matrix Frobenius law across successive \(p\)-blocks.

## (3) Is there a uniform gcd method behind denominator lemmas?

The uniform part is the Casoratian/Smith-normal-form reduction. Denominator lemmas control negative valuations; the excess gcd requires a separate horizontal primitivity theorem. No general CMF theorem of the required strength is currently known.

## (4) Does modular origin help?

Yes, through integral Frobenius structures, formal Brauer groups, Hasse invariants, and supercongruences. It does not automatically prove horizontal sparsity, and generic modular origin should not be conflated with CM. The decisive modular object must include the inhomogeneous companion extension, not only the pure period.

## Bottom line

The local theory is already strong enough to reduce the conjecture to a clean horizontal statement, and the reflection symmetry can be proved exactly. The unsolved part is not a better bound for \(\nu_p\) at one prime. It is a **uniform low-height description of the large primes for which the cleared companion loses primitivity**. A matrix Lucas/Dwork theorem plus the block-compression estimate (6.1)–(6.2) is the most concrete route presently visible.
