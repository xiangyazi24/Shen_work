# Verdict

**Package-II is a bounded local formal-group lemma, not a full formal-log project.**

For a one-dimensional integral formal group over the valuation ring \(\mathcal O_L\), with the normalized additive valuation

\[
v(\pi)=1,\qquad v(3)=3,
\]

one has the universal two-jet identity

\[
[3]_F(T)=3T+3cT^2+T^3H(T)
\tag{1}
\]

for some \(c\in\mathcal O_L\) and \(H(T)\in\mathcal O_L[[T]]\). Therefore, for every nonzero \(z\) with \(s=v(z)\ge 2\),

\[
v([3]_F(z))=s+3.
\tag{2}
\]

Thus multiplication by \(3\) has trivial kernel on \(\mathfrak m^2\), and iteration gives

\[
v([3^r]_F(z))=s+3r<\infty.
\]

After separating the prime-to-\(3\) part of an arbitrary torsion order, this proves that \(\widehat E_0(\mathfrak m^2)\) is torsion-free.

The supersingular height-2 hypothesis gives a stronger description of the series, but it is **not needed** for the depth-two torsion-freeness argument. The only special formal-group calculation needed is the coefficient of \(T^2\) in \([3](T)\).

# 1. Direct multiplication-series argument

## 1.1 The apparent quadratic obstruction is not real

If one knows only that

\[
[3](T)=3T+a_2T^2+a_3T^3+\cdots
\]

with all \(a_i\in\mathcal O_L\), then the crude estimate is indeed

\[
v([3](z))\ge \min\{3+s,2s\}.
\]

At \(s=2\), this gives only \(v([3](z))\ge4\), and says nothing useful about injectivity.

But \(a_2\) is **not an arbitrary integral coefficient**. For every one-dimensional formal group law

\[
F(X,Y)=X+Y+cXY+\text{terms of total degree at least }3,
\]

one computes modulo \(T^3\):

\[
[2](T)=F(T,T)=2T+cT^2\pmod{T^3},
\]

and hence

\[
\begin{aligned}
[3](T)
  &=F([2](T),T)\\
  &\equiv (2T+cT^2)+T+c(2T)T\pmod{T^3}\\
  &=3T+3cT^2\pmod{T^3}.
\end{aligned}
\]

This is exactly (1):

\[
[3](T)=3T+3cT^2+T^3H(T),\qquad H(T)\in\mathcal O_L[[T]].
\]

So the quadratic contribution has valuation at least

\[
v(3cz^2)\ge 3+2s,
\]

not merely \(2s\).

This is the \(p=3\) specialization of the general congruence

\[
[p]_F(T)=pT+p\sum_{2\le i<p}b_iT^i+T^pC(T),
\]

which is the multiplication-series input behind the usual radius \(s>e/(p-1)\). For this question there is no reason to formalize that general theorem: the two-jet computation above is enough.

## 1.2 Exact valuation at depth two

Let \(z\ne0\), and put \(s=v(z)\ge2\). Evaluating (1),

\[
[3](z)=3z+3cz^2+z^3H(z).
\]

Because \(z\in\mathfrak m\) and \(H\) has integral coefficients, the series converges and \(H(z)\in\mathcal O_L\), so \(v(H(z))\ge0\). Therefore

\[
\begin{aligned}
v(3z)&=s+3,\\
v(3cz^2)&\ge3+2s>s+3,\\
v(z^3H(z))&\ge3s>s+3.
\end{aligned}
\]

The last strict inequality is precisely

\[
3s>s+3\iff 2s>3,
\]

which holds for integral \(s\ge2\). Thus \(3z\) is the unique lowest-valuation term. By the strong triangle equality,

\[
\boxed{v([3](z))=v(z)+3.}
\]

In particular, \([3](z)\ne0\). Also \([3](z)\in\mathfrak m^2\), so the same argument can be repeated. Inductively,

\[
\boxed{v([3^r](z))=v(z)+3r}
\]

for every \(r\ge0\).

### What iteration does and does not say

For a nontorsion formal point, \([3^r](z)\to0\) and its valuation tends to infinity. That is normal and is not itself a contradiction.

For a point killed by \(3^r\), however, one would have \([3^r](z)=0\), whose valuation is \(+\infty\), whereas the exact formula gives the finite value \(v(z)+3r\). That is the contradiction.

A mere lower bound is insufficient, because \(+\infty\) satisfies every lower bound. The proof needs the **exact unique-leading-term valuation**, not only monotone growth.

## 1.3 Relation to height two

For the supersingular reduction, height two means

\[
[3]_{\bar F}(T)=\bar uT^9+\text{terms of degree greater than }9,
\qquad \bar u\ne0.
\]

Lifting to \(\mathcal O_L\), this says that the series can be written more finely as

\[
[3]_F(T)
 =3T+3cT^2
   +\pi c_3T^3+\cdots+\pi c_8T^8
   +uT^9+T^{10}J(T),
\tag{3}
\]

with \(c,c_3,\ldots,c_8\in\mathcal O_L\), \(u\in\mathcal O_L^\times\), and \(J\in\mathcal O_L[[T]]\).

At \(s=2\), the relevant valuations are

\[
\begin{array}{c|c}
\text{term} & \text{valuation lower bound}\\ \hline
3z & 5\\
3cz^2 & 7\\
\pi c_3z^3 & 7\\
u z^9 & 18.
\end{array}
\]

Thus the height-two information is perfectly compatible with the direct argument, but it is stronger than needed. In fact, (1) proves the same depth-two injectivity for **every** integral one-dimensional formal group with \(v(3)=3\), ordinary or supersingular.

The answer to the question about “\(2v\) versus \(3v\)” is therefore:

* the completely structure-free bound sees a possible unit quadratic coefficient and gives \(2v\);
* for a multiplication-by-\(3\) series, the quadratic coefficient is divisible by \(3\), so its actual bound is \(3+2v\);
* the first term whose coefficient might merely be integral occurs in degree \(3\), hence the decisive competing bound is \(3v\);
* at \(v\ge2\), both \(3+2v\) and \(3v\) are strictly larger than \(3+v\).

## 1.4 Prime-to-three multiplication

For every positive integer \(q\), the linear coefficient of \([q]_F(T)\) is \(q\). Hence

\[
[q]_F(T)=qT+T^2H_q(T),\qquad H_q(T)\in\mathcal O_L[[T]].
\tag{4}
\]

If \(3\nmid q\), then \(q\in\mathcal O_L^\times\), so for every nonzero \(z\in\mathfrak m\),

\[
v(qz)=v(z),\qquad v(z^2H_q(z))\ge2v(z)>v(z).
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{v([q]_F(z))=v(z)\quad(3\nmid q).}
\tag{5}
\]

In particular, prime-to-three multiplication also has trivial kernel on \(\mathfrak m^2\).

## 1.5 Arbitrary torsion order

Suppose \([n]_F(z)=0\) with \(n>0\) and \(z\in\mathfrak m^2\). Write

\[
n=3^rq,\qquad 3\nmid q.
\]

Set \(w=[q]_F(z)\). If \(z\ne0\), then (5) gives

\[
w\ne0,\qquad v(w)=v(z)\ge2.
\]

Using \([n]=[3^r]\circ[q]\), equation (2) iterated gives

\[
v([n](z))=v([3^r](w))=v(w)+3r<\infty,
\]

contradicting \([n](z)=0\). Hence \(z=0\).

Therefore

\[
\boxed{\widehat E_0(\mathfrak m^2)\text{ is torsion-free}.}
\]

# 2. Lean-shaped implementation

The cleanest implementation is to isolate one algebraic two-jet lemma and one valuation lemma.

The names below are schematic where they depend on the project's existing formal-kernel API, but the shapes are intended to be used directly.

## 2.1 Multiplication-series two-jet

```lean
-- `O` is the valuation ring.  `F` is the integral 1-dimensional formal law.
-- `mulSeries F n` is the power series [n]_F(T).

theorem three_mulSeries_decomposition
    (F : OneDimFormalGroup O) :
    ∃ c : O, ∃ H : PowerSeries O,
      mulSeries F 3 =
        PowerSeries.C (3 : O) * PowerSeries.X +
        PowerSeries.C (3 * c) * PowerSeries.X ^ 2 +
        PowerSeries.X ^ 3 * H
```

A particularly robust proof strategy is:

1. compute only `PowerSeries.trunc 3 (mulSeries F 3)`;
2. prove it is the polynomial \(3X+3cX^2\);
3. split the tail with
   `PowerSeries.eq_X_pow_mul_shift_add_trunc 3`.

The pinned Mathlib already contains exactly that tail-splitting lemma:

```lean
PowerSeries.eq_X_pow_mul_shift_add_trunc
```

in `Mathlib/RingTheory/PowerSeries/Trunc.lean`.

The formal calculation needs only the degree-two truncation

```lean
F(X,Y) = X + Y + c * X * Y  mod (X,Y)^3.
```

It does not need the height, the Hasse invariant, or any coefficient through degree nine.

## 2.2 Evaluation and valuation

```lean
-- `ord : K → WithTop ℤ` is the normalized additive valuation.
-- The project may instead package the same facts through Mathlib's `AddValuation`.

theorem ord_three_formal_nsmul_eq
    {z : O}
    (hz0 : (z : K) ≠ 0)
    (hz2 : (2 : ℤ) ≤ ord (z : K)) :
    ord (formalNsmul F 3 z : K) = ord (z : K) + 3
```

The proof evaluates `three_mulSeries_decomposition`. `PowerSeries.eval₂Hom` is already available in the pinned Mathlib and turns evaluation into a ring homomorphism, so after obtaining the decomposition no term-by-term infinite-sum manipulation is necessary.

The valuation proof should be factored through a generic “dominant summand” lemma, for example:

```lean
theorem add_eq_left_of_ord_lt
    {x y : K}
    (h : ord x < ord y) :
    ord (x + y) = ord x
```

or the corresponding existing multiplicative-valuation theorem. In Mathlib's `Valuation` orientation, the relevant existing names are

```lean
Valuation.map_add_eq_of_lt_left
Valuation.map_add_eq_of_lt_right
Valuation.map_eq_of_sub_lt
```

in `Mathlib/RingTheory/Valuation/Basic.lean`.

Then expose the kernel statement actually needed downstream:

```lean
theorem three_formal_nsmul_eq_zero_iff
    {z : FormalM2 F} :
    (3 • z = 0) ↔ z = 0
```

## 2.3 Prime-to-three part

```lean
theorem mulSeries_linear_decomposition
    (F : OneDimFormalGroup O) (q : ℕ) :
    ∃ Hq : PowerSeries O,
      mulSeries F q =
        PowerSeries.C (q : O) * PowerSeries.X +
        PowerSeries.X ^ 2 * Hq


theorem ord_formal_nsmul_eq_of_not_three_dvd
    {q : ℕ} (hq : ¬ 3 ∣ q)
    {z : O}
    (hz0 : (z : K) ≠ 0)
    (hz1 : (1 : ℤ) ≤ ord (z : K)) :
    ord (formalNsmul F q z : K) = ord (z : K)
```

The first theorem is just “constant coefficient zero, linear coefficient \(q\), so the rest is divisible by \(X^2\).”

## 2.4 Final torsion theorem

```lean
theorem formalM2_nsmul_eq_zero
    (z : FormalM2 F)
    {n : ℕ} (hn : n ≠ 0)
    (h : n • z = 0) :
    z = 0
```

A convenient proof factors \(n=3^rq\) with \(3\nmid q\), applies the prime-to-three valuation theorem to `[q]z`, and then iterates `ord_three_formal_nsmul_eq`.

Equivalently, after proving the displayed theorem, install the project's preferred torsion-free class or expose the exact proposition required by Package-II.

## 2.5 A filtration formulation may be even easier in Lean

Instead of explicitly carrying `WithTop ℤ`, the key statement can be expressed using powers of the maximal ideal. For \(r\ge2\):

\[
[3](z)-3z\in\mathfrak m^{r+4}
\qquad(z\in\mathfrak m^r).
\tag{6}
\]

Indeed:

* \(3cz^2\in\mathfrak m^{3+2r}\subseteq\mathfrak m^{r+4}\);
* \(z^3H(z)\in\mathfrak m^{3r}\subseteq\mathfrak m^{r+4}\).

Since \(3\in\mathfrak m^3\setminus\mathfrak m^4\), if

\[
z\in\mathfrak m^r\setminus\mathfrak m^{r+1},
\]

then

\[
[3](z)\in\mathfrak m^{r+3}\setminus\mathfrak m^{r+4}.
\]

Thus `[3]` induces on associated graded pieces the map

\[
\operatorname{gr}^r\longrightarrow\operatorname{gr}^{r+3},
\qquad \bar z\longmapsto \overline{(3/\pi^3)z},
\]

and \(3/\pi^3\) is a unit. This is the same proof as the valuation argument, but it may align better with an implementation where `FormalM2` is already a subgroup defined by ideal membership.

A good central Lean lemma is therefore either

```lean
theorem three_nsmul_ord_eq ...
```

or

```lean
theorem three_nsmul_sub_linear_mem
    {r : ℕ} (hr : 2 ≤ r) {z : O}
    (hz : z ∈ maximalIdeal O ^ r) :
    formalNsmul F 3 z - 3 * z ∈ maximalIdeal O ^ (r + 4)
```

The second version avoids most arithmetic with infinite valuations.

# 4. Minimal formal-log fallback

Iteration **does** close, so no formal logarithm is needed. For completeness, the smallest log theorem that would suffice is much weaker than an isomorphism theorem and does not require an exponential.

Let

\[
\log_F(T)=T+\sum_{m\ge2}\ell_mT^m\in K[[T]].
\]

It is enough to prove:

1. the formal homomorphism identity
   \[
   \log_F(F(X,Y))=\log_F(X)+\log_F(Y);
   \]
2. the denominator bound
   \[
   m\ell_m\in\mathcal O_L\qquad(m\ge1);
   \]
3. evaluation of this series converges for \(v(z)\ge2\).

The coefficient bound gives

\[
v(\ell_mz^m)
 \ge m s-v(m)
 =ms-3\operatorname{ord}_3(m).
\]

For \(m\ge2\) and \(s\ge2\),

\[
ms-3\operatorname{ord}_3(m)>s,
\tag{7}
\]

or equivalently

\[
2(m-1)>3\operatorname{ord}_3(m).
\]

Thus every nonlinear term has valuation strictly greater than \(v(z)\), the tail converges, and

\[
v(\log_F(z)-z)>v(z),\qquad v(\log_F(z))=v(z).
\]

Consequently `log_F` is injective on \(\mathfrak m^2\). If \([n](z)=0\), then

\[
0=\log_F([n](z))=n\log_F(z),
\]

so \(\log_F(z)=0\) in characteristic zero, and hence \(z=0\).

A Lean-shaped fallback package would be:

```lean
structure DeepFormalLogData
    (F : OneDimFormalGroup O) where
  logSeries : PowerSeries K
  coeff_zero : PowerSeries.coeff 0 logSeries = 0
  coeff_one  : PowerSeries.coeff 1 logSeries = 1
  denominator_bound :
    ∀ m : ℕ, 1 ≤ m →
      (m : K) * PowerSeries.coeff m logSeries ∈ O
  map_add_formal :
    -- formal bivariate identity log(F(X,Y)) = log(X)+log(Y)
    ...
```

Then the only analytic consumer is:

```lean
theorem deepFormalLog_eval_ord_eq
    (D : DeepFormalLogData F)
    {z : O} (hz0 : (z : K) ≠ 0)
    (hz2 : (2 : ℤ) ≤ ord (z : K)) :
    ord (eval D.logSeries z) = ord (z : K)
```

However, this is **not** a finite truncation argument. The denominator estimate and convergence concern every coefficient. Constructing the log from the invariant differential requires:

* forming the invariant differential power series;
* inverting its unit power series over \(\mathcal O_L\);
* dividing the degree-\(m-1\) coefficient by \(m\);
* proving the formal homomorphism identity, usually by formal differentiation and a chain rule;
* transferring formal identities to convergent evaluations.

That is substantially more infrastructure than (1). No exponential is needed for torsion-freeness, but even this injective-log fragment is not “a handful of explicitly computed coefficients.”

# 5. Height-two and Tate-module alternatives

## 5.1 Honda/Lubin–Tate-style identification

Over an algebraically closed residue field, one-dimensional formal groups of height two are classified up to isomorphism. This does not immediately give a useful integral isomorphism over \(\mathcal O_L\):

* the lift over the ramified characteristic-zero ring is not determined solely by the reduced height;
* a coordinate change must be proved integral with unit linear coefficient;
* one must prove that it preserves the exact depth \(\mathfrak m^2\), not merely the formal identity neighborhood after an unspecified extension;
* one still needs convergence/evaluation control.

Formalizing such an identification is much heavier than the two-jet argument and buys nothing for this goal.

## 5.2 The \(3\)-adic Tate module

The freeness

\[
T_3(E)\cong\mathbb Z_3^2
\]

is not the desired statement. The Tate module is an inverse limit of geometric \(3^r\)-torsion over an algebraic closure. It is torsion-free as a \(\mathbb Z_3\)-module, but that does not prohibit finite-level \(3\)-power torsion points in the formal group over ramified extensions. Indeed, such geometric torsion exists.

The question here is field- and radius-sensitive: no nonzero **\(L_\pi\)-point with parameter in \(\mathfrak m^2\)** is torsion. The Tate-module statement does not encode that valuation cutoff. Recovering it from Galois representations or the connected \(3\)-divisible group would require more theory than the direct estimate.

## 5.3 Newton polygon

One can also study the Newton polygon of \([3](T)/T\). Height two constrains the degree-nine coefficient and the valuations of lower coefficients, and this excludes roots of valuation at least two. But proving precisely the required Newton-polygon coefficient bounds is essentially a repackaging of the same calculation, with more formal infrastructure.

The associated-graded argument in §2.5 is the slick structural version worth using.

# 6. Honest scope assessment

Assuming the project already has:

* the integral formal group law attached to the chosen good model;
* evaluation of the formal law on \(\mathfrak m\);
* the identification of formal-point group multiplication with evaluated formal series;
* the normalized valuation facts \(v(\pi)=1\) and \(v(3)=3\);

then Package-II reduces to:

1. one degree-two `[3]`-series computation;
2. one dominant-term valuation lemma;
3. a short factorization of the torsion order into \(3^rq\).

That is a bounded, low-hundreds-of-lines development, and conceptually a **single lemma package**. It is not a multi-week formal-log formalization.

If the project does not yet have the evaluated formal-group/nsmul bridge, that bridge is the main additional work. Even then, the pinned Mathlib already supplies power-series evaluation, truncation, and valuation exactness. The missing code remains a local interface plus the two-jet calculation, rather than general local formal logarithm theory.

## Final recommendation

Implement the following theorem first and make everything else consume it:

```lean
theorem formal_three_nsmul_ord
    {z : FormalKernel E}
    (hz0 : z ≠ 0)
    (hz2 : 2 ≤ formalOrd z) :
    formalOrd (3 • z) = formalOrd z + 3
```

Prove it from

```lean
[3](T) = 3*T + 3*c*T^2 + T^3*H(T).
```

Then derive

```lean
theorem formal_m2_torsion_free
    {z : FormalM2 E} {n : ℕ}
    (hn : n ≠ 0) (h : n • z = 0) :
    z = 0
```

No height-two normal form, no Tate module, and no formal logarithm are required.
