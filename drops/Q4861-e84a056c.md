# 1. Audit against the committed raw recurrence

The committed raw scalar recurrence has Newton degree pattern

\[
(28,21,14,7)

and Poincaré polynomial

\[
P_{\rm raw}(c)
=c^3+560c^2+8960c+4096
=(c+16)(c^2+544c+256).
\tag{1.1}

The Pochhammer step satisfies

\[
\frac{H_{n+1}}{H_n}

\[
\frac{P_{\rm raw}(-16\xi)}{-4096}

By contrast, the supplied degree-13 arrays give

\begin{aligned}

 +3440640\xi-24576\\
&\qquad=24576(\xi^3-70\xi^2+140\xi-1).
\end{aligned}
\tag{1.3}
\]

Equations (1.2) and (1.3) are incompatible.

The discrepancy is exactly a factor \(2\) in the \(\xi^2\) coefficient and a factor \(4\) in the \(\xi\) coefficient. This explains the repair (E3).

# 3. Factorization of the shift operator

Use the forward-shift Ore algebra

\[
Sn=(n+1)S
\]

and write

\[
L=c_0(n)+c_1(n)S+c_2(n)S^2+c_3(n)S^3.
\tag{3.1}
\]

All four coefficients have the same degree. Hence the Newton polygon at infinity has only slope \(0\), with multiplicity three. Its characteristic polynomial is (E1):

\[
\chi_{\rm print}(\xi)=\xi^3-70\xi^2+140\xi-1.
\]

This cubic is irreducible over \(\mathbf Q\). Since it is monic, a rational root would have to be \(\pm1\), but

\[
\chi_{\rm print}(1)=70,
\qquad
\chi_{\rm print}(-1)=-212.
\]

Now suppose that \(L\) had a nontrivial factorization over \(\mathbf Q(n)\):

\[
L=L_aL_b,
\qquad
0<\operatorname{ord}L_a,\operatorname{ord}L_b<3.
\tag{3.2}
\]

The Newton polygon of a product is the Minkowski sum of the Newton polygons of its factors. Since every slope of \(L\) is zero, every slope of each factor is zero. The characteristic polynomial at infinity of the product is then the product of the characteristic polynomials of the two factors, over the constant field \(\mathbf Q\). This would factor the irreducible cubic \(\chi_{\rm print}\), a contradiction.

Therefore

\[
\boxed{L\text{ is irreducible in }\mathbf Q(n)\langle S\rangle.}
\tag{3.3}
\]

In particular,

\[
\boxed{L\ne L_2\circ L_1}
\]

for every order-one \(L_1\) and order-two \(L_2\) over \(\mathbf Q(n)\).

This is stronger than merely running Petkovšek's hypergeometric-solution test: it rules out both possible order splittings \(1+2\) and \(2+1\).

## 3.1 The intended repaired operator

For the repaired coefficients (E3), the characteristic polynomial becomes

\[
\chi_*(\xi)
=\xi^3-35\xi^2+35\xi-1
=(\xi-1)(\xi^2-34\xi+1).
\tag{3.4}
\]

Thus the simple irreducibility obstruction disappears. It does **not** follow that the operator factors. Poincaré factorization is only a necessary condition. The current repository at commit `254cbe77...` still describes the Ore intertwiner as a search problem and does not contain a certified factorization or an explicit intertwiner.

The repaired operator must therefore be tested separately by van Hoeij's algorithm; its result cannot be inferred from the arrays printed in this question.

# 4. Exact ordinary-generating-function operator

Let

\[
\widehat F(z)=\sum_{n\ge0}\widehat Q_nz^n,
\qquad
\theta=z\frac{d}{dz}.
\]

For a recurrence

\[
\sum_{j=0}^3c_j(n)\widehat Q_{n+j}=0,
\]

multiply by \(z^{n+3}\) and sum over \(n\ge0\). The exact Mellin-transformed operator is

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathscr D_{13}={}&c_3(\theta-3)
+z\,c_2(\theta-2)\\
&+z^2c_1(\theta-1)
+z^3c_0(\theta).
\end{aligned}}
\tag{4.1}
\]

It has differential order \(13\).

The startup polynomial is especially simple because of the endpoint factors in Section 2:

\[
c_3(-3)=c_2(-2)=c_3(-2)=0.
\]

One obtains

\[
\boxed{
\mathscr D_{13}\widehat F(z)=\beta z^2,
}
\tag{4.2}
\]

where

\[
\boxed{
\beta
=c_1(-1)\widehat Q_0
+c_2(-1)\widehat Q_1
+c_3(-1)\widehat Q_2.
}
\tag{4.3}
\]

The question supplies only two initial values for an order-three recurrence. Thus the minimal homogeneous ODE for the sequence is not well posed until \(\widehat Q_2\) or the starting index of the recurrence is specified.

There are two cases.

### Case A: the recurrence is imposed already at \(n=-1\)

Because \(c_0(-1)=0\), its \(n=-1\) equation is exactly

\[
c_3(-1)\widehat Q_2
+c_2(-1)\widehat Q_1
+c_1(-1)\widehat Q_0=0.
\]

Thus

\[
\boxed{
\widehat Q_2
=-\frac{c_1(-1)\widehat Q_0+c_2(-1)\widehat Q_1}
        {c_3(-1)},
}
\tag{4.4}
\]

and \(\beta=0\). Then

\[
\boxed{\mathscr D_{13}\widehat F=0.}
\tag{4.5}
\]

The Mellin transform

\[
n\longmapsto\theta,
\qquad
S\longmapsto z^{-1}
\]

is an isomorphism of the corresponding localized Ore algebras; indeed

\[
z^{-1}\theta=(\theta+1)z^{-1}
\]

matches \(Sn=(n+1)S\). Multiplication by the unit \(z^3\) gives (4.1). Therefore irreducibility of \(L\) implies irreducibility of \(\mathscr D_{13}\) over \(\mathbf Q(z)\).

Hence in Case A,

\[
\boxed{
\mathscr D_{13}\text{ is the minimal ODE of }\widehat F,
\quad \operatorname{ord}\mathscr D_{13}=13.
}
\tag{4.6}
\]

### Case B: the recurrence starts only at \(n=0\)

Then \(\widehat Q_2\) is an independent third initial value and generally \(\beta\ne0\). Equation (4.2) is the exact inhomogeneous ODE. A homogeneous annihilator is

\[
\boxed{(\theta-2)\mathscr D_{13},}
\tag{4.7}
\]

of order \(14\). Determining whether a special initial triple has a smaller homogeneous annihilator requires the missing value \(\widehat Q_2\). It cannot be decided from \(\widehat Q_0,\widehat Q_1\) alone.

## 4.1 Leading coefficient

The coefficient of \(\theta^{13}\) in (4.1) is

\[
24576(1-70z+140z^2-z^3).
\tag{4.8}
\]

Since

\[
\theta^{13}=z^{13}\frac{d^{13}}{dz^{13}}
+\text{lower derivatives},
\]

the ordinary-derivative leading coefficient is

\[
\boxed{
\operatorname{lc}_{D_z}(\mathscr D_{13})
=24576z^{13}(1-70z+140z^2-z^3).
}
\tag{4.9}
\]

Thus the finite characteristic singularities of the printed operator are the roots of

\[
1-70z+140z^2-z^3,
\]

not \(z=1\) and the two roots of \(\Delta(z)=1-34z+z^2\).

## 4.2 Full ordinary-derivative coefficients

Formula (4.1) is already an exact ODE. If

\[
c_j(n)=\sum_{m=0}^{13}c_{j,m}n^m
\]

and \(S(a,r)\) denotes a Stirling number of the second kind, then

\[
\mathscr D_{13}
=\sum_{r=0}^{13}A_r(z)\frac{d^r}{dz^r},
\]

with the completely explicit formula

\[
\boxed{
A_r(z)=z^r\sum_{j=0}^3z^{3-j}
\sum_{m=0}^{13}c_{j,m}
\sum_{a=r}^m
\binom ma(-j)^{m-a}S(a,r).
}
\tag{4.10}
\]

This is preferable to printing fourteen very large expanded polynomials and is exact over \(\mathbf Z[z]\).

# 5. Comparison with the integrated-\(K\) equation

Let

\[
Y(k)=\frac12\int_0^kK(s)\,ds.
\]

It satisfies the rank-three equation

\[
\boxed{
k(1-k^2)Y'''(k)+(1-3k^2)Y''(k)-kY'(k)=0.}
\tag{5.1}
\]

After the Brafman change of variable, one obtains an order-three equation whose singular divisor contains

\[
\Delta(z)=1-34z+z^2.
\]

The version quoted in the question has leading coefficient

\[
4z^2(1-z^2)^2\Delta(z).
\tag{5.2}
\]

The printed CMF-derived ODE and the integrated-\(K\) ODE cannot match for three independent reasons.

1. **Rank:** \(\mathscr D_{13}\) has order \(13\), while (5.1) and (5.2) have order \(3\).
2. **Irreducibility:** \(\mathscr D_{13}\) is irreducible over \(\mathbf Q(z)\) in Case A, so it has no order-three differential submodule or right factor.
3. **Singular/characteristic divisor:** the printed operator has
   \[
   1-70z+140z^2-z^3,
   \]
   whereas the integrated-\(K\) equation is controlled by
   \[
   (1-z^2)^2\Delta(z).
   \]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{
\text{the ODEs do not match, and no rational gauge transformation exists.}
}
\tag{5.3}
\]

This includes scalar gauges and general finite differential intertwiners: an intertwiner from the order-three integrated-\(K\) module into an irreducible order-thirteen module would create a nonzero proper submodule.

## 5.1 What happens after the spectral repair

For the repaired middle coefficients (E3), the top coefficient becomes

\[
\begin{aligned}
\operatorname{lc}_{D_z}(\mathscr D_{13}^*)
&=24576z^{13}(1-35z+35z^2-z^3)\\
&=24576z^{13}(1-z)(1-34z+z^2).
\end{aligned}
\tag{5.4}
\]

This restores the expected finite spectral divisor

\[
(1-z)\Delta(z).
\]

It still does not prove that \(\mathscr D_{13}^*\) has an order-three factor, much less that such a factor is gauge-equivalent to (5.2). An exact factorization or Ore-intertwiner identity is required:

\[
\boxed{
\mathscr D_{13}^*T=R\mathscr D_{\rm intK},
\qquad
T=t_0(z)+t_1(z)D_z+t_2(z)D_z^2.
}
\tag{5.5}
\]

No such \(T\) is present in the repository at commit `254cbe77...`; `problems/2.5/proof.tex` still describes this as the missing finite certificate. Hence there is presently no explicit gauge to print.

# 6. Direct answers to the five tasks

## (a) Polynomial factorization and gcd

The complete factorizations are (2.6)--(2.9). The residual factors \(P_6,\widetilde P_6,R_9,R_{10}\) are irreducible over \(\mathbf Q\), and

\[
\gcd(c_0,c_1,c_2,c_3)=1.
\]

## (b) Difference-operator factorization

For the arrays exactly as supplied,

\[
L\text{ is irreducible over }\mathbf Q(n).
\]

Therefore no factorization \(L=L_2\circ L_1\) exists.

## (c) ODE and minimal ODE

The exact OGF operator is (4.1). If the two initial values are completed by the \(n=-1\) recurrence, it is homogeneous, irreducible, minimal, and has order \(13\). If the recurrence begins at \(n=0\), the missing third initial value is required; generically one has the order-14 homogeneous annihilator (4.7).

## (d) Comparison with integrated \(K\)

For the arrays as printed, they do not match: ranks, Poincaré polynomials, and singular divisors differ.

## (e) Gauge transformation

There is no gauge for the printed operator. After correcting the middle coefficients, spectral agreement is restored but an exact order-three factor/intertwiner remains uncomputed, so no verified gauge transformation is currently available.

# Bottom line

The polynomial factorization itself is clean and exact, but it uncovers a transcription/normalization error before the deeper ODE comparison begins. For the arrays actually printed, the operator is irreducible and its minimal homogeneous ODE (under the natural \(n=-1\) initialization) has order \(13\), not \(3\). It is not the integrated-\(K\) system and admits no gauge to it.

The next valid step is to regenerate the normalized recurrence directly from the committed raw degree-pattern \((28,21,14,7)\) operator using

\[
\widehat c_j(n)
=c_j^{\rm raw}(n)
\prod_{r=0}^{j-1}\frac{H_{n+r+1}}{H_{n+r}},
\]

divide by the single common polynomial gcd, and verify the resulting coefficients on exact matrix-product terms. Only after that audit should van Hoeij factorization and the integrated-\(K\) gauge search be rerun.
