# 3. Van Hoeij factorization of the shift operator

Let \(S\) denote the forward shift, \(Sf(n)=f(n+1)\), and write

\[
L=c_0(n)+c_1(n)S+c_2(n)S^2+c_3(n)S^3.
\tag{3.1}
\]

All four coefficients have degree \(13\), so the Newton polygon at infinity has only slope \(0\). The characteristic polynomial is

\[
\chi_{\rm print}(\xi)=\xi^3-70\xi^2+140\xi-1.
\]

It is irreducible over \(\mathbf Q\): since it is monic, a rational root would have to be \(\pm1\), but

\[
\chi_{\rm print}(1)=70,
\qquad
\chi_{\rm print}(-1)=-212.
\]

Suppose

\[
L=L_aL_b,
\qquad
0<\operatorname{ord}L_a,\operatorname{ord}L_b<3.
\]

The Newton polygon of a product is the Minkowski sum of the Newton polygons of the factors. Since the product has only slope zero, both factors are regular singular at infinity. Their characteristic polynomials multiply to \(\chi_{\rm print}\), yielding a nontrivial factorization over \(\mathbf Q\), impossible.

Therefore

\[
\boxed{L\text{ is irreducible in }\mathbf Q(n)\langle S\rangle.}
\tag{3.2}
\]

In particular,

\[
\boxed{L\ne L_2\circ L_1}
\]

for every order-one \(L_1\) and order-two \(L_2\) over \(\mathbf Q(n)\).

For the repaired operator \(L^*\) from (E3), the characteristic polynomial becomes

\[
\chi_*(\xi)
=(\xi-1)(\xi^2-34\xi+1).
\tag{3.3}
\]

That removes the leading obstruction but does **not** prove factorization. A separate van Hoeij computation is required. The repository at `254cbe77...` does not contain a certified factorization of this repaired operator.

# 4. Exact OGF differential operator

Let

\[
\widehat F(z)=\sum_{n\ge0}\widehat Q_nz^n,
\qquad
\theta=z\frac{d}{dz}.
\]

Multiplying the recurrence by \(z^{n+3}\) and summing gives the exact Euler-form operator

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathscr D_{13}={}&c_3(\theta-3)
+z\,c_2(\theta-2)\\
&+z^2c_1(\theta-1)
+z^3c_0(\theta).
\end{aligned}}
\tag{4.1}
\]

It has differential order \(13\).

Because

\[
c_3(-3)=c_2(-2)=c_3(-2)=0,
\]

the startup polynomial reduces to one term:

\[
\boxed{
\mathscr D_{13}\widehat F(z)=\beta z^2,
}
\tag{4.2}
\]

where

\[
\boxed{
\beta
=c_1(-1)\widehat Q_0
+c_2(-1)\widehat Q_1
+c_3(-1)\widehat Q_2.
}
\tag{4.3}
\]

The prompt gives only two initial values for an order-three recurrence. Thus the sequence and its minimal homogeneous ODE are not determined unless either \(\widehat Q_2\) or the starting index is supplied.

## 4.1 If the recurrence is imposed at \(n=-1\)

Since \(c_0(-1)=0\), the \(n=-1\) relation forces

\[
\boxed{
\widehat Q_2
=-\frac{c_1(-1)\widehat Q_0+c_2(-1)\widehat Q_1}
        {c_3(-1)}.
}
\tag{4.4}
\]

Then \(\beta=0\), so

\[
\mathscr D_{13}\widehat F=0.
\tag{4.5}
\]

This proves that an order-13 ODE annihilates the resulting series. It does **not** by itself prove that order \(13\) is minimal. The minimality question is the factorization problem for \(\mathscr D_{13}\) in

\[
\mathbf Q(z)\langle D_z\rangle.
\]

A Mellin transform relates the recurrence and differential operators, but a differential factorization over the rational coefficient field involves a localization larger than the polynomial shift algebra. Therefore the shift irreducibility in Section 3 should not be used as a substitute for `D13.factor()`.

## 4.2 If the recurrence starts at \(n=0\)

Then \(\widehat Q_2\) is a third independent initial value and generally \(\beta\ne0\). Equation (4.2) is the exact inhomogeneous ODE. One homogeneous annihilator is

\[
\boxed{(\theta-2)\mathscr D_{13},}
\tag{4.6}
\]

of order \(14\). A smaller sequence-specific annihilator is possible only after the actual third initial value is inserted and the resulting differential operator is factored.

## 4.3 Leading coefficient

The coefficient of \(\theta^{13}\) in (4.1) is

\[
24576(1-70z+140z^2-z^3).
\]

Since

\[
\theta^{13}=z^{13}D_z^{13}+\text{lower derivatives},
\]

the ordinary-derivative head coefficient is

\[
\boxed{
\operatorname{lc}_{D_z}(\mathscr D_{13})
=24576z^{13}(1-70z+140z^2-z^3).
}
\tag{4.7}
\]

For the repaired middle coefficients (E3), it becomes

\[
\boxed{
\operatorname{lc}_{D_z}(\mathscr D_{13}^*)
=24576z^{13}(1-z)(1-34z+z^2).
}
\tag{4.8}
\]

## 4.4 Exact ordinary-derivative expansion

Write

\[
c_j(n)=\sum_{m=0}^{13}c_{j,m}n^m.
\]

If \(S(a,r)\) is a Stirling number of the second kind, then

\[
\mathscr D_{13}
=\sum_{r=0}^{13}A_r(z)D_z^r,
\]

where

\[
\boxed{
A_r(z)=z^r\sum_{j=0}^3z^{3-j}
\sum_{m=0}^{13}c_{j,m}
\sum_{a=r}^m
\binom ma(-j)^{m-a}S(a,r).
}
\tag{4.9}
\]

This is the complete exact ODE in ordinary-derivative form without printing fourteen very large expanded polynomials.

# 5. Comparison with the integrated-\(K\) equation

Let

\[
Y(k)=\frac12\int_0^kK(s)\,ds.
\]

It satisfies

\[
\boxed{
k(1-k^2)Y'''(k)+(1-3k^2)Y''(k)-kY'(k)=0.}
\tag{5.1}
\]

After the Brafman pullback, the order-three equation has the quadratic spectral factor

\[
\Delta(z)=1-34z+z^2.
\]

The version quoted in the question has leading coefficient

\[
4z^2(1-z^2)^2\Delta(z).
\tag{5.2}
\]

For the arrays as printed, the CMF-derived Euler operator instead has the characteristic divisor

\[
1-70z+140z^2-z^3.
\]

Thus the claimed Delannoy/integrated-\(K\) spectral identification already fails at the principal level. The two operators do not match.

A scalar rational gauge cannot change the Poincaré roots. A more general differential intertwiner would first require an order-three differential factor of \(\mathscr D_{13}\). No such factor is certified, and the printed principal polynomial is not the Delannoy polynomial.

Therefore, for the arrays exactly as supplied,

\[
\boxed{
\text{there is no verified gauge transformation to the integrated-}K\text{ equation.}
}
\tag{5.3}
\]

After applying the spectral repair (E3), the head coefficient acquires the correct factor

\[
(1-z)\Delta(z).
\]

That is only a necessary spectral match. One must still factor \(\mathscr D_{13}^*\) and, if an order-three factor appears, solve an intertwiner equation such as

\[
\boxed{
\mathscr D_{13}^*T=R\mathscr D_{\rm intK},
\qquad
T=t_0(z)+t_1(z)D_z+t_2(z)D_z^2.
}
\tag{5.4}
\]

No such \(T\) is present in the repository at commit `254cbe77...`; `problems/2.5/proof.tex` still presents the finite Ore certificate as missing.

The exact canonical OGF operator is (4.1), of order \(13\). It is homogeneous if the two initial values are completed by the \(n=-1\) relation. # Bottom line

The factorization request has a clean exact answer, but it exposes a normalization error before the harder ODE question begins. The arrays printed in the question define an irreducible order-three **difference** operator with characteristic cubic

\[
\xi^3-70\xi^2+140\xi-1,
\]

not the Delannoy cubic. Their exact OGF transform has differential order \(13\), but its minimal differential factorization must be computed separately and cannot be inferred from the claimed spectrum.

The correct next step is to regenerate the normalized recurrence directly from the committed raw degree-pattern \((28,21,14,7)\) operator via

\[
\widehat c_j(n)
=c_j^{\rm raw}(n)
\prod_{r=0}^{j-1}\frac{H_{n+r+1}}{H_{n+r}},
\]

divide all four coefficients by **one common gcd**, and verify the result on exact matrix-product terms. Only after that audit should differential factorization and the integrated-\(K\) gauge search be rerun.
