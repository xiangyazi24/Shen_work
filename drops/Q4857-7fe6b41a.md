# Problem 2.5: the integrated-\(K\) pullback, its coefficient recurrence, and the exact CMF mismatch

## Executive conclusion

Let

\[
Y(k)=\frac12\int_0^k K(s)\,ds,\qquad
k(1-k^2)Y_{kkk}+(1-3k^2)Y_{kk}-kY_k=0,
\]

and make the Brafman substitution

\[
k=k(z)=\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z},\qquad
\Delta(z)=z^2-34z+1.
\]

Writing \(y(z)=Y(k(z))\), the exact pulled-back scalar equation is

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&4z^2(1-z^2)^2\Delta(z)\,y'''(z)\\
&\quad+8z(1-z^2)
 \bigl(1-54z+103z^2+48z^3-2z^4\bigr)y''(z)\\
&\quad+\bigl(1-158z+1127z^2-852z^3-529z^4-110z^5+9z^6\bigr)y'(z)=0.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{1}
\]

The direct answer to the key question is:

\[
\boxed{\text{The recurrence obtained directly from (1) is not the printed CMF recurrence.}}
\]

More precisely:

1. At \(z=0\), equation (1) has Frobenius exponents
   \[
   \boxed{0,\ \frac12,\ \frac12,}
   \]
   not the Birkhoff indices \(0,-3,0\). These are different invariants: the former are local differential exponents at \(z=0\); the latter are asymptotic powers of a normalized difference equation as \(n\to\infty\).

2. A nonconstant local solution is not a Taylor series in \(z\). It has the form
   \[
   y(z)=C+z^{1/2}\sum_{n\ge0}a_nz^n,
   \]
   together with a logarithmic companion because the exponent \(1/2\) is repeated.

3. The resulting direct coefficient recurrence has seven terms and cubic polynomial coefficients. Its Poincare polynomial is
   \[
   \boxed{
   (\lambda^2-1)^2(\lambda^2-34\lambda+1),}
   \]
   whereas the hypergeometrically normalized Problem 2.5 CMF has
   \[
   \boxed{
   (\lambda-1)(\lambda^2-34\lambda+1).}
   \]
   The silver-ratio quadratic factor agrees exactly, but the direct pullback has one extra \(\lambda=1\) factor and a double \(\lambda=-1\) factor.

4. The closest exact match is not \(Y\) itself but the homogeneous Brafman period
   \[
   F(z)=\frac{2}{\pi(1-z)}K\!\left(\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z}\right)
       =\sum_{n\ge0}D_n^2z^n.
   \]
   Its coefficients satisfy a four-term degree-three recurrence whose Poincare polynomial is exactly
   \((\lambda-1)(\lambda^2-34\lambda+1)\).

5. Passing from that homogeneous period to \(Y\) is the Abel extension
   \[
   \boxed{
   y(z)=\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
   \int_0^z\frac{1+t}{\sqrt t(1-t)}F(t)\,dt,}
   \tag{2}
   \]
   normalized by \(y(0)=0\). At
   \(\rho=17-12\sqrt2\), one has \(k(\rho)=1\) and therefore
   \[
   y(\rho)=Y(1)=G.
   \]

Thus the Brafman/integrated-\(K\) construction gives the correct candidate period and the correct silver-ratio spectrum, but it does not by itself identify the printed CMF cyclic recurrence with that period module. The missing proof object remains an explicit hypergeometric coefficient twist followed by a rational differential/Ore intertwiner and initial-value matching.

# 1. Chain-rule derivation of the \(z\)-equation

Put

\[
p(z)=k'(z)=\frac{2\sqrt2(1+z)}{\sqrt z(1-z)^2}.
\]

For \(y(z)=Y(k(z))\),

\[
Y_k=\frac{y'}p,
\]

\[
Y_{kk}=\frac{y''}{p^2}-\frac{p'}{p^3}y',
\]

and

\[
Y_{kkk}=\frac{y'''}{p^3}
-3\frac{p'}{p^4}y''
+\left(3\frac{(p')^2}{p^5}-\frac{p''}{p^4}\right)y'.
\]

Substitution into

\[
k(1-k^2)Y_{kkk}+(1-3k^2)Y_{kk}-kY_k=0
\]

and clearing denominators gives (1).

A useful Euler-operator form uses

\[
\theta=z\frac d{dz},
\]

\[
A_0(z)=(1-z^2)^2\Delta(z),
\]

\[
B_0(z)=(1-z^2)(1-54z+103z^2+48z^3-2z^4),
\]

and

\[
C_0(z)=1-158z+1127z^2-852z^3-529z^4-110z^5+9z^6.
\]

Then (1) is equivalent to

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{int},z}
=\bigl[4A_0(z)(\theta-1)(\theta-2)
+8B_0(z)(\theta-1)+C_0(z)\bigr]\theta.}
\tag{3}
\]

The right factor \(\theta\) is the constant submodule. Equation (3) is the differential realization of the extension

\[
0\longrightarrow\mathbf1
\longrightarrow M_{\mathrm{int}}
\longrightarrow M_K
\longrightarrow0.
\]

# 2. Singularities and indicial equations

The leading coefficient of (1) is

\[
4z^2(1-z^2)^2\Delta(z).
\]

Consequently the displayed scalar cyclic equation has singular points

\[
\boxed{z=0,\quad z=-1,\quad z=1,\quad
z=\rho,\quad z=\rho^{-1},\quad z=\infty,}
\tag{4}
\]

where

\[
\rho=17-12\sqrt2,\qquad \rho^{-1}=17+12\sqrt2.
\]

This corrects the proposed singularity list. In particular, \(z=1\) cannot be omitted: it is the inverse image of \(k=\infty\), a genuine singular point of the elliptic equation.

The point \(z=-1\) is different. Since \(k'(-1)=0\) while \(k(-1)=2i\sqrt2\) is an ordinary point of the original equation, \(z=-1\) is an apparent ramification singularity of this scalar cyclic vector. Its exponents are

\[
0,2,4.
\]

It can be removed at the system level by changing to a better basis, for example one involving the Brafman period \(F\), but it is present in the direct scalar equation for \(y\).

## 2.1 Indicial equation at \(z=0\)

Near zero,

\[
4z^2y'''+8zy''+y'=0+\text{higher powers of }z.
\]

Putting \(y=z^r\) gives

\[
4r(r-1)(r-2)+8r(r-1)+r
=r(2r-1)^2.
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{\mathcal I_0(r)=r(2r-1)^2,\qquad
r=0,\frac12,\frac12.}
\tag{5}
\]

At \(z=1\) the indicial polynomial is \(r^3\), so all three exponents are zero, with logarithms. At either zero of \(\Delta\), the exponents are \(0,1,1\), as expected from the logarithmic singularity of \(K\) at \(k=1\).

The Birkhoff powers \(0,-3,0\) of the normalized CMF recurrence are not supposed to equal (5). They describe a difference equation at \(n=\infty\), whereas (5) describes a differential equation at \(z=0\).

# 3. Frobenius coefficient recurrence

Write the nonconstant regular branch as

\[
y(z)=z^{1/2}A(z),\qquad
A(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_nz^n.
\]

The first coefficients, normalized by \(a_0=1\), are

\[
A(z)=1+\frac{11}{3}z+\frac{189}{5}z^2+\cdots.
\]

To state the recurrence compactly, define

\[
\begin{array}{ll}
P_0(x)=x(2x-1)^2,
& P_1(x)=-2x(68x^2+12x-1),\\[1mm]
P_2(x)=x(-4x^2+828x+303),
& P_3(x)=4x(68x^2-281),\\[1mm]
P_4(x)=x(-4x^2-828x+303),
& P_5(x)=-2x(68x^2-12x-1),\\[1mm]
P_6(x)=x(2x+1)^2.
\end{array}
\tag{6}
\]

Then, with \(a_m=0\) for \(m<0\),

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{j=0}^{6}
P_j\!\left(n-j+\frac12\right)a_{n-j}=0
\qquad(n\ge0).}
\tag{7}
\]

For example, the \(n=1\) equation is

\[
6a_1-22a_0=0,
\]

which gives \(a_1=11/3\).

## 3.1 Poincare polynomial of the direct recurrence

The leading cubic coefficients of \(P_0,\ldots,P_6\) are

\[
4,-136,-4,272,-4,-136,4.
\]

Thus, for \(a_n\sim\lambda^n\), the characteristic polynomial is

\[
\begin{aligned}
\chi_{\mathrm{direct}}(\lambda)
&=\lambda^6-34\lambda^5-\lambda^4+68\lambda^3
-\lambda^2-34\lambda+1\\
&=\boxed{(\lambda^2-1)^2(\lambda^2-34\lambda+1).}
\end{aligned}
\tag{8}
\]

The extra factor has a geometric explanation:

- the double root \(\lambda=-1\) comes from the apparent ramification point \(z=-1\);
- the extra multiplicity at \(\lambda=1\) comes from the direct cyclic equation at \(z=1\).

A rational system gauge and a different cyclic vector may remove these extra factors. They are nevertheless present in the recurrence obtained by the literal operation requested in the question.

# 4. The homogeneous Brafman period is the exact spectral match

Define the central Delannoy numbers

\[
D_n=P_n(3),\qquad 1,3,13,63,\ldots,
\]

and

\[
F(z)=\sum_{n\ge0}D_n^2z^n.
\]

Brafman's identity gives

\[
\boxed{
F(z)=\frac{2}{\pi(1-z)}
K\!\left(\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z}\right).}
\tag{9}
\]

The coefficients \(d_n=D_n^2\) satisfy

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&(2n+3)(n+3)^2d_{n+3}\\
&\quad-(2n+5)(35n^2+140n+131)d_{n+2}\\
&\quad+(2n+3)(35n^2+140n+131)d_{n+1}\\
&\quad-(2n+5)(n+1)^2d_n=0.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{10}
\]

All four coefficients have degree three. Its Poincare polynomial is

\[
\boxed{
\chi_D(\lambda)
=\lambda^3-35\lambda^2+35\lambda-1
=(\lambda-1)(\lambda^2-34\lambda+1).}
\tag{11}
\]

This is exactly the normalized Problem 2.5 polynomial. Indeed, with \(c=-16\lambda\),

\[
c^3+560c^2+8960c+4096
=-4096\bigl(\lambda^3-35\lambda^2+35\lambda-1\bigr).
\]

Thus the CMF and the Brafman period have the same normalized exponential spectrum. This is a strong fingerprint, but it identifies the homogeneous quotient, not the extension class selecting Catalan's constant.

# 5. How the degree pattern fits, and why it still does not prove equality

The Problem 2.5 gamma product is

\[
H_n=(-16)^n(2)_n^2(3)_n^2
\left(\frac52\right)_n
\left(\frac72\right)_n^2,
\]

with step ratio

\[
\delta(n)=\frac{H_{n+1}}{H_n}
=-2(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(2n+5)(2n+7)^2,
\]

a polynomial of degree seven.

If one puts \(u_n=H_nd_n\) in the Delannoy-square recurrence (10) and clears the denominators by multiplying by \(H_{n+3}\), the coefficient degrees, in the order

\[
u_n,\ u_{n+1},\ u_{n+2},\ u_{n+3},
\]

are

\[
\boxed{24,\ 17,\ 10,\ 3.}
\tag{12}
\]

Multiplying the whole recurrence by a common quartic produces

\[
28,\ 21,\ 14,\ 7,
\]

which is exactly the CMF degree staircase. Therefore the degree pattern is fully compatible with

\[
\text{Delannoy square}
+\text{degree-seven Pochhammer twist}
+\text{apparent common factor}.
\]

But compatibility is not coefficient equality. In the presently extracted primitive CMF recurrence, after the \(H_n\) twist and cancellation of common factors, the coefficients still contain higher-degree apparent factors; they do not reduce directly to (10). A scalar gamma twist alone therefore does not prove the identification. One still needs a non-scalar rational Ore intertwiner or a rational matrix gauge.

There is a second categorical distinction. The transformation

\[
u_n\longmapsto \frac{u_n}{H_n}
\]

is coefficientwise. On generating functions it is a hypergeometric Hadamard/Borel transformation, not multiplication by a rational function of \(z\). It must be performed before any rational differential gauge comparison.

# 6. The precise gauge problem

A complete proof through the integrated elliptic period would consist of the following finite certificates.

## 6.1 Hypergeometric normalization

Start from the full printed CMF scalar recurrence and set

\[
v_n=\frac{u_n}{H_n}.
\]

Form its ordinary generating operator \(\mathscr D_{25}\) exactly. The Poincare polynomial alone is insufficient: all lower-order coefficient polynomials are needed.

## 6.2 Desingularization and order-three factor

Factor or desingularize \(\mathscr D_{25}\) to obtain the actual rank-three differential factor

\[
\mathcal L_{25}.
\]

The raw recurrence order is three, but the direct recurrence-to-differential conversion can have differential order much larger than three because the coefficient polynomials have high degree.

## 6.3 Work on the quadratic cover

Put \(z=x^2\). In the rescaled \(k\)-jet basis

\[
\hat X=(Y,\ Y_k/\sqrt2,\ Y_{kk})^T,
\]

the integrated-period connection is rational:

\[
\boxed{
\hat A_{\mathrm{int}}(x)=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&\dfrac{8(1+x^2)}{(1-x^2)^2}&0\\[2mm]
0&0&\dfrac{4(1+x^2)}{(1-x^2)^2}\\[2mm]
0&\dfrac{8(1+x^2)}{x^4-34x^2+1}
&-\dfrac{(1+x^2)(x^4-98x^2+1)}
{x(1-x^2)(x^4-34x^2+1)}
\end{pmatrix}.}
\tag{13}
\]

If \(A_{25}(x)\) is a companion connection for the normalized CMF factor, the required rational gauge satisfies, in column convention,

\[
\boxed{
U'(x)=\hat A_{\mathrm{int}}(x)U(x)-U(x)A_{25}(x).}
\tag{14}
\]

Equivalently, one may search for an Ore intertwiner of order at most two between the two scalar rank-three operators.

## 6.4 Match the extension normalization

Even an isomorphism of the homogeneous rank-three modules is not enough. The integrated equation has the constant freedom

\[
Y(k)\longmapsto Y(k)+C.
\]

This changes \(Y(1)\) while leaving \(Y'=K/2\) and the quotient monodromy unchanged. Therefore local monodromy at \(z=\rho\) does not by itself prove that the CMF connection constant is \(G\).

One must prove that the CMF initial rows correspond to the normalized section

\[
Y(0)=0,\qquad Y_k(0)=\frac{\pi}{4},
\]

or to an explicitly equivalent normalization. Once this initial matching is certified, (2) gives

\[
Y(k(\rho))=Y(1)=G,
\]

and the Catalan limit follows.

# 7. Answers to the five requested items

## (a) Explicit \(z\)-ODE

It is equation (1). The scalar cyclic equation has singularities at

\[
0,-1,1,\rho,\rho^{-1},\infty.
\]

The point \(-1\) is apparent; \(1\) is genuine. The true differential module, after removing the apparent point, has singular support

\[
0,1,\rho,\rho^{-1},\infty.
\]

## (b) Indicial equation at zero

\[
\boxed{r(2r-1)^2=0.}
\]

The exponents are \(0,1/2,1/2\). They are not the CMF Birkhoff indices.

## (c) Does its coefficient recurrence match the CMF recurrence?

Not directly. The literal Frobenius recurrence is the seven-term recurrence (7), with characteristic polynomial (8). It shares the silver quadratic factor with the CMF but has extra \(\pm1\) factors.

The homogeneous Brafman coefficient recurrence (10), however, has exactly the normalized CMF characteristic polynomial (11). Its degree-three coefficients, after the degree-seven \(H_n\) twist and a common quartic, reproduce the CMF degree staircase. This proves compatibility, not equality.

## (d) Would a match prove the Catalan value?

Yes, provided the certificate includes the extension normalization. An explicit gauge/intertwiner plus matching of the CMF initial rows to \(Y(0)=0\) would give

\[
L=Y(1)=G.
\]

Monodromy or singular-location matching alone cannot fix the additive constant in \(Y\).

## (e) What is the precise difference?

There are four layers:

1. a quadratic ramification \(z=x^2\), producing half-integral exponents;
2. the degree-seven coefficientwise Pochhammer twist \(H_n\);
3. apparent/cyclic-vector factors, including the direct \(z=-1\) singularity;
4. the nontrivial extension class and its initial splitting.

The required relation is therefore not a simple scalar gauge. It is a hypergeometric Hadamard twist followed by a rational rank-three differential/Ore transformation and initial-value matching.

# 8. Symbolic verification code

The following SymPy script derives and verifies (1), produces the recurrence polynomials (6), and factors (8). It is included for reproducibility; the formulas above do not depend on floating-point computation.

```python
import sympy as sp

z, x, lam = sp.symbols('z x lam')

k = 4 * sp.sqrt(2*z) / (1-z)
p = sp.diff(k, z)
pp = sp.diff(p, z)
ppp = sp.diff(pp, z)

# After multiplying the pulled-back equation by p^3 and dividing by k:
a3 = sp.simplify(1-k**2)
a2 = sp.simplify(-3*(1-k**2)*pp/p + (1-3*k**2)*p/k)
a1 = sp.simplify(
    (1-k**2)*(3*(pp/p)**2 - ppp/p)
    -(1-3*k**2)*pp/k
    -p**2
)

Delta = z**2 - 34*z + 1
B = 1 - 54*z + 103*z**2 + 48*z**3 - 2*z**4
C = 1 - 158*z + 1127*z**2 - 852*z**3 - 529*z**4 - 110*z**5 + 9*z**6

common = 4*z**2*(1-z)**4*(1+z)**2

assert sp.simplify(common*a3 - 4*z**2*(1-z**2)**2*Delta) == 0
assert sp.simplify(common*a2 - 8*z*(1-z**2)*B) == 0
assert sp.simplify(common*a1 - C) == 0

# Coefficients in the Euler-operator form.
A0 = sp.expand((1-z**2)**2 * Delta)
B0 = sp.expand((1-z**2) * B)
C0 = sp.expand(C)

a = [A0.coeff(z, j) for j in range(7)]
b = [B0.coeff(z, j) for j in range(7)]
c = [C0.coeff(z, j) for j in range(7)]

P = []
for j in range(7):
    Pj = sp.expand(x * (
        4*a[j]*(x-1)*(x-2)
        + 8*b[j]*(x-1)
        + c[j]
    ))
    P.append(Pj)
    print(j, sp.factor(Pj))

leading = [sp.Poly(Pj, x).LC() for Pj in P]
chi = sp.expand(sum(leading[j] * lam**(6-j) for j in range(7)) / 4)
print('characteristic polynomial =', sp.factor(chi))
assert sp.factor(chi) == (lam-1)**2*(lam+1)**2*(lam**2-34*lam+1)

# First Frobenius coefficients for r=1/2.
r = sp.Rational(1, 2)
a0 = sp.Integer(1)
a1 = sp.solve(
    sp.Eq(P[0].subs(x, 1+r)*sp.Symbol('a1')
          + P[1].subs(x, r)*a0, 0),
    sp.Symbol('a1')
)[0]
print('a1 =', a1)  # 11/3
```

The computation does not yet prove the CMF limit, but it sharply identifies the remaining finite task. The silver-ratio spectrum is exactly the spectrum of the Brafman homogeneous period. The Catalan value is exactly the endpoint of its normalized Abel extension. What remains is not another asymptotic argument: it is the explicit coefficientwise \(H_n\) normalization, rational rank-three intertwiner, and initial splitting that sends the printed CMF rows to the normalized solution \(Y(0)=0\).
