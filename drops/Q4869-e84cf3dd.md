# Problem 2.5: an explicit neutral mixed-cycle integral, and the exact remaining Catalan certificate

## Executive conclusion

There is an explicit, independently defined double integral with all of the **neutral-mode analytic features** required by the Problem 2.5 error:

- its phase has critical value \(1\), not \(17-12\sqrt2\);
- the dominant and recessive Legendre factors cancel to first order;
- it is positive;
- and it satisfies
  
  \[
  \mathcal N_n\sim \frac{1}{16\sqrt2}\,n^{-3}.
  \]

The integral is

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal N_n={}&
\bigl(P_n(3)-\Lambda^{-1}P_{n+1}(3)\bigr)
\bigl(\mathsf Q_n(3)-\Lambda\mathsf Q_{n+1}(3)\bigr)\\[1mm]
={}&\frac{8}{\pi\Lambda}
\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\left(
\frac{(\Lambda^{-1}+4\sqrt2\,x)y}
     {\sqrt2y^2+3y+\sqrt2}
\right)^n
\frac{(1-y)^2\sqrt{1-x}}
     {\sqrt{x}\,(\sqrt2y^2+3y+\sqrt2)^2}
\,dx\,dy,
\end{aligned}}
\tag{0.1}
\]

where

\[
\Lambda=3+2\sqrt2,
\qquad
\Lambda^{-1}=3-2\sqrt2,
\qquad
\rho=\Lambda^{-2}=17-12\sqrt2,
\]

and \(\mathsf Q_n\) is the standard Legendre function of the second kind, normalized by

\[
\mathsf Q_0(x)=\frac12\log\frac{x+1}{x-1}.
\]

Formula (0.1) is a rigorous model of the neutral thimble. It is **not yet the CMF error integral**. In particular, it carries the ordinary Legendre connection constant \(\log 2\), not the Catalan normal function. No exact calculation presently identifies

\[
\mathcal N_n
\quad\text{or a displayed rational contiguous transform of it with}\quad
\widehat e_n=G\widehat Q_n-\widehat P_n.
\]

The requested Catalan integral therefore remains equivalent to a finite certificate:

1. construct an independently defined polynomial family \(R_n(X)\);
2. prove by creative telescoping that its Catalan moments satisfy the normalized CMF recurrence;
3. verify three initial values;
4. prove the neutral \(O(n^{-3})\) bound.

The exact scalar certificate equation is given in Section 5. Once it is found, the proof is complete. The matrix spectrum and the \(n^{-3}\) formal index alone do not determine \(R_n\).

There is also an important correction to the premise:

\[
(\xi-1)(\xi^2-34\xi+1)
\]

has a **simple**, not triple, root at \(\xi=1\). Indeed,

\[
\chi'(1)=-32\ne0.

The power \(n^{-3}\) is the Birkhoff formal exponent attached to this simple root. It is not a multiplicity-three characteristic root.

# 2. Exact neutral double integral in the Delannoy--Legendre carrier

The central Delannoy numbers are

\[
D_n=P_n(3).
\]

For \(x>1\), the standard Laplace and Heine integral representations are

\[
P_n(x)=\frac1\pi\int_0^\pi
\left(x+\sqrt{x^2-1}\cos\theta\right)^n\,d\theta,
\tag{2.1}
\]

and

\[
\mathsf Q_n(x)=\int_0^\infty
\left(x+\sqrt{x^2-1}\cosh u\right)^{-n-1}\,du.
\tag{2.2}
\]

These normalizations give

\[
\mathsf Q_0(3)=\frac12\log2.
\]

For reference, these are standard Legendre integral formulas; see NIST DLMF, Section 14.12.

Put

\[
A(x)=\Lambda^{-1}+4\sqrt2\,x,
\qquad
B(y)=\sqrt2y^2+3y+\sqrt2,
\qquad
r(y)=\frac{y}{B(y)}.
\tag{2.3}
\]

The changes of variables

\[
x=\frac{1+\cos\theta}{2},
\qquad
y=e^{-u}
\]

give

\[
P_n(3)=\frac1\pi\int_0^1
\frac{A(x)^n}{\sqrt{x(1-x)}}\,dx,
\tag{2.4}
\]

and

\[
\mathsf Q_n(3)=\int_0^1\frac{r(y)^n}{B(y)}\,dy.
\tag{2.5}
\]

Now use the two exact endpoint-cancellation identities

\[
1-\frac{A(x)}{\Lambda}
=\frac{4\sqrt2}{\Lambda}(1-x),
\tag{2.6}
\]

and

\[
1-\Lambda r(y)
=\frac{\sqrt2(1-y)^2}{B(y)}.
\tag{2.7}
\]

Equations (2.4)--(2.7) immediately yield (0.1).

## 2.1 A completely rational version

The square root in (0.1) is only the arcsine parametrization. It can be removed by the tangent half-angle substitution

\[
q=\tan\frac\theta2=\frac{s}{1-s},
\qquad 0\le s\le1.
\]

Define

\[
d(s)=1-2s+2s^2,
\]

\[
a(s)=3+2\sqrt2\,\frac{1-2s}{d(s)},
\]

and retain \(B(y)\) and \(r(y)=y/B(y)\). Then

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal N_n=
\frac2\pi\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\bigl(a(s)r(y)\bigr)^n
\frac{\bigl(1-a(s)/\Lambda\bigr)
      \bigl(1-\Lambda r(y)\bigr)}
     {d(s)B(y)}\,ds\,dy.}
\tag{2.8}
\]

Every factor in (2.8) is rational in \(s,y\) over \(\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)\). Thus an explicit rational double-integral neutral carrier does exist.

# 3. Rigorous \(n^{-3}\) asymptotics

The phase in (0.1) is

\[
\Phi(x,y)=A(x)r(y).
\]

It satisfies

\[
0<\Phi(x,y)\le1
\qquad(0<x,y\le1),
\tag{3.1}
\]

with equality only at \((x,y)=(1,1)\). Indeed,

\[
A(x)\le\Lambda,
\]

and

\[
B(y)-\Lambda y=\sqrt2(1-y)^2\ge0,
\]

so \(r(y)\le\Lambda^{-1}\).

Put

\[
u=1-x,
\qquad v=1-y.
\]

Near the unique saddle,

\[
\frac{A(x)}{\Lambda}
=1-cu,
\qquad
c=\frac{4\sqrt2}{\Lambda},
\tag{3.2}
\]

and

\[
\Lambda r(y)
=1-dv^2+O(v^3),
\qquad
 d=\frac{\sqrt2}{\Lambda}.
\tag{3.3}
\]

The amplitude in (0.1) is

\[
\frac{8}{\pi\Lambda}
\frac{v^2u^{1/2}}{\Lambda^2}
\left(1+O(u+v)\right).
\tag{3.4}
\]

Therefore two elementary Laplace integrals give

\[
\int_0^\infty u^{1/2}e^{-ncu}\,du
=\frac{\sqrt\pi}{2}(nc)^{-3/2},
\]

and

\[
\int_0^\infty v^2e^{-ndv^2}\,dv
=\frac{\sqrt\pi}{4}(nd)^{-3/2}.
\]

Since

\[
cd=\frac8{\Lambda^2},
\]

we obtain

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal N_n
=\frac{1}{16\sqrt2}\,n^{-3}
\left(1+O(n^{-1/2})\right).}
\tag{3.5}
\]

Standard two-variable Laplace estimates improve the error to a full expansion in inverse powers of \(n\). For the convergence proof, the weaker bound

\[
0<\mathcal N_n\le Cn^{-3}
\tag{3.6}
\]

is immediate by splitting the square into a small neighborhood of \((1,1)\) and its complement.

This calculation explains the formal index \(-3\) geometrically:

- the \(P\)-side endpoint cancellation contributes \(n^{-3/2}\);
- the \(Q\)-side quadratic endpoint cancellation contributes \(n^{-3/2}\);
- their product contributes \(n^{-3}\).

There is no triple characteristic root and no cubic degenerate saddle.

# 4. Why (0.1) is not yet the Catalan CMF error

Formula (0.1) solves the **analytic saddle problem** but not the **connection-coefficient problem**.

The sequence \(\mathcal N_n\) belongs to a mixed Legendre \(P\)-\(Q\) construction. Its constants lie in the period algebra generated by \(\log2\). The CMF linear form is

\[
\widehat e_n=G\widehat Q_n-\widehat P_n,
\]

and belongs to a nontrivial integrated elliptic extension whose endpoint value is

\[
G=\frac12\int_0^1K(k)\,dk.
\]

The common silver-ratio spectrum does not identify these extension classes.

There are three concrete mismatches.

1. **No operator certificate.** It has not been proved that \(\mathcal N_n\) satisfies the degree-13 normalized CMF recurrence.
2. **Mode-dependent shearing.** The CMF formal powers are \(0,-3,0\). A scalar gauge of the homogeneous Delannoy symmetric square shifts all three powers equally and cannot by itself create this pattern.
3. **Wrong normal function.** The Legendre second-kind period carries \(\log2\), while the challenge extension carries \(G\). Replacing one by the other is exactly the missing global period identification.

Consequently, stating

\[
\widehat e_n=C\mathcal N_n
\]

without an Ore, WZ, differential, or initial-value certificate would be circular.

# 5. Exact Catalan moment formulation

For a polynomial \(R(X)\in\mathbb Q[X]\), define

\[
\mathcal C[R]
=\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}R(t^2)\,dt.
\tag{5.1}
\]

The moments are

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal C[X^k]
=(-1)^k\left(
G-\sum_{j=0}^{k-1}\frac{(-1)^j}{(2j+1)^2}
\right),}
\tag{5.2}
\]

where the finite sum is empty for \(k=0\). Therefore

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal C[R]=G R(-1)-\mathcal A[R],}
\tag{5.3}
\]

with \(\mathcal A[R]\in\mathbb Q\) explicitly computable coefficient by coefficient.

Thus an independently defined polynomial kernel \(R_n(X)\) proves the challenge precisely when

\[
R_n(-1)=\widehat Q_n,
\qquad
\mathcal A[R_n]=\widehat P_n,
\tag{5.4}
\]

or, equivalently,

\[
\mathcal C[R_n]=\widehat e_n.
\tag{5.5}
\]

## 5.1 The scalar creative-telescoping certificate

Set \(X=t^2\) and introduce the parameterized weight

\[
w_\varepsilon(X)=\frac{X^{\varepsilon-1/2}}{1+X}.
\]

Then

\[
\mathcal C[R]
=-\frac14\left.
\frac{\partial}{\partial\varepsilon}
\int_0^1w_\varepsilon(X)R(X)\,dX
\right|_{\varepsilon=0}.
\tag{5.6}
\]

Let the normalized CMF recurrence be

\[
\ell_0(n)u_n+\ell_1(n)u_{n+1}
+\ell_2(n)u_{n+2}+\ell_3(n)u_{n+3}=0.
\tag{5.7}
\]

A complete WZ certificate consists of rational functions or polynomials

\[
R_n(X;\varepsilon),
\qquad C_n(X;\varepsilon),
\]

satisfying

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\sum_{j=0}^3\ell_j(n)R_{n+j}(X;\varepsilon)
={}&\frac{\partial C_n}{\partial X}\\
&+\left(
\frac{\varepsilon-\frac12}{X}
-\frac1{1+X}
\right)C_n.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{5.8}
\]

Indeed, the right-hand side is exactly

\[
\frac1{w_\varepsilon(X)}
\frac{d}{dX}
\bigl(w_\varepsilon(X)C_n(X;\varepsilon)\bigr).
\]

If

\[
\left.w_\varepsilon C_n\right|_{X=0}^{X=1}=0,
\tag{5.9}
\]

then integration gives the recurrence for the parameter family, and differentiating at \(\varepsilon=0\) gives the recurrence for its Catalan derivative.

Finally, the three initial identities

\[
\mathcal C[R_j]=\widehat e_j,
\qquad j=0,1,2,
\tag{5.10}
\]

force equality for all \(n\).

Equations (5.8)--(5.10) are the exact load-bearing finite certificate. They are linear after denominator and degree supports for \(R_n,C_n\) are fixed.

# 6. The Christoffel--Darboux clue from \(\widehat Q_n/D_n^2\sim Cn\)

The observed linear growth relative to \(D_n^2\) strongly suggests a Christoffel--Darboux or parameter-derivative construction.

Define the explicit polynomial

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal K_n(X)
&=\sum_{k=0}^n(2k+1)D_kP_k(1-2X)\\
&=\frac{n+1}{2(1+X)}
\left[
D_{n+1}P_n(1-2X)
-D_nP_{n+1}(1-2X)
\right].
\end{aligned}}
\tag{6.1}
\]

The apparent pole at \(X=-1\) is removable. At that point,

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal K_n(-1)
=\sum_{k=0}^n(2k+1)D_k^2.}
\tag{6.2}
\]

Since

\[
\frac{D_{n-r}^2}{D_n^2}\longrightarrow\rho^r,
\]

we have

\[
\boxed{
\frac{\mathcal K_n(-1)}{D_n^2}
\sim\frac{2}{1-\rho}\,n
=\left(1+\frac{3\sqrt2}{4}\right)n.}
\tag{6.3}
\]

Thus (6.1) explains, without any fitting, why a natural kernel in this problem can have denominator growth \(nD_n^2\).

It also gives an explicit Catalan linear form

\[
I_n^{\rm CD}
=\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}
\mathcal K_n(t^2)\,dt.
\tag{6.4}
\]

However, (6.4) is only a **discovery ansatz**. Neither the normalized CMF recurrence nor the \(n^{-3}\) bound has been proved for \(I_n^{\rm CD}\). The observed asymptotic proportionality of denominators does not imply equality.

The first practical scalar search should therefore use the finite band

\[
R_n(X)=\sum_{r=0}^{m}c_r(n)\mathcal K_{n+r}(X)
+\sum_{r=0}^{m}d_r(n)P_{n+r}(3)P_{n+r}(1-2X),
\tag{6.5}
\]

with small \(m\), rational functions \(c_r,d_r\), and the certificate equation (5.8). This is much smaller than a full \(3\times3\) matrix AZ search.

A successful solution of (5.8) automatically decides whether the Christoffel--Darboux clue is the hidden parametric family or only an asymptotic coincidence.

# 7. The most faithful multivariate period ansatz

The normalized Delannoy phase can be written

\[
\Phi(u,v)
=\left(3+\sqrt2(u+u^{-1})\right)
 \left(3+\sqrt2(v+v^{-1})\right).
\tag{7.1}
\]

Its four critical points have values

\[
\Lambda^2,\quad 1,\quad1,\quad\Lambda^{-2}.
\]

The Catalan parameter family is

\[
h_n(u,v,t;\varepsilon)
=\Phi(u,v)^n
\frac{t^\varepsilon}{uv(1+t^2)}.
\tag{7.2}
\]

The exact logarithmic derivatives are rational:

\[
\frac{h_{n+1}}{h_n}=\Phi(u,v),
\]

\[
\frac{\partial_u h_n}{h_n}
=n\frac{\phi'(u)}{\phi(u)}-\frac1u,
\qquad
\frac{\partial_v h_n}{h_n}
=n\frac{\phi'(v)}{\phi(v)}-\frac1v,
\]

and

\[
\frac{\partial_t h_n}{h_n}
=\frac{\varepsilon+(\varepsilon-2)t^2}
       {t(1+t^2)}.
\]

The matrix or row-valued AZ identity to search is

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal P_{n+1}\Phi
-\mathcal P_nM_H(n)
=\mathcal D_uA_n+\mathcal D_vB_n+\mathcal D_tC_n,}
\tag{7.3}
\]

where each \(\mathcal D\) is the corresponding logarithmic-derivative operator. The \(u,v\) contours should be chosen as the mixed Lefschetz cycles \(\Gamma_+\times\Gamma_-\), so that the selected critical value is \(1\). The \(t=1\) boundary, or differentiation at \(\varepsilon=0\), supplies the Catalan extension.

A solution of (7.3) can often be reduced to a real double integral after one contour integral is evaluated as a Legendre polynomial. Without the numerator matrices \(\mathcal P,A,B,C\), however, (7.2) is only a carrier, not the CMF proof.

# 8. Verification code for the neutral model and the CD candidate

The following script does not use the CMF to define either candidate.

```python
from __future__ import annotations

import mpmath as mp

mp.mp.dps = 80
sqrt2 = mp.sqrt(2)
Lam = 3 + 2*sqrt2
rho = Lam**-2


def legendre_P_values(N):
    P = [mp.mpf(1)]
    if N == 0:
        return P
    P.append(mp.mpf(3))
    for n in range(1, N):
        P.append((3*(2*n+1)*P[n] - n*P[n-1])/(n+1))
    return P


def legendre_Q_values(N):
    Q = [mp.log(2)/2]
    if N == 0:
        return Q
    Q.append(3*Q[0] - 1)
    for n in range(1, N):
        Q.append((3*(2*n+1)*Q[n] - n*Q[n-1])/(n+1))
    return Q


def neutral_closed(n):
    P = legendre_P_values(n+1)
    Q = legendre_Q_values(n+1)
    return (P[n] - P[n+1]/Lam)*(Q[n] - Lam*Q[n+1])


def neutral_integral(n):
    def A(x):
        return 1/Lam + 4*sqrt2*x

    def B(y):
        return sqrt2*y*y + 3*y + sqrt2

    def outer(x):
        def inner(y):
            phase = A(x)*y/B(y)
            amp = ((1-y)**2 * mp.sqrt(1-x)
                   / (mp.sqrt(x)*B(y)**2))
            return phase**n * amp
        return mp.quad(inner, [0, 1])

    return 8/(mp.pi*Lam) * mp.quad(outer, [0, 1])


for n in [0, 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 40]:
    a = neutral_closed(n)
    b = neutral_integral(n)
    print(n)
    print(' closed   =', mp.nstr(a, 40))
    print(' integral =', mp.nstr(b, 40))
    print(' residual =', mp.nstr(a-b, 10))
    print(' n^3 value=', mp.nstr((n**3)*a if n else a, 30))

print('predicted limit =', mp.nstr(1/(16*sqrt2), 40))
```

from fractions import Fraction


def catalan_moment_pair(k):
    # Integral t^(2k)*(-log t)/(1+t^2) dt = q*G - p.
    q = Fraction((-1)**k, 1)
    partial = sum(
        Fraction((-1)**j, (2*j+1)**2)
        for j in range(k)
    )
    p = Fraction((-1)**k, 1) * partial
    return q, p


def polynomial_moment_pair(coeffs):
    # coeffs[k] is the coefficient of X^k.
    q = Fraction(0)
    p = Fraction(0)
    for k, c in enumerate(coeffs):
        qk, pk = catalan_moment_pair(k)
        q += Fraction(c) * qk
        p += Fraction(c) * pk
    return q, p

# 9. Complete proof criterion

A complete integral proof of Problem 2.5 can be reduced to the following finite theorem.

> **Target theorem.** There are explicitly printed \(R_n(X;\varepsilon)\) and \(C_n(X;\varepsilon)\), rational in all variables, such that:
> 1. the telescoping identity (5.8) holds identically;
> 2. the boundary condition (5.9) holds;
> 3. the differentiated moments at \(n=0,1,2\) equal the normalized CMF errors;
> 4. the resulting integral is a mixed-cycle integral whose phase is at most \(1\), with endpoint amplitude of total Laplace order \(n^{-3}\).

Items 1--3 prove

\[
I_n=G\widehat Q_n-\widehat P_n
\]

for every \(n\). Item 4 proves

\[
|I_n|\le Cn^{-3}.
\]

Together with

\[
|\widehat Q_n|\ge c\Lambda^{2n}
\]

for large \(n\), this gives

\[
\boxed{
\left|\frac{P_n}{Q_n}-G\right|
\le\frac Cc\,n^{-3}\rho^n.}
\]

The explicit neutral integral (0.1) proves that the required saddle mechanism is viable and determines the cancellation orders. The Christoffel--Darboux polynomial (6.1) explains the observed \(nD_n^2\) denominator scale. What remains genuinely load-bearing is the rational telescoping numerator that joins those two structures to the Catalan moment functional and to the printed CMF initial data.
