# Problem 2.7 audit: the displayed recurrence is the Delannoy symmetric square, not the mixed-zeta CMF

## Executive conclusion

The requested construction cannot be carried out as stated, because three different mathematical problems have been conflated.

1. The recurrence displayed in the question is exactly the symmetric square of the central-Delannoy recurrence. It is the order-three model discussed in connection with **Problem 2.5 (Catalan's constant)**, not the actual scalar recurrence of **Problem 2.7**.
2. The repository statement of Problem 2.7 asks only to prove
   \[
   \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{p_n}{q_n}=\zeta(2)+\zeta(3),
   \]
   not to prove irrationality. See `problems/2.7/notes.md` in `xiangyazi24/Ramanujan_Challenge`.
3. The irrationality of
   \[
   \zeta(2)+\zeta(3)=\frac{\pi^2}{6}+\zeta(3)
   \]
   is not presently known. Apéry's theorem proves the irrationality of \(\zeta(3)\), but it does not rule out a relation
   \[
   \zeta(3)=r-s\pi^2,
   \qquad r,s\in\mathbf Q.
   \]

There is nevertheless a complete answer to the recurrence part of the question. Every solution of the displayed order-three recurrence is a quadratic product of two solutions of the Delannoy recurrence. An explicit two-parameter double-integral family is

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal I_n(s,t)
=\bigl(P_n(3)+sQ_n(3)\bigr)
 \bigl(P_n(3)+tQ_n(3)\bigr),
}
\tag{E1}
\]

where \(P_n\) and \(Q_n\) are the Legendre functions of the first and second kinds. In integral form,

\[
P_n(3)=\frac1\pi\int_0^\pi
       \bigl(3+2\sqrt2\cos\theta\bigr)^n\,d\theta,
\tag{E2}
\]

\[
Q_n(3)=\int_0^\infty
       \bigl(3+2\sqrt2\cosh u\bigr)^{-n-1}\,du.
\tag{E3}
\]

Thus \(\mathcal I_n(s,t)\) is an explicit sum of three double integrals, printed in Section 3 below. It satisfies the displayed recurrence identically for every \(s,t\).

However, this homogeneous symmetric-square module has connection field \(\mathbf Q(\log 2)\). More precisely, every solution with rational initial values is a rational linear combination of

\[
D_n^2,\qquad D_nE_n,\qquad E_n^2,
\]

where \(D_n=P_n(3)\) and \(E_0=0,E_1=1\). Any finite quotient limit of two such rational solutions belongs to \(\mathbf Q(\log 2)\). The mixed period \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\) does not arise from this recurrence by the proposed Beukers mechanism unless one first proves the unexpected identity

\[
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\in\mathbf Q(\log2).
\]

No such identity is known.

The correct conceptual mechanism for the mixed period is a **parameter-derivative extension**, not a homogeneous symmetric square. The elementary family

\[
\Phi(s)=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{(xy)^s}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy
=\zeta(2,s+1)
\]

satisfies

\[
\boxed{
\Phi(0)-\frac12\Phi'(0)
=\zeta(2)+\zeta(3).
}
\tag{E4}
\]

Differentiation in the parameter normally turns a homogeneous recurrence into an inhomogeneous equation, or equivalently an extension of difference modules. That is why the mixed weight-two/weight-three period cannot be manufactured by the homogeneous Delannoy symmetric square alone.

# 1. Audit of the problem statement

## 1.1 What Problem 2.7 actually says

The repository file `problems/2.7/notes.md` states:

> Two solutions \(p_n,q_n\) with large integer initial conditions. Prove: \(\lim p_n/q_n=\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\).

The actual Problem 2.7 scalar operator is order three with degree-18 coefficients and scaled Poincaré polynomial

\[
F(\mu)=4\mu^3-220\mu^2+8\mu-1,
\tag{1.1}
\]

which is irreducible over \(\mathbf Q\). This is recorded in `problems/2.7/proof.tex`. In particular, its Poincaré roots are not

\[
1,\quad17+12\sqrt2,\quad17-12\sqrt2.
\]

The latter are the symmetric-square Delannoy roots and occur in the spectral analysis of Problem 2.5.

## 1.2 Why the irrationality claim is a separate open problem

The equality

\[
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\in\mathbf Q
\]

would be equivalent to a rational linear relation among

\[
1,\quad\pi^2,\quad\zeta(3).
\]

Apéry's theorem establishes only that \(1\) and \(\zeta(3)\) are linearly independent over \(\mathbf Q\). It does not establish the three-dimensional independence above. Simultaneous constructions for \(\zeta(2)\) and \(\zeta(3)\), such as those of Dauguet and Zudilin, prove the irrationality of each value separately and give lower bounds for certain linear forms; they do not prove the irrationality of every prescribed rational combination.

Therefore an integral family satisfying all of the requested arithmetic properties would constitute a new irrationality proof, not a routine completion of the stated challenge.

# 2. Exact derivation of the displayed recurrence

Let \(y_n\) satisfy the central-Delannoy recurrence

\[
\boxed{
(n+1)y_{n+1}=3(2n+1)y_n-ny_{n-1}.
}
\tag{2.1}
\]

The distinguished polynomial solution is

\[
D_n=P_n(3)
={}_2F_1\!\left(
\begin{matrix}-n,n+1\\1\end{matrix};-1
\right)
=\sum_{k=0}^n\binom nk\binom{n+k}{k},
\tag{2.2}
\]

with

\[
D_0,D_1,D_2,D_3,D_4=1,3,13,63,321.
\]

Shift (2.1) once and write it as

\[
y_{n+2}=a_ny_{n+1}+b_ny_n,
\qquad
 a_n=\frac{3(2n+3)}{n+2},
\quad
 b_n=-\frac{n+1}{n+2}.
\tag{2.3}
\]

If \(y_n,z_n\) are two solutions and \(U_n=y_nz_n\), elimination of the mixed term

\[
y_{n+1}z_n+y_nz_{n+1}
\]

gives the general discrete symmetric-square formula

\[
\begin{aligned}
U_{n+3}={}&
\left(a_{n+1}^2+\frac{a_{n+1}b_{n+1}}{a_n}\right)U_{n+2}\\
&+\left(a_na_{n+1}b_{n+1}+b_{n+1}^2\right)U_{n+1}\\
&-\frac{a_{n+1}b_{n+1}b_n^2}{a_n}U_n.
\end{aligned}
\tag{2.4}
\]

Substitution of (2.3), followed by clearing denominators, yields

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
0={}&(2n+3)(n+3)^2U_{n+3}\\
&-(2n+5)(35n^2+140n+131)U_{n+2}\\
&+(2n+3)(35n^2+140n+131)U_{n+1}\\
&-(2n+5)(n+1)^2U_n.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{2.5}
\]

This is exactly the recurrence in the question.

Its Poincaré polynomial is

\[
\lambda^3-35\lambda^2+35\lambda-1
=(\lambda-1)(\lambda^2-34\lambda+1),
\tag{2.6}
\]

with roots

\[
\lambda_+=(3+2\sqrt2)^2=17+12\sqrt2,
\quad
\lambda_0=1,
\quad
\lambda_-=(3-2\sqrt2)^2=17-12\sqrt2.
\]

# 3. An explicit double-integral family satisfying the recurrence

Put

\[
A(\theta)=3+2\sqrt2\cos\theta,
\qquad
B(u)=3+2\sqrt2\cosh u.
\]

The standard integral representations are

\[
P_n(3)=\frac1\pi\int_0^\pi A(\theta)^n\,d\theta,
\tag{3.1}
\]

\[
Q_n(3)=\int_0^\infty B(u)^{-n-1}\,du.
\tag{3.2}
\]

Both sequences satisfy (2.1). Hence, for arbitrary constants \(s,t\),

\[
Y_n^{(s)}=P_n(3)+sQ_n(3),
\qquad
Y_n^{(t)}=P_n(3)+tQ_n(3)
\]

are two Delannoy solutions, and their product satisfies (2.5).

Expanding (E1) gives the promised explicit double-integral family:

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal I_n(s,t)={}&
\frac1{\pi^2}\int_0^\pi\!\int_0^\pi
 A(\theta)^nA(\phi)^n\,d\theta\,d\phi\\
&+\frac{s+t}{\pi}\int_0^\pi\!\int_0^\infty
 A(\theta)^nB(u)^{-n-1}\,du\,d\theta\\
&+st\int_0^\infty\!\int_0^\infty
 B(u)^{-n-1}B(v)^{-n-1}\,du\,dv.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{3.3}
\]

For every \(s,t\),

\[
\boxed{L_{\mathrm{Sym}^2}\mathcal I(s,t)=0.}
\tag{3.4}
\]

## 3.1 Unit-square form

Let

\[
\Lambda=3+2\sqrt2,
\qquad \Lambda^{-1}=3-2\sqrt2.
\]

The substitutions

\[
x=\frac{1+\cos\theta}{2},
\qquad
r=\tanh\frac u2
\]

give

\[
P_n(3)=\frac1\pi\int_0^1
\frac{\bigl(3+2\sqrt2(2x-1)\bigr)^n}
{\sqrt{x(1-x)}}\,dx,
\tag{3.5}
\]

\[
Q_n(3)=2\int_0^1
\frac{(1-r^2)^n}
{\bigl(\Lambda-\Lambda^{-1}r^2\bigr)^{n+1}}\,dr.
\tag{3.6}
\]

Substituting (3.5) and (3.6) into (E1) expresses the entire family as a sum of three integrals over \([0,1]^2\).

# 4. The complete rational solution space and its connection constants

Set

\[
L:=\frac{\log2}{2}.
\tag{4.1}
\]

For integer \(n\), the Legendre function of the second kind has the exact form

\[
\boxed{
Q_n(3)=L D_n-E_n,
}
\tag{4.2}
\]

where \(E_n\) is the rational solution of (2.1) initialized by

\[
E_0=0,\qquad E_1=1.
\tag{4.3}
\]

Indeed,

\[
Q_0(3)=\frac12\log\frac{3+1}{3-1}=L,
\]

\[
Q_1(3)=3L-1,
\]

and recurrence uniqueness proves (4.2) for all \(n\).

The three sequences

\[
D_n^2,\qquad D_nE_n,\qquad E_n^2
\tag{4.4}
\]

are rational and form a basis of the solution space of (2.5). At \(n=0,1,2\), their value matrix is

\[
\begin{pmatrix}
1&0&0\\
9&3&1\\
169&117/2&81/4
\end{pmatrix},
\]

whose determinant is \(9/4\neq0\).

Hence a rational solution \(U_n\) with initial values \(U_0,U_1,U_2\in\mathbf Q\) has the unique expression

\[
U_n=\alpha D_n^2+\beta D_nE_n+\gamma E_n^2,
\tag{4.5}
\]

where

\[
\boxed{
\alpha=U_0,
}
\tag{4.6}
\]

\[
\boxed{
\beta=\frac{81U_1-4U_2-53U_0}{9},
}
\tag{4.7}
\]

\[
\boxed{
\gamma=\frac{26U_0-78U_1+4U_2}{3}.
}
\tag{4.8}
\]

Using (4.2), (4.5) becomes

\[
\boxed{
U_n=
(\alpha+\beta L+\gamma L^2)D_n^2
-(\beta+2\gamma L)D_nQ_n(3)
+\gamma Q_n(3)^2.
}
\tag{4.9}
\]

This formula identifies all three connection coefficients exactly.

## 4.1 Asymptotic modes

Write

\[
3=\cosh\eta,
\qquad
e^\eta=3+2\sqrt2=\Lambda,
\qquad
\sinh\eta=2\sqrt2.
\]

The standard large-degree asymptotics are

\[
P_n(3)\sim
\frac{e^{(n+1/2)\eta}}
{\sqrt{2\pi n\sinh\eta}},
\tag{4.10}
\]

\[
Q_n(3)\sim
\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2n\sinh\eta}}
 e^{-(n+1/2)\eta}.
\tag{4.11}
\]

Therefore

\[
P_n(3)^2\asymp \Lambda^{2n}n^{-1},
\]

\[
P_n(3)Q_n(3)\sim\frac1{2n\sinh\eta}
=\frac1{4\sqrt2\,n},
\tag{4.12}
\]

\[
Q_n(3)^2\asymp \Lambda^{-2n}n^{-1}.
\]

These are precisely the dominant, neutral, and recessive modes of (2.5).

## 4.2 Quotient limits of rational solutions

Let \(p_n,q_n\) be rational solutions of (2.5), and denote their coefficients in (4.5) by

\[
(\alpha_p,\beta_p,\gamma_p),
\qquad
(\alpha_q,\beta_q,\gamma_q).
\]

If the dominant coefficient of \(q_n\) is nonzero, then

\[
\boxed{
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{p_n}{q_n}
=
\frac{\alpha_p+\beta_pL+\gamma_pL^2}
     {\alpha_q+\beta_qL+\gamma_qL^2}.
}
\tag{4.13}
\]

Thus every generic quotient limit produced by rational initial data lies in

\[
\boxed{\mathbf Q(\log2).}
\tag{4.14}
\]

If the dominant coefficient cancels, the neutral coefficient in (4.9) gives the limit; it is again in \(\mathbf Q(\log2)\). If both dominant and neutral coefficients cancel, only the recessive mode remains and the quotient is rational. Consequently every finite quotient limit of two rational solutions of the displayed recurrence lies in \(\mathbf Q(\log2)\).

This is the decisive structural obstruction to the proposed mixed-zeta interpretation.

# 5. Where the mixed period really comes from

Define, for \(\Re s>-1\),

\[
\Phi(s)
:=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{(xy)^s}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
\tag{5.1}
\]

Expanding the geometric series gives

\[
\Phi(s)=\sum_{m=0}^\infty\frac1{(m+s+1)^2}
=\zeta(2,s+1).
\tag{5.2}
\]

Hence

\[
\Phi(0)=\zeta(2),
\qquad
\Phi'(0)=-2\zeta(3),
\]

and therefore

\[
\boxed{
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{1-\frac12\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
}
\tag{5.3}
\]

This identity is exact, but it is not tied to (2.5).

## 5.1 Why a parameter derivative changes the module

Suppose a parameter-dependent sequence \(J_n(s)\) satisfies

\[
L(s)J(s)=0.
\]

Differentiating at \(s=0\) gives

\[
\boxed{
L(0)J'(0)=-L'(0)J(0).
}
\tag{5.4}
\]

Unless \(L'(0)=0\), the derivative is not another solution of the same homogeneous operator. It belongs to an extension, represented in first-order form by a block-triangular system. This is the standard mechanism by which a logarithm and a higher-weight period enter.

A natural Beukers-style family is

\[
\mathcal B_n(s)
=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{\widetilde P_n(x)\widetilde P_n(y)(xy)^s}
     {1-xy}\,dx\,dy,
\tag{5.5}
\]

where

\[
\widetilde P_n(x)=P_n(2x-1)
=\frac1{n!}\frac{d^n}{dx^n}
  \bigl[x^n(x-1)^n\bigr].
\]

Then

\[
\mathcal B_n(0)-\frac12\mathcal B_n'(0)
\]

is a genuine mixed \(\zeta(2),\zeta(3)\) linear form. But its recurrence is not (2.5); the parameter derivative produces precisely the extension phenomenon described in (5.4).

This is also why the actual Problem 2.7 operator has a different, irreducible Poincaré cubic rather than the rational neutral root forced by a symmetric square.

# 6. Why the requested irrationality conclusion does not follow

An Apéry/Beukers irrationality proof needs all of the following:

1. integers, or rational numbers with explicitly controlled common denominators, \(p_n,q_n\);
2. nonzero linear forms
   \[
   0<|q_n\alpha-p_n|;
   \]
3. an upper bound decaying faster than the possible denominator growth under the assumption \(\alpha\in\mathbf Q\);
4. a sign, positivity, or zero-exclusion argument ensuring the linear form is not eventually zero.

The existence of a homogeneous recurrence and exponential convergence alone is not enough. In the present case the stronger obstruction occurs earlier: the displayed recurrence is the wrong recurrence and its rational connection field is \(\mathbf Q(\log2)\), not a known mixed-zeta period field.

Therefore no honest proof can provide items (1)–(4) requested in the question for this recurrence. The correct task is to work with the full degree-18 Problem 2.7 operator and construct an integral or modular extension that is certified to satisfy that operator.

# 7. Exact Python verification for \(n=0,\ldots,10\)

The following script uses exact rational arithmetic for the recurrence and high-precision quadrature for the integral formulas. It requires only the Python standard library and `mpmath`.

```python
from fractions import Fraction
from mpmath import mp


def delannoy_basis(max_n: int):
    """Return D_n and E_n through index max_n+3 exactly."""
    D = [Fraction(1), Fraction(3)]
    E = [Fraction(0), Fraction(1)]

    # At step n, append the value with index n+1.
    for n in range(1, max_n + 3):
        D.append(
            (3 * (2 * n + 1) * D[n] - n * D[n - 1])
            / Fraction(n + 1)
        )
        E.append(
            (3 * (2 * n + 1) * E[n] - n * E[n - 1])
            / Fraction(n + 1)
        )
    return D, E


def sym2_residual(U, n: int):
    h = 35 * n * n + 140 * n + 131
    return (
        (2 * n + 3) * (n + 3) ** 2 * U[n + 3]
        - (2 * n + 5) * h * U[n + 2]
        + (2 * n + 3) * h * U[n + 1]
        - (2 * n + 5) * (n + 1) ** 2 * U[n]
    )


def exact_check():
    D, E = delannoy_basis(10)
    basis = {
        "D^2": [D[n] ** 2 for n in range(len(D))],
        "D*E": [D[n] * E[n] for n in range(len(D))],
        "E^2": [E[n] ** 2 for n in range(len(D))],
    }

    for name, U in basis.items():
        for n in range(11):
            r = sym2_residual(U, n)
            assert r == 0, (name, n, r)
        print(f"{name}: exact recurrence verified for n=0,...,10")

    print("D[0:7] =", D[:7])
    print("E[0:7] =", E[:7])


# High-precision integral checks.
mp.dps = 80
sqrt2 = mp.sqrt(2)
Lambda = 3 + 2 * sqrt2
Lambda_inv = 1 / Lambda


def P_integral(n: int):
    return mp.quad(
        lambda th: (3 + 2 * sqrt2 * mp.cos(th)) ** n,
        [0, mp.pi],
    ) / mp.pi


def Q_integral(n: int):
    # u=tanh(t/2) transforms the infinite interval to [0,1].
    return 2 * mp.quad(
        lambda u: (1 - u * u) ** n
        / (Lambda - Lambda_inv * u * u) ** (n + 1),
        [0, 1],
    )


def integral_family(n: int, s, t):
    P = P_integral(n)
    Q = Q_integral(n)
    return (P + s * Q) * (P + t * Q)


def numerical_integral_check():
    s = mp.mpf("0.3")
    t = mp.mpf("-0.7")
    U = [integral_family(n, s, t) for n in range(14)]

    max_residual = mp.mpf("0")
    for n in range(11):
        h = 35 * n * n + 140 * n + 131
        r = (
            (2 * n + 3) * (n + 3) ** 2 * U[n + 3]
            - (2 * n + 5) * h * U[n + 2]
            + (2 * n + 3) * h * U[n + 1]
            - (2 * n + 5) * (n + 1) ** 2 * U[n]
        )
        max_residual = max(max_residual, abs(r))

    print("max integral-family residual =", mp.nstr(max_residual, 12))
    print("Q_0(3) - log(2)/2 =",
          mp.nstr(Q_integral(0) - mp.log(2) / 2, 12))


# Verification of the mixed period by parameter differentiation.
def mixed_period_check():
    Phi = lambda s: mp.zeta(2, 1 + s)  # Hurwitz zeta
    value = Phi(0) - mp.diff(Phi, 0) / 2
    target = mp.zeta(2) + mp.zeta(3)
    print("mixed period =", mp.nstr(value, 60))
    print("difference    =", mp.nstr(value - target, 12))


if __name__ == "__main__":
    exact_check()
    numerical_integral_check()
    mixed_period_check()
```

Expected exact output begins with

```text
D^2: exact recurrence verified for n=0,...,10
D*E: exact recurrence verified for n=0,...,10
E^2: exact recurrence verified for n=0,...,10
```

The high-precision residual is limited only by numerical quadrature precision.

# 8. Correct proof architecture for the actual Problem 2.7

A valid proof of the actual challenge would require the following finite certificates.

1. **Use the actual operator.** Start from the degree-18 order-three Problem 2.7 recurrence, not (2.5).
2. **Construct a mixed-period kernel.** A plausible form is a parameter family
   \[
   \mathcal J_n(s)=
   \int_0^1\!\int_0^1
   R_n(x,y;s)\frac{(xy)^s}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy,
   \]
   with
   \[
   \mathcal J_n(0)-\frac12\partial_s\mathcal J_n(0)
   =q_n(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))-p_n.
   \]
3. **Creative telescoping.** Print rational certificates proving that the value and parameter derivative satisfy the full recurrence or its block-triangular extension.
4. **Initial matching.** Verify enough initial values to identify the CMF numerator and denominator solutions.
5. **Arithmetic.** Prove denominator bounds for \(p_n,q_n\).
6. **Decay and nonvanishing.** Produce a positive or controlled-sign integral for the error.

Steps 2–6 are not supplied by the symmetric-square Delannoy recurrence. They are the substantive unresolved part of the mixed-period problem.
