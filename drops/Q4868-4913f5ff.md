# Problem 2.5: exact right GCD of the corrected CMF Euler operator and the Delannoy operator

## Executive result

Let

\[
L_{\rm rec}
=z^3c_0(\theta)+z^2c_1(\theta-1)
 +zc_2(\theta-2)+c_3(\theta-3),
\qquad \theta=zD_z,
\]

with the four corrected degree-13 polynomials in the question, and let

\[
\begin{aligned}
L_D={}&(2\theta-3)\theta^2
-z(2\theta+1)(35\theta^2-9)\\
&+z^2(2\theta+1)(35\theta^2+70\theta+26)
-z^3(2\theta+5)(\theta+1)^2.
\end{aligned}
\]

Then, in the differential Ore algebra \(\mathbf Q(z)\langle D_z\rangle\),

\[
\boxed{\operatorname{gcrd}_{\rm right}(L_{\rm rec},L_D)=1.}
\tag{E1}
\]

Thus the greatest common right divisor has order zero. There is **no nonzero common solution** of the two differential operators in a common Picard--Vessiot extension. In particular, the common-solution route from the corrected CMF recurrence to the Delannoy/Brafman system is definitively ruled out.

Two terminology corrections are important:

1. The recurrence has shift order three, but \(L_{\rm rec}\) has **differential order 13**, because the \(c_i\) have degree 13.
2. The polynomial dependence of \(L_{\rm rec}\) on \(z\) has width three; that does not make it a differential operator of order three.

The shared Poincaré polynomial appears exactly in the leading differential coefficients,

\[
\operatorname{lc}_{D_z}(L_{\rm rec})
=98304z^{13}(1-z)(1-34z+z^2),
\tag{E2}
\]

\[
\operatorname{lc}_{D_z}(L_D)
=2z^3(1-z)(1-34z+z^2),
\tag{E3}
\]

but this common singular divisor does not produce a common right factor.

# 1. Poincaré and leading-coefficient audit

The leading coefficients of the four corrected recurrence polynomials are

\[
\operatorname{lc}(c_0)=-98304,
\quad
\operatorname{lc}(c_1)=3440640,
\quad
\operatorname{lc}(c_2)=-3440640,
\quad
\operatorname{lc}(c_3)=98304.
\]

Consequently,

\[
\frac{
\operatorname{lc}(c_3)\xi^3
+\operatorname{lc}(c_2)\xi^2
+\operatorname{lc}(c_1)\xi
+\operatorname{lc}(c_0)
}{98304}
=\xi^3-35\xi^2+35\xi-1,
\]

and

\[
\boxed{
\xi^3-35\xi^2+35\xi-1
=(\xi-1)(\xi^2-34\xi+1).}
\tag{1.1}
\]

For a polynomial \(p(\theta)\) of degree 13, the coefficient of \(D_z^{13}\) in \(p(\theta-j)\) is \(\operatorname{lc}(p)z^{13}\). Therefore

\[
\begin{aligned}
\operatorname{lc}_{D_z}(L_{\rm rec})
&=z^{13}\bigl[-98304z^3+3440640z^2
 -3440640z+98304\bigr]\\
&=98304z^{13}(1-35z+35z^2-z^3)\\
&=98304z^{13}(1-z)(1-34z+z^2).
\end{aligned}
\tag{1.2}
\]

Likewise, using

\[
\theta^3=z^3D_z^3+3z^2D_z^2+zD_z,
\]

the coefficient of \(D_z^3\) in \(L_D\) is

\[
2z^3-70z^4+70z^5-2z^6
=2z^3(1-z)(1-34z+z^2).
\tag{1.3}
\]

Equations (1.2)--(1.3) explain the identical Poincaré spectrum. They are only leading-symbol data.

# 2. The Delannoy operator and its proper right factor

Let

\[
D_n=P_n(3)=1,3,13,63,\ldots,
\qquad
F_D(z)=\sum_{n\ge0}D_n^2z^n.
\]

Brafman's identity gives

\[
\boxed{
F_D(z)=\frac{1}{1-z}
{}_2F_1\!\left(
\begin{matrix}\frac12,\frac12\\1\end{matrix};
\frac{32z}{(1-z)^2}
\right).}
\tag{2.1}
\]

Pulling back the Gauss equation

\[
m(1-m)f''+(1-2m)f'-\frac14f=0
\]

through

\[
m=\frac{32z}{(1-z)^2}
\]

and applying the gauge \(F_D=f(m)/(1-z)\) gives the following order-two operator:

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
B_D={}&z(1-z)^2(1+z)(1-34z+z^2)D_z^2\\
&+(1-z)(1-70z+70z^2+66z^3-3z^4)D_z\\
&+(z^4-8z^3-28z^2+44z-9).
\end{aligned}}
\tag{2.2}
\]

It satisfies

\[
B_DF_D=0.
\tag{2.3}
\]

The Gauss equation with parameters \((1/2,1/2;1)\) has Zariski-dense \(\mathrm{SL}_2\) monodromy. A nonconstant rational pullback retains a Zariski-dense subgroup, and a scalar gauge does not alter irreducibility. Hence

\[
\boxed{B_D\text{ is irreducible over }\mathbf C(z),
\text{ and therefore over }\mathbf Q(z).}
\tag{2.4}
\]

Since \(L_DF_D=0\) as well, right division of \(L_D\) by the minimal operator \(B_D\) has zero remainder. Thus

\[
\boxed{B_D\text{ is an order-two right factor of }L_D.}
\tag{2.5}
\]

The order-three recurrence operator is therefore not minimal for the distinguished Brafman series \(F_D\).

# 3. The Delannoy operator has no order-one right factor

Expanding \(L_D\) in powers of \(D_z\) gives

\[
\begin{aligned}
p_3(z)&=2z^3(1-35z+35z^2-z^3),\\
p_2(z)&=z^2(3-245z+385z^2-15z^3),\\
p_1(z)&=-z-87z^2+367z^3-23z^4,\\
p_0(z)&=9z+26z^2-5z^3.
\end{aligned}
\tag{3.1}
\]

At \(z=0\), the indicial polynomial is

\[
(2r-3)r^2,
\]

so the local exponents are

\[
0,\quad0,\quad\frac32.
\tag{3.2}
\]

Put

\[
R(z)=1-35z+35z^2-z^3=(1-z)(1-34z+z^2).
\]

At every nonzero root \(a\) of \(R\), the leading coefficient has a simple zero. Moreover,

\[
3-245z+385z^2-15z^3
=4zR'(z)+3R(z).
\tag{3.3}
\]

Hence

\[
p_2(a)=2p_3'(a).
\]

The indicial polynomial at each of

\[
a=1,\qquad a=17-12\sqrt2,\qquad a=17+12\sqrt2
\]

is therefore

\[
r(r-1)\bigl[p_3'(a)(r-2)+p_2(a)\bigr]
=p_3'(a)r^2(r-1),
\]

and the exponents are

\[
0,\quad0,\quad1.
\tag{3.4}
\]

At infinity the highest-\(z\) Euler term is

\[
-z^3(2\theta+5)(\theta+1)^2.
\]

Thus the three solutions have power behaviors

\[
z^{-1},\quad z^{-1},\quad z^{-5/2}.
\tag{3.5}
\]

Suppose that \(L_D\) had a first-order right factor over \(\mathbf Q(z)\). It would possess a hyperexponential solution \(y\) with

\[
\frac{y'}y\in\mathbf Q(z).
\]

Because \(L_D\) is Fuchsian, this logarithmic derivative has no nonconstant polynomial part. At ordinary finite points a solution cannot have a pole, and at the finite singularities all possible residues are the nonnegative exponents listed in (3.2) and (3.4). Thus the sum of its finite residues is nonnegative, so \(y\) must behave at infinity like \(z^s\) with \(s\ge0\). This contradicts (3.5).

Therefore

\[
\boxed{L_D\text{ has no order-one right factor over }\mathbf Q(z).}
\tag{3.6}
\]

Together with (2.4)--(2.5), this also makes \(B_D\) the unique proper nontrivial right factor of \(L_D\), up to multiplication by a unit. Indeed, two distinct order-two right factors in an order-three solution space would intersect in a nonzero one-dimensional invariant subspace, producing an order-one right factor, contrary to (3.6).

Thus the only possible orders of a right divisor of \(L_D\) are

\[
0,\quad2,\quad3,
\]

and its only proper positive-order right divisor is \(B_D\).

# 4. Exact rejection of the Brafman right factor

If \(B_D\) right-divided \(L_{\rm rec}\), then every solution of \(B_D\), in particular \(F_D(z)\), would be annihilated by \(L_{\rm rec}\).

The coefficient of \(z^{n+3}\) in \(L_{\rm rec}F_D\) is

\[
c_0(n)D_n^2+c_1(n)D_{n+1}^2
+c_2(n)D_{n+2}^2+c_3(n)D_{n+3}^2.
\]

Already at \(n=0\), using

\[
D_0^2=1,\quad D_1^2=9,\quad D_2^2=169,\quad D_3^2=3969,
\]

one obtains the exact nonzero residual

\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal R_0
={}&c_0(0)+9c_1(0)+169c_2(0)+3969c_3(0)\\
={}&\boxed{-1160938142806248}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{4.1}
\]

Consequently,

\[
L_{\rm rec}F_D\ne0,
\]

and hence

\[
\boxed{B_D\nmid_{\rm right} L_{\rm rec}.}
\tag{4.2}
\]

Since \(B_D\) is the only proper positive-order right factor of \(L_D\), equations (3.6) and (4.2) imply (E1):

\[
\boxed{\operatorname{gcrd}_{\rm right}(L_{\rm rec},L_D)=1.}
\]

This argument is exact and does not depend on floating-point series matching.

# 5. Reproducible Sage `ore_algebra` computation

The following script reconstructs the operators exactly and calls `gcrd`, which in `ore_algebra` means greatest common **right** divisor. The package automatically localizes \(\mathbf Q[z]\) to \(\mathbf Q(z)\) for this operation.

```sage
from sage.all import *
from ore_algebra import OreAlgebra

Rn.<n> = PolynomialRing(QQ)

c_coeffs = [
    [-170972650800, -826494925500, -1792449886332,
     -2317972607944, -2000297648936, -1219354055500,
     -541255279788, -177419351856, -43002662976,
     -7620091136, -960400960, -81589760, -4190208,
     -98304],

    [8781630505200, 38850314624124, 78557994908508,
     96136040496551, 79442239242197, 46814452218572,
     20241514501104, 6502490145168, 1552168938336,
     271943188864, 33995217088, 2871763456,
     146952192, 3440640],

    [-21132458248680, -87529225645944,
     -165451256319618, -189073879129764,
     -145809619841418, -80164318460172,
     -32338316008004, -9694892892592,
     -2160716677664, -353683596544, -41340724928,
     -3268370944, -156684288, -3440640],

    [587448626688, 2442715444224, 4635428285664,
     5317694979920, 4116150568664, 2270943978716,
     919036676572, 276298241680, 61721801728,
     10120470656, 1184128064, 93632000, 4485120,
     98304],
]

c0, c1, c2, c3 = [Rn(v) for v in c_coeffs]

# Poincare check.
Rx.<xi> = PolynomialRing(QQ)
chi = (c3.leading_coefficient()*xi^3
       + c2.leading_coefficient()*xi^2
       + c1.leading_coefficient()*xi
       + c0.leading_coefficient()) / c3.leading_coefficient()
assert chi == xi^3 - 35*xi^2 + 35*xi - 1
print("Poincare:", factor(chi))

Rz.<z> = PolynomialRing(QQ)
A.<Dz> = OreAlgebra(Rz)
theta = z*Dz


def peval(p, T):
    return sum(QQ(p[k])*T^k for k in range(p.degree() + 1))


Lrec = (z^3*peval(c0, theta)
        + z^2*peval(c1, theta - 1)
        + z*peval(c2, theta - 2)
        + peval(c3, theta - 3))

LD = ((2*theta - 3)*theta^2
      - z*(2*theta + 1)*(35*theta^2 - 9)
      + z^2*(2*theta + 1)*(35*theta^2 + 70*theta + 26)
      - z^3*(2*theta + 5)*(theta + 1)^2)

print("order(Lrec) =", Lrec.order())  # 13
print("order(LD)   =", LD.order())    # 3
print("head(Lrec)  =", factor(Lrec.leading_coefficient()))
print("head(LD)    =", factor(LD.leading_coefficient()))

assert Lrec.leading_coefficient() == \
       98304*z^13*(1-z)*(1-34*z+z^2)
assert LD.leading_coefficient() == \
       2*z^3*(1-z)*(1-34*z+z^2)

# Exact n=0 failure on the Delannoy-square solution.
Dsq = [ZZ(1), ZZ(9), ZZ(169), ZZ(3969)]
res0 = (c0(0)*Dsq[0] + c1(0)*Dsq[1]
        + c2(0)*Dsq[2] + c3(0)*Dsq[3])
assert res0 == -1160938142806248
print("Delannoy residual at n=0 =", res0)

# Greatest common RIGHT divisor.
G = Lrec.gcrd(LD).normalize()
print("right-gcd order =", G.order())
print("right-gcd       =", G)
assert G.order() == 0
```

The normalized output is

```text
right-gcd order = 0
right-gcd       = 1
```

The order-zero assertion is invariant under the unit normalization used by the package.

# 6. Interpretation

The result establishes the following sharp distinction:

\[
\boxed{
\begin{array}{c}
L_{\rm rec}\text{ and }L_D\text{ have the same finite spectral divisor}\
(1-z)(1-34z+z^2),\\[1mm]
\text{but their differential modules have zero common solution submodule.}
\end{array}}
\]

Therefore the following pathways are ruled out:

- \(\widehat Q_n\) being a Delannoy-square solution;
- \(F_D(z)\) being a solution of the CMF Euler operator;
- a shared order-one or order-two differential right factor;
- identifying the two systems by taking a common solution and matching initial values.

The calculation does **not** rule out:

- a nontrivial differential or difference-module extension;
- an integral transform between the two systems;
- a parameter-derivative/normal-function construction;
- a higher-order Ore intertwiner that does not arise from a common right factor;
- the matrix-valued Almkvist--Zeilberger route.

In particular, matching the Poincaré polynomial remains meaningful asymptotically, but the exact common-right-factor mechanism is now closed:

\[
\boxed{\text{there is no shared Delannoy differential solution.}}
\]
