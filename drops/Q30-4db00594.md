# Generating-function factorization versus coefficient collision energy

Let

\[
A_p(t):=\sum_{n=0}^{p-1}b_nt^n\in\mathbf F_p[t],
\qquad
E(p):=\sum_{a\in\mathbf F_p}N_p(a)^2,
\]

where

\[
N_p(a)=\#\{0\le n<p:b_n=a\}.
\]

I use \(A_p(t)\) for the **generating polynomial** in order to distinguish it from the Lagrange interpolation polynomial in the index variable.

## Executive conclusions

1. The square phenomenon is no longer merely computational. Caruso--Fürnsinn--Vargas-Montoya--Zudilin prove that
   \[
   A_p(t)=R_p(t)^2
   \]
   when \(\left(\frac{-6}{p}\right)=1\), and
   \[
   A_p(t)=(t^2-34t+1)R_p(t)^2
   \]
   when \(\left(\frac{-6}{p}\right)=-1\). See Theorem 2 of [*Galois Groups of Apéry-like Series Modulo Primes*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.23298), revision of 22 March 2026.

2. The assertion that **all irreducible factors have degree at most two** is a separate, stronger statement. It does not follow formally from the square/defect-square theorem: the square root \(R_p\) could a priori have irreducible factors of arbitrary degree. The empirical splitting claim is equivalent to
   \[
   \operatorname{rad}(A_p)\mid t^{p^2}-t.
   \]
   It deserves its own proof, probably through the supersingular/modular interpretation of the square root.

3. Even the conjunction
   \[
   \text{perfect square}+
   \text{complete splitting}+
   \text{palindromy}+
   \text{squarefree square root}
   \]
   does **not** imply \(E(p)=O(p)\). With \(d=(p-1)/2\),
   \[
   A(t)=(1-t^d)^2=1-2t^d+t^{p-1}
   \]
   has all those properties, but
   \[
   E(A)=(p-3)^2+2^2+1^2=p^2-6p+14.
   \]

4. There is no formula for \(E(p)\) depending only on the degrees and multiplicities of the irreducible factors. Two polynomials with exactly the same factorization type can have energies \(p^2\) and \(p+2\), respectively.

5. The correct finite-field Fourier transform is not a \(p\)-point additive DFT. It is the multiplicative Mellin transform on \(\mathbf F_p^\times\): for \(1\le j\le p-2\),
   \[
   \boxed{
   b_j=-\sum_{t\in\mathbf F_p^\times}A_p(t)t^{-j}.
   }
   \]
   Thus the square factorization turns the problem into character-aspect anti-concentration for Mellin transforms of \(R_p(t)^2\), or of \((t^2-34t+1)R_p(t)^2\). This is useful conceptually, but no black-box theorem gives the required anti-concentration.

6. The most promising use of the discovery is **not** to expand \(A_p\) as a product of its many linear and quadratic factors. It is to exploit the low-complexity origin of the factorization:
   \[
   f_{\mathrm{Apéry}}(t)=(1+x)h(x)^2,
   \qquad
   t=\frac{x(1-8x)}{1+x},
   \]
   where \(h\) is the Franel/elliptic period. The goal should be a bounded-rank identity for the square root, a Christoffel--Darboux/Racah divided-difference formula, or a bounded-conductor Mellin realization. The individual factorization into \(O(p)\) roots has growing complexity and by itself cannot yield \(E(p)=O(p)\).

# 1. What is now proved about \(A_p(t)\)

The \(p\)-Lucas congruence gives

\[
f_{\mathrm{Apéry}}(t)=A_p(t)f_{\mathrm{Apéry}}(t)^p
\quad\text{in }\mathbf F_p[[t]].
\]

Caruso--Fürnsinn--Vargas-Montoya--Zudilin prove the following exact factorization.

## Theorem

For every prime \(p\ge5\), there exists \(R_p\in\mathbf F_p[t]\) such that

\[
A_p(t)=
\begin{cases}
R_p(t)^2,
& p\equiv1,5,7,11\pmod{24},\\[3pt]
(t^2-34t+1)R_p(t)^2,
& p\equiv13,17,19,23\pmod{24}.
\end{cases}
\tag{1.1}
\]

These are precisely the cases

\[
\left(\frac{-6}{p}\right)=1
\quad\text{and}\quad
\left(\frac{-6}{p}\right)=-1.
\]

Since \(b_{p-1}\equiv1\pmod p\), \(A_p\) has degree \(p-1\). Therefore

\[
\deg R_p=
\begin{cases}
(p-1)/2,&(-6/p)=1,\\
(p-3)/2,&(-6/p)=-1.
\end{cases}
\]

Also \(A_p(0)=1\), and the square root may be normalized by \(R_p(0)=1\).

Because

\[
\Delta(t):=t^2-34t+1
\]

is squarefree for \(p\ge5\), equation (1.1) says that the squarefree part of \(A_p\) is exactly \(1\) or \(\Delta\).

## Important distinction

Equation (1.1) does **not** imply that the irreducible factors of \(R_p\), and hence of \(A_p\), have degree at most two. For example, \(R_p\) could be an irreducible polynomial of degree \((p-1)/2\), and \(R_p^2\) would still satisfy the theorem.

The empirical assertion

\[
\text{all roots of }A_p\text{ lie in }\mathbf F_{p^2}
\tag{1.2}
\]

is equivalent to

\[
\operatorname{rad}(A_p)\mid t^{p^2}-t.
\tag{1.3}
\]

If, in addition, the square root is squarefree, then

\[
A_p(t)\mid (t^{p^2}-t)^2.
\tag{1.4}
\]

This is a clean target for a separate proposition.

A plausible explanation is modular. The proof of (1.1) passes through the relation

\[
f_{\mathrm{Apéry}}(t)=(1+x)h(x)^2,
\qquad
 t=\frac{x(1-8x)}{1+x},
\tag{1.5}
\]

where \(h\) is a rank-two Franel/elliptic period. If the truncation of \(h\) is identified with the Hasse invariant of the associated elliptic modular family, its roots are supersingular parameters. Supersingular elliptic curves are defined over \(\mathbf F_{p^2}\); after checking the level structure and the degree-two map \(x\mapsto t\), this should explain (1.2). That final check is not contained merely in the algebraic factorization theorem and should be written out carefully.

# 2. The correct coefficient/evaluation transform

The user's concern about the absence of nontrivial \(p\)-th roots of unity in \(\mathbf F_p\) is correct. The appropriate transform uses the cyclic multiplicative group

\[
G=\mathbf F_p^\times,
\qquad |G|=p-1.
\]

For \(j\in\mathbf Z/(p-1)\mathbf Z\), define the algebraic Mellin transform

\[
\mathcal M_pA_p(j)
:=\sum_{t\in\mathbf F_p^\times}A_p(t)t^{-j}.
\tag{2.1}
\]

The orthogonality identity in \(\mathbf F_p\) is

\[
\sum_{t\in\mathbf F_p^\times}t^k
=
\begin{cases}
-1,&p-1\mid k,\\
0,&p-1\nmid k.
\end{cases}
\tag{2.2}
\]

For \(1\le j\le p-2\), only the term of degree \(j\) contributes, and hence

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal M_pA_p(j)=-b_j.
}
\tag{2.3}
\]

At the trivial character, degrees \(0\) and \(p-1\) alias:

\[
\mathcal M_pA_p(0)=-(b_0+b_{p-1})=-2.
\tag{2.4}
\]

Therefore, in the square case,

\[
\boxed{
 b_j=-\sum_{t\ne0}R_p(t)^2t^{-j}
 \qquad(1\le j\le p-2),
}
\tag{2.5}
\]

and in the defect-square case,

\[
\boxed{
 b_j=-\sum_{t\ne0}\Delta(t)R_p(t)^2t^{-j}.
}
\tag{2.6}
\]

This is the exact coefficient-root/evaluation bridge. The problem becomes:

> How many tame multiplicative characters have the same Mellin trace against the square-valued function \(R_p(t)^2\), or against the defect-square function \(\Delta(t)R_p(t)^2\)?

The factorization controls the zero divisor and the quadratic character of \(A_p(t)\):

\[
\left(\frac{A_p(t)}p\right)=1
\]

at every nonzero value in the square case, while in the other case it equals \(\left(\frac{\Delta(t)}p\right)\). But this is only a multiplicative statistic of the evaluations. It does not imply additive anti-concentration of their Mellin transforms.

# 3. Exact root-to-coefficient formula

Because \(A_p(0)=1\), none of its roots is zero. Write the roots with multiplicity as

\[
\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_{p-1}
\]

in an algebraic closure, and put

\[
\gamma_i=\alpha_i^{-1}.
\]

Then

\[
A_p(t)=\prod_{i=1}^{p-1}(1-\gamma_it),
\]

so

\[
\boxed{
 b_n=(-1)^ne_n(\gamma_1,\ldots,\gamma_{p-1}),
}
\tag{3.1}
\]

where \(e_n\) is the elementary symmetric function.

Consequently

\[
E(p)
=
\#\left\{(n,m):
(-1)^ne_n(\gamma)=(-1)^me_m(\gamma)
\right\}.
\tag{3.2}
\]

Equivalently, for any nontrivial additive character \(\psi\) of \(\mathbf F_p\),

\[
\boxed{
E(p)=\frac1p\sum_{u\in\mathbf F_p}
\left|
\sum_{n=0}^{p-1}\psi\!\left(u(-1)^ne_n(\gamma)\right)
\right|^2.
}
\tag{3.3}
\]

This is an exact formula in terms of the **root locations**. It does not simplify to a function of only the degrees and multiplicities of the irreducible factors.

## Newton-sum bridge

Let

\[
P_j=\sum_i\gamma_i^j.
\]

The logarithmic derivative gives

\[
\frac{A_p'(t)}{A_p(t)}
=-\sum_{j\ge1}P_jt^{j-1},
\]

and therefore

\[
\boxed{
 n b_n=-\sum_{j=1}^nP_jb_{n-j}.
}
\tag{3.4}
\]

If a quadratic factor has roots \(\beta,\beta^p\), its contribution to \(P_j\) is

\[
u_j=\beta^j+\beta^{pj}.
\]

Writing

\[
s=\beta+\beta^p,
\qquad
q=\beta^{p+1},
\]

this sequence satisfies the second-order recurrence

\[
\boxed{
 u_{j+2}=s u_{j+1}-q u_j.
}
\tag{3.5}
\]

Thus the linear/quadratic factorization expresses the Newton sums \(P_j\) as a sum of many rank-one or rank-two recurrence sequences. The number of summands is \(\asymp p\), however, so the resulting recurrence for \(P_j\) has order \(\asymp p\), not bounded order. No gain follows without a further theorem on the collective distribution of the trace/norm parameters.

# 4. Why factorization type cannot determine \(E(p)\)

There is a decisive exact counterexample.

For \(g\in\mathbf F_p^\times\), consider

\[
F_g(t)=(t-g)^{p-1}.
\tag{4.1}
\]

Every \(F_g\) has exactly the same factorization type: one linear factor of multiplicity \(p-1\). Since \(p-1\) is even, every \(F_g\) is a perfect square, and all roots lie in \(\mathbf F_p\).

Using

\[
\binom{p-1}{n}\equiv(-1)^n\pmod p,
\]

we get

\[
[t^n]F_g(t)
=\binom{p-1}{n}(-g)^{p-1-n}
=g^{-n}.
\tag{4.2}
\]

### Case \(g=1\)

Every coefficient equals \(1\), so

\[
E(F_1)=p^2.
\tag{4.3}
\]

### Case \(g\) primitive

The values

\[
1,g^{-1},\ldots,g^{-(p-2)}
\]

enumerate \(\mathbf F_p^\times\), and the final coefficient at \(n=p-1\) repeats \(1\). Hence

\[
N(1)=2,
\qquad
N(a)=1\quad(a\in\mathbf F_p^\times\setminus\{1\}),
\]

and

\[
\boxed{
E(F_g)=4+(p-2)=p+2.
}
\tag{4.4}
\]

Thus the same factorization type supports both maximal energy and optimal linear energy.

Even adding palindromy and a squarefree split square root does not fix the issue. With \(d=(p-1)/2\),

\[
F(t)=(1-t^d)^2
\]

is palindromic, is a perfect square, and has a squarefree root polynomial \(1-t^d\) splitting completely over \(\mathbf F_p\). Yet

\[
\boxed{
E(F)=p^2-6p+14.
}
\tag{4.5}
\]

Therefore the root-splitting facts are genuinely insufficient as standalone hypotheses.

# 5. What the square convolution really gives

Suppose first that

\[
A_p(t)=R_p(t)^2,
\qquad
R_p(t)=1+r_1t+\cdots+r_dt^d.
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{
 b_n=\sum_{k=0}^nr_kr_{n-k}.
}
\tag{5.1}
\]

This is ordinary index convolution. It does not imply value anti-concentration: the example \(R=1-t^d\) produces \(p-3\) zero coefficients.

There is, however, an exact and useful triangular statement.

## Proposition: triangular universality

Let

\[
S(t)=1+s_1t+s_2t^2
\]

be fixed, allowing \(S=1\), and put

\[
A(t)=S(t)R(t)^2.
\]

For \(1\le n\le d\), the coefficient \(a_n=[t^n]A(t)\) has the form

\[
\boxed{
 a_n=2r_n+P_n(r_1,\ldots,r_{n-1}),
}
\tag{5.2}
\]

where \(P_n\) is a polynomial depending on \(S\).

Because \(2\ne0\) in \(\mathbf F_p\), the map

\[
(r_1,\ldots,r_d)
\longmapsto
(a_1,\ldots,a_d)
\tag{5.3}
\]

is a triangular polynomial automorphism of \(\mathbf A^d\).

### Consequences

1. In the class of all polynomials \(S R^2\), the first \(d\) coefficients can be prescribed **arbitrarily**. Squarehood imposes no algebraic relation on that half of the coefficient vector.

2. If \(R\) is chosen uniformly at random, then
   \[
   a_1,\ldots,a_d
   \]
   are exactly independent uniform elements of \(\mathbf F_p\), not merely asymptotically so.

3. Since \(A_p\) is palindromic, \(R_p\) is reciprocal or anti-reciprocal after normalization. This halves the number of free \(r_i\), but the first roughly \(d/2\asymp p/4\) coefficients remain triangularly free. Thus even squarehood plus palindromy leaves linearly many degrees of freedom.

This explains two apparently contradictory facts:

- the factorization is extraordinarily rigid in the **root** variable;
- the low-half coefficients can nevertheless look completely random.

It also says exactly what is missing. To use (5.1), one must prove that the **specific** square root \(R_p\) occupies a pseudorandom point of this large coefficient space. The identity \(A_p=S R_p^2\) alone does not do so.

# 6. Product of simple factors and coefficient convolution

Suppose

\[
A_p(t)=\prod_{i=1}^M Q_i(t),
\qquad \deg Q_i\le2.
\]

If \(q_i\) denotes the coefficient vector of \(Q_i\), the coefficient vector of \(A_p\) is the iterated ordinary convolution

\[
(b_0,\ldots,b_{p-1})
=q_1*q_2*\cdots*q_M.
\tag{6.1}
\]

This is an exact decomposition into simple pieces. Unfortunately, the collision energy

\[
\mathcal E(c)=\sum_a\#\{n:c_n=a\}^2
\]

has no useful additive, multiplicative, or monotone behavior under index convolution.

Indeed,

\[
\mathcal E(c)
=\frac1p\sum_{u\in\mathbf F_p}
\left|\sum_n\psi(uc_n)\right|^2,
\tag{6.2}
\]

and if

\[
c_n=\sum_kv_kw_{n-k},
\]

the convolution remains **inside** the additive character:

\[
\psi\!\left(u\sum_kv_kw_{n-k}\right).
\]

It does not factor into a product of transforms of \(v\) and \(w\).

This is unlike the usual \(\ell^2\)-norm of a complex convolution, where Fourier diagonalization applies. Here the norm is taken after grouping coefficients by their **field values**, a nonlinear operation.

A product-factor approach would require an additional mixing theorem saying that the successive linear and quadratic factors randomize coefficient values. The deterministic example \((1-t^d)^2\) shows that no such statement can hold from low factor degree alone.

# 7. What the degree-two trace and norm data control

For a quadratic orbit \(\{\beta,\beta^p\}\), the factor is

\[
(t-\beta)(t-\beta^p)
=t^2-s t+q,
\]

where

\[
s=\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathbf F_{p^2}/\mathbf F_p}(\beta),
\qquad
q=\operatorname{Nm}_{\mathbf F_{p^2}/\mathbf F_p}(\beta).
\]

For \(t\in\mathbf F_p\),

\[
t^2-st+q
=\operatorname{Nm}_{\mathbf F_{p^2}/\mathbf F_p}(t-\beta).
\tag{7.1}
\]

Thus the trace/norm factorization is useful for:

- locating the zeros of \(A_p\);
- evaluating quadratic characters of \(A_p(t)\);
- interpreting roots as Frobenius orbits;
- proving that the divisor is defined over \(\mathbf F_{p^2}\);
- relating the roots to supersingular points.

It is much less directly useful for \(E(p)\), because every coefficient is a global elementary symmetric function of **all** roots. Marginal equidistribution of the traces \(s_j\) and norms \(q_j\) would not suffice; one would need strong joint control of high-order symmetric sums.

A potentially meaningful statistic is the sequence of root power sums

\[
P_k=\sum_i\gamma_i^k.
\]

The trace/norm pairs make each local contribution obey a second-order recurrence, as in (3.5). One should compute experimentally whether the **aggregate** \(P_k\) satisfies an unexpectedly low-order recurrence or a Hecke relation. If it did, Newton's identity (3.4) would turn the root divisor into a bounded-rank coefficient mechanism. Without such a collapse, the order remains \(\asymp p\).

# 8. Evaluation moments are not collision energy

The user's calculation of

\[
\sum_{t\in\mathbf F_p}A_p(t)^2
\]

produces a genuine identity, but not the desired statistic.

Expanding and using multiplicative orthogonality gives

\[
\sum_{t\in\mathbf F_p}A_p(t)^2
=-1-\sum_{n=0}^{p-1}b_nb_{p-1-n}.
\tag{8.1}
\]

By palindromy,

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{t\in\mathbf F_p}A_p(t)^2
=-1-\sum_{n=0}^{p-1}b_n^2.
}
\tag{8.2}
\]

This controls the field-valued quadratic moment \(\sum b_n^2\in\mathbf F_p\). Collision energy instead counts exact equalities among the integer representatives of field elements:

\[
E(p)=\sum_{n,m}\mathbf1_{\{b_n=b_m\}}.
\]

The latter requires additive-character orthogonality, not the algebraic square moment. A sequence can have the same value of \(\sum b_n^2\) and completely different collision energy.

The polynomial naturally encoding \(E(p)\) is

\[
V_p(Y)=\prod_{n=0}^{p-1}(Y-b_n),
\]

whose root multiplicities are the \(N_p(a)\). This is a **coefficient-value polynomial**, not the generating polynomial \(A_p(t)\). No general operation transports the root factorization of \(A_p(t)\) to the root multiplicities of \(V_p(Y)\).

# 9. Comparison with a random polynomial

Let \(q=p\) and write \(p-1=2d\).

## 9.1 Squarefreeness

A uniformly random monic polynomial of degree at least two over \(\mathbf F_q\) is squarefree with probability

\[
1-\frac1q.
\]

The Apéry polynomial is instead a square or a fixed quadratic times a square. It is therefore maximally non-squarefree.

## 9.2 Perfect-square probability

There are \(q^{2d}\) monic polynomials of degree \(2d\), and \(q^d\) monic squares of that degree. Hence

\[
\Pr(F\text{ is a square})=q^{-d}.
\tag{9.1}
\]

For \(q=p\),

\[
\boxed{
\Pr(F\text{ is a square})=p^{-(p-1)/2}.
}
\tag{9.2}
\]

## 9.3 Squarefree part of degree at most two

Every monic polynomial has a unique decomposition

\[
F=S R^2
\]

with \(S\) monic and squarefree. For degree \(2d\), the cases \(\deg S=0\) and \(\deg S=2\) contain respectively

\[
q^d
\]

and

\[
(q^2-q)q^{d-1}
\]

polynomials. Their sum is \(q^{d+1}\). Therefore

\[
\boxed{
\Pr(\deg\operatorname{sqf}(F)\le2)
=q^{1-d}.
}
\tag{9.3}
\]

For the Apéry degree \(p-1\), this is

\[
\boxed{
p^{-(p-3)/2}.}
\tag{9.4}
\]

The exact Apéry statement is stronger still: the squarefree part is not an arbitrary quadratic but the fixed polynomial \(t^2-34t+1\).

## 9.4 Factor degrees

For a random squarefree polynomial, the factor-degree pattern is modeled by the cycle structure of a random permutation: the expected number of degree-\(r\) factors is about \(1/r\), and large irreducible factors are typical. Under that benchmark, requiring all cycles to have length at most two is analogous to requiring a random permutation to be an involution; its probability decays roughly like

\[
\exp\!\left(-\tfrac12 n\log n+O(n)\right).
\]

Thus the empirical degree-\(\le2\) splitting is extremely nonrandom even after conditioning on squarehood.

The important lesson is that **root factorization can be extraordinarily nonrandom while coefficient values remain birthday-random**. The two statistics live in different transforms of the polynomial.

# 10. The most promising ways to use the discovery

## 10.1 Prove the \(\mathbf F_{p^2}\)-splitting geometrically

Do not leave the degree-\(\le2\) observation as a computational proposition. A realistic proof program is:

1. identify the Franel truncation \(H_p(x)\) in the CFVMZ pullback as the Hasse invariant of the underlying elliptic modular family;
2. identify its zero divisor with the supersingular divisor on the relevant genus-zero modular curve;
3. prove Frobenius squared fixes the level structure and therefore every supersingular parameter;
4. descend through
   \[
   t=\frac{x(1-8x)}{1+x}.
   \]

This should prove

\[
\operatorname{rad}(A_p)\mid t^{p^2}-t.
\]

It would be a worthwhile structural theorem, though not by itself an energy theorem.

## 10.2 Study the square root, not its individual factors

The CFVMZ identity shows that \(R_p\) is a descent of a rank-two Franel/elliptic Hasse polynomial. Seek:

- an explicit formula for the coefficients of \(R_p\);
- a fixed-order recurrence for those coefficients;
- a second-order difference equation in the coefficient index;
- a Christoffel--Darboux identity for their autocorrelation;
- a determinant of fixed size for \(b_n-b_m\).

The factorization is valuable because it points back to a **rank-two source**. Expanding \(O(p)\) separate factors destroys precisely that bounded-rank information.

## 10.3 Racah/secant divided differences

The Apéry numbers admit a symmetric Racah-type representation. The square structure suggests that the quotient

\[
\frac{b_{x+h}-b_x}{h(2x+h+1)}
\]

may admit a Christoffel--Darboux or low-displacement-rank formula inherited from the rank-two square root. If its zero curve has bounded genus, or a bounded-degree cover, then Weil gives

\[
E(p)=O(p).
\]

This is the most direct route from the square discovery to collision energy: use the square root to lower the complexity of the **divided difference**, not merely to list the roots of \(A_p\).

## 10.4 Mellin anti-concentration from a fixed sheaf

Equations (2.5)--(2.6) formulate \(b_j\) as character-aspect Mellin traces. A sufficient geometric package would be:

1. realize \(t\mapsto R_p(t)\), after the quadratic pullback, as a trace/Hasse function of a fixed bounded-complexity rank-two family;
2. identify the convolution square cohomologically;
3. prove a mod-\(p\) local-limit or second-moment theorem for the traces as the tame character \(j\) varies.

Ordinary complex Katz equidistribution is not enough: \(b_j=b_k\pmod p\) is a characteristic-\(p\) congruence at probability scale \(1/p\). Nevertheless, the low-rank sheaf is the correct place to search for a proof.

## 10.5 Computational diagnostics that distinguish useful structure

For each prime, compute the normalized square root \(R_p\) and record:

1. collision energy of its coefficient sequence;
2. additive Fourier spectrum of its coefficients;
3. Mellin transforms \(\sum_tR_p(t)t^{-j}\);
4. root power sums \(P_k\) and their minimal recurrence order;
5. trace/norm distributions of quadratic factors;
6. whether the roots coincide with supersingular parameters of the associated elliptic family;
7. displacement rank of the matrix whose entries are coefficients of \(R_p(t)^2\);
8. factorization and genus of the divided difference of \(b_x\).

The key comparison ensembles should be:

- arbitrary random reciprocal squares;
- reciprocal squares whose roots all lie in \(\mathbf F_{p^2}\);
- supersingular polynomials for the same modular curve;
- the actual Apéry square root.

If the first two already reproduce \(E(p)\sim3p\), the factorization supplies a probabilistic explanation but not an arithmetic proof. If only the supersingular ensemble does, the modular geometry is load-bearing.

# 11. Direct answers to the five questions

## 1. Can the product of linear and quadratic factors decompose \(E(p)\)?

It decomposes the **coefficient vector** as an iterated index convolution and gives the Newton-sum recurrence (3.4). It does not decompose collision energy into a sum of factorwise energies. Equality of coefficient values is nonlinear, and the trivial product-factor expansion has complexity \(\asymp p\).

## 2. Does \(A_p=R_p^2\) help?

It gives the exact convolution

\[
b_n=\sum_kr_kr_{n-k}
\]

and the triangular automorphism (5.2). This is useful for constructing a rigorous random-square model and for locating a rank-two square root. It does not give a deterministic bound: even a palindromic split square with squarefree square root can have \(E(p)\asymp p^2\).

## 3. Do the trace and norm parameters of quadratic factors help?

They give norm formulas for evaluations and second-order recurrences for root power sums. They could become useful if the aggregate trace/norm divisor is shown to have bounded-rank modular structure. Their marginal distribution alone cannot control the high-order symmetric sums that form the coefficients.

## 4. Is \(E(p)\) determined by factorization type?

No. The family \((t-g)^{p-1}\) has one linear factor of multiplicity \(p-1\) for every \(g\ne0\), yet its energy is \(p^2\) for \(g=1\) and \(p+2\) for a primitive \(g\). Full root locations determine \(E\) through (3.1)--(3.3), but degrees and multiplicities do not.

## 5. How does the factorization compare with a random polynomial?

It is extraordinarily nonrandom. A random degree-\(p-1\) polynomial is squarefree with probability \(1-1/p\), is a perfect square with probability \(p^{-(p-1)/2}\), and has squarefree part of degree at most two with probability \(p^{-(p-3)/2}\). Typical random factorization contains factors of many degrees. The Apéry polynomial's root divisor is therefore extremely structured even though its coefficient-value energy looks random-palindromic.

# Final assessment

The new factorization is a major structural theorem, but its immediate implications point in two opposite directions:

- it reveals a hidden rank-two/symmetric-square origin that could ultimately yield a proof;
- taken merely as a statement about roots, squares, or degree-two factors, it is far too weak to control coefficient collisions.

The most promising path is

\[
\boxed{
\text{CFVMZ quadratic pullback}
\longrightarrow
\text{explicit rank-two square root}
\longrightarrow
\text{Christoffel--Darboux/Racah divided difference}
\longrightarrow
\#\{b_x=b_y\}=O(p).
}
\]

The wrong path is to treat the \(O(p)\) linear and quadratic factors as independent local pieces. The sparse counterexamples show that no factor-by-factor argument can succeed without an additional global mixing or low-rank identity.
