# Problem 2.5: the Brafman–Catalan period, the exact integrated elliptic system, and what is still missing from the CMF proof

## Executive conclusion

The Brafman identity gives an exact and useful representation of Catalan's constant, but it does **not** by itself identify the finite Problem 2.5 CMF product with partial sums of the three symmetric-square sequences

\[
D_n^2,\qquad D_nE_n,\qquad E_n^2.
\]

In fact, the established nonexistence of a rational Ore intertwiner rules out the requested formula in its natural rational form. The correct rank-three object is not the rational symmetric square of the Delannoy recurrence. It is an **integrated rank-two elliptic period system**:

\[
Y'(k)=K(k),
\]

where \(K\) is the complete elliptic integral. Its exact third-order equation is

\[
\boxed{
 k(1-k^2)Y'''(k)+(1-3k^2)Y''(k)-kY'(k)=0.}
\tag{0.1}
\]

This is an extension of the rank-two Gauss/elliptic module by the constant solution, not a symmetric square. It is precisely the type of rank-three system whose connection matrix can contain Catalan's constant.

The exact Brafman evaluation is as follows. Put

\[
D_n=P_n(3),\qquad
F(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}D_n^2z^n,
\]

\[
a=3-2\sqrt2=(\sqrt2-1)^2,
\qquad
\rho=a^2=17-12\sqrt2.
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{
F(z)=\frac{2}{\pi(1-z)}
K\!\left(\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z}\right),}
\tag{0.2}
\]

and

\[
\boxed{
G=\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\int_0^\rho
\frac{1+z}{\sqrt z(1-z)}F(z)\,dz.}
\tag{0.3}
\]

Termwise integration gives the completely explicit series

\[
\boxed{
G=\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}D_n^2 I_n,}
\tag{0.4}
\]

where

\[
\boxed{
I_n=
\log2
-4\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}\frac{a^{2j+1}}{2j+1}
-\frac{2a^{2n+1}}{2n+1}.}
\tag{0.5}
\]

An equivalent single-sum form, containing only finite sums of Delannoy squares, is

\[
\boxed{
G=\pi\sqrt2\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}
\frac{a^{2m+1}}{2m+1}
\left(D_m^2+2\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}D_j^2\right).}
\tag{0.6}
\]

These formulas are exact. They define a natural sequence of Brafman partial sums, but those partial sums converge only polynomially, essentially as \(O(1/N)\). They are therefore **not** the 51-digit-at-\(N=60\) CMF approximants.

The correct CMF proof architecture is:

1. state the actual CMF approximant as a ratio of two rows of the initialized product;
2. remove the Newton-slope-seven factorial scale by an exact Borel/gamma normalization;
3. convert the normalized scalar recurrence to a rank-three differential system;
4. prove that this system is algebraically gauge-equivalent to the pullback of (0.1), not to \(\operatorname{Sym}^2\) of the Delannoy recurrence;
5. match three initial coefficients;
6. evaluate the connection coefficient at \(k=1\), where it is \(G\);
7. use singularity analysis or an integral remainder to prove convergence.

No presently supplied calculation gives the required finite differential gauge. Consequently the constants and rational functions requested in part (a) cannot honestly be printed: their existence in the proposed class would contradict the established Ore-Hom obstruction.

# 2. The Delannoy basis and the origin of \(\log2/2\)

The central Delannoy numbers satisfy

\[
(n+1)D_{n+1}=3(2n+1)D_n-nD_{n-1},
\qquad D_0=1,\quad D_1=3,
\tag{2.1}
\]

and

\[
D_n=P_n(3)
={}_2F_1\!\left(
\begin{matrix}-n,n+1\\1\end{matrix};-1
\right).
\tag{2.2}
\]

Define the rational second solution \(E_n\) by

\[
E_0=0,\qquad E_1=1,
\]

and the same recurrence (2.1). It has the exact convolution formula

\[
\boxed{
E_n=\sum_{j=1}^{n}
\frac{D_{j-1}D_{n-j}}{j}.}
\tag{2.3}
\]

For the Legendre function of the second kind,

\[
Q_0(3)=\frac12\log\frac{3+1}{3-1}
=\frac{\log2}{2},
\]

and the standard polynomial-part identity gives

\[
\boxed{
Q_n(3)=\frac{\log2}{2}D_n-E_n.}
\tag{2.4}
\]

Since \(Q_n(3)/P_n(3)\to0\), one obtains

\[
\boxed{
\frac{E_n}{D_n}\longrightarrow\frac{\log2}{2}.}
\tag{2.5}
\]

This explains the numerical observation exactly. The three quadratic products

\[
D_n^2,\qquad D_nE_n,\qquad E_n^2
\tag{2.6}
\]

have connection ratios generated by

\[
1,\qquad \frac{\log2}{2},\qquad
\left(\frac{\log2}{2}\right)^2.
\]

A fixed rational combination of these homogeneous solutions therefore belongs to the Legendre connection field generated by \(\log2\). Catalan's constant is not produced by merely choosing different constant coefficients in (2.6). It enters only after applying a nonlocal operation—here, integration of the elliptic period.

# 3. Why the requested rational finite-sum decomposition is obstructed

Let

\[
U_n=
\begin{pmatrix}
D_n^2\\D_nE_n\\E_n^2
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
U_{n+1}=A_{\rm sym}(n)U_n,
\tag{3.1}
\]

where \(A_{\rm sym}(n)\) is a first-order companion realization of the symmetric-square recurrence.

Suppose one tries to construct

\[
s_N=c+\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\ell(k)U_k,
\qquad
\ell(k)\in\mathbf Q(k)^{1\times3}.
\tag{3.2}
\]

For this indefinite sum to reduce to a rank-three homogeneous system rather than a generic rank-four extension, the row cocycle \(\ell\) must be a rational coboundary. Explicitly, there must exist

\[
r(k)\in\mathbf Q(k)^{1\times3}
\]

such that

\[
\boxed{
\ell(k)=r(k+1)A_{\rm sym}(k)-r(k).}
\tag{3.3}
\]

Then and only then does (3.2) telescope inside the original rank-three module:

\[
\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\ell(k)U_k
=r(N)U_N-r(0)U_0.
\tag{3.4}
\]

Equation (3.3) is the first-order-system form of a rational Ore intertwiner. Therefore the established nonexistence of a rational Ore map between the normalized Problem 2.5 operator and the symmetric-square Delannoy operator has the following consequence:

> There is no universal representation of the CMF fundamental solutions by sums of the form (3.2) with rational \(f_1,f_2,f_3\) that closes back to the same rank-three homogeneous module.

A rank-four nontrivial extension can certainly contain such sums, but that is a different operator. Alternatively, one may allow algebraic, elliptic, or transcendental coefficient functions, but then the requested \(f_j\in\mathbf Q(k)\) condition has been abandoned.

For a single accidentally chosen numerical sequence, rational interpolation from finitely many terms would not prove an identity. One must provide either (3.3), an Ore certificate, or a differential-module gauge.

Thus part (a), as stated, has a negative answer in the requested rational class.

# 4. Exact derivation of the Brafman–Catalan integral

Brafman's identity at \(x=3\) is

\[
F(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}P_n(3)^2z^n
=\frac{1}{1-z}
{}_2F_1\!\left(
\frac12,\frac12;1;
\frac{32z}{(1-z)^2}
\right).
\tag{4.1}
\]

In the elliptic-modulus convention

\[
K(k)=\frac{\pi}{2}
{}_2F_1\!\left(\frac12,\frac12;1;k^2\right),
\]

this becomes (0.2), with

\[
k(z)=\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z}.
\tag{4.2}
\]

A direct differentiation gives

\[
\boxed{
k'(z)=
\frac{2\sqrt2(1+z)}{\sqrt z(1-z)^2}.}
\tag{4.3}
\]

Furthermore,

\[
k(0)=0,
\qquad
k(\rho)=1,
\tag{4.4}
\]

because

\[
1-\rho=4\sqrt2\sqrt\rho.
\]

Combining (0.2) and (4.3),

\[
\begin{aligned}
&\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\frac{1+z}{\sqrt z(1-z)}F(z)\,dz\\
&\qquad=
\sqrt2\frac{1+z}{\sqrt z(1-z)^2}K(k(z))\,dz\\
&\qquad=\frac12K(k)\,dk.
\end{aligned}
\tag{4.5}
\]

Hence

\[
\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\int_0^\rho
\frac{1+z}{\sqrt z(1-z)}F(z)\,dz
=\frac12\int_0^1K(k)\,dk.
\tag{4.6}
\]

Finally,

\[
K(k)=\int_0^{\pi/2}
\frac{d\theta}{\sqrt{1-k^2\sin^2\theta}}.
\]

Fubini's theorem gives

\[
\frac12\int_0^1K(k)\,dk
=\frac12\int_0^{\pi/2}\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\,d\theta.
\]

With \(t=\tan(\theta/2)\), this becomes

\[
\int_0^1\frac{\arctan t}{t}\,dt=G.
\tag{4.7}
\]

This proves (0.3) exactly.

# 5. Exact termwise formulas and the real role of \(\log2\)

For \(0\le z\le\rho\), all terms are nonnegative and termwise integration is justified. Define

\[
I_n=\int_0^\rho
z^{n-1/2}\frac{1+z}{1-z}\,dz.
\tag{5.1}
\]

Putting \(z=t^2\) gives

\[
I_n=2\int_0^a
t^{2n}\frac{1+t^2}{1-t^2}\,dt.
\tag{5.2}
\]

Since

\[
\frac{1+t^2}{1-t^2}=\frac{2}{1-t^2}-1
\]

and

\[
\int_0^a\frac{t^{2n}}{1-t^2}\,dt
=\operatorname{artanh}a
-\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}\frac{a^{2j+1}}{2j+1},
\]

we obtain

\[
I_n=4\operatorname{artanh}a
-4\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}\frac{a^{2j+1}}{2j+1}
-\frac{2a^{2n+1}}{2n+1}.
\]

But

\[
\frac{1+a}{1-a}=\sqrt2,
\qquad
4\operatorname{artanh}a=\log2,
\]

which proves (0.5).

An important warning follows. The formal split

\[
I_n=\log2-(\text{finite algebraic expression})
\]

cannot be summed termwise as two separate series, because

\[
\sum_{n\ge0}D_n^2
\]

diverges. The \(\log2\) term and the finite algebraic sum cancel to exponentially small order **inside each \(I_n\)**. Thus there is no convergent decomposition in which a standalone \(D_nE_n\) series supplies \(\log2/2\) and then cancels a standalone \(D_n^2\) series. Any such statement requires a regularization or an exact nonlocal connection formula.

There is a cleaner equivalent form. Set

\[
C_m=D_m^2+2\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}D_j^2.
\tag{5.3}
\]

Because

\[
\frac{1+t^2}{1-t^2}F(t^2)
=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}C_mt^{2m},
\]

(0.3) becomes

\[
G=\pi\sqrt2\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}
\frac{C_ma^{2m+1}}{2m+1},
\]

which is (0.6).

Asymptotically,

\[
D_m^2\asymp\frac{(17+12\sqrt2)^m}{m},
\qquad
a^{2m}=\rho^m=(17+12\sqrt2)^{-m},
\]

so the terms in (0.6) are of order \(m^{-2}\). The truncation error is therefore \(O(1/N)\), confirming that this is an evaluation formula, not the fast CMF approximation scheme.

# 6. Exact Beukers-type integral representations

## 6.1 A two-variable Euler integral directly from Brafman

Euler's integral for the Gauss function gives

\[
{}_2F_1\!\left(\frac12,\frac12;1;x\right)
=\frac1\pi\int_0^1
\frac{du}{\sqrt{u(1-u)(1-xu)}}.
\]

Substitution into (0.3) yields the exact double integral

\[
\boxed{
G=\frac{\sqrt2}{2}
\int_0^\rho\int_0^1
\frac{1+z}
{\sqrt z(1-z)
\sqrt{u(1-u)\bigl((1-z)^2-32zu\bigr)}}
\,du\,dz.}
\tag{6.1}
\]

Equivalently, with \(z=t^2\),

\[
\boxed{
G=\sqrt2\int_0^a\int_0^1
\frac{1+t^2}
{(1-t^2)
\sqrt{u(1-u)\bigl((1-t^2)^2-32t^2u\bigr)}}
\,du\,dt.}
\tag{6.2}
\]

This is the most direct Brafman–Beukers double integral. Its algebraic denominator is not

\[
(1-6xy+x^2y^2)^{3/2}.
\]

The latter polynomial is associated with the ordinary Delannoy generating function, whereas Catalan's constant here comes from the elliptic square-generating function and an additional integration.

## 6.2 A Legendre double integral for \(F\), and a triple integral for \(G\)

Let

\[
\alpha=3+2\sqrt2,
\qquad
A(x)=\alpha-4\sqrt2\,x.
\]

The Laplace integral for Legendre polynomials gives

\[
D_n=\frac1\pi\int_0^1
\frac{A(x)^n}{\sqrt{x(1-x)}}\,dx.
\tag{6.3}
\]

Consequently

\[
\boxed{
F(z)=\frac1{\pi^2}
\int_0^1\int_0^1
\frac{dx\,dy}
{\sqrt{x(1-x)y(1-y)}
\bigl(1-zA(x)A(y)\bigr)}.}
\tag{6.4}
\]

Substitution into (0.3) gives

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
G={}&\frac{\sqrt2}{2\pi}
\int_0^1\int_0^1
\frac{dx\,dy}{\sqrt{x(1-x)y(1-y)}}\\
&\times\int_0^\rho
\frac{1+z}
{\sqrt z(1-z)
\bigl(1-zA(x)A(y)\bigr)}\,dz.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{6.5}
\]

This is an exact three-variable period integral.

The \(z\)-integral can be evaluated explicitly. For

\[
c=A(x)A(y),
\]

put

\[
\Phi(c)=2\int_0^a
\frac{1+t^2}{(1-t^2)(1-ct^2)}\,dt.
\]

Partial fractions give

\[
\boxed{
\Phi(c)=
\frac{\log2-
2(1+c)c^{-1/2}\operatorname{artanh}(a\sqrt c)}{1-c},}
\tag{6.6}
\]

with the removable cases interpreted by continuity. Thus

\[
\boxed{
G=\frac{\sqrt2}{2\pi}
\int_0^1\int_0^1
\frac{\Phi(A(x)A(y))}
{\sqrt{x(1-x)y(1-y)}}\,dx\,dy.}
\tag{6.7}
\]

This is a Beukers-type double integral with an algebraic-logarithmic kernel.

For comparison, the elementary double integral

\[
\boxed{
G=\int_0^1\int_0^1
\frac{dx\,dy}{1+x^2y^2}}
\tag{6.8}
\]

follows immediately by expanding the denominator. Formula (6.8) proves the period value but contains no information about the Problem 2.5 CMF acceleration.

## 6.3 Exact finite Brafman truncations

The natural truncation

\[
S_N^{\rm Br}
=\frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2}
\int_0^\rho
\frac{1+z}{\sqrt z(1-z)}
\sum_{n=0}^{N}D_n^2z^n\,dz
\tag{6.9}
\]

has the exact integral representation

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
S_N^{\rm Br}
={}&\frac{\sqrt2}{2\pi}
\int_0^1\int_0^1
\frac{dx\,dy}{\sqrt{x(1-x)y(1-y)}}\\
&\times\int_0^\rho
\frac{1+z}{\sqrt z(1-z)}
\frac{1-\bigl(zA(x)A(y)\bigr)^{N+1}}
{1-zA(x)A(y)}\,dz.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{6.10}
\]

The remainder is obtained by replacing the numerator in (6.10) with

\[
\bigl(zA(x)A(y)\bigr)^{N+1}.
\]

This supplies an explicit polynomial/geometric numerator, but it is the remainder for the slow Brafman truncation, not for the challenge CMF approximant.

# 7. The correct rank-three differential system

Let

\[
\mathcal Y(k)=\frac12\int_0^kK(s)\,ds.
\tag{7.1}
\]

Then

\[
\mathcal Y(0)=0,
\qquad
\mathcal Y(1)=G.
\tag{7.2}
\]

The complete elliptic integral satisfies

\[
k(1-k^2)K''+(1-3k^2)K'-kK=0.
\tag{7.3}
\]

Since \(\mathcal Y'=K/2\), equation (7.3) gives (0.1). In first-order form, for

\[
X(k)=
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathcal Y(k)\\K(k)\\K'(k)
\end{pmatrix},
\]

one has

\[
\boxed{
\frac{dX}{dk}=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&\tfrac12&0\\
0&0&1\\
0&\dfrac1{1-k^2}&-
\dfrac{1-3k^2}{k(1-k^2)}
\end{pmatrix}X.}
\tag{7.4}
\]

A harmless rescaling of the first coordinate removes the factor \(1/2\).

The module defined by (7.4) fits into an exact sequence

\[
0\longrightarrow{\bf1}
\longrightarrow\mathcal M_{\rm int}
\longrightarrow\mathcal M_K
\longrightarrow0,
\tag{7.5}
\]

where \(\mathcal M_K\) is the rank-two elliptic/Gauss module. This extension is the conceptual source of the new period \(G\).

The Brafman pullback is exactly

\[
k=k(z)=\frac{4\sqrt{2z}}{1-z}.
\tag{7.6}
\]

Thus a finite proof should compare the normalized CMF generating system with the pullback of (7.4). It should **not** search for another rational coefficient-level intertwiner with \(D_n^2,D_nE_n,E_n^2\), because that search targets the wrong rank-three module.

This also explains the apparently contradictory facts:

- the Poincaré roots have a symmetric-square-looking set;
- Brafman's identity reduces the square-generating function to one elliptic \({}_2F_1\);
- no rational Ore intertwiner to the symmetric-square recurrence exists;
- the connection coefficient is nevertheless Catalan's constant.

The rank-three structure is produced by integrating the rank-two elliptic period.

# 8. The finite certificate that would complete the CMF proof

Let \(L_{25}\) be the scalar recurrence extracted from the CMF. Its Newton slope is seven. First choose an exact gamma/Borel normalization which removes the factorial growth. A basic version is

\[
\widehat q(z)=
\sum_{n\ge0}
\frac{q_n}{(-16)^n(n!)^7}z^n,
\tag{8.1}
\]

although the full endpoint factorization suggests using the more refined Pochhammer product already isolated from the determinant line. The precise normalization must be checked against all four coefficients of \(L_{25}\).

After this normalization, convert the recurrence to a differential companion system

\[
\frac{dZ}{dz}=A_{25}(z)Z.
\tag{8.2}
\]

Pull (7.4) back by (7.6), obtaining

\[
\frac{dX}{dz}=k'(z)A_{\rm int}(k(z))X.
\tag{8.3}
\]

The decisive finite certificate is an algebraic gauge matrix

\[
U(z)\in\operatorname{GL}_3
\bigl(\mathbf Q(\sqrt2,\sqrt z,z)\bigr)
\]

satisfying

\[
\boxed{
U'(z)=k'(z)A_{\rm int}(k(z))U(z)-U(z)A_{25}(z).}
\tag{8.4}
\]

Equivalently,

\[
Z(z)=U(z)^{-1}X(k(z))
\]

must satisfy the CMF-derived system. Equation (8.4) is linear in the nine entries of \(U\). Once denominator and degree bounds are chosen, it is a finite linear algebra calculation.

A scalar-operator version is

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal L_{25}^{\rm Borel}T
=R\,\operatorname{pullback}_{k(z)}
\left(
[k(1-k^2)D_k^2+(1-3k^2)D_k-k]D_k
\right),}
\tag{8.5}
\]

with differential intertwiners \(T,R\). This is the differential analogue of the failed difference-Ore search, but it targets the integrated elliptic module and therefore does not contradict the established Ore-Hom obstruction.

After (8.4) or (8.5) is found, the rest is finite:

1. match three Taylor coefficients at \(z=0\);
2. identify which CMF row combination corresponds to \(\mathcal Y\);
3. use \(k(\rho)=1\);
4. conclude that the connection coefficient is
   \[
   \mathcal Y(1)=G;

5. use the singular expansion at \(k=1\), together with the dominant Poincaré mode, to prove convergence of the ratios (1.2).

# 9. Which proof route is most promising?

## 9.1 Creative telescoping

Creative telescoping is excellent **after** an integral family has been identified. One would seek three contiguous integrals

\[
I_n^{(0)},\ I_n^{(1)},\ I_n^{(2)}
\]

whose transfer matrix is exactly \(M(n)\), and certificates

\[
M(n)I_{n+1}-I_n
=\partial_x\Phi_n+\partial_y\Psi_n.
\]

At present no such family is known. Starting a blind WZ search from the degree-seven matrix is likely much harder than solving the differential gauge (8.4).

## 9.2 Integral representation and residues

The exact integrals (6.1), (6.5), and (6.7) evaluate the period. They do not yet encode the fast CMF denominators. To prove the challenge by this route, one must derive an integral remainder for the actual rational approximants, analogous to a Padé or Hermite–Padé error formula.

## 9.3 Monodromy and connection coefficients

This is the best current route. The target differential system is explicit, the endpoint \(k=1\) is explicit, and the connection coefficient is exactly \(G\). The only missing item is the finite algebraic gauge (8.4) from the normalized CMF system.

## 9.4 Continued fractions and Hermite–Padé approximation

A \(3\times3\) transfer matrix often encodes multiple orthogonal polynomials or a Hermite–Padé table. If one can identify the two Markov functions or the underlying measure, the CMF ratios acquire an integral remainder automatically. This may ultimately give the cleanest arithmetic proof, but reconstructing the measure from \(M(n)\) is a separate inverse problem.

Therefore the recommended order is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{Borel normalization}
\to\text{differential gauge (8.4)}
\to\text{initial-value match}
\to\text{monodromy/period evaluation}
\to\text{integral remainder}.}
\tag{9.1}
\]

# 10. Direct answers to the four questions

## (a)

No constants \(c_1,c_2,c_3\) and rational functions \(f_1,f_2,f_3\) of the proposed universal form are presently available. More strongly, a rational finite-sum representation that closes in rank three would give the rational coboundary (3.3), hence a rational Ore map; the established Ore-Hom computation rules that out.

## (b)

The \(D_n^2\) elliptic period produces \(G\) through (0.3). The quantity \(E_n/D_n\to\log2/2\) is explained by the Legendre identity (2.4). There is no convergent standalone \(D_nE_n\) contribution whose \(\log2/2\) term cancels another standalone series. The cancellation occurs inside the exact weights \(I_n\) in (0.5), or in the connection matrix of the integrated elliptic system.

## (c)

Yes. Exact Beukers-type representations are (6.1), (6.2), (6.5), and (6.7). The simple universal integral (6.8) is also available. The proposed denominator \((1-6xy+x^2y^2)^{3/2}\) is not the natural Brafman denominator; the correct elliptic discriminant is

\[
(1-z)^2-32zu.
\]

An explicit \(N\)-dependent kernel for the slow Brafman truncations is given in (6.10). An \(R_N\) for the actual CMF approximants remains to be derived.
