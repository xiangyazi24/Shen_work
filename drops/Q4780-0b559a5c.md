# Problem 3.1 audit: exact \(7_2\) shape data and Bloch/Rogers certificate

## Executive verdict

There is a fundamental mismatch in the question’s premise.

The current Ramanujan Challenge Problem 3.1 is **not** a recurrence problem asserting

\[
\operatorname{Vol}(S^3\setminus 7_2)=\sum_{n\ge0}q_n.
\]

It asks for a proof of the real \(A\)-polynomial path integral

\[
\boxed{
\int_{\alpha}^{\beta}
\left(\log x\,\frac{dy}{y}-\log y\,\frac{dx}{x}\right)
=\frac{4\pi^2}{85}.
}
\tag{0.1}
\]

The repository statement and its numerical verifier contain no sequence \(q_n\), no recurrence, no generating function, and no Picard–Fuchs operator. The complete hyperbolic volume is instead

\[
\operatorname{Vol}(S^3\setminus7_2)
=3.33174423\ldots,
\]

whereas

\[
\frac{4\pi^2}{85}=0.4644519718159698\ldots.
\]

These are different regulators:

- the complete hyperbolic volume is the **Bloch–Wigner/Borel regulator** of the complete geometric representation;
- (0.1) is a **relative Rogers–Chern–Simons/Godbillon–Vey regulator** along a positive real branch of the \(A\)-polynomial curve.

Nevertheless, the complete \(7_2\) shape data can be given exactly, and they yield a particularly clean Bloch-group certificate:

\[
\boxed{
\beta_{7_2}=[t]+3[u]\in\mathcal B(K),
\qquad
K=\mathbb Q(u),
}
\tag{0.2}
\]

where

\[
\boxed{
u^5-4u^4+10u^3-13u^2+10u-3=0}
\tag{0.3}
\]

and the geometric embedding is the one described below. Its regulator is

\[
\boxed{
D(t)+3D(u)=\operatorname{Vol}(S^3\setminus7_2).
}
\tag{0.4}
\]

The exact cancellation proving that (0.2) is a Bloch class is

\[
\boxed{
 t\wedge(1-t)+3u\wedge(1-u)=0.
}
\tag{0.5}
\]

No WZ certificate or Picard–Fuchs operator for a Problem-3.1 sequence is currently available, because no such sequence is part of the stated problem. A specific operator can only be computed after the alleged recurrence is supplied.

# 2. Which four-tetrahedron triangulation?

The word “canonical” has a technical meaning in hyperbolic geometry, usually referring to the Epstein–Penner canonical cell decomposition. Shape coordinates also depend on vertex ordering.

The exact data below refer to the explicit geometric triangulation \(X_5\) constructed by Fathi Ben Aribi, François Guéritaud, and Eiichi Piguet-Nakazawa. Their notation \(K_n\) denotes the twist knot with \(n\) half-twists and \(n+2\) crossings. Their table identifies

\[
K_5=7_2
\]

and gives its volume as \(3.33174423\ldots\). For odd \(n\), their triangulation has

\[
p=\frac{n-3}{2}
\]

and \(p+3\) tetrahedra. Hence \(n=5\) gives \(p=1\) and exactly four tetrahedra

\[
T_1,\quad U,\quad V,\quad W.
\]

This triangulation is proved geometric. If one instead starts with a SnapPy canonical triangulation, the individual shape list may be related by vertex permutations and Pachner moves. The resulting Bloch class and volume are unchanged.

For any shape \(z\), use the standard cyclic parameters

\[
z'=\frac1{1-z},
\qquad
z''=\frac{z-1}{z}.
\tag{2.1}
\]

Write

\[
t=z_{T_1},\qquad u=z_U,\qquad v=z_V,\qquad w=z_W.
\]

# 3. Exact complete gluing equations

Specializing the complete gluing system of \(X_n\) to \(p=1\) gives

\[
\boxed{
 t't^2u^2=1,
}
\tag{3.1}
\]

\[
\boxed{
 \frac{t'(u'')^2}{w}=1,
}
\tag{3.2}
\]

\[
\boxed{
 \frac{w''}{u}=1,
}
\tag{3.3}
\]

\[
\boxed{v=u.}
\tag{3.4}
\]

The logarithmic versions, with the geometric branches, are

\[
\Log t'+2\Log t+2\Log u=2\pi i,
\]

\[
\Log t'+2\Log u''-\Log w=0,
\]

\[
\Log w''-\Log u=0,
\qquad
\Log v-\Log u=0.
\]

Equation (3.3) gives

\[
\frac{w-1}{w}=u,
\qquad
\boxed{w=\frac1{1-u}.}
\tag{3.5}
\]

Using (3.5), equation (3.2) becomes

\[
\boxed{
1-t=\frac{(1-u)^3}{u^2}.
}
\tag{3.6}
\]

Equation (3.1) becomes

\[
\boxed{1-t=t^2u^2.}
\tag{3.7}
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{
 t=1-\frac{(1-u)^3}{u^2}
   =\frac{u^3-2u^2+3u-1}{u^2},
}
\tag{3.8}
\]

and, by equating (3.6) and (3.7),

\[
\boxed{t^2u^4=(1-u)^3.}
\tag{3.9}
\]

Substituting (3.8) into (3.7) gives

\[
\bigl(u^3-2u^2+3u-1\bigr)^2=(1-u)^3.
\]

The left side minus the right side factors as

\[
u\bigl(u^5-4u^4+10u^3-13u^2+10u-3\bigr).
\]

The root \(u=0\) is degenerate and is discarded. Thus the complete shape field is generated by a root of the quintic (0.3).

# 4. The exact shape parameters and their minimal polynomials

Let \(u_0\) be the unique root of

\[
f_U(X)=X^5-4X^4+10X^3-13X^2+10X-3
\tag{4.1}
\]

for which the four ordered shapes below all lie in the upper half-plane. Then

\[
\boxed{
 z_1=t_0=\frac{u_0^3-2u_0^2+3u_0-1}{u_0^2},
}
\tag{4.2}
\]

\[
\boxed{z_2=z_U=u_0,}
\tag{4.3}
\]

\[
\boxed{z_3=z_V=u_0,}
\tag{4.4}
\]

\[
\boxed{
 z_4=z_W=w_0=\frac1{1-u_0}.
}
\tag{4.5}
\]

For orientation and root selection, numerical approximations are

\[
\boxed{
 u_0\approx
 0.9418186226+1.6912791495\,i,
}
\tag{4.6}
\]

\[
\boxed{
 t_0\approx
 -0.1636911715+0.5641856323\,i,
}
\tag{4.7}
\]

\[
\boxed{
 w_0\approx
 0.02031607286+0.5905695598\,i.
}
\tag{4.8}
\]

The approximations are only selectors; equations (4.1)–(4.5) are the exact data.

## 4.1 Minimal polynomial of \(u_0\)

The polynomial \(f_U\) is irreducible over \(\mathbb Q\). A compact certificate is reduction modulo \(2\):

\[
\overline f_U(X)=X^5+X^2+1.
\]

It has no root in \(\mathbb F_2\). It is not divisible by the only irreducible quadratic \(X^2+X+1\), and reduction modulo the two irreducible cubics gives nonzero remainders:

\[
\overline f_U\bmod(X^3+X+1)=X,
\]

\[
\overline f_U\bmod(X^3+X^2+1)=X^2+X.
\]

A reducible quintic over \(\mathbb F_2\) would have a factor of degree \(1\) or a factorization of degrees \(2+3\). Hence \(\overline f_U\), and therefore \(f_U\), is irreducible.

Thus

\[
K=\mathbb Q(u_0)
\]

is a quintic field.

## 4.2 Minimal polynomial of \(w_0\)

Since \(u=1-1/w\), substitution into (4.1) and multiplication by \(w^5\) gives

\[
\boxed{
 f_W(W)=W^5-3W^4+3W^3-4W^2+W-1.
}
\tag{4.9}
\]

The transformation \(u=1-1/w\) is invertible, so

\[
\mathbb Q(w_0)=\mathbb Q(u_0)
\]

and (4.9) is irreducible and is the minimal polynomial of \(w_0\).

## 4.3 Minimal polynomial of \(t_0\)

Equations (3.6)–(3.8) imply the rational inverse

\[
\boxed{
 u=\frac{2-t}{3t^2-t+1}.
}
\tag{4.10}
\]

They also give

\[
 u^2=\frac{1-t}{t^2}.
\]

Substitution of (4.10) yields

\[
 t^2(2-t)^2-(1-t)(3t^2-t+1)^2=0,
\]

which expands to

\[
\boxed{
 f_T(T)=9T^5-14T^4+9T^3-5T^2+3T-1.
}
\tag{4.11}
\]

Because (4.10) recovers \(u_0\) rationally from \(t_0\),

\[
\mathbb Q(t_0)=\mathbb Q(u_0),
\]

so the primitive polynomial (4.11) is irreducible and is the minimal polynomial of \(t_0\) over \(\mathbb Q\) up to multiplication by a nonzero rational number.

Thus all four shapes lie in the same quintic field, and each of \(t_0,u_0,w_0\) generates it.

# 5. Exact Bloch–Wigner volume formula

For an oriented ideal tetrahedron of shape \(z\in\mathbb H\), its volume is

\[
D(z)=\operatorname{Im}\operatorname{Li}_2(z)
    +\arg(1-z)\log|z|.
\]

All four tetrahedra of the geometric triangulation have positive orientation. Therefore

\[
\boxed{
\operatorname{Vol}(S^3\setminus7_2)
=D(t_0)+D(u_0)+D(v_0)+D(w_0).
}
\tag{5.1}
\]

Since \(v_0=u_0\),

\[
\operatorname{Vol}(S^3\setminus7_2)
=D(t_0)+2D(u_0)+D(w_0).
\tag{5.2}
\]

Moreover

\[
w_0''=\frac{w_0-1}{w_0}=u_0,
\]

and the Bloch–Wigner function is invariant under the orientation-preserving cyclic shape changes

\[
D(z)=D\!\left(\frac1{1-z}\right)
    =D\!\left(\frac{z-1}{z}\right).
\]

Hence

\[
\boxed{
\operatorname{Vol}(S^3\setminus7_2)
=D(t_0)+3D(u_0)
=3.33174423\ldots.
}
\tag{5.3}
\]

This is the requested finite algebraic dilogarithm expression for the complete volume.

It does **not** equal the Problem-3.1 integral (0.1).

# 6. The five-term relation and the exact Bloch certificate

The pre-Bloch group \(\mathcal P(K)\) is generated by symbols \([z]\), \(z\in K\setminus\{0,1\}\), modulo the five-term relation

\[
\boxed{
[x]-[y]
+\left[\frac yx\right]
-\left[\frac{1-x^{-1}}{1-y^{-1}}\right]
+\left[\frac{1-x}{1-y}\right]=0.
}
\tag{6.1}
\]

The Bloch group is the kernel of the boundary map

\[
\partial:\mathcal P(K)\longrightarrow
\bigwedge
olimits^2K^\times,
\qquad
[z]\longmapsto 2z\wedge(1-z).
\tag{6.2}
\]

For tetrahedron \(W\), use the cyclic shape coordinate \(w''=u\). Thus the four-tetrahedron Bloch element may be represented as

\[
\boxed{
\beta_{7_2}=[t]+3[u].
}
\tag{6.3}
\]

Now compute its boundary. Ignoring the harmless common factor \(2\) in (6.2), equation (3.7) gives

\[
\begin{aligned}
 t\wedge(1-t)
 &=t\wedge(t^2u^2)\\
 &=2t\wedge u.
\end{aligned}
\tag{6.4}
\]

On the other hand, wedge equation (3.9) with \(u\):

\[
(t^2u^4)\wedge u=(1-u)^3\wedge u.
\]

This simplifies to

\[
2t\wedge u
=3(1-u)\wedge u
=-3u\wedge(1-u).
\tag{6.5}
\]

Combining (6.4) and (6.5),

\[
\boxed{
 t\wedge(1-t)+3u\wedge(1-u)=0.
}
\tag{6.6}
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{
\partial\beta_{7_2}=0,
\qquad
\beta_{7_2}\in\mathcal B(K).
}
\tag{6.7}
\]

This is the exact algebraic \(K\)-theoretic certificate behind the volume formula.

## What the five-term relation does—and does not—say

There is no five-term calculation that should reduce \(eta_{7_2}\) to zero. If it did, its Bloch–Wigner regulator would vanish, contradicting the positive volume.

Rather:

1. the five-term relation is the defining relation of \(\mathcal P(K)\);
2. Pachner \(2\leftrightarrow3\) changes of triangulation alter the shape presentation by five-term relations;
3. equation (6.7) says that the resulting nonzero class lies in the Bloch group;
4. applying the Bloch–Wigner regulator gives (5.3).

Thus (6.6), not a fictitious vanishing five-term sum, is the finite exact check required for the four algebraic arguments.

# 7. Rogers dilogarithm formulation

Define, on chosen logarithm branches,

\[
L(z)=\operatorname{Li}_2(z)
+\frac12\Log z\,\Log(1-z).
\tag{7.1}
\]

For complex \(z\), the imaginary part of the ordinary Rogers function is not exactly the Bloch–Wigner function. If

\[
\theta=\Arg z,\qquad
\phi=\Arg(1-z),
\]

then

\[
\boxed{
D(z)=\operatorname{Im}L(z)
+\frac12\left(
\log|z|\,\phi-\log|1-z|\,\theta
\right).
}
\tag{7.2}
\]

Consequently an exact ordinary-Rogers expression is

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\operatorname{Vol}(S^3\setminus7_2)
={}&\operatorname{Im}\bigl(L(t_0)+3L(u_0)\bigr)\\
&+\frac12\Bigl(
\log|t_0|\Arg(1-t_0)
-\log|1-t_0|\Arg(t_0)\\
&\hspace{16mm}
+3\log|u_0|\Arg(1-u_0)
-3\log|1-u_0|\Arg(u_0)
\Bigr).
\end{aligned}
}
\tag{7.3}
\]

Every dilogarithm argument in (7.3) is algebraic and is specified by (4.1)–(4.2).

## 7.1 The correct global object: the extended Rogers dilogarithm

Ordinary \(L(z)\) is multivalued. To recover the complete complex volume—including Chern–Simons data—one chooses integer flattenings \((p,q)\) and uses Neumann’s extended Rogers function

\[
\boxed{
\widehat L(z;p,q)
=\operatorname{Li}_2(z)
+\frac12\Log z\,\Log(1-z)
+\frac{\pi i}{2}
 \bigl(p\Log(1-z)+q\Log z\bigr)
-\frac{\pi^2}{6},
}
\tag{7.4}
\]

with conventions adjusted, if necessary, to the chosen sign for the second log-parameter.

A strong flattening of the triangulation gives an extended Bloch class

\[
\widehat\beta_{7_2}\in\widehat{\mathcal B}(\mathbb C)
\]

such that

\[
\boxed{
\widehat L(\widehat\beta_{7_2})
\equiv i\bigl(\operatorname{Vol}(7_2)+i\operatorname{CS}(7_2)\bigr)
\pmod{\pi^2\mathbb Z}.
}
\tag{7.5}
\]

Its imaginary part is the volume. The lifted five-term relation is exactly the branch-corrected functional equation satisfied by (7.4).

The shape parameters alone do not determine the integer flattenings: they also depend on face pairings, edge log-branches, and the peripheral normalization. Therefore a request for a unique sum of **principal-branch** Rogers values is underdetermined. Equations (7.3) and (7.5) state the branch-independent content precisely.

# 8. Why this does not prove the Challenge integral

The complete Bloch class is attached to one point of the deformation variety: the complete hyperbolic representation. Problem 3.1 integrates along a real arc of the \(A\)-polynomial curve between two incomplete/filling representations.

For nonzero functions \(x,y\), the real Bloch–Wigner regulator form is

\[
\eta(x,y)=\log|x|\,d\arg y-\log|y|\,d\arg x.
\tag{8.1}
\]

Along the challenge branch, \(x>0\) and \(y>0\), so

\[
d\arg x=d\arg y=0,
\qquad
\eta(x,y)=0.
\tag{8.2}
\]

Thus the Bloch–Wigner volume regulator does not vary along this branch. By contrast, the challenge form

\[
\omega=\log x\,d\log y-\log y\,d\log x
\tag{8.3}
\]

can be nonzero. It is the holomorphic/extended regulator component related to Chern–Simons and Godbillon–Vey invariants.

Vu The Khoi’s 2008 paper explicitly distinguishes these phenomena. It explains that the relevant real \(A\)-polynomial integrals are computed from differences of Godbillon–Vey invariants of endpoint filling representations. It also remarks that, for the higher-genus \(A\)-polynomial curves considered there, no direct dilogarithmic primitive was known.

Therefore the four complete shapes are important geometric data, but they are not by themselves a proof that (0.1) equals \(4\pi^2/85\).

# 9. Question (3): there is no supplied Picard–Fuchs operator

A Picard–Fuchs operator can be attached only after specifying an algebraic family and a period or normal function. The current Problem 3.1 supplies an \(A\)-polynomial path integral; it does not supply a sequence \(q_n\) or a power series

\[
Q(z)=\sum_{n\ge0}q_nz^n.
\]

Accordingly, the following requested data are undefined:

- the order and coefficients of the alleged recurrence;
- the differential operator annihilating \(Q\);
- its singular points;
- its local exponents and monodromy;
- a distinguished singular point whose connection constant equals the volume.

The geometric equations (3.1)–(3.4) are critical-point equations for a dilogarithmic potential/state integral. They are not a Picard–Fuchs equation for a one-variable generating function.

## 9.1 How the operator would be computed if a recurrence were supplied

Suppose a different source gives an explicit recurrence

\[
\sum_{j=0}^{r}p_j(n)q_{n+j}=0
\qquad(n\ge0),
\tag{9.1}
\]

with \(p_j\in\mathbb Q[n]\). Put

\[
Q(z)=\sum_{n\ge0}q_nz^n,
\qquad
\theta=z\frac{d}{dz}.
\]

Multiplying (9.1) by \(z^{n+r}\) and summing gives

\[
\boxed{
\left(
\sum_{j=0}^{r}z^{r-j}p_j(\theta-j)
\right)Q(z)=P_{\mathrm{init}}(z),
}
\tag{9.2}
\]

where \(P_{\mathrm{init}}\) is an explicitly computable polynomial determined by \(q_0,\ldots,q_{r-1}\). One then homogenizes (9.2), factors the resulting Ore operator, finds its singularities from the leading differential coefficient, and computes local exponents from the indicial polynomials.

Without the \(p_j(n)\) and initial data, none of this can be specialized. The four shape polynomials do not determine such an operator uniquely.

## 9.2 A regulator normal function is not an ordinary period

Even after choosing a one-parameter deformation of shapes, the dilogarithm normally defines a **normal function**. Such a function often satisfies an inhomogeneous Picard–Fuchs equation, not the homogeneous Picard–Fuchs equation of a holomorphic period. To identify a connection constant with \(\operatorname{Vol}(7_2)\), one would need all of the following:

1. an explicit algebraic family over a parameter \(z\);
2. a cycle or higher-Chow class;
3. a differential equation for its regulator normal function;
4. a normalization at a base point;
5. an analytic-continuation path to the complete geometric point.

None of these data is encoded merely by saying that the complete triangulation has four tetrahedra.

# 10. Question (4): WZ and creative telescoping

A Wilf–Zeilberger certificate starts from a specified hypergeometric summand \(F(n,k)\) and proves an identity such as

\[
F(n+1,k)-F(n,k)
=G(n,k+1)-G(n,k).
\tag{10.1}
\]

No such summand appears in the statement of Problem 3.1. I found no published WZ certificate proving either

\[
\sum q_n=\operatorname{Vol}(7_2)
\]

or the actual integral identity (0.1).

The closest rigorous result of this type for the complete volume is structurally different. Ben Aribi–Guéritaud–Piguet-Nakazawa write the Teichmüller-TQFT invariant of every twist knot as a quantum-dilogarithm state integral and prove that its semiclassical exponential rate is the hyperbolic volume. Their proof uses:

- the geometric ideal triangulation;
- a classical dilogarithmic potential;
- uniqueness and nondegeneracy of the complete critical point;
- the saddle-point method;
- uniform estimates for the quantum dilogarithm.

It is not a rational WZ summation proof.

Likewise, \(q\)-holonomic recurrences for colored Jones polynomials or state integrals are conceptually different from a rational hypergeometric WZ pair. Their classical characteristic varieties are related to \(A\)-polynomials, but this does not create the missing \(q_n\) sequence in the challenge statement.

# 11. The proof route that actually matches Problem 3.1

The most direct proof architecture for (0.1) is the one suggested by Khoi’s work.

## Certificate A: representation path

Prove that the selected real \(A\)-polynomial branch lifts to a continuous path

\[
\rho_s:\pi_1(S^3\setminus7_2)
\longrightarrow\widetilde{\mathrm{SL}}_2(\mathbb R)
\]

with peripheral eigenvalues matching the exact \(x(s),y(s)\) convention used by the challenge.

## Certificate B: endpoint fillings

Identify the two endpoint representations as representations of specified Dehn fillings. This requires exact algebraic intersection certificates for

\[
y^2=x
\quad\text{and}\quad
y=x
\]

in the challenge’s peripheral normalization.

## Certificate C: Godbillon–Vey/Seifert volumes

Compute the Godbillon–Vey invariants of the two filled representations. For Seifert-fibered fillings these are rational multiples of \(\pi^2\).

## Certificate D: variation formula

Apply the Schläfli/Khoi variation identity. Depending on the meridian/longitude and orientation conventions, it has the form

\[
GV(\rho_1)-GV(\rho_0)
=4\int
\left(
\log y\,\frac{dx}{x}
-\log x\,\frac{dy}{y}
\right).
\tag{11.1}
\]

After matching signs and squared-eigenvalue conventions, the endpoint difference must become the required multiple of \(\pi^2\), proving (0.1).

## Equivalent extended-Bloch formulation

Instead of computing Godbillon–Vey invariants directly, one may construct shape functions on the deformation curve, choose compatible flattenings, and prove a Neumann–Zagier identity of the form

\[
N\,x\wedge y
=\sum_j\varepsilon_jz_j\wedge(1-z_j)
\tag{11.2}
\]

in the function field of the curve. Integrating the corresponding Rogers differential reduces (0.1) to an extended Rogers value at the two endpoint shape systems. The final step is then a finite lifted-five-term/flattening calculation showing that their difference is \(4\pi^2/85\).

This is the correct place where the denominator \(85\) should appear. It is a relative Chern–Simons/Godbillon–Vey denominator, not a denominator extracted from the complete volume \(3.33174423\ldots\).

# 12. Exact verification snippet

The following SageMath fragment verifies the field relations and minimal polynomials without numerical recognition:

```python
R.<X> = PolynomialRing(QQ)
fU = X^5 - 4*X^4 + 10*X^3 - 13*X^2 + 10*X - 3
K.<u> = NumberField(fU)

t = (u^3 - 2*u^2 + 3*u - 1) / u^2
v = u
w = 1 / (1-u)

assert 1-t == t^2*u^2
assert 1-t == (1-u)^3/u^2
assert (w-1)/w == u

fW = X^5 - 3*X^4 + 3*X^3 - 4*X^2 + X - 1
fT = 9*X^5 - 14*X^4 + 9*X^3 - 5*X^2 + 3*X - 1

assert fW(w) == 0
assert fT(t) == 0
assert (2-t)/(3*t^2-t+1) == u
```

The wedge cancellation (6.6) is then a formal consequence of

```plain
1 - t = t^2 u^2,
t^2 u^4 = (1-u)^3.
```

# Final conclusions

- The exact four shapes for the explicit geometric \(X_5\) triangulation are given by (4.2)–(4.5).
- Their minimal polynomials are (4.1), (4.9), and (4.11).
- The complete volume is
  
  \[
  \operatorname{Vol}(7_2)=D(t_0)+3D(u_0).
  \]
- The exact Bloch certificate is
  
  \[
  \partial([t_0]+3[u_0])=0.
  \]
- The ordinary five-term relation controls changes of triangulation; it does not annihilate this nonzero volume class.
- A global Rogers formula requires flattenings and lives naturally in the extended Bloch group.
- The current Problem 3.1 has no \(q_n\), recurrence, Picard–Fuchs operator, or WZ summand. Its correct proof target is the relative Chern–Simons/Godbillon–Vey regulator (0.1), not the complete Bloch–Wigner volume.
