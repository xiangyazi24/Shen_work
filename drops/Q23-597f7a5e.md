# Two-dimensional Apéry-state near-injectivity: what it proves, the exact algebraic return condition, and why a low-degree curve is unlikely

## Executive verdict

The new computation is genuinely valuable, but its main consequence is more subtle than “the state orbit lies on a low-degree curve.”

Let

\[
s_n=(b_n,b_{n+1})\in\mathbf F_p^2,
\qquad 0\le n\le p-2,
\]

and let

\[
Q_2(p):=\#\{(n,m):0\le n,m\le p-2,\ s_n=s_m\}
\]

be the **ordered** state-collision energy.  Your observation says

\[
Q_2(p)=p-1+O(1)
\]

through the tested range.

The correct conclusions are:

1. **Near-injectivity of the state map is a strong pseudorandomness diagnostic, but it does not imply the desired one-coordinate energy bound.**  A set of \(p\) distinct points in \(\mathbf F_p^2\) can lie on one vertical line, in which case the state map is injective but the first-coordinate energy is \(p^2\).

2. There is, however, an exact finite-geometric identity showing what the discovery buys.  For every projective direction \([u:v]\in\mathbf P^1(\mathbf F_p)\), define
   \[
   E_{u,v}(p)
   :=\#\{(n,m):u b_n+v b_{n+1}=u b_m+v b_{m+1}\}.
   \]
   If \(M=p-1\), then
   \[
   \boxed{
   \sum_{[u:v]\in\mathbf P^1(\mathbf F_p)}E_{u,v}(p)
   =M^2+pQ_2(p).
   }
   \tag{0.1}
   \]
   Hence \(Q_2(p)=M+O(1)\) implies that the **average projection energy** is \(2p+O(1)\).  Most linear projections of the state orbit therefore have the desired \(O(p)\) energy.  The remaining issue is to prove that the fixed horizontal projection \([1:0]\), which gives the Apéry values, is not exceptional.

3. Palindromy explains the dominant structured state collisions exactly:
   \[
   \boxed{s_{p-2-n}=(b_{n+1},b_n).}
   \tag{0.2}
   \]
   Thus a reflected pair \(n+m=p-2\) is a state collision precisely when
   \[
   b_n=b_{n+1}.
   \]
   The \(p=131\) fourfold state is two reflected diagonal pairs,
   \[
   10+119=53+76=129=p-2,
   \]
   whose common diagonal value happens to agree.

4. A pointwise absolute bound \(Q_2(p)-(p-1)=O(1)\) is not the most natural conjecture.  A random set of \(p\) states in \(p^2\) positions has a Poisson-scale number of collisions, hence bounded mean but unbounded support.  The palindromic model adds another constant-scale compound-Poisson contribution.  The realistic conjectures are bounded mean over primes, a limiting distribution, or \(p^{o(1)}\) pointwise—not necessarily one universal numerical ceiling.

5. There is a concrete **fixed-gap return polynomial**.  For every fixed gap \(h\), a collision \(s_{n+h}=s_n\) forces \(n\) to be a root of a nonzero polynomial of degree at most \(3h\).  This is the state-return analogue of the gap continuant.  It can control a fixed \(h\), but summing over all \(h<p\) does not give an \(O(1)\) total bound.

6. The recurrence hypersurface is real but too large.  It is a three-dimensional phase-space hypersurface containing every local solution of the recurrence, not a curve isolating the Apéry orbit.  A bounded-degree **surface** in \(\mathbf A^3\) would not bound horizontal fibers anyway.

7. More strongly, for every fixed \(D\), all sufficiently large primes \(p\) admit **no nonzero plane polynomial of degree at most \(D\)** vanishing on every graph point \((n,b_n)\), \(0\le n<p\).  Consequently the state set cannot lie, for infinitely many \(p\), on a bounded-degree curve in \(\mathbf A^3\): projecting such a curve to \((n,b_n)\) would produce a bounded-degree plane relation.

8. The best general algebraic formula for \(b_n\bmod p\) is not a bounded-degree function of \(n\).  It is the character-aspect Mellin identity
   \[
   \boxed{
   -b_n=\sum_{t\in\mathbf F_p^\times}A_p(t)t^{-n},
   \qquad
   A_p(t)=\sum_{r=0}^{p-1}b_rt^r.
   }
   \tag{0.3}
   \]
   Thus \(n\) is a Kummer-character parameter.  Special bounded-order characters, such as \(n=(p-1)/2\), have modular or geometric formulas; a general \(n\) has character order growing with \(p\), and no fixed low-genus point-count formula is known.

The most promising use of the discovery is therefore not to search for one low-degree surface.  It is to search for either:

- a large symmetry group of the state set that moves the horizontal direction through \(\gg p\) projective directions, allowing (0.1) to control it;
- uniform cancellation in the two-variable sums
  \[
  \sum_{n=0}^{p-2}e_p(u b_n+v b_{n+1});
  \]
- or a three-equal-values determinant, which attacks the one-coordinate energy directly.

# 1. Indexing correction for the transfer matrix

Write

\[
P(n)=(2n+1)(17n^2+17n+5)
=34n^3+51n^2+27n+5.
\]

The recurrence is

\[
(n+1)^3b_{n+1}=P(n)b_n-n^3b_{n-1}.
\tag{1.1}
\]

The matrix displayed in the question,

\[
M_n=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&1\\
-\dfrac{n^3}{(n+1)^3}&\dfrac{P(n)}{(n+1)^3}
\end{pmatrix},
\tag{1.2}
\]

maps

\[
(b_{n-1},b_n)^T\longmapsto(b_n,b_{n+1})^T.
\]

For the state convention

\[
s_n=(b_n,b_{n+1})^T,
\]

the correct transition is shifted by one:

\[
\boxed{
 s_{n+1}=A_n s_n,
 \qquad
 A_n=
 \begin{pmatrix}
 0&1\\
 -\dfrac{(n+1)^3}{(n+2)^3}&
 \dfrac{P(n+1)}{(n+2)^3}
 \end{pmatrix}.
}
\tag{1.3}
\]

For \(0\le n\le p-3\), the denominators in (1.3) are nonzero modulo \(p\).

The determinant telescopes:

\[
\det A_n=\left(\frac{n+1}{n+2}\right)^3.
\tag{1.4}
\]

This determinant identity is the starting point for an exact return polynomial.

# 2. The exact meaning of two-dimensional near-injectivity

Let

\[
\mu_p(x,y)
=\#\{0\le n\le p-2:s_n=(x,y)\}.
\]

Then

\[
Q_2(p)=\sum_{(x,y)\in\mathbf F_p^2}\mu_p(x,y)^2.
\tag{2.1}
\]

If \(C_2(p)\) denotes the number of unordered pairs \(n<m\) with \(s_n=s_m\), then

\[
Q_2(p)=p-1+2C_2(p).
\tag{2.2}
\]

Your computation is therefore the statement

\[
Q_2(p)=p+O(1).
\tag{2.3}
\]

This is the minimum possible scale up to constants: the diagonal \(n=m\) already contributes \(p-1\).

## 2.1 Why it does not imply the one-dimensional energy bound

For \(a\in\mathbf F_p\), put

\[
N_p^*(a)=\#\{0\le n\le p-2:b_n=a\}.
\]

The truncated value energy is

\[
E^*(p)=\sum_aN_p^*(a)^2.
\tag{2.4}
\]

The desired full energy differs only by the endpoint \(b_{p-1}=b_0=1\):

\[
E(p)=E^*(p)+2N_p^*(1)+1,
\]

so \(E^*(p)=O(p)\) is equivalent, at the required scale, to \(E(p)=O(p)\).

Near-injectivity says that for fixed \(a\), the pairs

\[
(a,b_{n+1})
\]

are mostly distinct.  It does not bound how many distinct values of \(b_{n+1}\) can occur over the same \(a\).

The sharp counterexample is

\[
S=\{(0,y):y\in\mathbf F_p\}.
\]

This set is perfectly injective as a set of two-dimensional states, but its first coordinate is constant, so

\[
E_x(S)=p^2.
\]

Thus the new observation removes state multiplicity but not **vertical concentration**.

# 3. Exact average-over-directions identity

This is the strongest unconditional consequence of the state computation.

For a projective direction

\[
\ell=[u:v]\in\mathbf P^1(\mathbf F_p),
\]

define

\[
L_\ell(x,y)=ux+vy
\]

and the projection energy

\[
E_\ell
=\#\{(n,m):L_\ell(s_n)=L_\ell(s_m)\}.
\tag{3.1}
\]

The horizontal direction is

\[
E_{[1:0]}=E^*(p).
\]

## Proposition 3.1 — projection-energy identity

Let \(M=p-1\). Then

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{\ell\in\mathbf P^1(\mathbf F_p)}E_\ell
=M^2+pQ_2(p).
}
\tag{3.2}
\]

### Proof

Sum over ordered pairs \((n,m)\).

- If \(s_n=s_m\), then every one of the \(p+1\) projective linear forms has equal values.
- If \(s_n\ne s_m\), then there is exactly one projective direction \([u:v]\) perpendicular to \(s_n-s_m\), hence exactly one direction for which the projections agree.

There are \(Q_2(p)\) equal-state ordered pairs and \(M^2-Q_2(p)\) unequal-state ordered pairs. Therefore

\[
\sum_\ell E_\ell
=(p+1)Q_2(p)+(M^2-Q_2(p))
=M^2+pQ_2(p).
\]

∎

## Corollary 3.2

If

\[
Q_2(p)=p-1+O(1),
\]

then

\[
\frac1{p+1}\sum_{\ell\in\mathbf P^1(\mathbf F_p)}E_\ell
=2p+O(1).
\tag{3.3}
\]

Thus the target \(O(p)\) energy holds for a typical linear projection of the state set.

This puts the obstacle in an exceptionally clean form:

> **Why is the horizontal direction \([1:0]\) not an exceptional projection?**

Palindromy gives only the coordinate-swap symmetry, so

\[
E_{[1:0]}=E_{[0:1]}.
\]

It does not move the horizontal direction through a positive proportion of all \(p+1\) directions.

A genuinely decisive hidden structure would be a group \(G_p\le\operatorname{GL}_2(\mathbf F_p)\) preserving the state multiset and having an orbit of size \(\gg p\) on the dual projective line containing \([1:0]\).  Energies would be equal on that orbit, and (3.2) would immediately give

\[
E(p)=O(p).
\]

At present the only evident linear symmetry is the swap involution coming from reflection.

# 4. Palindromy and the structured state collisions

For \(0\le n\le p-2\), the Apéry palindrome is

\[
b_{p-1-j}=b_j.
\tag{4.1}
\]

Set

\[
m=p-2-n.
\]

Then

\[
b_m=b_{p-2-n}=b_{n+1}
\]

and

\[
b_{m+1}=b_{p-1-n}=b_n.
\]

Hence

\[
\boxed{
 s_{p-2-n}=J s_n,
 \qquad
 J=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}.
}
\tag{4.2}
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{
 s_{p-2-n}=s_n
 \iff
 b_n=b_{n+1}.
}
\tag{4.3}
\]

This exactly explains the observed relation

\[
n+m=p-2
\]

and the diagonal-state condition.

## 4.1 The \(p=131\) fourfold state

The indices split into two reflection pairs:

\[
10\leftrightarrow119,
\qquad
53\leftrightarrow76.
\]

If all four states are \((15,15)\), then the event is not four independent accidents.  It consists of:

1. one diagonal state at \(n=10\), forced to repeat at \(119\);
2. another diagonal state at \(53\), forced to repeat at \(76\);
3. one accidental equality between the two diagonal reflection orbits.

A single accidental equality between two reflected diagonal orbits creates a multiplicity-four state and hence six unordered index pairs.  This is exactly the kind of compound-Poisson tail that makes a universal pointwise ceiling doubtful.

# 5. Random benchmark: bounded mean is natural, bounded support is not

Ignore reflection first.  If \(M=p-1\) independent states are sampled uniformly from \(p^2\) possible states, then the expected number of unordered collisions is

\[
\binom{M}{2}\frac1{p^2}
=\frac12+O(p^{-1}).
\tag{5.1}
\]

The collision count has a Poisson-type limiting law, hence constant mean but unbounded support.

In a palindromic state model, the states occur in swap-orbits

\[
(x,y),\ (y,x).
\]

There are two constant-scale sources of collisions:

- a swap-orbit collapses when \(x=y\), giving the reflected diagonal collisions;
- two distinct swap-orbits can coincide.

Both have constant expected total count.  The resulting limit should be a small compound-Poisson law rather than a bounded-support law.

Consequently the data through \(p=1009\) support statements such as

\[
\frac1{\pi(X)}\sum_{p\le X} C_2(p)=O(1)
\]

or convergence in distribution.  They do not yet support the stronger assertion that one absolute \(C\) satisfies

\[
C_2(p)\le C
\]

for every prime.

# 6. A fixed-gap state-return polynomial

The nonautonomous nature of the recurrence does not eliminate algebraic control completely.  It gives a useful polynomial for each fixed return gap.

For \(h\ge1\), define the transfer matrix

\[
U_h(X)=A_{X+h-1}\cdots A_X,
\tag{6.1}
\]

where \(A_X\) is the rational matrix in (1.3).  Put

\[
D_h(X)=\prod_{r=2}^{h+1}(X+r)^3
\]

and

\[
\widetilde U_h(X)=D_h(X)U_h(X)\in M_2(\mathbf Z[X]).
\]

The determinant telescopes:

\[
\det U_h(X)
=\left(\frac{X+1}{X+h+1}\right)^3.
\tag{6.2}
\]

If

\[
s_{n+h}=s_n\ne0,
\]

then \(U_h(n)\) has eigenvalue \(1\).  For a \(2\times2\) matrix this is equivalent to

\[
\operatorname{tr}U_h(n)=1+\det U_h(n).
\]

After clearing denominators, define

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
R_h(X)
:={}&\operatorname{tr}\widetilde U_h(X)
-D_h(X)\\
&-(X+1)^3\prod_{r=2}^{h}(X+r)^3.
\end{aligned}
}
\tag{6.3}
\]

Then every state collision of gap \(h\) satisfies

\[
\boxed{R_h(n)\equiv0\pmod p.}
\tag{6.4}
\]

## 6.1 Degree and nonvanishing over characteristic zero

Every term in (6.3) has degree at most \(3h\), so

\[
\deg R_h\le3h.
\]

In fact the degree is exactly \(3h\).  As \(X\to\infty\),

\[
A_X\longrightarrow
A_\infty=
\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\-1&34\end{pmatrix}.
\]

The leading coefficient of \(R_h\) is therefore

\[
\operatorname{tr}(A_\infty^h)-2.
\]

The eigenvalues of \(A_\infty\) are

\[
17+12\sqrt2,
\qquad
17-12\sqrt2,
\]

so

\[
\operatorname{tr}(A_\infty^h)>2
\]

for every \(h\ge1\).  Thus \(R_h\ne0\) over \(\mathbf Z[X]\).

For a fixed \(h\), and for every prime not dividing the content of \(R_h\), the number of candidate starting indices is at most \(3h\).

## 6.2 First example

For \(h=1\),

\[
R_1(X)
=P(X+1)-(X+2)^3-(X+1)^3
=4(2X+3)^3.
\tag{6.5}
\]

A one-step state return means

\[
s_{n+1}=s_n,
\]

so three consecutive Apéry values are equal.  The only candidate is the central reversal index

\[
2n+3\equiv0\pmod p.
\]

## 6.3 Reflection factor

For a collision of gap \(h\), the reflected relation is

\[
n+(n+h)=p-2,
\]

or

\[
2n+h+2\equiv0\pmod p.
\tag{6.6}
\]

The reversal involution on the two-dimensional solution space forces a fixed line at this central transfer.  Accordingly, the return determinant has a forced central root; for \(h=1\) it appears with multiplicity three in (6.5).  A useful symbolic experiment is to factor

\[
R_h(X)
\]

and remove the factor corresponding to \(2X+h+2\) before studying accidental returns.

## 6.4 Why this does not prove \(O(1)\) total state collisions

The fixed-gap result gives at best

\[
\#\{n:s_{n+h}=s_n\}\ll h.
\]

Summing this absolute bound over \(1\le h<p\) is quadratic.  Unlike the zero-set gap argument, there is no single ordered set of occurrences whose adjacent gaps pack into an interval: different repeated states define different fibers, and their gap intervals overlap freely.

Moreover, \(R_h(n)=0\) is only necessary.  It says that the transfer matrix has some fixed vector; the Apéry state must also lie on that fixed line.

Thus \(R_h\) is an excellent classifier and a possible fixed-\(h\) Chebotarev object, but it is not a proof of a uniform total bound.

# 7. Why the recurrence hypersurface does not isolate the orbit

Introduce coordinates

\[
(N,X_-,X_0,X_+)
\]

corresponding to

\[
(n,b_{n-1},b_n,b_{n+1}).
\]

The recurrence gives the bounded-degree hypersurface

\[
\boxed{
(N+1)^3X_+-P(N)X_0+N^3X_-=0.
}
\tag{7.1}
\]

This is one equation in four variables, hence a three-dimensional variety.

It contains the local data of **every** solution of the second-order recurrence.  Away from \(N=0\), arbitrary values of \(N,X_0,X_+\) determine \(X_-\) from (7.1).  In particular, after projecting to

\[
(N,b_n,b_{n+1}),
\]

the local phase space is generically all of \(\mathbf A^3\).  The recurrence supplies no algebraic equation in those three coordinates alone.

The Apéry orbit is selected by the initial condition at \(n=0\), which is a global iteration condition.  Encoding it at time \(n\) requires the product

\[
A_{n-1}\cdots A_0,
\]

whose complexity grows with \(n\).

## 7.1 A surface is insufficient even if one exists

Suppose \(T_p\subset\mathbf A^3\) lies on a surface of degree \(D\).  Intersecting with the horizontal plane

\[
b_n=a
\]

usually gives a curve, which may contain \(O(p)\) points.  Thus a surface gives no bounded fiber degree.

To deduce \(E(p)=O(p)\), one needs a bounded-degree **curve** \(C_p\) containing \(T_p\), with the projection

\[
(n,x,y)\longmapsto x
\]

having bounded degree and no vertical component.  Then every value \(a\) would have \(O_D(1)\) preimages and

\[
E(p)\ll_Dp.
\]

One recurrence equation does not provide such a curve.

# 8. A no-go theorem for bounded-degree graph curves

There is a rigorous reason not to expect the graph

\[
G_p=\{(n,b_n\bmod p):0\le n<p\}
\]

to lie on a curve of uniformly bounded degree.

## Proposition 8.1

Fix \(D\ge0\).  There is a nonzero integer \(\Delta_D\) such that, for every sufficiently large prime \(p\nmid\Delta_D\), no nonzero polynomial

\[
F_p(X,Y)\in\mathbf F_p[X,Y]
\]

of total degree at most \(D\) vanishes at every point

\[
(n,b_n\bmod p),
\qquad 0\le n<p.
\]

### Proof

The Apéry asymptotic is

\[
b_n\sim C(17+12\sqrt2)^n n^{-3/2}
\]

with \(C>0\).

Consider the finitely many integer sequences

\[
f_{i,j}(n)=n^i b_n^j,
\qquad i+j\le D.
\]

They are linearly independent over \(\mathbf Q\).  Indeed, suppose

\[
\sum_{j=0}^D P_j(n)b_n^j=0
\]

for every large \(n\), with polynomial \(P_j\).  Let \(J\) be maximal with \(P_J\ne0\).  Dividing by \(b_n^J\) gives

\[
P_J(n)
=-\sum_{j<J}P_j(n)b_n^{j-J}.
\]

The right side tends to zero exponentially, while a nonzero polynomial cannot tend to zero along the positive integers.  Hence \(P_J=0\), a contradiction.

Let \(M\) be the number of monomials \(X^iY^j\) with \(i+j\le D\).  Linear independence implies that there exist fixed indices

\[
0\le n_1<\cdots<n_M
\]

for which the evaluation determinant

\[
\Delta_D
=\det\bigl(n_r^i b_{n_r}^j\bigr)_{r,(i,j)}
\]

is a nonzero integer.

If a degree-\(D\) polynomial \(F_p\) vanished on the full graph modulo \(p\), its coefficient vector would lie in the kernel of this fixed \(M\times M\) evaluation matrix modulo \(p\).  For \(p\nmid\Delta_D\), that matrix is invertible.  Therefore all coefficients vanish, contradiction.

∎

## Corollary 8.2 — no bounded-degree state curve

Fix \(D\).  For all but finitely many primes, \(T_p\) cannot lie on an algebraic curve in \(\mathbf A^3\) of degree at most \(D\).

Indeed, the projection of such a curve to the \((n,b_n)\)-plane has one-dimensional Zariski closure of degree bounded in terms of \(D\).  Elimination therefore produces a nonzero plane polynomial of bounded degree vanishing on \(G_p\), contradicting Proposition 8.1.

This does not exclude surfaces.  It shows that the exact geometric object needed for the easy Bézout proof—a bounded-degree curve—is not available uniformly in \(p\).

# 9. What a computational low-degree search would actually measure

A finite set always lies on moderately low-degree hypersurfaces by dimension counting.

## 9.1 Graph in \(\mathbf A^2\)

The space of bivariate polynomials of total degree at most \(d\) has dimension

\[
\binom{d+2}{2}.
\]

Therefore any \(p\) graph points lie on a nonzero plane polynomial once

\[
\binom{d+2}{2}>p,
\]

that is, at the generic interpolation scale

\[
d\asymp\sqrt{2p}.
\]

Finding a relation of degree about \(\sqrt p\) is not evidence of hidden geometry.

## 9.2 States in \(\mathbf A^3\)

The space of trivariate polynomials of degree at most \(d\) has dimension

\[
\binom{d+3}{3}.
\]

Thus any \(p\) state points lie on some surface once

\[
\binom{d+3}{3}>p,
\]

or

\[
d\asymp(6p)^{1/3}.
\]

Again, such a surface exists for a completely random point set.

## 9.3 The right diagnostic is the Hilbert function

For each \(d\), form the evaluation matrix of all monomials of degree at most \(d\) on \(T_p\).  Compare its rank with a random \(p\)-point set.

- Generic points have rank
  \[
  \min\left(p,\binom{d+3}{3}\right).
  \]
- Points on a fixed-degree surface eventually show \(O(d^2)\) Hilbert growth.
- Points on a fixed-degree curve show \(O(d)\) Hilbert growth.

A Gröbner-basis computation should therefore report the full Hilbert function, not merely the first vanishing polynomial.

Proposition 8.1 predicts that the projection to \((n,b_n)\) will show no fixed-degree relation as \(p\) grows.

# 10. Hypergeometric and geometric formulas for \(b_n\bmod p\)

## 10.1 Classical hypergeometric formula

For every integer \(n\ge0\),

\[
\boxed{
 b_n={}_4F_3\!\left(
 \begin{matrix}-n,-n,n+1,n+1\\1,1,1\end{matrix};1
 \right).
}
\tag{10.1}
\]

This is a terminating hypergeometric value.  Modulo \(p\), the parameter \(n\) can be encoded by Teichmüller characters or \(p\)-adic gamma functions, but the character order generally grows with \(p\).

## 10.2 The exact character-aspect formula

Let

\[
A_p(t)=\sum_{r=0}^{p-1}b_rt^r
\]

be the Hasse polynomial of the Apéry K3 pencil.  For \(1\le n\le p-2\), multiplicative orthogonality gives

\[
\boxed{
-b_n=\sum_{t\in\mathbf F_p^\times}A_p(t)t^{-n}.
}
\tag{10.2}
\]

Thus \(b_n\) is the finite-field Mellin transform of the fiberwise Hasse invariant.  If \(\omega\) is the Teichmüller character, \(n\) labels the Kummer twist \(\omega^{-n}\).

After lifting \(A_p(t)\) to the Frobenius trace of the K3 family, (10.2) becomes a Frobenius trace on bounded-dimensional global twisted cohomology

\[
H_c^1\bigl(U,\mathcal V_{\mathrm{tr}}\otimes
\mathcal L_{\omega^{-n}}\bigr),
\]

up to finitely many boundary terms.

This is the correct geometric interpretation for a general index.  It is not a point count of one fiber with parameter \(t=n\).

## 10.3 Special indices

When \(n\) is a fixed rational fraction of \(p-1\), the Kummer character has bounded order.  Such cases can be associated with fixed geometric or modular objects.

The best-known example is

\[
n=\frac{p-1}{2},
\]

where Ahlgren and Ono proved the Beukers supercongruence relating the Apéry number to the \(p\)-th coefficient of the weight-four eta product.  Generalized Apéry sums at other rational fractions of \(p-1\) are expressible through Greene Gaussian hypergeometric functions; see Kalita--Chetry.

These results do not extend to an arbitrary index \(n\) with a bounded-order character.

## 10.4 What Stienstra--Beukers computes

Stienstra and Beukers compute the Picard--Fuchs equation, formal Brauer group, and fiberwise Hasse/unit-root function in the K3 parameter.  The coefficients of the period are the Apéry numbers, and the truncated period is the Hasse invariant.

They do not provide a bounded-degree algebraic function

\[
n\longmapsto b_n\pmod p.
\]

The coefficient index is Fourier-dual to the geometric parameter.

## 10.5 Correction to the “degree at most two” statement

Caruso--Fürnsinn--Vargas-Montoya--Zudilin prove

\[
A_p(t)=B_p(t)^2
\]

or

\[
A_p(t)=(t^2-34t+1)B_p(t)^2,
\]

depending on \(p\bmod24\).

The squarefree defect has degree at most two, but

\[
\deg B_p\asymp p/2.
\]

Thus the Hasse polynomial is not a product of uniformly bounded-degree factors.  The theorem reflects the symmetric-square structure in the **fiber parameter**, not a bounded-degree relation in the coefficient index.

The same paper shows that the reduced generating series is algebraic over \(\mathbf F_p(t)\), with a cyclic Kummer Galois group of order comparable with \(p\).  This is growing, rather than bounded, complexity.

# 11. Can the \(O(1)\) state-collision count be proved?

No current theorem appears to prove it, and the strongest pointwise version is probably not the right target.

The fixed-gap polynomial \(R_h\) gives:

- a degree-\(O(h)\) necessary condition;
- a way to separate the forced central reflection root;
- a possible fixed-\(h\) Chebotarev problem as \(p\) varies.

What it does not give is uniform control while \(h\) ranges through \(1,\ldots,p\).

A plausible rigorous hierarchy is:

1. **Fixed gap.**  For each fixed \(h\), prove that the primitive quotient of \(R_h\) has bounded mean root count over primes and that the Apéry fixed-line condition removes most roots.
2. **Mesoscopic gaps.**  Prove an aggregate estimate for \(h\le p^\theta\).
3. **All gaps.**  Establish bounded average state-collision count over primes, or a compound-Poisson law.

The computation through \(p=1009\) is excellent evidence for the third statement, but not for bounded support.

---

# 12. A more direct route from the discovery to \(E(p)=O(p)\)

The state observation suggests two concrete programs.

## 12.1 Prove that the horizontal projection is generic

Define

\[
S_{u,v}(p)=\sum_{n=0}^{p-2}e_p(u b_n+v b_{n+1}).
\]

Additive Parseval gives

\[
E_{u,v}(p)=\frac1p\sum_{r\in\mathbf F_p}
|S_{ru,rv}(p)|^2.
\tag{12.1}
\]

A uniform estimate

\[
|S_{u,v}(p)|\ll\sqrt p
\qquad((u,v)\ne(0,0))
\tag{12.2}
\]

would prove \(E_{u,v}(p)=O(p)\) for every direction, including the horizontal one.

The state near-injectivity says the two-dimensional map has the correct global collision scale, but (12.2) is still a bounded-conductor trace-function problem for the residual sequence.  No existing K3 sheaf directly realizes

\[
n\longmapsto e_p(u b_n+v b_{n+1})
\]

with bounded conductor.

A weaker symmetry route would also suffice: find many fixed linear transformations preserving the state multiset and moving \([1:0]\) through many directions.  Palindromy supplies only the swap.

## 12.2 Count three equal first coordinates

Let

\[
T_3(p)=\sum_{a\in\mathbf F_p}\binom{N_p(a)}3.
\]

For every integer \(k\ge0\),

\[
k^2\le2k+6\binom{k}{3}.
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{
E(p)\le2p+6T_3(p).
}
\tag{12.3}
\]

It is enough to prove

\[
T_3(p)=O(p).
\]

Three equal values are more compatible with recurrence elimination than two equal values.  If

\[
b_i=b_j=b_k=a,
\]

then shifting \(c_n=b_n-a\) turns the homogeneous Apéry recurrence into an inhomogeneous recurrence whose forcing is linear in \(a\).  Two endpoint equalities leave both the initial slope and \(a\) free; three equalities give enough equations to eliminate them.  This produces an augmented continuant determinant in the two gaps.

The observed near-injectivity of the two-dimensional states removes one possible degeneracy: repeated equal values usually come with distinct next coordinates.  It does not prove the triple bound, but it makes the augmented-determinant program more plausible than a low-degree-state-curve program.

# 13. Recommended computational tests

The next computation should record more than the total state-collision count.

## 13.1 Split collisions by source

For every prime, record:

- reflected diagonal collisions \(n+m=p-2\);
- accidental diagonal-orbit coincidences;
- genuinely non-diagonal accidental collisions;
- state multiplicity histogram.

This will test a compound-Poisson model and clarify the \(p=131\) event.

## 13.2 Fixed-gap return polynomials

For small \(h\):

1. compute \(R_h(X)\) from (6.3);
2. factor out the central reversal factor;
3. compare its roots modulo \(p\) with actual Apéry state returns;
4. compute the Galois group of the primitive quotient.

This identifies how much of near-injectivity comes from the transfer matrix and how much from the special initial state.

## 13.3 Projection-energy spectrum

For every \([u:v]\in\mathbf P^1(\mathbf F_p)\), compute \(E_{u,v}\).  Record:

- the mean, which must satisfy (3.2);
- the rank of the horizontal direction among all directions;
- the maximum energy;
- whether high-energy directions form an orbit under any visible symmetry.

If the horizontal direction is consistently typical after subtracting forced palindrome collisions, that is stronger evidence for a uniform exponential-sum theorem.

## 13.4 Hilbert-function test

For \(T_p\subset\mathbf A^3\), compute the ranks of monomial evaluation matrices for increasing degree \(d\), and compare with

\[
\min\left(p-1,\binom{d+3}{3}\right).
\]

Do not interpret the first vanishing surface at degree \(\asymp p^{1/3}\) as special.  Look for persistent \(O(d)\) or \(O(d^2)\) Hilbert growth well below the generic interpolation threshold.

# 14. Direct answers

## (i) Is the \(O(1)\) two-dimensional collision bound provable?

Not from current Apéry, K3, or recurrence technology.  For each fixed gap there is an explicit degree-\(O(h)\) return polynomial, but no theorem sums these conditions over all \(h<p\).  A bounded-average or compound-Poisson conjecture is more natural than a universal bounded-support conjecture.

## (ii) Can \(T=\{(n,b_n,b_{n+1})\}\) lie on a low-degree algebraic variety?

It lies on many surfaces, including generic interpolation surfaces of degree \(O(p^{1/3})\), and the four-coordinate recurrence data lie on the fixed cubic-in-\(n\), linear-in-state hypersurface (7.1).  None of these surfaces controls horizontal fibers.

A bounded-degree curve would be decisive, but Proposition 8.1 and elimination show that no such curve can contain the full state orbit for all sufficiently large primes.

## (iii) Is there a known algebraic formula for general \(b_n\bmod p\)?

There are three useful formulas:

1. the terminating classical hypergeometric expression (10.1);
2. the Mellin/Kummer formula (10.2), valid for every \(1\le n\le p-2\);
3. special modular and point-count formulas when \(n/(p-1)\) has bounded denominator.

There is no known bounded-degree algebraic function of the integer index \(n\) valid for all \(n<p\).  Stienstra--Beukers works in the K3 parameter, and Ahlgren--Ono-type identities concern special characters or special indices.

## Final assessment

The near-injectivity discovery is not a low-degree-curve theorem, but it is more than a curiosity.  It proves empirically that the Apéry state orbit has essentially minimal two-dimensional collision energy.  Identity (3.2) then shows that the desired one-dimensional energy bound already holds in almost every projection direction.

The remaining challenge is sharply localized:

\[
\boxed{
\text{prove that the horizontal projection is not an exceptional direction.}
}
\]

That can come from a large state-set symmetry, a uniform two-variable exponential-sum estimate, or a direct triple-equal-values determinant.  The recurrence hypersurface alone cannot provide it.
