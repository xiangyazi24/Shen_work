# Problem 2.7: the level-11 modular interpretation, the quadratic trace transform, and the mixed period \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\)

## Executive answer

The level-11 connection is real, but it has to be stated in the correct category.

1. Cooper's numbers \(T_n\) are **not Fourier coefficients** of a level-11 modular form. They are Taylor coefficients of a weight-two modular form in a local modular coordinate:
   \[
   Z_{11}(\tau)=\sum_{n\ge 0}T_nX_{11}(\tau)^n.
   \]
   No published identity presently identifies the Problem 2.7 denominator \(q_n\) as the Fourier coefficients of a fixed holomorphic level-11 form. The natural expectation is instead that its generating function is a rational differential transform of a quadratic pullback-and-trace of \(Z_{11}\).

2. The centered-binomial transform has an exact geometric meaning. It is the **trace along a degree-two covering**, after a Möbius change of Cooper's local coordinate. It is not, on present evidence, an Atkin-Lehner operator, a Rankin-Cohen bracket, or a Shimura lift.

3. The observed factor \(q_n/W_n\sim Cn^{3/2}\) is evidence for a half-integral **local exponent or gauge**, not for a half-integral-weight modular form. A Shimura correspondence would require Hecke relations and a square-index divisor-sum formula, neither of which is implied by this asymptotic.

4. There is an exact level-11 Eisenstein-period formula
   \[
   \boxed{
   \zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
   =L(\mathcal E_3,2)-\frac{11}{1200}L(\mathcal E_4^-,3),
   }
   \]
   where \(\mathcal E_3\) is a weight-three level-11 Eisenstein series with quadratic character and
   \[
   \mathcal E_4^-(\tau)=E_4(\tau)-11^2E_4(11\tau).
   \]
   Thus the target is naturally a sum of an Eisenstein period of weight two and an Eisenstein Eichler period of weight three.

5. Motivically, \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\) is not a homogeneous pure period. It is naturally a period of a mixed object whose graded pieces contain the Tate motive \(\mathbf Q(2)\) and the mixed-Tate extension carrying \(\zeta(3)\). Any genuine level-11 proof must show that the elliptic/cuspidal periods cancel and that only these Eisenstein-Tate pieces survive.

The exact finite step still missing is an operator or creative-telescoping certificate transporting both the homogeneous Cooper period and the required Eisenstein extension to the printed Problem 2.7 recurrence and initial data.

# 1. Cooper's precise level-11 modular object

Put \(q=e^{2\pi i\tau}\). Cooper uses

\[
\Theta_{11}(\tau)
=\sum_{j,k\in\mathbf Z}q^{j^2+jk+3k^2},
\qquad
Z_{11}(\tau)=\Theta_{11}(\tau)^2,
\tag{1.1}
\]

and the local modular coordinate

\[
\boxed{
X_{11}(\tau)
=\frac{\eta(\tau)^2\eta(11\tau)^2}{Z_{11}(\tau)}.
}
\tag{1.2}
\]

The numerator

\[
g_{11}(\tau)=\eta(\tau)^2\eta(11\tau)^2
\tag{1.3}
\]

is the weight-two level-11 cusp form spanning \(S_2(\Gamma_0(11))\). Its Fourier coefficients are the Hecke eigenvalues of the conductor-11 elliptic curve. They are not the numbers \(T_n\).

The Cooper sequence is defined by the local expansion

\[
\boxed{
Z_{11}(\tau)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}T_nX_{11}(\tau)^n,
}
\tag{1.4}
\]

with

\[
T_0,T_1,T_2,T_3,T_4,\ldots
=1,4,28,268,3004,\ldots,
\]

and satisfies

\[
\begin{aligned}
(n+1)^3T_{n+1}={}&
2(2n+1)(5n^2+5n+2)T_n\\
&-8n(7n^2+1)T_{n-1}\\
&+22n(2n-1)(n-1)T_{n-2}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{1.5}
\]

The associated genus-one algebraic data are

\[
B^2=1-20X+56X^2-44X^3,
\qquad
H=4X(1-8X+11X^2).
\tag{1.6}
\]

The leading characteristic polynomial is

\[
C_{11}(t)=t^3-20t^2+56t-44.
\tag{1.7}
\]

This distinction answers an important part of question (a): the modularity of \(T_n\) is modularity of a **Taylor expansion in \(X_{11}\)**, not modularity of the sequence as a \(q\)-expansion.

## 1.1 What can presently be said about \(q_n\)

Let

\[
Q(z)=\sum_{n\ge0}q_nz^n.
\]

No formula is presently certified of the form

\[
Q(e^{2\pi i\tau})=f(\tau),
\qquad f\in M_k(\Gamma_0(11),\chi),
\tag{1.8}
\]

for a fixed holomorphic modular form \(f\). In fact, a fixed holomorphic modular form has Fourier coefficients of polynomial size, whereas a Poincare-recursive sequence such as \(q_n\) has a nontrivial geometric base. That behavior is natural for Taylor coefficients at a finite singularity, not for the ordinary Fourier coefficients at a cusp. A weakly holomorphic form could have exponential Fourier growth, but there is no evidence or Hecke structure identifying \(q_n\) with such a form.

The natural modular ansatz is instead

\[
\boxed{
Q(z)=\mathcal D_z\,\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathbf Q(r)/\mathbf Q(z)}
\left(A(r)Z_{11}(\tau(X_+(r)))\right),
\qquad z=256r^2,
}
\tag{1.9}
\]

where \(A(r)\) is an algebraic/rational gauge and \(\mathcal D_z\) is a rational differential operator, plausibly of order at most two. Formula (1.9) is a precise target, not a proved identity. It becomes a theorem only after an exact differential-operator or Ore-intertwiner calculation and initial-value matching.

# 2. Exact interpretation of the centered-binomial/even transform

Let

\[
T(X)=\sum_{j\ge0}T_jX^j
\]

and define the full centered binomial transform

\[
S_m=\sum_{j=0}^m\binom mj(-2)^{m-j}T_j.
\tag{2.1}
\]

Its ordinary generating function is elementary:

\[
\begin{aligned}
\sum_{m\ge0}S_mr^m
&=\sum_{j\ge0}T_j
  \sum_{m\ge j}\binom mj(-2)^{m-j}r^m\\
&=\frac1{1+2r}
  T\!\left(\frac r{1+2r}\right).
\end{aligned}
\tag{2.2}
\]

Now set

\[
W_n=\frac1{256^n}S_{2n},
\qquad
W(z)=\sum_{n\ge0}W_nz^n,
\qquad
r=\frac{\sqrt z}{16}.
\]

Taking the even part of (2.2) gives the exact formula

\[
\boxed{
W(z)=\frac12\left[
\frac{T\!\left(\dfrac r{1+2r}\right)}{1+2r}
+
\frac{T\!\left(-\dfrac r{1-2r}\right)}{1-2r}
\right],
\qquad z=256r^2.
}
\tag{2.3}
\]

Thus the transform consists of:

1. the rational pullback
   \[
   X_+(r)=\frac r{1+2r};
   \]
2. its conjugate branch
   \[
   X_-(r)=-\frac r{1-2r};
   \]
3. a weighted trace under \(r\mapsto-r\).

The two \(X\)-coordinates are related by the Mobius involution

\[
\boxed{
X_- = \iota(X_+),
\qquad
\iota(X)=-\frac{X}{1-4X}.
}
\tag{2.4}
\]

If \(t=1/X\), then

\[
\boxed{t\longmapsto4-t.}
\tag{2.5}
\]

This is exactly why the characteristic-polynomial identity contains

\[
C_{11}(t)C_{11}(4-t).
\]

For a pure exponential mode \(T_j=t^j\), one obtains

\[
W_n=\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}\right)^n.
\tag{2.6}
\]

So the spectral map is a **centered square**

\[
t\longmapsto\frac{(t-2)^2}{256},
\tag{2.7}
\]

not the literal square \(t\mapsto t^2\).

## 2.1 The modular category of the transform

Equation (2.3) is most naturally described as the **direct image/trace of a modular differential system along a quadratic algebraic cover**. If \(r\), \(X_+(r)\), and \(X_-(r)\) can all be identified as functions on a modular correspondence, then the trace will again be modularly parametrized, possibly at a higher level. But that modular-correspondence identification is an additional theorem; the characteristic polynomial alone does not prove it.

### It is not yet an Atkin-Lehner identity

The Fricke involution \(W_{11}\) acts on \(\tau\) and on the function field of the level-11 curve. The involution (2.4) acts on the particular local coordinate used in the binomial transform. To identify the two, one must compare divisors or enough exact \(q\)-expansion coefficients. No such identification has been established here. In Cooper's genus-one model the Fricke/elliptic involution is already part of the modular curve structure, while (2.4) is a second, explicitly introduced Mobius operation.

### It is not a Rankin-Cohen bracket

Rankin-Cohen brackets are bilinear differential operators on modular forms and change the modular weight. Formula (2.3) is linear and is produced by pullback plus trace; no Rankin-Cohen differentiation occurs.

### It is not a Shimura lift on present evidence

For a half-integral-weight form

\[
g(\tau)=\sum_{m\ge1}c(m)q^m,
\]

a Shimura lift has coefficients of the schematic form

\[
A_t(n)=\sum_{d\mid n}\chi_t(d)d^{k-1}
 c\!\left(\frac{tn^2}{d^2}\right).
\tag{2.8}
\]

The centered-binomial coefficients

\[
256^{-n}\binom{2n}{j}(-2)^{2n-j}
\]

have neither the square-index structure nor the divisor sum in (2.8). Hence the transform is not a Shimura lift unless a further, presently unknown, identification is supplied.

# 3. Why the factor \(n^{3/2}\) does not imply half-integral modular weight

Suppose

\[
\frac{q_n}{W_n}\sim Cn^{3/2}.
\tag{3.1}
\]

This says that two solutions with the same exponential base have Birkhoff powers differing by \(3/2\). There are several routine local explanations:

- a gamma/Pochhammer gauge
  \[
  \frac{\Gamma(n+a)}{\Gamma(n+b)}\sim n^{a-b};
  \]
- an algebraic prefactor at the dominant singularity;
- ramification under \(z=256r^2\);
- a rational differential intertwiner combined with a square-root gauge.

At the generating-function level, multiplying by

\[
(1-z/z_0)^{-3/2}
\]

changes coefficient asymptotics by a factor of order \(n^{3/2}\), without creating a half-integral-weight modular form.

Half-integral modular weight is a **global transformation law** under a metaplectic cover. To establish a Shimura correspondence one would need, at minimum:

1. an explicit form in \(S_{k+1/2}^+(4N,\chi)\);
2. Kohnen plus-space support conditions;
3. Hecke relations for \(T(p^2)\);
4. the square-index divisor-sum relation (2.8).

None of these follows from (3.1). The conservative conclusion is therefore

\[
\boxed{
n^{3/2}\text{ indicates a half-integral local exponent, not a proved Shimura lift.}
}
\tag{3.2}
\]

# 4. An exact level-11 modular period for \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\)

There is no need to force both constants into a single pure modular form. They arise naturally from two Eisenstein pieces of different weights.

## 4.1 The \(\zeta(2)\) piece

Let

\[
\chi(n)=\left(\frac n{11}\right)
\]

be the odd quadratic character modulo \(11\). Let \(\mathcal E_3\in M_3(\Gamma_0(11),\chi)\) be the standard weight-three Eisenstein series whose positive Fourier coefficients are

\[
a_n=\sum_{d\mid n}\chi(d)d^2.
\tag{4.1}
\]

Its Dirichlet series is

\[
\begin{aligned}
L(\mathcal E_3,s)
&=\sum_{n\ge1}\frac{a_n}{n^s}\\
&=\sum_{d,m\ge1}\frac{\chi(d)d^2}{(dm)^s}\\
&=\zeta(s)L(s-2,\chi).
\end{aligned}
\tag{4.2}
\]

The quadratic residues modulo \(11\) are \(1,3,4,5,9\), so

\[
\sum_{a=1}^{10}\chi(a)a
=(1+3+4+5+9)-(2+6+7+8+10)=-11.
\]

For a primitive odd character,

\[
L(0,\chi)=-\frac1{11}\sum_{a=1}^{10}\chi(a)a=1.
\tag{4.3}
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{L(\mathcal E_3,2)=\zeta(2).}
\tag{4.4}
\]

## 4.2 The \(\zeta(3)\) piece

Define the Fricke-odd weight-four Eisenstein form

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal E_4^-(\tau)=E_4(\tau)-11^2E_4(11\tau).
}
\tag{4.5}
\]

Using

\[
E_4(\tau)=1+240\sum_{n\ge1}\sigma_3(n)q^n,
\]

its positive Fourier coefficients have Dirichlet series

\[
\boxed{
L(\mathcal E_4^-,s)
=240\bigl(1-11^{2-s}\bigr)\zeta(s)\zeta(s-3).
}
\tag{4.6}
\]

At \(s=3\), since \(\zeta(0)=-1/2\),

\[
\boxed{
L(\mathcal E_4^-,3)
=-\frac{1200}{11}\zeta(3).
}
\tag{4.7}
\]

Combining (4.4) and (4.7) gives the exact level-11 identity

\[
\boxed{
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
=L(\mathcal E_3,2)
-\frac{11}{1200}L(\mathcal E_4^-,3).
}
\tag{4.8}
\]

This is the cleanest precise modular answer to part (d).

## 4.3 Mellin-period form

With the convention

\[
\operatorname{Reg}\int_0^\infty f(iy)y^{s-1}\,dy
=\frac{\Gamma(s)}{(2\pi)^s}L(f,s),
\tag{4.9}
\]

where Eisenstein constant terms are regularized at the cusps, (4.8) becomes

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
={}&4\pi^2\,
\operatorname{Reg}\int_0^\infty\mathcal E_3(iy)y\,dy\\
&-\frac{11\pi^3}{300}\,
\operatorname{Reg}\int_0^\infty\mathcal E_4^-(iy)y^2\,dy.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{4.10}
\]

For comparison, the same mixed period has the elementary absolutely convergent expression

\[
\boxed{
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
=\int_0^1
\frac{-\log t+\tfrac12\log^2t}{1-t}\,dt.
}
\tag{4.11}
\]

Equation (4.10) is modular; equation (4.11) is the corresponding elementary mixed-Tate period.

## 4.4 How this could enter Problem 2.7

Let

\[
\mathscr E_3(\tau)=\sum_{n\ge1}\frac{a_n}{n^2}q^n,
\qquad
\mathscr E_4^-(\tau)=\sum_{n\ge1}\frac{b_n}{n^3}q^n
\tag{4.12}
\]

be Eichler primitives of the two Eisenstein series. Their Fricke connection formulas contain the critical values in (4.8). A plausible modular normal function for the challenge is therefore a rationally normalized combination of

\[
\mathscr E_3-\frac{11}{1200}\mathscr E_4^-.
\tag{4.13}
\]

A proof must still establish all of the following:

1. express (4.13) in Cooper's coordinate \(X_{11}\);
2. derive its inhomogeneous Picard-Fuchs equation;
3. pass it through the quadratic trace (2.3);
4. identify the resulting coefficient sequence with the printed numerator row;
5. prove that the denominator row is the corresponding homogeneous period;
6. check the initial values, hence the dominant connection coefficient.

The spectral identity only addresses the homogeneous exponential bases in step 3. It does not prove the Eisenstein extension or the initial-value identification.

## 4.5 The cusp-form obstruction

The level-11 spaces also contain cusp forms. If the extension forcing has a nonzero cusp component, its Eichler integral contributes critical values such as

\[
L(f_{\mathrm{cusp}},2),
\qquad
L(f_{\mathrm{cusp}},3),
\]

which are not generally rational combinations of \(\zeta(2)\) and \(\zeta(3)\). Therefore a complete proof of the exact target must certify that

\[
\boxed{
\text{the relevant extension is Eisenstein, or all level-11 cusp periods cancel.}
}
\tag{4.14}
\]

This is the modular analogue of proving dominant-mode cancellation in the recurrence.

# 5. Motivic meaning of the mixed weight

In the usual period-weight convention:

- \(\zeta(2)=\pi^2/6\) is a pure Tate period, belonging to \(\mathbf Q(2)\);
- \(\zeta(3)\) is represented by a nontrivial mixed-Tate extension
  \[
  0\longrightarrow\mathbf Q(3)
  \longrightarrow\mathcal E_{\zeta(3)}
  \longrightarrow\mathbf Q(0)
  \longrightarrow0.
  \tag{5.1}
  \]

Consequently

\[
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
\]

is not a canonical period of a single pure motive of one weight. It is naturally a matrix coefficient of a mixed object such as

\[
\mathbf Q(2)\oplus\mathcal E_{\zeta(3)},
\tag{5.2}
\]

or of a larger extension whose weight-graded pieces contain

\[
\operatorname{gr}\mathcal M
\supset
\mathbf Q(0)\oplus\mathbf Q(2)\oplus\mathbf Q(3).
\tag{5.3}
\]

The level-11 Cooper system contributes an elliptic/modular local system. Thus the most plausible full structure is not a pure level-11 motive with period \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\), but an extension of the level-11 local system by Tate-Eisenstein pieces. A boundary functional or initial CMF row can then produce the numerical sum of periods of different weights.

This explains both the plausibility and the difficulty:

- the homogeneous recurrence records the level-11 modular carrier;
- the numerator row records an extension/normal function;
- the special value is determined by the extension class and initial framing;
- proving that only \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\) remains requires eliminating every elliptic cusp period.

# 6. Exact finite computation that would settle the modular identification

Let \(L_C\) denote Cooper's order-three difference operator, and let \(K(n,j)\) be the centered-binomial kernel

\[
K(n,j)=256^{-n}\binom{2n}{j}(-2)^{2n-j}.
\tag{6.1}
\]

A complete homogeneous certificate is a mixed creative-telescoping identity

\[
\boxed{
L_{27}^{(n)}K(n,j)
=U(n,j)L_C^{*,(j)}+\Delta_jV(n,j),
}
\tag{6.2}
\]

possibly followed by a rational gauge or order-at-most-two Ore intertwiner. Summation in \(j\), with boundary terms checked, proves the transformed Cooper sequence satisfies the challenge operator.

The extension certificate is the corresponding parameter-differentiated or Eichler identity for the modular normal function (4.13). Finally, three initial values determine the selected solution of the order-three recurrence.

Therefore the modular proof can be reduced to three finite, checkable artifacts:

1. **homogeneous trace certificate:** (6.2) or an equivalent operator identity;
2. **Eisenstein extension certificate:** transport of (4.13), with cusp projection zero;
3. **initial-row certificate:** exact matching of the printed \(p_n,q_n\) at three consecutive indices.

Once these are available, Poincare-Perron/Birkhoff theory supplies the already observed exponential convergence.

# Answers to the five questions

## (a) A level-11 modular form with coefficients \(q_n\)?

No such ordinary Fourier-coefficient identity is presently certified. Cooper's \(T_n\) are Taylor coefficients of \(Z_{11}\) in \(X_{11}\), not Fourier coefficients. The best precise candidate for \(q_n\) is a coefficient sequence obtained from the quadratic trace (2.3), followed by an algebraic/rational differential gauge. Proving that candidate is an exact finite operator calculation.

## (b) Modular meaning of the centered-binomial transform?

Yes, at the differential-equation level: it is a pullback followed by the trace along \(z=256r^2\), with the two Cooper coordinates related by \(X\mapsto-X/(1-4X)\), or \(t\mapsto4-t\). It is not presently identified with Atkin-Lehner, Rankin-Cohen, or Shimura operations.

## (c) Does \(n^{3/2}\) indicate Shimura correspondence?

No. It indicates a local exponent shift of \(3/2\), which can come from ramification or a gamma/algebraic gauge. A Shimura lift requires half-integral-weight modularity and Hecke square-index relations, none of which follows from the observed ratio.

## (d) Precise level-11 period formula?

Yes:

\[
\boxed{
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
=L(\mathcal E_3,2)-\frac{11}{1200}L(\mathcal E_4^-,3),
}
\]

with \(\mathcal E_3\in M_3(\Gamma_0(11),\chi_{11})\) defined by (4.1) and \(\mathcal E_4^-=E_4-121E_4(11\tau)\). The equivalent regularized Mellin formula is (4.10).

## (e) Motivic explanation?

The target is a mixed, nonhomogeneous period. Its Tate pieces are \(\mathbf Q(2)\) and the extension carrying \(\zeta(3)\). In a level-11 construction these Tate pieces must occur as an Eisenstein extension of the modular local system. The exact value \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\) requires vanishing or cancellation of all cusp/elliptic periods.
