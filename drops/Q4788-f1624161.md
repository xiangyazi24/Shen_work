# Problem 3.1: what the denominator 85 can mean, and what is still missing

## Executive verdict

The identity

\[
\int_\alpha^\beta\left(\log x\,\frac{dy}{y}-\log y\,\frac{dx}{x}\right)
=\frac{4\pi^2}{85}
\tag{1}
\]

is numerically compelling, but I do not find a published proof for the specified \(7_2\) arc. The current challenge repository likewise gives a high-precision ODE verification, not the topological certificate needed by Khoi's method.

The main conclusions are:

1. **The integer 85 is not the order of first homology of either endpoint filling.** With the degree-22 polynomial interpreted in the standard A-polynomial variables, the endpoint relations are slopes \(-1/2\) and \(-1\), and both filled manifolds are integral homology spheres. Thus \(|H_1|=1\), not 85.
2. **It is not the raw Euler denominator of the obvious Seifert fibration either.** The unit surgery on the five-twist knot is, up to mirror and orientation conventions, the Brieskorn sphere \(\Sigma(2,3,17)\), whose normalized Seifert Euler number has denominator \(102\), not \(85\). Its maximal Seifert volume is \(242\pi^2/51\), not a simple multiple of the target.
3. **The literal first endpoint is half-integral.** The classification of exceptional surgeries on hyperbolic two-bridge knots implies that this half-integral filling is not one of the small-Seifert fillings of the twist knot. Therefore the most naive extension of Khoi's two-Seifert-endpoint calculation does not apply to the variables used by the numerical script.
4. **A squared-meridian reinterpretation changes the slopes to integral ones, but also changes the polynomial and doubles the logarithmic symplectic form.** One cannot use the integral-surgery interpretation while silently retaining the degree-22 polynomial and the original normalization of (1).
5. **I find no published proof that this exact real A-polynomial arc is the peripheral image of a continuous purely hyperbolic \(\widetilde{\mathrm{SL}}_2(\mathbb R)\) path.** General descriptions of real representations of twist knots are not enough; the specific connected arc and its endpoint lifts must be certified.
6. **The Rogers-dilogarithm mechanism is structurally correct, but no explicit flattened dilogarithm evaluation of this arc is presently available.** Khoi himself remarks that the higher-genus examples were not known to him as ordinary dilogarithm sums.
7. **The complete hyperbolic volume and the target integral are different parts of a common regulator story, not scalar multiples of one another.** The volume is obtained from the Bloch-Wigner imaginary part at the complete \(\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb C)\) holonomy; the integral is a real/Godbillon-Vey or Chern-Simons variation along a real \(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb R)\) branch.

The shortest honest proof target is therefore not “show that 85 is a surgery homology order.” It is the representation-specific identity

\[
\boxed{
GV(\rho_\beta)-GV(\rho_\alpha)
=-\frac{16\pi^2}{85}
}
\tag{2}
\]

in the literal meridian-eigenvalue convention and with the orientation used in (1). Signs reverse if the path or longitude orientation is reversed. Once (2) and the purely hyperbolic lift are established, Khoi's Schläfli formula proves (1 immediately).

# 1. First fix the peripheral-coordinate convention

Khoi uses actual eigenvalues of the preferred meridian and longitude:

\[
\rho(\mu)\sim
\begin{pmatrix}M&0\\0&M^{-1}\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\rho(\lambda)\sim
\begin{pmatrix}L&0\\0&L^{-1}\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{3}
\]

In these variables, a character extends over \(p/q\)-surgery exactly when

\[
M^pL^q=1.
\tag{4}
\]

The challenge polynomial is degree 22 in its first variable and uses only even powers of that variable. That is the standard behavior of the A-polynomial in the actual meridian eigenvalue \(M\); it does **not** mean that the variable has automatically been renamed \(M^2\).

The numerical script imposes

\[
L=\sqrt M\quad\text{at }\alpha,
\qquad
L=M\quad\text{at }\beta.
\tag{5}
\]

Therefore, under the literal standard convention,

\[
M L^{-2}=1\quad\Longrightarrow\quad p/q=-\frac12,
\tag{6}
\]

and

\[
M L^{-1}=1\quad\Longrightarrow\quad p/q=-1.
\tag{7}
\]

The signs depend on the choice of preferred longitude and mirror, but the distinction “half-integral versus integral” does not.

## 1.1 Why \(85\neq |H_1|\)

For \(p/q\)-surgery on a knot in \(S^3\), the longitude is null-homologous in the knot exterior, so abelianization adds the relation \(p[\mu]=0\). Hence

\[
H_1\bigl(S^3_{p/q}(K);\mathbb Z\bigr)
\cong \mathbb Z/|p|\mathbb Z.
\tag{8}
\]

Both relations (6) and (7) have \(|p|=1\). Thus

\[
\boxed{|H_1(X_{-1/2})|=|H_1(X_{-1})|=1.}
\tag{9}
\]

In particular, both endpoints are integral homology spheres. Their \(H^2\) also vanishes, so 85 is not the order of a torsion Euler class of a circle bundle over either closed endpoint manifold.

This settles the first proposed interpretation decisively:

\[
\boxed{85\text{ is not a Dehn-filling homology order.}}
\]

# 2. What the denominator can mean in the Godbillon-Vey formula

Khoi's input is the Schläfli-type variation formula for a purely hyperbolic path of lifted \(\widetilde{\mathrm{SL}}_2(\mathbb R)\) representations. If

\[
M=e^a,\qquad L=e^b,
\]

then, in Khoi's normalization,

\[
GV(\rho_1)-GV(\rho_0)
=4\int (\dot a\,b-a\,\dot b)\,dt.
\tag{10}
\]

The form in (10) descends to

\[
\log L\,\frac{dM}{M}
-\log M\,\frac{dL}{L}.
\tag{11}
\]

The challenge form is the negative of (11):

\[
\omega=\log M\,\frac{dL}{L}
-\log L\,\frac{dM}{M}.
\tag{12}
\]

Consequently,

\[
GV(\rho_\beta)-GV(\rho_\alpha)
=-4\int_\alpha^\beta\omega.
\tag{13}
\]

Thus (1) is equivalent, with these conventions, to (2):

\[
GV(\rho_\beta)-GV(\rho_\alpha)
=-\frac{16\pi^2}{85}.
\]

This is the correct place for the denominator 85. It belongs to a **difference of two representation-specific secondary invariants**, not to first homology alone.

## 2.1 It is not the raw Seifert Euler number

For a Seifert fibration with orbifold Euler characteristic \(\chi\) and rational Euler number \(e\), Brooks-Goldman give the maximal Seifert volume

\[
\{Y\}=\frac{4\pi^2\chi^2}{|e|}
\tag{14}
\]

when \(\chi<0\) and \(e\ne0\).

In the common twist-knot convention, the five-twist knot is \(K_5\), and unit surgery is, up to mirror and orientation,

\[
K_5(1)\cong\Sigma(2,3,17).
\tag{15}
\]

For this Brieskorn homology sphere,

\[
\chi=\frac12+\frac13+\frac1{17}-1=-\frac{11}{102},
\qquad
|e|=\frac1{2\cdot3\cdot17}=\frac1{102}.
\tag{16}
\]

Therefore

\[
\{\Sigma(2,3,17)\}
=\frac{4\pi^2(11/102)^2}{1/102}
=\frac{242\pi^2}{51}.
\tag{17}
\]

Neither the Euler denominator \(102\) nor the maximal volume coefficient \(242/51\) explains \(4/85\) directly.

The factorization

\[
85=5\cdot17
\]

makes the appearance of \(17\) suggestive, because \(17\) occurs in the Brieskorn sphere (15). But the additional factor 5 has not been derived from the endpoint topology. It could arise from the particular \(\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb R)\) representation selected by the real branch, from the second endpoint, from a peripheral-index normalization, or from a combination of these. At present this is an observation, not a proof.

## 2.2 The half-integral endpoint is a serious obstruction

Brittenham and Wu classify exceptional surgeries on hyperbolic two-bridge knots. For a non-figure-eight twist knot, the small-Seifert slopes are integral; the remaining exceptional slopes are also explicitly listed. Hence the half-integral filling in (6) is not one of the small-Seifert fillings.

Under the literal degree-22 convention, the first endpoint therefore cannot be evaluated simply by inserting Seifert invariants into (14). One must instead compute the Godbillon-Vey invariant of the **specific** closed-manifold representation extending that endpoint. The closed manifold can be hyperbolic while still admitting unrelated \(\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb R)\) representations; the hyperbolic \(\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb C)\) holonomy is not the representation used by Khoi's real method.

That representation-specific endpoint computation is the main missing certificate.

# 3. The tempting squared-meridian interpretation

Because the polynomial uses only even powers of \(M\), one may introduce

\[
X=M^2.
\tag{18}
\]

If the first challenge coordinate were really \(X\), then the endpoint equations would become

\[
L=\sqrt X=M,
\qquad
L=X=M^2,
\tag{19}
\]

which correspond to integral slopes \(-1\) and \(-2\), respectively. Those are among the standard small-Seifert slopes of a twist knot, so this reading would restore the familiar shape of Khoi's \(5_2\) argument.

However, three corrections are mandatory.

### 3.1 The polynomial changes

If \(A(M,L)\) is the degree-22 polynomial, then the polynomial in \(X=M^2\) is

\[
B(X,L)=A(\sqrt X,L),
\tag{20}
\]

which has degree 11 in \(X\), not degree 22.

### 3.2 The one-form changes

Since \(dX/X=2\,dM/M\) and \(\log X=2\log M\),

\[
\log X\,\frac{dL}{L}
-\log L\,\frac{dX}{X}
=2\left(
\log M\,\frac{dL}{L}
-\log L\,\frac{dM}{M}
\right).
\tag{21}
\]

So changing from \(M\) to \(X=M^2\) changes the integral by a factor of two.

### 3.3 The peripheral basis may also change

A-polynomial coordinates are tied to a primitive meridian-longitude basis. Replacing the meridian eigenvalue by its square is a twofold covering of the boundary eigenvalue torus, not a primitive \(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb Z)\) change of peripheral basis. An index can therefore enter the Neumann-Zagier boundary form.

Thus one cannot combine:

- the degree-22 polynomial in \(M\),
- the surgery slopes inferred from \(X=M^2\), and
- the original normalization of (1)

without an explicit conversion proof.

If the challenge authors intended \(x=M^2\), the statement should be rewritten with \(B(X,L)\), and the factor of two in (21) must be propagated through the target constant.

# 4. The explicit A-polynomial and its components

Let \(A^{\mathrm{na}}_{7_2}(M,L)\) denote the normalized nonabelian factor, with the abelian factor removed. The polynomial used by the challenge computation is

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
A^{\mathrm{na}}_{7_2}(M,L)={}&L^5\\
&+(M^{14}-M^{12}+3M^4+4M^2-2)L^4\\
&+(-2M^{18}+5M^{16}+M^{14}-4M^{12}
  +6M^8+5M^6+2M^4-4M^2+1)L^3\\
&+(M^{22}-4M^{20}+2M^{18}+5M^{16}+6M^{14}
  -4M^{10}+M^8+5M^6-2M^4)L^2\\
&+(-2M^{22}+4M^{20}+3M^{18}-M^{10}+M^8)L\\
&+M^{22}.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{22}
\]

The full A-polynomial, in the convention that retains the reducible component, is

\[
\boxed{
A^{\mathrm{full}}_{7_2}(M,L)
=(L-1)A^{\mathrm{na}}_{7_2}(M,L)
}
\tag{23}
\]

up to an overall sign and monomial. The factor \(L-1\) is the abelian component.

Useful checks are

\[
A^{\mathrm{na}}_{7_2}(1,L)=(L+1)^5
\tag{24}
\]

and the reciprocity identity, up to the usual monomial normalization,

\[
A^{\mathrm{na}}_{7_2}(M,L)
=M^{22}L^5
A^{\mathrm{na}}_{7_2}(M^{-1},L^{-1}).
\tag{25}
\]

The even powers permit the degree-11 form

\[
A^{\mathrm{na}}_{7_2}(M,L)=\widetilde A(M^2,L).
\tag{26}
\]

## 4.1 Is there more than one nonabelian component?

For twist knots, the irreducible characters are described by a single algebraic curve in the standard character coordinates. Correspondingly, (22) is the standard nonabelian factor and contains the geometric character, i.e. the lift of the discrete faithful hyperbolic representation.

There are still several distinctions to keep straight:

- one complex irreducible component can have several disconnected real arcs;
- the peripheral eigenvalue map can ramify or identify characters;
- a real point of the A-polynomial need not by itself specify an \(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb R)\) lift;
- the A-polynomial traditionally suppresses multiplicities.

Thus “one nonabelian polynomial factor” does not mean that the real branch problem is trivial. The challenge arc is one particular real segment within the peripheral image of this component.

For a formal algebraic certificate, one should also factor (22) over \(\mathbb Z[M,L]\) in the chosen CAS and retain the factorization transcript. The standard twist-knot formula of Mathews produces (22) as the nonabelian factor.

# 5. Has the required \(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb R)\) path been identified?

I find no published treatment that proves all of the following for the exact arc in the challenge:

1. every point \((M,L)\) of the selected positive real arc lifts to a character of the \(7_2\) group;
2. the lift can be chosen continuously in \(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb R)\), and then in \(\widetilde{\mathrm{SL}}_2(\mathbb R)\);
3. both peripheral elements remain hyperbolic throughout the path;
4. the arc contains no reducible character, singular point, or change of character component;
5. the endpoint characters extend over precisely the claimed Dehn fillings;
6. the logarithm branches used by the boundary normal form agree with the positive real logarithms in (1).

General Riley-polynomial and character-variety descriptions of twist or double-twist knots do provide raw material. They can establish the existence of many real representations and often parametrize the nonabelian character curve. But they do not automatically certify this exact nonparabolic branch with these endpoint conditions.

The fact that \((M,L)\) is a positive real point of the A-polynomial only says that it occurs in the Zariski closure of peripheral eigenvalue data. It does **not** by itself prove that the associated interior representation is conjugate into \(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb R)\), rather than \(\mathrm{SU}(2)\) or a genuinely complex subgroup, nor that one can choose a continuous lift without crossing a singularity.

## 5.1 What a checkable branch certificate would look like

A rigorous implementation can use a two-generator presentation of the twist-knot group. Put the meridian matrices into a Riley normal form, for example

\[
A=
\begin{pmatrix}M&1\\0&M^{-1}\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
B=
\begin{pmatrix}M&0\\s&M^{-1}\end{pmatrix},
\tag{27}
\]

with a convention-dependent real parameter \(s\). The knot relation gives a Riley polynomial

\[
\Phi_{7_2}(M,s)=0.
\tag{28}
\]

The preferred longitude gives a rational function

\[
L=\Lambda_{7_2}(M,s).
\tag{29}
\]

A complete certificate would then provide:

- explicit \(\Phi_{7_2}\) and \(\Lambda_{7_2}\);
- a semialgebraic interval or box chain selecting one real solution \(s=s(M)\);
- nonvanishing of \(\partial\Phi/\partial s\) along the chain;
- inequalities proving the matrices are conjugate into \(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb R)\);
- inequalities proving \(|\operatorname{tr}\rho(\mu)|>2\) and \(|\operatorname{tr}\rho(\lambda)|>2\);
- exact endpoint elimination identities matching (5);
- a continuous lift to \(\widetilde{\mathrm{SL}}_2(\mathbb R)\).

This is finite algebra plus certified real-root continuation. It appears feasible, but it has not been supplied in the challenge repository.

# 6. The common structure of Khoi-type proofs

Khoi's 2008 note explicitly carries this out for the figure-eight knot \(4_1\) and for \(5_2\). It should not be cited as a proof for \(6_1\) without a separate reference; if an analogous \(6_1\) computation is being used, its source and peripheral conventions should be stated independently.

The proved examples have the following common architecture.

## Step 1: an explicit real character branch

One writes down a parameterization of a connected piece of the \(\widetilde{\mathrm{SL}}_2(\mathbb R)\) character variety and its peripheral holonomies

\[
M(t)=e^{a(t)},\qquad L(t)=e^{b(t)}.
\]

One proves the path is purely hyperbolic.

## Step 2: endpoint surgery equations

One finds intersections with monomial curves

\[
M^pL^q=1.
\]

These identify characters extending over specific Dehn fillings.

For example, Khoi uses the \(-1\) and \(-2\) fillings of \(4_1\), and the \(+1\) and \(+2\) fillings of \(5_2\), modulo orientation conventions.

## Step 3: topologically computable endpoint invariants

The selected endpoint fillings are small Seifert manifolds. Their relevant \(\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb R)\) characters are identified, and their Godbillon-Vey invariants are computed from Seifert data or by the cut-and-paste formalism.

This is more precise than computing only the maximal Seifert volume: when a filling has several real characters, the branch selects one particular character.

## Step 4: Schläfli variation

The boundary formula gives

\[
GV(\rho_1)-GV(\rho_0)
=4\int(\log L\,d\log M-\log M\,d\log L).
\]

Reversing the sign gives the challenge orientation.

## Step 5: uniqueness and normalization

One must verify which endpoint character lies on the chosen arc, the preferred-longitude sign, the lift integers in \(\widetilde{\mathrm{SL}}_2(\mathbb R)\), and the path orientation.

## 6.1 A systematized proof pipeline

The method can be made algorithmic:

1. **Character certificate:** group presentation, Riley polynomial, longitude word.
2. **Real-arc certificate:** Sturm/interval-Newton boxes and nonsingularity.
3. **Peripheral certificate:** explicit formulas for \(M,L\) and monomial surgery relations.
4. **Topology certificate:** Kirby calculus, Regina/SnapPy triangulation, or a published surgery identification.
5. **Representation certificate:** identify the exact triangle-group or Seifert representation at each endpoint.
6. **GV certificate:** compute rotation data, Euler data, and the representation-specific Godbillon-Vey number.
7. **Variation certificate:** verify Khoi's hypotheses and integrate the boundary form.
8. **Convention certificate:** account for mirror, orientation, \(L\mapsto L^{-1}\), and any \(M\mapsto M^2\) cover.

For \(7_2\), steps 1 and much of 2 are computationally accessible. The load-bearing missing items are steps 5 and 6, together with the coordinate audit in step 8.

# 7. Rogers dilogarithms and the expected regulator formula

The local differential identity is elementary. For

\[
R(z)=\operatorname{Li}_2(z)
+\frac12\log z\log(1-z)-\frac{\pi^2}{6},
\]

one has

\[
2\,dR(z)
=\log z\,d\log(1-z)
-\log(1-z)\,d\log z.
\tag{30}
\]

An ideal triangulation with shapes \(z_1,\dots,z_t\) turns the sum of (30) into a boundary symplectic form after the internal edge terms cancel by the Neumann-Zagier equations.

Globally one must use the **extended** Rogers dilogarithm. A standard normalization is

\[
\widehat R(z;p,q)
=\operatorname{Li}_2(z)
+\frac12(\Log z+p\pi i)(\Log(1-z)+q\pi i)
-\frac{\pi^2}{6},
\tag{31}
\]

where \(p,q\in\mathbb Z\) record a flattening. For oriented tetrahedra,

\[
\mathcal R=\sum_j\epsilon_j\widehat R(z_j;p_j,q_j).
\tag{32}
\]

After imposing the gluing equations, one obtains, with convention-dependent sign and possibly a peripheral index \(N\),

\[
2\,d\mathcal R
=N\bigl(\log M\,d\log L-\log L\,d\log M\bigr).
\tag{33}
\]

This is the precise sense in which the integral should be a Rogers-dilogarithm regulator.

## 7.1 Why (33) is not yet an evaluation

To integrate (33) along the challenge arc one needs:

1. algebraic or certified analytic shape functions \(z_j(t)\) lifting the entire A-polynomial arc to the gluing variety;
2. the exact peripheral exponent matrices for the preferred meridian and longitude;
3. proof that the lifted component eliminates to the factor (22), not an extraneous component;
4. integral flattenings \((p_j,q_j)\) compatible with every edge equation;
5. continuous logarithm branches and explicit branch-jump corrections;
6. the endpoint extended-Bloch-group classes;
7. the exact index \(N\) in (33).

No explicit certificate of this kind is currently present in the challenge repository, and I did not find one in the literature for this exact \(7_2\) arc.

Khoi explicitly remarks that the A-polynomial curves in his examples have genus greater than one and that he did not know how to express the corresponding real integrals as ordinary dilogarithm sums. He expected a Rogers-dilogarithm interpretation because ideal simplices in \(\widetilde{\mathrm{SL}}_2(\mathbb R)\) admit such a description.

Therefore the honest answer to question (e) is:

\[
\boxed{
\begin{minipage}{0.86\linewidth}
The value should be an extended Rogers-dilogarithm regulator, and the endpoint shapes should be algebraic. But no explicit identity of the form
\(4\pi^2/85=\sum_j c_j\operatorname{Li}_2(\xi_j)\)
with certified algebraic \(\xi_j\), branch choices, and flattenings is presently established for this path.
\end{minipage}}
\]

An identity modulo \(\pi^2\) in the extended Bloch group is more natural than a naive equality of principal-branch classical dilogarithms.

# 8. Bloch-Wigner volume versus the target integral

For an ideal tetrahedron of complex shape \(z\), the Bloch-Wigner function

\[
D(z)=\Im\operatorname{Li}_2(z)
+\arg(1-z)\log|z|
\tag{34}
\]

is its signed hyperbolic volume. For a geometric ideal triangulation of the complete \(7_2\) complement,

\[
\operatorname{Vol}(S^3\setminus7_2)
=\sum_j\epsilon_jD(z_j^{\mathrm{complete}}).
\tag{35}
\]

Using the value quoted in the question,

\[
\operatorname{Vol}(S^3\setminus7_2)
=3.33174\ldots.
\]

The target

\[
\frac{4\pi^2}{85}=0.4644519718\ldots
\]

is not obtained by dividing this volume by 85 or by \(4\pi^2\). There is no reason for such a scalar relation.

The conceptual relationship is instead:

- the complete hyperbolic volume is the Bloch-Wigner/imaginary regulator evaluated at the complete \(\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb C)\) holonomy;
- Chern-Simons data are the complementary real part of the extended complex regulator, modulo its natural period;
- the challenge path lies in a real \(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb R)\) character branch and measures a **difference** between incomplete or filled endpoint representations;
- the complete hyperbolic representation is generally not on that positive-real arc.

Thus volume and the logarithmic integral are siblings in the complex-volume formalism, but they are evaluated on different representations and different parts of the regulator.

For real shape parameters, the ordinary Bloch-Wigner function may vanish while the extended Rogers regulator remains nontrivial because of logarithm branches and \(\pi^2\)-terms. That is exactly why a rational multiple of \(\pi^2\) can appear without any comparable relation to the positive hyperbolic volume.

# 9. What would now constitute a proof of the challenge identity

There are two viable routes.

## Route A: Godbillon-Vey / Dehn filling

Prove the following finite list of lemmas.

### A1. Coordinate lemma

State whether the challenge variable is \(M\) or \(M^2\), and prove the exact relationship between its polynomial, its logarithmic one-form, and a primitive meridian-longitude basis.

### A2. Real-lift lemma

Construct a continuous path

\[
\rho_t:\pi_1(S^3\setminus7_2)
\longrightarrow\widetilde{\mathrm{SL}}_2(\mathbb R)
\]

whose peripheral eigenvalues trace the selected arc, and prove that the path is purely hyperbolic.

### A3. Endpoint lemma

Identify the exact filled manifolds and the exact endpoint characters. Under the literal convention these are the \(-1/2\) and \(-1\) fillings. Under a squared-coordinate convention they may become integral slopes, but the normalization must be changed accordingly.

### A4. Endpoint GV lemma

Prove

\[
GV(\rho_\beta)-GV(\rho_\alpha)
=-\frac{16\pi^2}{85}
\]

in the literal convention.

### A5. Variation lemma

Apply Khoi's formula with the certified orientation and logarithm lifts.

This route is shortest if an existing cut-and-paste calculation of the two endpoint representations can be found. Under the literal coordinates, however, the half-integral endpoint is not a small-Seifert filling, so a new endpoint calculation appears necessary.

## Route B: extended Bloch group / Rogers dilogarithm

Produce:

1. an explicit ideal triangulation;
2. gluing equations and peripheral exponent vectors;
3. a lift of the real A-polynomial arc to shape space;
4. a strong flattening along the arc;
5. exact endpoint flattenings;
6. an extended Bloch-group relation whose Rogers regulator is \(4\pi^2/85\).

This route avoids identifying both endpoints as Seifert manifolds, but it replaces that topology with a substantial exact flattening computation.

# 10. Direct answers to (a)–(f)

## (a) Is \(85=|H_1|\), or an Euler number?

No. In the literal standard coordinates, the endpoint slopes have numerator \(p=1\), so both fillings have trivial first homology. The unit-surgery Seifert fibration \(\Sigma(2,3,17)\) has rational Euler denominator 102, not 85. The number 85 must arise from the difference of the selected endpoint Godbillon-Vey invariants, perhaps together with a peripheral-cover normalization. The factor 17 is topologically suggestive; the factor 5 is not yet explained.

## (b) Has the required \(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb R)\) branch been proved for \(7_2\)?

I find no published proof for this exact arc and these endpoints. General twist-knot real-representation results do not supply all the needed branch, lift, hyperbolicity, and endpoint certificates.

## (c) What is the factorization and what are the components?

The full A-polynomial is

\[
(L-1)A^{\mathrm{na}}_{7_2}(M,L),
\]

where \(A^{\mathrm{na}}_{7_2}\) is the degree-(22,5) polynomial in (22). The factor \(L-1\) is abelian. The remaining factor is the standard twist-knot nonabelian/geometric factor. It contains one complex irreducible character curve but several real arcs may occur.

## (d) What is common to the proved cases?

An explicit purely hyperbolic real character path, monomial endpoint surgery equations, topologically identifiable Seifert fillings, representation-specific endpoint GV values, and Khoi's Schläfli formula. This is systematizable as the eight-certificate pipeline in Section 6.1. Khoi's 2008 note explicitly treats \(4_1\) and \(5_2\); a \(6_1\) claim needs a separate source.

## (e) Is it a dilogarithm value?

Structurally yes: it should be an extended Rogers-dilogarithm regulator of a flattened ideal triangulation. No explicit certified sum of classical dilogarithms at algebraic numbers is currently known for the specified arc.

## (f) How is it related to the hyperbolic volume?

They come from different components of the same complex-regulator framework. The volume is a Bloch-Wigner sum at the complete complex holonomy; the target is a real/GV variation along a real character path. There is no expected direct numerical ratio.
