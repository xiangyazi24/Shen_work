# Problem 2.5: what an explicit decaying remainder integral would have to represent

## Executive verdict

The requested estimate cannot hold for the polynomial

\[
R_{N,j}(s)=Q_{N,j}-(1+s)P_{N,j}
\]

itself.  In fact,

\[
\frac{R_{N,j}(s)}{1+s}
=Q_{N,j}\left(\frac1{1+s}-\frac{P_{N,j}}{Q_{N,j}}\right),
\]

so, if \(P_{N,j}/Q_{N,j}\to G\), then uniformly for \(0\le s\le1\),

\[
\frac{1}{Q_{N,j}}\frac{R_{N,j}(s)}{1+s}
\longrightarrow \frac1{1+s}-G.
\]

The limiting function is not zero.  Indeed,

\[
\max_{0\le s\le1}\left|\frac1{1+s}-G\right|=G-\frac12>0.
\]

Thus

\[
\boxed{
\sup_{0\le s\le1}\left|\frac{R_{N,j}(s)}{1+s}\right|
\asymp |Q_{N,j}|,
}
\]

not \(O(\rho^N)\).  More elementarily, a uniform bound tending to zero would force both

\[
Q_N-P_N=R_N(0)\to0,
\qquad
Q_N-2P_N=R_N(1)\to0,
\]

and hence \(P_N,Q_N\to0\), contrary to the dominant \((17+12\sqrt2)^N\) mode of the normalized CMF.

The small object is

\[
E_{N,j}:=GQ_{N,j}-P_{N,j},
\]

or, projectively,

\[
\frac{E_{N,j}}{Q_{N,j}}
=G-\frac{P_{N,j}}{Q_{N,j}}.
\]

For the determinant-adapted Pochhammer normalization, the formal data predict

\[
Q_{N,j}\asymp C_j(17+12\sqrt2)^N,
\qquad
E_{N,j}=O(N^{-3}),
\]

and therefore

\[
\boxed{
\frac{E_{N,j}}{Q_{N,j}}
=O\!\left(N^{-3}(17-12\sqrt2)^N\right).
}
\]

This is the correct form of the desired estimate.  In the raw, untwisted matrix product, both \(P_N\) and \(Q_N\), and hence also the absolute error, are multiplied by the common huge factor \(H_N\); only their ratio is invariant.

There is an explicit double integral that realizes exactly the *formal neutral mode* with exponent \(-3\) and gives the silver-ratio bound immediately.  It is built from a compact Legendre \(P_n\) cycle and a noncompact Legendre \(Q_n\) cycle.  What is not yet known is an exact finite-\(N\) identification of the CMF error with that mixed cycle.  Producing that identification is precisely the remaining certificate.

# 1. Why the existing moment integral can be small while \(R_N(s)\) is large

Put

\[
d\mu(s)=\frac{-\log s}{4\sqrt s}\,ds,
\qquad 0<s<1.
\]

Then

\[
\int_0^1d\mu(s)=1,
\qquad
\int_0^1\frac{d\mu(s)}{1+s}=G.
\]

The exact moment identity is

\[
E_N
=\int_0^1\frac{R_N(s)}{1+s}\,d\mu(s).
\]

Define the fixed function

\[
Z(s):=1-(1+s)G.
\]

A direct rearrangement gives

\[
\boxed{
R_N(s)=Q_NZ(s)+(1+s)E_N.
}
\tag{1}
\]

The large term in (1) is invisible to the moment functional, because

\[
\int_0^1\frac{Z(s)}{1+s}\,d\mu(s)
=\int_0^1\left(\frac1{1+s}-G\right)d\mu(s)=0.
\]

Thus the integral is small by a cancellation in a fixed two-dimensional moment space; it is not small because the integrand is pointwise small.

This identity also explains why an absolute-value estimate applied directly to the existing integral loses all useful information:

\[
|E_N|
\le \int_0^1\left|\frac{R_N(s)}{1+s}\right|d\mu(s)
\asymp |Q_N|.
\]

The desired proof requires a new representation of \(E_N\), not a new estimate of the already-known degree-one representation.

# 2. The three exact silver-ratio cycles from Legendre functions

Let

\[
a:=3+2\sqrt2=(1+\sqrt2)^2,
\qquad
b:=3-2\sqrt2=a^{-1},
\]

and

\[
\lambda_+=a^2=17+12\sqrt2,
\qquad
\rho=\lambda_+^{-1}=b^2=17-12\sqrt2.
\]

Write

\[
\mathsf P_n:=P_n(3),
\qquad
\mathsf Q_n:=Q_n(3),
\]

where \(P_n,Q_n\) are the Legendre functions of the first and second kind.  The first sequence is the central Delannoy sequence.

For \(n\ge0\), the standard integral representations are

\[
\boxed{
\mathsf P_n
=\frac1\pi\int_0^\pi
\bigl(3+2\sqrt2\cos\theta\bigr)^n\,d\theta,
}
\tag{2}
\]

and

\[
\boxed{
\mathsf Q_n
=\int_0^\infty
\bigl(3+2\sqrt2\cosh u\bigr)^{-n-1}\,du.
}
\tag{3}
\]

For example,

\[
\mathsf Q_0=\frac12\log2.
\]

Equations (2) and (3) give three natural product cycles:

\[
\mathsf P_n^2,
\qquad
\mathsf P_n\mathsf Q_n,
\qquad
\mathsf Q_n^2.
\]

Their exponential bases are respectively

\[
\lambda_+,\qquad 1,\qquad \rho.
\]

This is the exact spectral triple

\[
17+12\sqrt2,\qquad1,\qquad17-12\sqrt2.
\]

## 2.1 The formal shearing that produces exponents \((0,-3,0)\)

All three pure symmetric-square modes have formal power \(-1\).  Define instead

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
V_n^+&=(n+1)\mathsf P_n^2,\\
V_n^0&=\frac{\mathsf P_n\mathsf Q_n}{(n+1)^2},\\
V_n^-&=(n+1)\mathsf Q_n^2.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{4}
\]

The standard Legendre asymptotics give

\[
\mathsf P_n
\sim
\frac{a^{n+1/2}}
{\sqrt{2\pi n}\,8^{1/4}},
\]

\[
\mathsf Q_n
\sim
\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2n}}\,
\frac{a^{-n-1/2}}{8^{1/4}}.
\]

Consequently,

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
V_n^+&\sim \frac{a}{4\pi\sqrt2}\,\lambda_+^n,\\
V_n^0&\sim \frac{1}{4\sqrt2}\,n^{-3},\\
V_n^-&\sim \frac{\pi a^{-1}}{4\sqrt2}\,\rho^n.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{5}
\]

Thus (4) has exactly the formal data

\[
(\lambda_+,0),\qquad(1,-3),\qquad(\rho,0),
\]

that were computed for the normalized Problem 2.5 recurrence.

This does **not** prove that the three functions in (4) solve the printed CMF recurrence.  It proves that they are the canonical integral model for its formal type at infinity.

# 3. An explicit integral with the desired \(\rho^N\) bound

Set

\[
\phi(\theta)=3+2\sqrt2\cos\theta,
\qquad
\psi(u)=3+2\sqrt2\cosh u.
\]

Then

\[
b\le\phi(\theta)\le a,
\qquad
\psi(u)\ge a.
\]

The neutral model mode has the exact representation

\[
\boxed{
V_N^0
=\frac{1}{\pi(N+1)^2}
\int_0^\pi\int_0^\infty
\frac1{\psi(u)}
\left(\frac{\phi(\theta)}{\psi(u)}\right)^N
\,du\,d\theta.
}
\tag{6}
\]

After division by the dominant exponential scale \(\lambda_+^N=a^{2N}\), this becomes

\[
\boxed{
\frac{V_N^0}{\lambda_+^N}
=\frac{1}{\pi(N+1)^2}
\int_0^\pi\int_0^\infty
\frac1{\psi(u)}
\left(
\frac{\phi(\theta)}{a^2\psi(u)}
\right)^N
\,du\,d\theta.
}
\tag{7}
\]

The phase in (7) satisfies

\[
0<\frac{\phi(\theta)}{a^2\psi(u)}\le\frac{a}{a^3}=a^{-2}=\rho.
\]

Hence

\[
\boxed{
0\le
\frac{V_N^0}{\lambda_+^N}
\le
\frac{\log2}{2(N+1)^2}\,\rho^N.
}
\tag{8}
\]

A two-variable Laplace estimate at the unique maximum \((\theta,u)=(0,0)\) sharpens (8) to

\[
\boxed{
\frac{V_N^0}{\lambda_+^N}
=O\!\left(N^{-3}\rho^N\right).
}
\tag{9}
\]

The recessive model has the similarly explicit integral

\[
\boxed{
V_N^-=(N+1)
\int_0^\infty\int_0^\infty
\frac1{\psi(u)\psi(v)}
\left(\frac1{\psi(u)\psi(v)}\right)^N
\,du\,dv.
}
\tag{10}
\]

Thus

\[
\frac{V_N^-}{\lambda_+^N}
=(N+1)
\int_0^\infty\int_0^\infty
\frac1{\psi(u)\psi(v)}
\left(
\frac1{a^2\psi(u)\psi(v)}
\right)^Ndu\,dv,
\]

and

\[
\boxed{
0\le\frac{V_N^-}{\lambda_+^N}
\le
(N+1)\left(\frac{\log2}{2}\right)^2\rho^{2N}.
}
\tag{11}
\]

Equations (7) and (10) are the explicit definite integrals the desired proof is trying to uncover inside the CMF.

## 3.1 The exact conditional conclusion

Suppose one proves, for a fixed column \(j\), an exact or asymptotically exact decomposition

\[
\widehat Q_{N,j}
=c_{j,+}V_N^++c_{j,0}V_N^0+c_{j,-}V_N^-,
\qquad c_{j,+}\ne0,
\tag{12}
\]

and

\[
\widehat E_{N,j}
:=G\widehat Q_{N,j}-\widehat P_{N,j}
=d_{j,0}V_N^0+d_{j,-}V_N^-,
\tag{13}
\]

where hats denote the \(M_H\)-normalized sequences and the coefficients are bounded rational functions of \(N\), with nonzero finite limits where appropriate.

Then (5), (9), and (11) imply

\[
\boxed{
\frac{\widehat E_{N,j}}{\widehat Q_{N,j}}
=O\!\left(N^{-3}\rho^N+\rho^{2N}\right).
}
\tag{14}
\]

Formula (13), or an equivalent block-extension/generating-function statement, is the missing finite certificate.  It cannot be inferred from the Poincaré polynomial alone.

# 4. A Nesterenko/Nörlund–Rice contour exists for the model, not yet for the CMF

The terminating hypergeometric expression

\[
\mathsf P_N
={}_2F_1(-N,N+1;1;-1)
=\sum_{k=0}^N
\frac{(N+k)!}{(N-k)!(k!)^2}
\]

has the exact Nörlund–Rice contour representation

\[
\boxed{
\mathsf P_N
=\frac1{2\pi i}
\oint_{\mathcal C_N}
\frac{\pi e^{\pi iz}}{\sin\pi z}
\frac{\Gamma(N+z+1)}
{\Gamma(N-z+1)\Gamma(z+1)^2}
\,dz,
}
\tag{15}
\]

where \(\mathcal C_N\) encloses the integers \(0,1,\ldots,N\) and no other poles.  The residue at \(z=k\) is exactly

\[
\frac{(N+k)!}{(N-k)!(k!)^2}.
\]

Combining (15) with (3) gives a mixed gamma/real integral for the neutral model:

\[
\boxed{
V_N^0
=\frac{1}{(N+1)^2}
\left[
\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_{\mathcal C_N}
\frac{\pi e^{\pi iz}}{\sin\pi z}
\frac{\Gamma(N+z+1)}
{\Gamma(N-z+1)\Gamma(z+1)^2}\,dz
\right]
\left[
\int_0^\infty\psi(u)^{-N-1}\,du
\right].
}
\tag{16}
\]

This is a genuine shifted-factorial contour formula with the correct neutral exponential scale.

However, there is presently no exact identity

\[
G\widehat Q_{N,j}-\widehat P_{N,j}=\text{the right side of (16)}
\]

or any displayed finite combination of its shifts.  A contour formula for the actual CMF error would be a new theorem, not a consequence of the determinant and formal roots.

A single gamma quotient is especially unlikely to be discoverable from the current data alone.  The scalar recurrence has no known first-order hypergeometric solution; its middle coefficients contain the full degree-nine and degree-ten polynomials, and the apparent sextic orbit.  A Mellin–Barnes integral may still exist, but its gamma parameters and contour must be obtained by inverse creative telescoping or an Ore-module identification.

Thus the answer to sub-question (a) is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{A Nesterenko-type formula exists for the canonical Legendre model, but none is yet certified for }R_N\text{ or }E_N.
}
\]

# 5. The fixed-phase double-integral interpretation

There is also a compact-contour form for the dominant symmetric-square mode.  Put

\[
\varphi(u)=3+\sqrt2\left(u+u^{-1}\right).
\]

Then

\[
\mathsf P_N=\operatorname{CT}_u\varphi(u)^N,
\]

and

\[
\boxed{
\mathsf P_N^2
=\frac1{(2\pi i)^2}
\oint\oint
[\varphi(u)\varphi(v)]^N
\frac{du}{u}\frac{dv}{v}.
}
\tag{17}
\]

The critical values of \(\varphi(u)\varphi(v)\) are

\[
\lambda_+,\qquad1,\qquad\rho.
\]

This is the cleanest answer to the spectral part of sub-question (b).  A phase with the desired three critical values is completely explicit.

But a fixed positive weight on the compact-contour cycle contains the dominant \((+,+)\) saddle and therefore grows like \(\lambda_+^N\).  To represent the error, one must do at least one of the following:

1. use a different, mixed compact/noncompact cycle, as in (6);
2. use a signed combination of cycles that cancels the \((+,+)\) saddle;
3. insert an \(N\)-dependent amplitude that annihilates the dominant saddle to all required orders;
4. produce a nontrivial extension cocycle whose boundary term is the Catalan normal function.

A fixed scalar kernel \(K(s,u,v)^N W(u,v)\) with a nonnegative weight and the dominant saddle present cannot represent the neutral error mode.  The mixed \(P\)-\(Q\) cycle in (6) is the canonical way to select the product eigenvalue \(a\cdot a^{-1}=1\).

What remains is to show that the challenge initial rows select precisely that mixed cycle after the rational Pochhammer twist and the finite apparent-singularity modification.

# 6. The correct meaning of “almost \(\mathrm{Sym}^2\)”

The equality of characteristic polynomials gives only a formal statement at infinity.

Order the symmetric-square eigenmodes as

\[
\lambda_+,\quad1,\quad\rho.
\]

The pure symmetric square has formal exponents

\[
(-1,-1,-1),
\]

whereas the balanced, Pochhammer-normalized CMF has

\[
(0,-3,0).
\]

Their difference is

\[
(1,-2,1).
\]

Therefore, in a formal eigenbasis at infinity, the required modification has the shape

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal Y_{25}(N)
\sim
\mathcal Y_{\mathrm{Sym}^2}(N)
\operatorname{diag}(N+1,(N+1)^{-2},N+1)
\left(I+\frac{C_1}{N}+\frac{C_2}{N^2}+\cdots\right).
}
\tag{18}
\]

This is a **formal Hecke modification** or rational shearing with partial indices

\[
(1,-2,1).
\]

It explains (4) exactly.

It does not imply a global rational coboundary.  The reported nullity-zero search through degree eleven says that no low-degree rational map to the homogeneous symmetric square has been found.  That is compatible with (18): formal equivalence at infinity is much weaker than global equivalence over \(\mathbb Q(N)\).

The integrated-elliptic interpretation suggests that the global object is instead a non-split normal-function extension.  Schematically one expects

\[
\boxed{
0\longrightarrow \mathbf1_{\sigma=-3}
\longrightarrow \mathcal M_{25}^{\sharp}
\longrightarrow \mathcal E
\longrightarrow0,
}
\tag{19}
\]

where \(\mathcal E\) is the rank-two elliptic period module carrying the two outer roots \(\lambda_+,\rho\), and the neutral line records an integrated period.  The endpoint of that primitive is Catalan's constant.

Equation (19) is the correct conceptual replacement for the statement that the CMF is literally a tensor square.  It is still a conjectural global description until an explicit block gauge, differential factor, or matrix-AZ certificate is written down.

# 7. The smallest certificates that would complete convergence

A full closed-form integral for every entry of the matrix product is not necessary.  Any one of the following is sufficient.

## 7.1 Mixed-cycle Ore certificate

Produce a rational Ore operator \(T\) and rational prefactors showing that the normalized error is a finite combination of the mixed and recessive cycles:

\[
\widehat E_{N,j}
=T_0(N)V_N^0+T_-(N)V_N^-,
\]

while \(\widehat Q_{N,j}\) has a nonzero \(V_N^+\) component.  Equations (7)--(11) then prove the rate.

## 7.2 Block-extension certificate

Find a rational or algebraic basis in which

\[
U(N)^{-1}M_H(N)U(N+1)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
A_{\mathrm{ell}}(N)&0\\
\eta(N)&r_0(N)
\end{pmatrix},
\]

with a two-dimensional elliptic block and an explicit extension cocycle \(\eta\).  Summing the cocycle must reproduce the integral of the elliptic period whose endpoint is \(G\).

## 7.3 Generating-function singularity cancellation

Let

\[
\mathcal Q_j(z)=\sum_{N\ge0}\widehat Q_{N,j}z^N,
\qquad
\mathcal E_j(z)=\sum_{N\ge0}\widehat E_{N,j}z^N.
\]

It is enough to prove:

- \(\mathcal Q_j\) has a nonzero dominant singular term at \(z=\rho\);
- \(\mathcal E_j\) is analytic through \(z=\rho\) and has its first singularity at \(z=1\), with local exponent corresponding to \(N^{-3}\).

Coefficient asymptotics then give (14).

## 7.4 Direct two-sided estimates

For convergence alone, the minimal analytic target is simply

\[
|\widehat E_{N,j}|\le C N^{-3},
\qquad
|\widehat Q_{N,j}|\ge c\lambda_+^N
\]

for all sufficiently large \(N\).  The second inequality can plausibly be obtained by a cone/Perron argument for the balanced matrix.  The first is the genuinely missing cancellation certificate.

The existing degree-one moment formula proves neither estimate.

# Final answer to the three sub-questions

## (a) Nesterenko-type contour

There is an explicit gamma contour for the canonical Legendre carrier, namely (15), and a mixed contour/real integral for its neutral mode, namely (16).  No exact map from the printed CMF remainder to that contour is currently known.  Such a map would itself solve the problem.

## (b) Double integral with silver-ratio phase

Yes, the symmetric-square carrier has the explicit phase (17), and the neutral mixed cycle has the explicit double integral (6).  After dominant normalization, its kernel is bounded by \(\rho\), as shown in (7)--(9).  What is missing is the finite-\(N\) identity identifying the challenge error with this mixed cycle.

## (c) Correct almost-symmetric-square decomposition

The exact formal relation is the shearing (18), with partial indices \((1,-2,1)\).  Globally, the expected object is not the homogeneous symmetric square but a nontrivial integrated-period extension of an elliptic rank-two module by a neutral line, as in (19).  Catalan's constant is the extension/connection coefficient.

Therefore the requested representation of \(R_N(s)\) with a uniform \(O(\rho^N)\) bound does not exist as stated.  The correct and genuinely attainable target is an explicit mixed-cycle representation of

\[
GQ_N-P_N
\]

or of its dominant-normalized version.  Equations (6)--(11) give the exact model and the desired bound; one finite Ore, block-gauge, generating-function, or matrix-AZ certificate is still needed to attach that model to the printed CMF.
