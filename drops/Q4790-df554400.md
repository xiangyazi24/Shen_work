# Problem 2.7: what Cooper level 11 actually gives, and what is still needed for a \(\zeta(3)\) limit

## Executive verdict

The proposed proof cannot start from the premises exactly as written, because three different objects have been conflated.

1. The tuple
   \[
   (a,b,c,d,e)=(10,4,-56,-8,22)
   \]
   is Cooper's parameter tuple for a **four-term, order-three recurrence**. It is not by itself a \(3\times3\) CMF matrix.

2. Cooper's level-11 recurrence is
   \[
   \boxed{
   \begin{aligned}
   (n+1)^3T_{n+1}={}&2(2n+1)(5n^2+5n+2)T_n\\
   &-8n(7n^2+1)T_{n-1}\\
   &+22n(2n-1)(n-1)T_{n-2},
   \end{aligned}}
   \tag{1}
   \]
   with
   \[
   T_0=1,\qquad T_1=4,\qquad T_2=28,\qquad T_3=268.
   \tag{2}
   \]
   The recurrence quoted in the question,
   \[
   n^3u_n=(2n-1)(11n^2-11n+3)u_{n-1}+(n-1)^3u_{n-2},
   \tag{3}
   \]
   is **not** Cooper level 11. It mixes the coefficient \(11n^2+11n+3\) from Zagier's level-5 family with a cubic Apéry template. With \(u_0=1,u_1=3\), it already gives
   \[
   u_2=\frac{3\cdot25\cdot3+1}{8}=\frac{113}{4},
   \]
   so it is not the claimed self-starting integer level-11 sequence. The genuine level-5 recurrence containing that quadratic is
   \[
   (n+1)^2t_{n+1}=(11n^2+11n+3)t_n+n^2t_{n-1}.
   \tag{4}
   \]

3. The identity
   \[
   16P_{27}\!\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{4}\right)
   =-C_{11}(t)C_{11}(4-t)
   \tag{5}
   \]
   is an exact **characteristic-polynomial identity**, where
   \[
   C_{11}(t)=t^3-20t^2+56t-44
   \tag{6}
   \]
   is Cooper's Poincaré polynomial and
   \[
   P_{27}(\mu)=4\mu^3-220\mu^2+8\mu-1
   \tag{7}
   \]
   is the normalized challenge Poincaré polynomial. Neither polynomial is a Hauptmodul. The actual level-11 modular coordinate is the function \(X(\tau)\) described below, and \(X_0(11)\) has genus one, so there is no genus-zero Hauptmodul in the usual sense.

The exact consequence is therefore:

\[
\boxed{
\text{Problem 2.7 is spectrally a quadratic image of Cooper level 11.}
}
\tag{8}
\]

That statement alone does **not** prove an Ore-module equivalence, does not identify an Apéry companion solution, and does not force a limit in \(\mathbf Q+\mathbf Q\zeta(3)\).

A proof of such a limit requires two extra certificates:

- an exact finite-\(n\) transform from the Cooper module to the CMF scalar module;
- an explicit mixed/Eichler extension whose connection coefficient is a rational multiple of \(\zeta(3)\), together with a dominant-mode cancellation argument.

No such pair of certificates is currently supplied by Cooper's paper or by the spectral identity.

# 1. Cooper's precise level-11 modular object

Cooper's general four-term template is

\[
\begin{aligned}
(n+1)^3T_{n+1}={}&(2n+1)(an^2+an+b)T_n\\
&+n(cn^2+d)T_{n-1}\\
&+en(2n-1)(n-1)T_{n-2}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{9}
\]

Putting

\[
(a,b,c,d,e)=(10,4,-56,-8,22)
\]

gives (1).

The modular parametrization is

\[
Z_{11}(\tau)=\Theta_{1,1,3}(\tau)^2,
\qquad
\Theta_{1,1,3}(\tau)=
\sum_{j,k\in\mathbf Z}q^{j^2+jk+3k^2},
\tag{10}
\]

and

\[
X_{11}(\tau)=
\frac{\eta(\tau)^2\eta(11\tau)^2}{Z_{11}(\tau)}.
\tag{11}
\]

Near the cusp \(q=0\),

\[
Z_{11}(\tau)=\sum_{n\ge0}T_nX_{11}(\tau)^n.
\tag{12}
\]

Cooper's genus-one branch data are

\[
B^2=1-20X+56X^2-44X^3,
\qquad
H=4X(1-8X+11X^2).
\tag{13}
\]

The characteristic polynomial (6) is obtained from the leading part of (1). Cooper's paper proves the modular parametrization, recurrence, integrality and several congruence/asymptotic properties. It does **not** construct a second solution whose quotient with \(T_n\) is \(\zeta(3)\), and it does not state a Beukers-type \(\zeta(3)\) Apéry limit for this level-11 sequence.

Primary reference: Shaun Cooper, *Apéry-like sequences defined by four-term recurrence relations*, arXiv:2302.00757v2.

# 2. What the spectral identity proves—and what it cannot prove

Let \(t_1,t_2,t_3\) be the roots of \(C_{11}\). Equation (5) shows that

\[
\mu_j=\frac{(t_j-2)^2}{4}
\tag{14}
\]

are the roots of \(P_{27}\). In the raw challenge normalization the corresponding exponential roots are

\[
\rho_j=\frac{\mu_j}{64}
=rac{(t_j-2)^2}{256}.
\tag{15}
\]

This is a strong invariant at infinity. It does not determine connection constants.

Indeed, let \(q_n\) be any solution with a nonzero dominant coefficient, and let \(e_n\) be a recessive solution. For every complex number \(L\), the sequence

\[
p_n^{(L)}=Lq_n+e_n
\tag{16}
\]

satisfies the same recurrence and obeys

\[
\frac{p_n^{(L)}}{q_n}\longrightarrow L.
\tag{17}
\]

Thus the homogeneous operator and its Poincaré roots are compatible with **every** possible limit. The arithmetic constant is encoded in the initial vector and in a connection matrix, not in the characteristic polynomial.

This observation rules out the argument

\[
\text{level-11 spectrum}\Longrightarrow
\text{limit in }\mathbf Q+\mathbf Q\zeta(3).
\]

The missing information is exactly the extension/connection class.

# 3. Part (a): the exact intertwiner is not presently known

## 3.1 Why a direct finite scalar Ore gauge is the wrong object

A finite rational Ore operator

\[
T=t_0(n)+t_1(n)S+t_2(n)S^2,
\qquad t_j(n)\in\mathbf Q(n),
\tag{18}
\]

acting on a formal solution

\[
y_n\sim t^n n^\alpha
\]

preserves the exponential base \(t\), except when its leading symbol vanishes on that particular branch. A scalar hypergeometric gauge multiplies all surviving bases by one common scalar. It cannot send three bases through the nonlinear map

\[
t\longmapsto\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}.
\tag{19}
\]

Therefore the quadratic spectral map cannot be implemented by an ordinary finite scalar Ore conjugacy between Cooper's recurrence and the challenge recurrence.

The correct transform, if the spectral observation lifts to finite \(n\), must be nonlocal: a binomial/integral transform, a pullback on generating functions, or a matrix correspondence.

## 3.2 The explicit candidate centered-binomial transform

The natural kernel realizing (19) on exponential modes is

\[
\boxed{
(\mathcal KT)_n=
\frac1{256^n}
\sum_{k=0}^{2n}
\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}T_k.
}
\tag{20}
\]

For \(T_k=t^k\), the binomial theorem gives

\[
(\mathcal KT)_n=
\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}\right)^n.
\tag{21}
\]

Thus (20) explains the spectral identity exactly.

However, \(\mathcal K\) is not a finite Ore operator in \(n\), and its even-section form is not invertible by itself. Introduce the odd companion

\[
W_n=rac1{256^n}
\sum_{k=0}^{2n+1}
\binom{2n+1}{k}(-2)^{2n+1-k}T_k,
\tag{22}
\]

and put \(V_n=(\mathcal KT)_n\). If \(E\) shifts \(n\), while \(S\) shifts the Cooper index \(k\), then the exact parity transform satisfies

\[
\begin{pmatrix}V\\W\end{pmatrix}_{ST}
=
\mathsf S
\begin{pmatrix}V\\W\end{pmatrix}_{T},
\qquad
\mathsf S=
\begin{pmatrix}
2&1\\
256E&2
\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{23}
\]

If \(N\) denotes multiplication by the Cooper index \(k\), then

\[
\begin{pmatrix}V\\W\end{pmatrix}_{NT}
=
\mathsf N
\begin{pmatrix}V\\W\end{pmatrix}_{T},
\qquad
\mathsf N=
\begin{pmatrix}
2n&\dfrac{n}{64}E^{-1}\\[2mm]
4n+2&2n+1
\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{24}
\]

These matrices satisfy

\[
\mathsf S\mathsf N=(\mathsf N+I)\mathsf S,
\tag{25}
\]

so substituting \(S\mapsto\mathsf S\), \(N\mapsto\mathsf N\) into Cooper's forward operator gives an exact \(2\times2\) matrix Ore image. Eliminating \(W\) produces a scalar annihilator \(L_K\) for \(V\).

The finite certificate still required is then one of:

\[
L_{27}=\rho(n)\,gL_Kg^{-1},
\tag{26}
\]

or more generally

\[
L_{27}T=RL_K
\tag{27}
\]

with explicit rational Ore operators \(T,R\).

That comparison has not yet been established.

## 3.3 The creative-telescoping form of the missing certificate

Let

\[
K(n,k)=256^{-n}\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}.
\tag{28}
\]

An exact proof that the transformed Cooper sequence satisfies the challenge operator would be a mixed-Ore identity

\[
\boxed{
L_{27}^{(n)}K(n,k)
=U(n,k)L_C^{*,(k)}+\Delta_kV(n,k),
}
\tag{29}
\]

where \(L_C^*\) is the adjoint Cooper operator in the summation variable. Summing (29) over \(k\), checking boundary terms, and using \(L_CT=0\) would prove

\[
L_{27}(\mathcal KT)=0.
\]

This is the correct notion of intertwiner for the quadratic spectral transform.

## 3.4 Standard rational-module computation after the transform

Once \(L_K\) is known, a rational homomorphism to the CMF scalar module is computed by solving either

\[
L_{27}T=RL_K,
\qquad \operatorname{ord}T<3,
\tag{30}
\]

or the companion-matrix equation

\[
U(n+1)A_K(n)=A_{27}(n)U(n).
\tag{31}
\]

Equation (31) is a linear difference system for the nine entries of \(U(n)\). Rational solutions are the horizontal sections of

\[
A_{27}\otimes A_K^\vee.
\]

An invertible rational solution proves gauge equivalence; a rank-one or rank-two solution proves only a submodule or quotient relation.

Without the complete primitive CMF scalar operator and without the scalar elimination of (23)–(24), there is no unique explicit intertwiner to print.

# 4. Part (b): how \(\zeta(3)\) can enter

## 4.1 It is not a derivative with respect to the level

The level \(11\) is discrete. There is no canonical operation

\[
\frac{\partial}{\partial N}\bigg|_{N=11}
\]

on modular forms of level \(N\).

A parameter derivative can be useful only after embedding the recurrence or integral into a genuine continuous family, for example:

- a deformation of a hypergeometric exponent;
- a deformation of a local Frobenius exponent;
- an auxiliary parameter in a rational integral kernel.

Differentiating such a family can create logarithms and harmonic sums, and a third derivative can produce \(\zeta(3)\). But that family must be explicitly constructed; it is not supplied by the phrase "differentiate the level".

## 4.2 The natural modular mechanism is a weight-four Eichler integral

Let

\[
f(\tau)=\sum_{m\ge0}a_mq^m
\in M_4(\Gamma_0(11)).
\]

Its Eichler integral is, up to polynomial terms,

\[
\mathcal E_f(\tau)=
\sum_{m\ge1}\frac{a_m}{m^3}q^m,
\tag{32}
\]

and satisfies

\[
\left(q\frac{d}{dq}\right)^3\mathcal E_f=f-a_0.
\tag{33}
\]

Under the Fricke involution \(W_{11}\), the difference between the two branches of \(\mathcal E_f\) is a quadratic period polynomial. Its coefficients are critical \(L\)-values of \(f\), including \(L(f,3)\).

This is the standard modular source of a \(\zeta(3)\) connection coefficient.

## 4.3 An explicit level-11 Eisenstein source of \(\zeta(3)\)

Take the Fricke-odd weight-four Eisenstein combination

\[
\boxed{
f_{11}^{-}(\tau)=E_4(\tau)-11^2E_4(11\tau).
}
\tag{34}
\]

Ignoring the constant term in the usual Dirichlet-series convention, its coefficients have \(L\)-series

\[
\boxed{
L(f_{11}^{-},s)
=240\bigl(1-11^{2-s}\bigr)\zeta(s)\zeta(s-3).
}
\tag{35}
\]

At \(s=3\), since \(\zeta(0)=-1/2\),

\[
\boxed{
L(f_{11}^{-},3)
=-\frac{1200}{11}\,\zeta(3).
}
\tag{36}
\]

Thus a level-11 Eisenstein Eichler extension gives a rational multiple of \(\zeta(3)\) completely explicitly.

The rational \(1\)-component arises from the polynomial ambiguity/normalization of the Eichler integral and from adding the holomorphic homogeneous solution. Therefore an extension solution can naturally have connection coefficient

\[
\alpha+\beta\zeta(3),
\qquad \alpha,\beta\in\mathbf Q.
\tag{37}
\]

## 4.4 The decisive cusp-form obstruction

The space \(M_4(\Gamma_0(11))\) also has a cusp-form part. If the forcing modular form has a nonzero cusp component \(f_{\mathrm{cusp}}\), then its Eichler integral contributes critical values such as

\[
L(f_{\mathrm{cusp}},3),
\]

which are not generally rational multiples of \(\zeta(3)\).

Therefore, to prove that the CMF limit lies specifically in

\[
\mathbf Q+\mathbf Q\zeta(3),
\]

one must prove one of the following:

1. the extension class is purely Eisenstein;
2. the cusp component vanishes under the selected boundary map;
3. cusp periods cancel between the numerator and denominator constructions.

This is an exact modular-form decomposition test. Spectral level-11 data do not decide it.

# 5. A precise conditional convergence theorem

Let \(L_{27}\) be the primitive order-three challenge operator, and assume its Poincaré roots have a unique dominant modulus. Let \(q_n,p_n\) be the selected denominator and numerator solutions.

Suppose the following are proved.

### Hypothesis I: exact transformed modular basis

There is a finite or summation-kernel equivalence carrying a modular basis to a basis

\[
Y^{(0)}_n,\quad Y^{(1)}_n,\quad Y^{(2)}_n
\]

of the challenge module, with \(Y^{(0)}\) the dominant solution.

### Hypothesis II: Eisenstein extension

There is an inhomogeneous or extended solution \(P_n\) whose modular pullback is an Eichler integral of a weight-four form with purely Eisenstein critical value, so that its dominant connection coefficient is

\[
\alpha+\beta\zeta(3),
\qquad \alpha,\beta\in\mathbf Q.
\]

### Hypothesis III: initial-vector identification

The CMF initial data satisfy

\[
q_n=A\,Y_n^{(0)}+	ext{recessive modes},
\tag{38}
\]

and

\[
p_n=A(\alpha+\beta\zeta(3))Y_n^{(0)}
+	ext{recessive modes},
\tag{39}
\]

with \(A\ne0\).

Then

\[
\boxed{
\frac{p_n}{q_n}
\longrightarrow
\alpha+\beta\zeta(3).
}
\tag{40}
\]

### Proof

Subtract the dominant multiple:

\[
e_n=p_n-(\alpha+\beta\zeta(3))q_n.
\]

By (38)–(39), the dominant component cancels, so \(e_n\) is a combination only of recessive modes. Poincaré–Perron/Birkhoff–Trjitzinsky asymptotics then give

\[
\frac{e_n}{q_n}\to0.
\]

Hence (40) follows.

This theorem isolates the exact work needed. The spectral identity contributes only to Hypothesis I at the level of formal roots; it does not prove any of the three hypotheses.

# 6. Part (c): is there a known Beukers-type integral for Cooper level 11?

## 6.1 The universal \(\zeta(3)\) integral

There is of course the classical Beukers/Hadjicostas kernel

\[
\boxed{
\zeta(3)
=-\frac12
\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
}
\tag{41}
\]

This evaluates \(\zeta(3)\), but it does not automatically produce Cooper's recurrence.

## 6.2 What a level-11 Beukers proof would require

One would need explicit rational or polynomial functions \(R_n(x,y)\) such that

\[
I_n=
\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
R_n(x,y)
\frac{-\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy
\tag{42}
\]

has the form

\[
I_n=A_n+B_n\zeta(3),
\tag{43}
\]

and creative telescoping proves that \(A_n\) and \(B_n\) satisfy the Cooper or transformed challenge recurrence.

The required certificate is

\[
L^{(n)}R_n(x,y)
=\partial_xU_n(x,y)+\partial_yV_n(x,y),
\tag{44}
\]

with boundary terms under control.

Cooper's paper does not provide such a kernel for level 11, and I did not find a published Beukers-type level-11 integral that gives the self-starting sequence (1) and a \(\zeta(3)\) companion. The published level-11 results concern the modular parametrization, recurrence, congruences, asymptotics, and Ramanujan–Sato-type \(1/\pi\) phenomena—not a proved \(\zeta(3)\) Apéry limit.

## 6.3 The modular Eichler route is currently more natural

For level 11 the most structured route is:

1. start with the modular coordinate \(X_{11}(\tau)\) and holomorphic solution \(Z_{11}(\tau)\);
2. choose the appropriate weight-four Eisenstein form, for example (34);
3. construct its Eichler integral \(\mathcal E_{11}\);
4. express \(\mathcal E_{11}\) as a function of \(X_{11}\) and derive its inhomogeneous differential equation;
5. expand it at \(X=0\), obtaining a companion coefficient sequence;
6. transport the pair through the exact binomial/CMF transform;
7. compute the Fricke connection formula, whose \(L(f,3)\) term is rational times \(\zeta(3)\);
8. verify the first three CMF initial values and prove dominant-mode cancellation.

This is a complete proof architecture. The genuinely missing calculations are the exact finite transform and the identification of the CMF boundary map with the Eisenstein extension.

# 7. How to test the conjecture computationally without circularity

The following sequence of exact computations would settle the issue.

## Step 1: fix the primitive challenge operator

Extract the full order-three scalar operator from the \(3\times3\) CMF by Casorati minors and divide by its polynomial content. Record the exact index convention.

## Step 2: derive the scalar operator of the centered transform

Use the parity matrices (23)–(24), substitute them into Cooper's operator, and eliminate the odd component \(W\). Alternatively use creative telescoping with kernel (28).

## Step 3: compare difference modules

Solve

\[
U(n+1)A_K(n)=A_{27}(n)U(n)
\]

over \(\mathbf Q(n)\), or over the smallest necessary algebraic extension. If \(U\) is invertible, the homogeneous modules are equivalent. If no rational solution exists, the level-11 relation remains only spectral.

## Step 4: compute a modular companion

Let

\[
\mathcal E_{11}(\tau)
=\sum_{m\ge1}\frac{a_m}{m^3}q^m
\]

for the Eisenstein form (34). Rewrite it in the coordinate \(X_{11}\), derive the inhomogeneous recurrence for its Taylor coefficients, and determine the connection constant using the Fricke involution.

## Step 5: compare initial vectors

Transport the holomorphic and Eichler solutions through the exact transform and solve a finite \(3\times3\) linear system matching the CMF initial data. This determines \(\alpha,\beta\) exactly.

## Step 6: prove the limit

Use the three Poincaré roots and the nonzero dominant coefficient to show that the error solution is recessive.

None of these steps can be replaced by characteristic-polynomial matching alone.

# 8. Direct answers

## (a) What is the exact intertwiner?

No exact finite rational Ore intertwiner between the challenge scalar operator and Cooper's level-11 operator has been established from the data given. A direct scalar Ore gauge is incompatible with the nonlinear root map.

The exact candidate transform explaining the spectral identity is the nonlocal centered-binomial kernel (20). Its finite algebraic realization is the two-component parity system (23)–(24). The missing certificate is either the creative-telescoping identity (29) or a rational module equivalence between the scalar elimination of that system and the primitive challenge operator.

## (b) How does \(\zeta(3)\) enter?

Not by differentiating the discrete level. The natural mechanism is a weight-four Eisenstein Eichler integral. The explicit level-11 form

\[
E_4(\tau)-121E_4(11\tau)
\]

has

\[
L(f,3)=-\frac{1200}{11}\zeta(3).
\]

A Fricke connection formula for its Eichler integral produces the \(\zeta(3)\) coefficient. To obtain only \(\mathbf Q+\mathbf Q\zeta(3)\), one must prove that the cusp-form component of the extension vanishes or cancels.

## (c) Is there a known Beukers-type level-11 integral?

The universal Beukers integral (41) is known, but no published kernel is presently identified that simultaneously:

- produces Cooper's self-starting level-11 sequence;
- produces a companion linear form in \(1\) and \(\zeta(3)\);
- telescopes to recurrence (1).

The modular Eichler-integral route is currently the more canonical route. Constructing a level-11 Beukers kernel would itself be a substantial new result.
