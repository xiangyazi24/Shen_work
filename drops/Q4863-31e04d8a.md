## Executive conclusions

1. **The sequence (0.1) is not a solution of the printed Problem 2.7 recurrence in the challenge repository.** With the repository's convention and the initial values \(u_0=1,u_1=5,u_2=43\), the recurrence predicts
   \[

2. **The stated Poincaré roots are incorrect.** The polynomial
   \[
   F(\mu)=4\mu^3-220\mu^2+8\mu-1

   has negative discriminant and hence one real root and one nonreal conjugate pair, not three positive real roots.
3. Consequently, the nine terms, the cubic \(F\), and the phrase “degree-12 four-term recurrence” do not determine a unique sequence, differential equation, hypergeometric sum, or integral. A general order-three recurrence with four degree-at-most-12 coefficients has 52 scalar coefficients before normalization; the nine terms provide only six recurrence equations.
4. The **genuine, explicit level-11 object** is Cooper's sequence
   \[
   T_{11}(n)=1,4,28,268,3004,36784,\ldots,
   \]
   with an exact modular parametrization and a third-order Picard–Fuchs operator. Cooper explicitly states that no binomial-sum formula for this level-11 sequence is presently known.
5. The challenge Poincaré cubic is an exact quadratic spectral image of Cooper's cubic:
   \[
   16F\!\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{4}\right)
   =-H_{11}(t)H_{11}(4-t),
   \qquad
   H_{11}(t)=t^3-20t^2+56t-44.

   This supplies a rigorous **spectral carrier**, obtained by a centered binomial transform and even section. It does not identify either the printed challenge solutions or (0.1).
6. An exact double integral for the mixed error can always be written from the already-known numerator and denominator sequences, but that representation is circular. A useful proof still requires a non-circular level-11 normal-function/Eichler-integral certificate.
7. The irreducible cubic rules out a direct rational symmetric square and a rank-one twist of a lower-order rational operator. The known relation is a quadratic pullback/even-section construction, not an ordinary tensor decomposition.

The rest of the note gives the exact audits, the closest available modular and integral formulas, and complete Sage/`ore_algebra` code for the calculation once the intended recurrence is supplied.

## 1.1 Why nine terms and a Poincaré cubic are insufficient

Write a general recurrence as

\[
\sum_{j=0}^{3}p_j(n)u_{n+j}=0,
\qquad \deg p_j\le12.
\tag{1.5}
\]

There are \(4\cdot13=52\) polynomial coefficients, or 51 degrees of freedom after an overall scalar normalization. The values \(u_0,\ldots,u_8\) give only the six equations corresponding to \(n=0,\ldots,5\). Fixing the ratios of the four leading coefficients through a Poincaré polynomial imposes at most three further independent conditions. Generically, at least

\[
51-6-3=42
\]

degrees of freedom remain. Therefore neither a “minimal recurrence” nor a generating differential equation can be recovered uniquely from the supplied information.

# 3. What is known exactly at level 11

Cooper's level-11 sequence is defined by

\[
\begin{aligned}
(n+1)^3T_{n+1}={}&2(2n+1)(5n^2+5n+2)T_n\\
&-8n(7n^2+1)T_{n-1}
+22n(2n-1)(n-1)T_{n-2},
\end{aligned}
\tag{3.1}
\]

with \(T_0=1\). It begins

\[
1,4,28,268,3004,36784,476476,6418192,88986172,\ldots.
\tag{3.2}
\]

Its generating function

\[
Z(X)=\sum_{n\ge0}T_nX^n
\]

is modularly parametrized by

\[
\boxed{
Z(q)=
\left(\sum_{j,k\in\mathbb Z}q^{j^2+jk+3k^2}\right)^2,
\qquad
X(q)=\frac{\eta(\tau)^2\eta(11\tau)^2}{Z(q)}.
}
\tag{3.3}

The quadratic form has discriminant \(-11\), explaining the level and the common quadratic resolvent field.

The exact third-order differential operator is

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal L_{11}={}&\theta_X^3
-2X(2\theta_X+1)(5\theta_X^2+5\theta_X+2)\\
&+8X^2(\theta_X+1)\bigl(7(\theta_X+1)^2+1\bigr)\\
&-22X^3(\theta_X+1)(\theta_X+2)(2\theta_X+3),

Cooper notes explicitly that a binomial-sum formula for \(T_n\) is unknown. Therefore even for the genuine level-11 carrier, one should not currently promise a finite balanced \({}_pF_q\) or a Legendre/Jacobi polynomial formula.

# 4. Integral representations

## 4.1 Modular coefficient integral for the genuine level-11 sequence

Equation (3.3) immediately gives a rigorous contour representation. For a small positively oriented loop around \(q=0\),

\[
\boxed{
T_n=\frac1{2\pi i}
\oint
\frac{Z(q)X'(q)}{X(q)^{n+1}}\,dq.
}
\tag{4.1}
\]

This is an exact period integral, though not an elementary Euler beta integral.

If a geometric period realization

\[
T_k=\int_\Gamma \Phi^k\,\omega
\tag{4.2}
\]

is chosen, then the binomial theorem turns (3.8) into

\[
\boxed{
W_n=\int_\Gamma
\left(\frac{(\Phi-2)^2}{256}\right)^n\omega.
}
\tag{4.3}
\]

Thus the exact spectral phase is

\[
\Psi=\frac{(\Phi-2)^2}{256}.
\tag{4.4}
\]

Formula (4.3) answers the phase question for the transformed Cooper carrier. The missing datum for the challenge is a rational weight, cycle, or Ore intertwiner that converts this carrier into the required finite-
\(n\) module and matches the initial conditions.

## 4.2 Why the requested Legendre/Jacobi kernel is not currently justified

An elementary formula of the form

\[
u_n=\int P_n(x)^2w(x)\,dx
\]

would normally imply a direct symmetric-square or classical orthogonal-polynomial structure. The cubic (2.1) has no rational root, while a direct rational symmetric square of a rank-two limiting matrix has eigenvalues

\[
r^2,\quad rs,\quad s^2
\]

and therefore the rational eigenvalue \(rs=\det A\). This is incompatible with the irreducibility of \(F\) over \(\mathbb Q\).

A representation after a nontrivial algebraic pullback, restriction of scalars, or extension is not ruled out. But it is precisely the additional certificate that must be constructed; it cannot be inferred from the first nine coefficients.

# 5. Generating differential equation

There are two separate answers.

## 5.1 For the genuine level-11 sequence

The answer is the third-order operator (3.4).

## 5.2 For the printed challenge recurrence

Recurrence order and differential order are different notions. A recurrence with four shifts and degree-12 coefficients does **not** automatically produce a third-order differential equation for its ordinary generating function. The differential order is controlled by the polynomial degrees after denominators are cleared.

Set \(m=n-2\) in (1.1):

\[
u_{m+3}-b(m)u_{m+2}+c(m)u_{m+1}-d(m)u_m=0,
\tag{5.1}
\]

where

\[
b(m)=\frac{B_{m+2}}{A_{m+2}},\qquad
c(m)=\frac{C_{m+1}}{A_{m+1}},\qquad
 d(m)=\frac{D_m}{A_m}.
\]

Choose a common denominator \(E(m)\) and put

\[
\begin{aligned}
p_3(m)&=E(m),\\
p_2(m)&=-E(m)b(m),\\
p_1(m)&=E(m)c(m),\\
p_0(m)&=-E(m)d(m).
\end{aligned}
\tag{5.2}
\]

Let

\[
F(z)=\sum_{n\ge0}u_nz^n,
\qquad \theta=z\frac d{dz}.
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{
\mathscr D=
 p_3(\theta-3)
+z p_2(\theta-2)
+z^2p_1(\theta-1)
+z^3p_0(\theta)
}
\tag{5.3}
\]

satisfies

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathscr DF={}&
\sum_{k=0}^{2}u_kp_3(k-3)z^k\\
&+z\sum_{k=0}^{1}u_kp_2(k-2)z^k
+z^2u_0p_1(-1).
\end{aligned}}
\tag{5.4}
\]

The right side is a startup polynomial. Multiplying on the left by

\[
\theta(\theta-1)\cdots(\theta-r),
\]

where \(r\) is its degree, gives a homogeneous annihilator.

For the rational recurrence (1.1), the naive common denominator can make the differential order as large as 36. A genuine rank-three Picard–Fuchs equation would have to appear as a certified right factor or quotient module. The Poincaré cubic alone does not produce that factor.

This also explains why identifying the recurrence with an entry in the AESZ Calabi–Yau table is premature. The classical AESZ table primarily concerns fourth-order MUM operators for Calabi–Yau threefolds. Cooper's level-11 object is a third-order modular/K3-type system; the printed challenge recurrence is a high-degree scalar presentation whose low-rank differential factor has not been exhibited.

# 6. Exact mixed-period error integral

Let

\[
L=\zeta(2)+\zeta(3).
\]

For any already-known pair of recurrence solutions \(P_n,Q_n\), define

\[
\boxed{
K_n^{\rm rec}(x,y;s)
=(xy)^s\left(\frac{P_n}{2}-\frac{Q_n}{1-xy}\right).
}
\tag{6.1}
\]

For \(\Re s>-1\),

\[
\begin{aligned}
I_n^{\rm rec}(s)
&=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1K_n^{\rm rec}(x,y;s)\,dx\,dy\\
&=-Q_n\zeta_H(2,s+1)+\frac{P_n}{2(s+1)^2}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{6.2}
\]

Since

\[
\zeta_H(2,1)=\zeta(2),
\qquad
\left.\frac d{ds}\zeta_H(2,s+1)\right|_{s=0}
=-2\zeta(3),
\]

we get

\[
\boxed{
I_n^{\rm rec}(0)-\frac12(I_n^{\rm rec})'(0)
=P_n-LQ_n.
}
\tag{6.3}
\]

Equivalently,

\[
LQ_n-P_n
=-I_n^{\rm rec}(0)+\frac12(I_n^{\rm rec})'(0).
\]

This is exact and satisfies the recurrence pointwise because its coefficients are \(P_n,Q_n\). It is nevertheless **circular** as a proof of convergence: the unknown approximation system has merely been inserted into the kernel.

A non-circular proof needs a family

\[
\widetilde K_n(x,y;s)
=(xy)^s\,R_n(x,y)/(1-xy)
\]

constructed independently from a hypergeometric or modular phase, together with a creative-telescoping identity

\[
\mathcal L_n\widetilde K_n
=\partial_x(U_n\widetilde K_n)
 +\partial_y(V_n\widetilde K_n),
\tag{6.4}
\]

initial matching, and a saddle/contour estimate. Because the target mixes weights two and three, the required object is naturally a modular normal function or an Eisenstein/Eichler extension of the homogeneous level-11 local system rather than one pure K3 period.

# 7. Symmetric square, tensor product, and Hadamard product tests

## 7.1 Direct symmetric square

A direct symmetric square over \(\mathbb Q(n)\) is ruled out at infinity by the rational middle eigenvalue argument above. The irreducible cubic has no rational root.

## 7.2 Tensor product

A rank-three module cannot be a nontrivial tensor product of two modules of ranks greater than one, since ranks multiply. The only rank factorization is \(3=1\cdot3\), namely a rank-one twist. A hypergeometric rank-one twist multiplies all Poincaré roots by the same limiting constant. It cannot realize

\[
t\longmapsto\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}.
\]

## 7.3 Hadamard product

A Hadamard product with a rank-one hypergeometric series again multiplies coefficient asymptotics by one common exponential constant. It therefore has the same obstruction. A higher-rank Hadamard product can have more complicated rank reduction, but no such factorization is implied by (3.6), and none is identified by the nine terms.

The operation actually visible in the spectrum is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{translation/binomial transform}
\;\longrightarrow\;
\text{even section}
\;\longrightarrow\;
\text{scaling}.
}
\]

That operation is holonomic but is not an ordinary scalar gauge or coefficientwise Hadamard product.

# 8. Complete Sage/`ore_algebra` audit code

The following code verifies the mismatch, constructs the recurrence operator, attempts Ore factorization, and builds the exact generating-function operator (5.3). It uses exact rational arithmetic.

```python
from sage.all import *
from ore_algebra import OreAlgebra

# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
# Printed Problem 2.7 data
# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
Pn.<n> = PolynomialRing(QQ)
Kn = Pn.fraction_field()
Rn.<Sn> = OreAlgebra(Kn)


def A(k):
    return 1024*(2*k+5)^4*(2*k+7)^3*(2*k+9)^3 * (
        946*k^2 + 6407*k + 10860
    )


def B(k):
    return 128*(2*k+7)^3*(2*k+9)^3 * (
        104060*k^6 + 1745370*k^5 + 12145238*k^4
        + 44886481*k^3 + 92943995*k^2
        + 102256019*k + 46709052
    )


def C(k):
    return 16*(k+3)^4*(2*k+9)^3 * (
        3784*k^5 + 57792*k^4 + 351019*k^3
        + 1059230*k^2 + 1587211*k + 944620
    )


def D(k):
    return (k+3)^4*(k+4)^6*(946*k^2 + 4515*k + 5399)

# Recurrence in m-indexed form:
# u[m+3] - b(m)u[m+2] + c(m)u[m+1] - d(m)u[m] = 0.
b = Kn(B(n+2) / A(n+2))
c = Kn(C(n+1) / A(n+1))
d = Kn(D(n) / A(n))

Lrec = Sn^3 - b*Sn^2 + c*Sn - d
print('Rational recurrence operator:')
print(Lrec)
print('\nOre factorization over Q(n):')
print(Lrec.factor())

# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
# Exact sequence mismatch
# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
u = [1, 5, 43, 491, 6693, 104067, 1791019, 33428723, 665881181]

for m in range(len(u)-3):
    residual = (
        QQ(u[m+3])
        - QQ(B(m+2))/QQ(A(m+2))*u[m+2]
        + QQ(C(m+1))/QQ(A(m+1))*u[m+1]
        - QQ(D(m))/QQ(A(m))*u[m]
    )
    print(f'm={m}, residual={residual}')

# Exact finite-field certificate for the first failure.
p = 101
F = GF(p)
predicted_mod_p = (
    F(B(2))/F(A(2))*F(43)
    - F(C(1))/F(A(1))*F(5)
    + F(D(0))/F(A(0))
)
print('predicted u3 mod 101 =', predicted_mod_p)
print('actual    u3 mod 101 =', F(491))

# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
# Clear recurrence denominators minimally
# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
rat = [-d, c, -b, Kn(1)]  # coefficients of u[m],...,u[m+3]
den = lcm([r.denominator() for r in rat])
pcoef = [Pn(den*r) for r in rat]
assert all(x in Pn for x in pcoef)

print('\ncommon denominator degree =', den.degree())
print('cleared coefficient degrees =', [x.degree() for x in pcoef])

# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
# Differential operator for F(z)=sum u_n z^n
# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
Pz.<z> = PolynomialRing(QQ)
Kz = Pz.fraction_field()
Rd.<Dz> = OreAlgebra(Kz)
theta = z*Dz


def eval_at_theta(poly, shift):
    """Evaluate poly(n) at theta-shift in the differential Ore ring."""
    poly = Pn(poly)
    return sum(Kz(poly[i]) * (theta-shift)^i
               for i in range(poly.degree()+1))

p0, p1, p2, p3 = pcoef
Dop = (
    eval_at_theta(p3, 3)
    + z*eval_at_theta(p2, 2)
    + z^2*eval_at_theta(p1, 1)
    + z^3*eval_at_theta(p0, 0)
)

print('\ninhomogeneous generating-function operator:')
print(Dop)
print('differential order =', Dop.order())

u0, u1, u2 = map(QQ, u[:3])
rhs = (
    u0*p3(-3)
    + u1*p3(-2)*z
    + u2*p3(-1)*z^2
    + z*(u0*p2(-2) + u1*p2(-1)*z)
    + z^2*u0*p1(-1)
)
print('\nstartup polynomial RHS =', rhs)

# A homogeneous annihilator is obtained by killing the startup polynomial.
if rhs == 0:
    H = Dop
else:
    killer = Rd(1)
    for j in range(rhs.degree()+1):
        killer *= (theta-j)
    H = killer*Dop

print('\nhomogeneous annihilator order =', H.order())
print('homogeneous factorization:')
print(H.factor())
```

For a different intended recurrence, replace only `A`, `B`, `C`, `D` and the indexing convention. At least 55 exact terms are advisable for independently guessing an order-three degree-12 operator, followed by substantial holdout verification.

# 9. What would complete the problem

A complete constructive solution now requires one of the following finite objects.

1. **The exact recurrence actually generating (0.1).** This is the minimum missing input.
2. **A hypergeometric certificate:** a finite sum for \(u_n\) and a creative-telescoping identity yielding that recurrence.
3. **A modular/Ore certificate:** rational functions \(r_0,r_1,r_2\) such that
   \[
   F_u(z)=r_0(z)\mathcal W(z)
         +r_1(z)\theta\mathcal W(z)
         +r_2(z)\theta^2\mathcal W(z),
   \]
   together with exact operator equality and enough initial coefficients.
4. **A normal-function certificate:** an explicit level-11 Eichler integral whose homogeneous part is the Cooper carrier and whose extension period is \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\).
5. **A Beukers certificate:** an independently defined kernel satisfying (6.4), initial matching, and a decay estimate.

Without one of these, a proposed binomial sum or elementary integral would be an unsupported guess.
