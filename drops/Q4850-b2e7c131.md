# Problem 2.5: the Birkhoff connection matrix, the exact dominant projector, and the missing Catalan period evaluation

## Executive verdict

The Birkhoff connection formalism gives a completely rigorous **equivalence**:

\[
\boxed{
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{P_{n,j}}{Q_{n,j}}=G
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
(\mathbf p_0-G\mathbf q_0)\,w_+(0)=0,
}
\tag{E1}
\]

provided the denominator has nonzero dominant coefficient and the selected coordinate of the dominant mode is nonzero. Here

\[
\mathbf p_0=(30921,-32972,8240),
\qquad
\mathbf q_0=(33750,-36000,9000),
\tag{E2}
\]

and \(w_+(n)\) is the adjoint solution dual to the dominant forward Birkhoff mode.

What Birkhoff–Trjitzinsky theory does **not** do is evaluate the global connection vector \(w_+(0)\). It constructs its asymptotic normalization at infinity and proves that backward transport converges to a well-defined vector at \(n=0\), but the resulting coordinates are global connection constants. Polynomial or rational recurrence coefficients do not make those constants algebraic.

With the convention below, let \(\mathcal F_n\) be the canonical Birkhoff fundamental matrix whose rows are the dominant, neutral, and recessive forward solutions, and put

\[
\mathcal C:=\mathcal F_0^{-1}
=\bigl(w_+(0),w_0(0),w_-(0)\bigr).
\tag{E3}
\]

Then the two challenge rows have Birkhoff coefficient matrix

\[
A\mathcal C,
\qquad
A=\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf p_0\\ \mathbf q_0\end{pmatrix},
\tag{E4}
\]

and therefore

\[
\boxed{
G=\frac{(A\mathcal C)_{1,+}}{(A\mathcal C)_{2,+}}
=\frac{\mathbf p_0w_+(0)}{\mathbf q_0w_+(0)}.
}
\tag{E5}
\]

Equation (E5) is the exact connection-coefficient statement that the 150-digit computation is measuring. It is not yet an exact evaluation of that quotient.

There is no universal Christoffel–Darboux or spectrum-only mechanism forcing the quotient to be \(G\). Keeping the same matrix cocycle and replacing

\[
\mathbf p_0\longmapsto \mathbf p_0+r\mathbf q_0
\]

changes the limit from \(G\) to \(G+r\) while leaving the Poincaré polynomial, the determinant, and the CMF matrices unchanged. The value of the limit is global boundary data, not local spectral data.

A complete proof can be closed in either of two equivalent ways:

1. **evaluate the dominant adjoint period exactly**, by constructing a vector-valued integral for \(w_+(n)\) and proving
   \[
   \mathbf p_0w_+(0)=\kappa G,
   \qquad
   \mathbf q_0w_+(0)=\kappa\ne0;
   \]
2. **prove an exact subdominant integral bound for the remainder row**
   \[
   \mathbf r_n:=\mathbf p_n-G\mathbf q_n,
   \]
   and use the invariant adjoint pairing to force its dominant coefficient to vanish.

The matrix Almkvist–Zeilberger program of Q4838/Q4846 is aimed precisely at constructing such a period or remainder certificate. Until its carrier, first-parameter jet, boundary terms, and initial matching are verified, the Birkhoff computation remains an extraordinarily strong numerical identification rather than the missing exact proof.

# 1. Normalize and balance the cocycle

Let the raw row trajectories satisfy

\[
X_{n+1}=X_nM(n).
\tag{1.1}
\]

Use the determinant-adapted Pochhammer factor

\[
H_n=(-16)^n(2)_n^2(3)_n^2
\left(\frac52\right)_n
\left(\frac72\right)_n^2,
\tag{1.2}
\]

with

\[
\delta(n):=\frac{H_{n+1}}{H_n}
=-2(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(2n+5)(2n+7)^2,
\tag{1.3}
\]

and the balancing shear

\[
D_n=\operatorname{diag}(1,n+1,(n+1)^2).
\tag{1.4}
\]

Write

\[
X_n=H_n\widehat X_nD_n^{-1}.
\]

Then

\[
\widehat X_{n+1}=\widehat X_n\overline M(n),
\qquad
\boxed{
\overline M(n)=D_n^{-1}\frac{M(n)}{\delta(n)}D_{n+1}.
}
\tag{1.5}
\]

At \(n=0\), \(H_0=1\) and \(D_0=I\), so the initial rows remain exactly (E2). The common scalar and coordinate gauges cancel in each projective ratio \(P_{n,j}/Q_{n,j}\).

The normalized eigenvalues at infinity are

\[
\lambda_+=17+12\sqrt2,
\qquad
\lambda_0=1,
\qquad
\lambda_-=17-12\sqrt2=\lambda_+^{-1},
\tag{1.6}
\]

and the normalized formal indices are

\[
\beta_+=0,
\qquad
\beta_0=-3,
\qquad
\beta_-=0.
\tag{1.7}
\]

Thus a canonical forward Birkhoff basis can be normalized in the form

\[
\begin{aligned}
f_+(n)&=\lambda_+^n\bigl(\ell_++O(n^{-1})\bigr),\\
f_0(n)&=n^{-3}\bigl(\ell_0+O(n^{-1})\bigr),\\
f_-(n)&=\lambda_-^n\bigl(\ell_-+O(n^{-1})\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\tag{1.8}
\]

The vectors \(\ell_j\) can be chosen algebraic because they come from the limiting matrix. The normalization constants obtained by transporting these modes from infinity to \(n=0\) are not determined by the limiting matrix.

# 2. Exact construction of the connection matrix

Form the \(3\times3\) matrix whose rows are the three canonical forward modes:

\[
\mathcal F_n=
\begin{pmatrix}
f_+(n)\\ f_0(n)\\ f_-(n)
\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{2.1}
\]

It obeys

\[
\mathcal F_{n+1}=\mathcal F_n\overline M(n).
\tag{2.2}
\]

Set

\[
\mathcal W_n:=\mathcal F_n^{-1}
=\bigl(w_+(n),w_0(n),w_-(n)\bigr).
\tag{2.3}
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{
\overline M(n)w_j(n+1)=w_j(n),
}
\tag{2.4}
\]

and the duality normalization is

\[
\boxed{
f_i(n)w_j(n)=\delta_{ij}.}
\tag{2.5}
\]

The vector called the “adjoint dominant eigenvector” in backward iteration is \(w_+\): it is dual to the dominant **forward** mode. Under forward iteration it is exponentially small,

\[
w_+(n)=\lambda_+^{-n}\bigl(v_++O(n^{-1})\bigr),
\tag{2.6}
\]

which is exactly why it can be selected stably by backward iteration.

Let

\[
E_n=\overline M(0)\overline M(1)\cdots\overline M(n-1),
\qquad E_0=I.
\]

Both \(E_n\) and \(\mathcal F_n\) are fundamental matrices for the same right recurrence, so

\[
E_n=\mathcal C\mathcal F_n
\]

for a constant matrix \(\mathcal C\). At \(n=0\),

\[
I=\mathcal C\mathcal F_0,
\]

hence

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal C=\mathcal F_0^{-1}=\mathcal W_0.
}
\tag{2.7}
\]

This is the connection matrix from the initial basis to the normalized Birkhoff basis. If the opposite convention is used, the matrix called \(C\) is \(\mathcal C^{-1}\); no mathematical statement changes.

For any initial row \(r_0\),

\[
\begin{aligned}
r_n
&=r_0E_n\\
&=r_0\mathcal C\mathcal F_n\\
&=(r_0w_+(0))f_+(n)
 +(r_0w_0(0))f_0(n)
 +(r_0w_-(0))f_-(n).
\end{aligned}
\tag{2.8}
\]

Therefore the exact coefficient of the dominant mode is

\[
\boxed{a_+(r_0)=r_0w_+(0).}
\tag{2.9}
\]

This is not an asymptotic approximation. It follows from exact matrix duality.

# 3. Exact ratio theorem and error expansion

Apply (2.8) to the two initial rows:

\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf q_n&=A_+f_+(n)+A_0f_0(n)+A_-f_-(n),\\
\mathbf p_n&=B_+f_+(n)+B_0f_0(n)+B_-f_-(n),
\end{aligned}
\tag{3.1}
\]

where

\[
A_j=\mathbf q_0w_j(0),
\qquad
B_j=\mathbf p_0w_j(0).
\tag{3.2}
\]

Assume \(A_+\ne0\) and choose a coordinate in which the dominant row mode has a nonzero component. Then

\[
\boxed{
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{P_{n,j}}{Q_{n,j}}
=\frac{B_+}{A_+}
=\frac{\mathbf p_0w_+(0)}{\mathbf q_0w_+(0)}.
}
\tag{3.3}
\]

Thus the equality with Catalan's constant is precisely

\[
\boxed{
(\mathbf p_0-G\mathbf q_0)w_+(0)=0.
}
\tag{3.4}
\]

In coordinates, if

\[
w_+(0)=(w_1,w_2,w_3)^T,
\]

then (3.4) is

\[
\boxed{
(30921-33750G)w_1
+(-32972+36000G)w_2
+(8240-9000G)w_3=0.
}
\tag{3.5}
\]

Equivalently,

\[
\boxed{
G=
\frac{30921w_1-32972w_2+8240w_3}
     {33750w_1-36000w_2+9000w_3}.
}
\tag{3.6}
\]

If (3.4) holds, then

\[
\mathbf p_n-G\mathbf q_n
=(B_0-GA_0)f_0(n)
 +(B_--GA_-)f_-(n).
\tag{3.7}
\]

Dividing by the dominant denominator mode gives

\[
\boxed{
\frac{P_{n,j}}{Q_{n,j}}-G
=\kappa_j n^{-3}(17-12\sqrt2)^n
 +\eta_j(17-12\sqrt2)^{2n}
 +o\!\left((17-12\sqrt2)^{2n}\right),
}
\tag{3.8}
\]

with constants determined by the connection matrix. This is the rigorous explanation of the observed approximately \(1.531\) decimal digits per step.

Conversely, if one independently proves

\[
\frac{P_{n,j}}{Q_{n,j}}\longrightarrow G,
\]

then (3.3) immediately implies (3.4). Thus the Birkhoff identity is both necessary and sufficient; it does not independently evaluate the quotient.

# 4. The exact invariant pairing: the discrete Green/Christoffel–Darboux analogue

Let a forward row and an adjoint column satisfy

\[
r_{n+1}=r_n\overline M(n),
\qquad
\overline M(n)w(n+1)=w(n).
\tag{4.1}
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{
r_{n+1}w(n+1)-r_nw(n)=0.}
\tag{4.2}
\]

Hence

\[
\boxed{r_nw(n)=r_0w(0)}
\tag{4.3}
\]

for every \(n\). This is the exact matrix version of the discrete Lagrange identity. In a scalar order-three representation it becomes a Casoratian or bilinear concomitant involving three consecutive values of the solution and of the adjoint solution.

Taking

\[
r_n=\mathbf p_n-G\mathbf q_n,
\qquad
w=w_+,
\]

gives

\[
(\mathbf p_n-G\mathbf q_n)w_+(n)
=(\mathbf p_0-G\mathbf q_0)w_+(0).
\tag{4.4}
\]

This identity gives a clean way to finish the proof **without explicitly computing the connection matrix**. It is enough to establish an independent bound such as

\[
\|\mathbf p_n-G\mathbf q_n\|
=O(n^{-3})
\tag{4.5}
\]

in the normalized balanced basis. Since

\[
\|w_+(n)\|=O(\lambda_+^{-n}),
\]

(4.4) then yields

\[
(\mathbf p_0-G\mathbf q_0)w_+(0)
=\lim_{n\to\infty}(\mathbf p_n-G\mathbf q_n)w_+(n)=0.
\tag{4.6}
\]

More generally, any bound strictly smaller than the dominant forward scale is enough.

This is the exact point at which a Beukers-type remainder integral or a matrix AZ certificate would close the argument. Birkhoff theory supplies the adjoint decay; the integral must supply the subdominant remainder bound.

A Christoffel–Darboux formula by itself cannot set the invariant (4.3) equal to zero. It only proves that the quantity is independent of \(n\). One still needs either:

- an endpoint where it can be evaluated exactly; or
- a decay estimate forcing that endpoint value to be zero.

# 5. Why no spectrum-only cancellation theorem can exist

Keep the same cocycle \(M(n)\), the same denominator row \(\mathbf q_0\), and replace the numerator row by

\[
\widetilde{\mathbf p}_0
=\mathbf p_0+r\mathbf q_0,
\qquad r\in\mathbf Q.
\tag{5.1}
\]

Then

\[
\widetilde{\mathbf p}_n
=\mathbf p_n+r\mathbf q_n,
\]

so

\[
\lim_{n\to\infty}
\frac{\widetilde P_{n,j}}{Q_{n,j}}
=G+r.
\tag{5.2}
\]

Nothing in the following data changes:

- the matrices \(M(n)\);
- the determinant;
- the Poincaré roots;
- the formal indices;
- the Birkhoff basis;
- the dominant adjoint vector.

Therefore neither the spectrum nor the fact that the matrices come from a CMF can force the value \(G\) for arbitrary initial data.

A conservative matrix field can force a value only when the full **boundary normalization** is included. Zero curvature may transport an exactly evaluated boundary period to the challenge trajectory, but the boundary period and the identification of the initial matrix \(A\) with it must be exhibited. The one-dimensional trajectory matrix and its Poincaré polynomial do not contain that evaluation by themselves.

This also answers the proposed “linear condition determined by CMF structure.” Such a condition exists after the dominant projector is known:

\[
\boxed{
(1,-G)A\,\mathcal C e_+=0.
}
\tag{5.3}
\]

But \(\mathcal C e_+=w_+(0)\) is precisely the unknown global connection column. Equation (5.3) is not a rational identity involving only the printed entries of \(A\).

# 6. The dominant projector

Define the rank-one projector

\[
\boxed{
\Pi_+(n)=w_+(n)f_+(n).
}
\tag{6.1}
\]

By duality,

\[
\Pi_+(n)^2=\Pi_+(n),
\]

and for every row \(r_n\),

\[
r_n\Pi_+(n)=(r_nw_+(n))f_+(n)
\]

is its dominant component.

The projector satisfies the exact covariance relation

\[
\boxed{
\overline M(n)\Pi_+(n+1)
=\Pi_+(n)\overline M(n).
}
\tag{6.2}
\]

At \(n=0\), cancellation is

\[
(\mathbf p_0-G\mathbf q_0)\Pi_+(0)=0.
\tag{6.3}
\]

The limiting spectral projector of the constant matrix at infinity is algebraic. The transported projector \(\Pi_+(0)\) generally is not. Transport across all finite indices is where the connection constants enter.

If a rational formula for \(\Pi_+(n)\) existed, the cocycle would possess a rational invariant line or rank-one factor. The previous hypergeometric-factor and Ore-Hom computations found no such rational structure. This is consistent with the dominant projector being a genuinely transcendental period object.

# 7. Why the connection matrix need not be algebraic

Polynomial recurrence coefficients do not imply algebraic connection constants. The first-order example

\[
u_{n+1}=\frac{n+a}{n+b}u_n,
\qquad u_0=1,
\tag{7.1}
\]

has the exact solution

\[
u_n=\frac{(a)_n}{(b)_n}
\sim
\frac{\Gamma(b)}{\Gamma(a)}n^{a-b}.
\tag{7.2}
\]

Thus the connection coefficient between the initial normalization and the canonical asymptotic normalization is

\[
\frac{\Gamma(b)}{\Gamma(a)}.
\]

For example, \(a=1/2,b=1\) gives \(1/\sqrt\pi\), even though the recurrence has rational coefficients.

Higher-rank recurrences similarly produce logarithms, elliptic periods, regulator values, and critical \(L\)-values in their connection matrices. The algebraic limiting eigenvectors and formal inverse-power series describe only the local formal type at infinity; the connection matrix records global transport through all finite singularities.

For Problem 2.5, one should expect the connection field to contain an elliptic period sector and the extension period

\[
G=L(\chi_{-4},2).
\]

This becomes a theorem only after a geometric, modular, or integral realization of the normalized recurrence has been established.

In particular, asserting that \(\mathcal C\) is algebraic would be far stronger than anything implied by the polynomial matrices. Combined with (E5), it would imply that the observed limit is algebraic. No such result is known.

# 8. A concrete Catalan-moment reformulation

The initial rows have the exact vector-polynomial combination

\[
\boxed{
\mathbf R_0(X)
=\mathbf q_0-(1+X)\mathbf p_0
=(2829-30921X,\,-3028+32972X,\,760-8240X).
}
\tag{8.1}
\]

Define

\[
\mathbf R_n(X)=\mathbf R_0(X)M(0)\cdots M(n-1).
\tag{8.2}
\]

The elementary Catalan moment identity from the previous CMF analysis is

\[
\boxed{
G\mathbf q_n-\mathbf p_n
=\int_0^1
\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}\,
\mathbf R_n(t^2)\,dt.
}
\tag{8.3}
\]

Pair this with the appropriately ungauged adjoint vector. Since forward and adjoint transport cancel,

\[
\mathbf R_n(X)w_+(n)=\mathbf R_0(X)w_+(0).
\tag{8.4}
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{
(G\mathbf q_0-\mathbf p_0)w_+(0)
=\int_0^1
\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}
\bigl(\mathbf R_0(t^2)w_+(0)\bigr)\,dt.
}
\tag{8.5}
\]

This is an exact one-dimensional period formulation of the missing connection condition. It is useful as a target, but it is still equivalent to (3.4): the unknown vector \(w_+(0)\) appears inside the integral.

The non-tautological step would be an independent integral representation

\[
w_+(0)=\int_\Gamma \Omega
\tag{8.6}
\]

for which the double integral obtained by substituting (8.6) into (8.5) can be reduced to zero by an exact symmetry, total derivative, contour deformation, or modular-symbol identity.

# 9. How the matrix AZ framework would close the proof

A successful matrix Almkvist–Zeilberger construction should produce a period matrix \(\mathcal P_n\) satisfying the exact normalized shift

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal P_{n+1}=\mathcal P_n\overline M(n).
}
\tag{9.1}
\]

The proof then has four finite obligations.

## 9.1 Exact telescoping identity

Find a matrix kernel \(K_n\) and certificate fluxes such that

\[
K_{n+1}-K_n\overline M(n)
=\partial_uU_n+\partial_vV_n+\partial_tT_n,
\tag{9.2}
\]

with all contour and endpoint terms explicitly controlled.

## 9.2 Identify the Birkhoff cycles

Choose three cycles whose saddle asymptotics are respectively

\[
\lambda_+^n,\qquad n^{-3},\qquad\lambda_-^n.
\]

This identifies the integral fundamental matrix with \(\mathcal F_n\) up to an explicit constant normalization.

## 9.3 Evaluate the parameter derivative

For the Catalan direction use an actual first-parameter jet, for example through

\[
I(\varepsilon)=\int_0^1\frac{t^\varepsilon}{1+t^2}\,dt,
\qquad -I'(0)=G.
\tag{9.3}
\]

As established in Q4846, differentiating an \(\varepsilon=0\) AZ identity introduces an additional forcing term. One must solve the first-jet certificate or retain that forcing as the nonzero extension cocycle. It cannot be discarded.

## 9.4 Initial matching

Finally verify exactly that the challenge matrix \(A\) is the change of functional from the period basis to the numerator/denominator basis. This is where the identities

\[
\mathbf p_0w_+(0)=\kappa G,
\qquad
\mathbf q_0w_+(0)=\kappa
\tag{9.4}
\]

would emerge.

Once (9.1)–(9.4) are proved, the Birkhoff equality (3.4) follows immediately and exactly. The matrix AZ search is therefore a legitimate route to the missing connection coefficient, but a certificate for the homogeneous Delannoy carrier alone is insufficient: the nontrivial Catalan extension and its boundary term must also be matched.

# 10. A second exact closure criterion: decay instead of evaluation

It is not necessary to compute \(w_+(0)\) in closed form. The following proposition is enough.

**Proposition.** Suppose there exists a non-tautological integral representation of the normalized remainder row \(\mathbf r_n=\mathbf p_n-G\mathbf q_n\) satisfying

\[
\|\mathbf r_n\|\le Cn^m\theta^n
\]

for some \(\theta<\lambda_+\). Then

\[
(\mathbf p_0-G\mathbf q_0)w_+(0)=0.
\]

**Proof.** By the adjoint invariant pairing,

\[
\mathbf r_0w_+(0)=\mathbf r_nw_+(n).
\]

Since \(w_+(n)=O(\lambda_+^{-n})\),

\[
|\mathbf r_nw_+(n)|
\le C'n^m(\theta/\lambda_+)^n\to0.
\]

Hence the invariant constant is zero. \(\square\)

For the expected neutral remainder one has \(\theta=1\) in the normalized cocycle, giving the sharper \(n^{-3}\lambda_+^{-n}\) ratio error.

This proposition pinpoints the exact role of the desired Beukers/AZ integral: it need not evaluate the connection matrix. It only needs to make the remainder visibly subdominant.

# 11. Answers to the five questions

## 1. Can the connection matrix prove the identity?

Yes, once one of its dominant entries is evaluated. With the convention (2.7),

\[
G=\frac{(A\mathcal C)_{1,+}}{(A\mathcal C)_{2,+}}.
\]

Birkhoff theory rigorously defines \(\mathcal C\) and proves the limit/rate, but it does not evaluate this quotient as \(G\).

## 2. Is the initial linear form in the kernel of the dominant projection?

Exactly when

\[
(1,-G)A\mathcal C e_+=0.
\]

This is the precise necessary-and-sufficient condition.

## 3. Does the rational initial matrix \(A\) alone force it?

No. It can be forced only after \(A\) is identified with exact boundary period functionals of the full CMF. The one-dimensional matrix trajectory and its Poincaré data do not supply that boundary evaluation.

## 4. Is there a universal Christoffel–Darboux or contiguous-relation argument?

No. The exact invariant pairing (4.2) is the general discrete Green identity, but it only conserves the dominant coefficient. It does not force it to vanish. The counterexample \(\mathbf p_0\mapsto\mathbf p_0+r\mathbf q_0\) rules out any spectrum-only theorem.

## 5. Is the connection matrix algebraic?

Not as a consequence of polynomial recurrence coefficients. Connection matrices commonly contain gamma values, logarithms, elliptic periods, and \(L\)-values. Here the expected extension entry is Catalan's constant itself. An exact computation should therefore seek a period or modular-symbol matrix, not merely algebraic eigenvectors.

# Bottom line

The 150-digit backward iteration has identified the correct theorem:

\[
\frac{\mathbf p_0w_+(0)}{\mathbf q_0w_+(0)}=G.
\]

The Birkhoff connection framework proves that this quotient is the unique projective limit and explains the full convergence rate. It does not by itself prove the Catalan evaluation.

The remaining exact certificate is one of the following equivalent objects:

1. a period formula for \(w_+(0)\) whose two row pairings evaluate to \(\kappa G\) and \(\kappa\);
2. a block-triangular matrix AZ identity including the first \(\varepsilon\)-jet and its Catalan boundary term;
3. a non-tautological remainder integral with a subdominant bound, which forces the invariant dominant pairing to vanish by (4.6);
4. a boundary path in the full conservative matrix field on which the period ratio is evaluated exactly.

Until one of these is printed and verified, the exact equality is the global connection problem, not a missing local step in Birkhoff asymptotics.
