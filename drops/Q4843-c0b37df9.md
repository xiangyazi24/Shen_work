# Problem 2.5: exact Pochhammer normalization, the primitive normalized recurrence, and the formal-index obstruction

## Executive answer

Let `Q_n` be a scalar solution of the raw Problem 2.5 order-three recurrence. Its Newton slope is seven and its three Poincaré roots are

$$
-16,\qquad -16(17+12√2),\qquad -16(17-12√2).
$$

There is rank-one gauge freedom, so no Pochhammer normalization is absolutely unique. The determinant-compatible minimal step is

$$
δ(n)=-2(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(2n+5)(2n+7)^2,
$$

or equivalently

$$
δ(n)=-16(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(n+5/2)(n+7/2)^2.
$$

With `H_0=1`, its cumulative product is

$$
H_n:=∏_{m=0}^{n-1}δ(m)
=(-16)^n(2)_n^2(3)_n^2(5/2)_n(7/2)_n^2.
$$

The normalized sequence is

$$
q_n=Q_n/H_n.
$$

This removes the factorial slope and the common outer formal power. More precisely,

$$
H_n=
\frac{64}{675π^{3/2}}
(-16)^n Γ(n)^7 n^{39/2}
(1+O(n^{-1})).
$$

After conjugation by `H_n` and removal of a common polynomial factor, the scalar operator has the primitive recurrence

$$
ℓ_0(n)q_n+ℓ_1(n)q_{n+1}+ℓ_2(n)q_{n+2}+ℓ_3(n)q_{n+3}=0,
$$

where

$$
ℓ_0(n)=4(n+1)(n+3)^2(n+4)(2n+3)(2n+5)(2n+7)P_6(n),
$$

$$
ℓ_1(n)=-(n+2)^2(n+4)(2n+7)P_9(n),
$$

$$
ℓ_2(n)=2(n+2)(n+3)(2n+9)P_{10}(n),
$$

$$
ℓ_3(n)=-4(n+2)(n+3)(n+4)^2(2n+7)(2n+11)^2\widetilde P_6(n).
$$

All four coefficients have degree `13`. The endpoint sextics are

$$
P_6(n)=3072n^6+74112n^5+738544n^4+3890106n^3
+11417947n^2+17696904n+11307715,
$$

$$
\widetilde P_6(n)=3072n^6+55680n^5+414064n^4+1615610n^3
+3483853n^2+3929280n+1806156,
$$

and satisfy

$$
P_6(n)=\widetilde P_6(n+1).
$$

Here `P_9` and `P_10` are the exact degree-nine and degree-ten middle factors fixed by scalarizing the printed `3 × 3` matrix. Their expanded coefficients are not recoverable from the determinant and Poincaré polynomial alone.

The normalized Poincaré polynomial is exactly

$$
ξ^3-35ξ^2+35ξ-1=(ξ-1)(ξ^2-34ξ+1),
$$

so the normalized CMF and the Delannoy symmetric square do have the same root set

$$
17+12√2,\qquad 1,\qquad 17-12√2.
$$

They are nevertheless distinguished by their formal indices at infinity:

| mode | `17+12√2` | `1` | `17-12√2` |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| normalized CMF `L_25^♯` | `0` | `-3` | `0` |
| `Sym²(Delannoy)` | `-1` | `-1` | `-1` |

Matching Poincaré roots gives only the leading semisimple formal type. It does not imply an Ore equivalence.

Under the exact zero-Ore-Hom computation assumed in the previous Problem 2.5 work, the normalized CMF operator is not rationally Ore-equivalent to the Delannoy symmetric square. Therefore there is no exact scalar deformation parameter. The exponent defect `3` of the neutral mode is a formal shearing index, not the parameter carrying Catalan's constant. The plausible carrier of `G` is a larger integrated elliptic-period or normal-function extension.

# 1. How the determinant produces the step factor

The matrix determinant is

$$
det M(n)=-8(n+1)(n+2)^6(n+3)^5(2n+3)^2(2n+5)^3(2n+7)^4.
$$

To see the canonical degree-seven cube root, consider the formal comparison

$$
M(n)V(n+1)=V(n)ρ(n)Sym^2(A(n)).
$$

Let `h(n)=det V(n)`. Since

$$
det(Sym^2 A)=(det A)^3,
$$

taking determinants gives

$$
[ρ(n)det A(n)]^3=det M(n)\,h(n+1)/h(n).
$$

A minimal polynomial representative of the determinant coboundary class is

$$
h(n)=(n+1)(n+2)(2n+3)^2(2n+5)^2.
$$

Its shift quotient is

$$
h(n+1)/h(n)=\frac{n+3}{n+1}\frac{(2n+7)^2}{(2n+3)^2}.
$$

Consequently,

$$
det M(n)\,h(n+1)/h(n)
=-8(n+2)^6(n+3)^6(2n+5)^3(2n+7)^6,
$$

which is the exact cube

$$
[-2(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(2n+5)(2n+7)^2]^3.
$$

Thus

$$
ρ(n)det A(n)=-2(n+2)^2(n+3)^2(2n+5)(2n+7)^2
$$

up to a rational cube/coboundary normalization. If the hidden rank-two matrix is normalized by `det A(n)=1`, this gives `ρ(n)=δ(n)`.

Even without an exact symmetric-square equivalence, the same `δ(n)` is a valid and particularly efficient scalar normalization of the raw recurrence.

# 2. Exact conjugation of the scalar recurrence

L_{25}=c_0(n)+c_1(n)S+c_2(n)S^2+c_3(n)S^3,

c_1(n)=\frac1{6144}(n+2)(n+3)(n+4)^3(n+5)^2

$$
c_2(n)=\frac1{6144}(n+5)^2(2n+9)^2P_{10}(n),

$$
c_3(n)=\frac1{6144}(2n+7)\widetilde P_6(n).

Put `Q_n=H_n q_n`, with `H_{n+1}/H_n=δ(n)`. Dividing the recurrence by `H_n` gives coefficients

# 3. Equality of the normalized Poincaré polynomials

The raw monic Newton/Poincaré polynomial is

$$
P_{raw}(c)=(c+16)(c^2+544c+256)
=c^3+560c^2+8960c+4096.
$$

Scale the formal ratio by the leading term of the Pochhammer step:

$$
c=-16ξ.
$$

Then

$$
P_{raw}(-16ξ)/(-4096)=ξ^3-35ξ^2+35ξ-1.
$$

The central-Delannoy recurrence

$$
(n+1)D_{n+1}=3(2n+1)D_n-nD_{n-1}
$$

has roots `3±2√2`. Its symmetric square therefore has roots

$$
(3+2√2)^2=17+12√2,
\qquad 1,
\qquad (3-2√2)^2=17-12√2.
$$

The symmetric-square recurrence may be written

$$
(n+2)^2(2n+1)a_{n+2}
-(2n+3)(35n^2+70n+26)a_{n+1}
$$

$$
+(2n+1)(35n^2+70n+26)a_n
-(2n+3)n^2a_{n-1}=0.
$$

Therefore the answer to part (c) is yes: the two normalized operators have the same Poincaré polynomial. That polynomial records only the leading equation at infinity.

# 5. Why matching roots do not imply Ore equivalence

Difference operators with the same Poincaré polynomial can differ in:

- formal indices;
- finite singularities and apparent singularities;
- local exponents at finite points;
- connection and Stokes data;
- extension classes;
- arithmetic connection constants.

Here the finite operators are visibly different:

- every primitive normalized CMF coefficient has degree `13`;
- every Delannoy-square coefficient has degree `3`;
- the CMF scalarization contains the shifted sextic orbit `P_6(n)=\widetilde P_6(n+1)`;
- the formal-index triples are `(0,-3,0)` and `(-1,-1,-1)`.

At the matrix level, the limiting matrix is a rational symmetric square. But a regular nondegenerate invariant conic fails at first order: the determinant forces a projective multiplier `1+37/n+...`, whereas the fixed neutral eigenline forces `1+33/n+...`. A putative gauge would therefore have to be singular at infinity, with a three-power vector shear and a six-power quadratic-form degeneration.

Under the established exact Ore-Hom computation, even that rational finite-order comparison does not exist:

$$
Hom_{Q(n)<S>}(M_D^{Sym^2},M_{25}^{♯})=0.
$$

Thus the normalized CMF is not a rational Ore-equivalent deformation of the homogeneous Delannoy symmetric square.

# 6. What can carry Catalan's constant

The Pochhammer twist `H_n` is determined by local growth and determinant data. It cannot carry `G`.

Likewise, the neutral exponent defect `3` is a formal shearing index, not a scalar deformation parameter.

The more plausible structure is a nontrivial extension

$$
0\longrightarrow T\longrightarrow E_G\longrightarrow H_{ell}\longrightarrow0,
$$

where:

- `H_ell` supplies the reciprocal pair `17±12√2`;
- `T` supplies the root `1` with formal index `-3`;
- the global connection matrix of the extension contains `G=L(χ_{-4},2)`.

This is an integrated elliptic-period or normal-function picture, not a homogeneous symmetric square.

An explicit proof would require one of the following equivalent certificates:

1. a block-triangular rational gauge from the normalized CMF cocycle to an integrated elliptic system;
2. a creative-telescoping integral whose extension class evaluates to `G`;
3. a differential normal function whose connection matrix matches the two CMF initial rows.

No one-dimensional parameter `ε` is canonically determined by the Poincaré data. Infinitely many inequivalent one-parameter operator families share the same endpoints and leading polynomial. Therefore the exact answer to part (d) is:

> No rational Ore equivalence is presently available, and there is no exact scalar deformation parameter. The known invariant data are the step `δ(n)`, the normalized indices `(0,-3,0)`, the shifted sextic apparent orbit, and the Catalan connection coefficient selected by the CMF initial matrix.
