# Problem 2.7: the exact level-11 trace pullback, and why the naive logarithmic MOP is a different system

## Executive answer

There is an explicit level-11 transformation, but it does **not** start from the standard type-II multiple-orthogonal-polynomial system for

\[
d\mu_2(t)=(-\log t)\,dt,
\qquad
d\mu_3(t)=\frac12\log^2(t)\,dt.
\]

It starts from Cooper's level-11 four-term sequence and consists of

1. a centered binomial transform;
2. extraction of the even subsequence;
3. the scale factor \(256^{-n}\);
4. equivalently, a quadratic algebraic pullback followed by trace over the two branches.

The first necessary correction is that the polynomial identity in the question cannot hold with

\[
C_{11}(t)=t^2-11t+1.
\]

The left side has degree six in \(t\), while the product of two quadratics has degree four. The relevant Cooper level-11 characteristic polynomial is the **cubic**

\[
\boxed{H_{11}(t)=t^3-20t^2+56t-44.}
\]

The exact identity is

\[
\boxed{
16P_{27}\!\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{4}\right)
=-H_{11}(t)H_{11}(4-t),
}
\tag{1}
\]

where

\[
P_{27}(\mu)=4\mu^3-220\mu^2+8\mu-1.
\]

At the level of the actual, unscaled Problem 2.7 Poincare roots \(c\), one has \(\mu=64c\), so the root map is

\[
\boxed{c=\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}.}
\tag{2}
\]

The corresponding exact sequence transform is

\[
\boxed{
W_n=rac1{256^n}
\sum_{k=0}^{2n}\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}T_k,
}
\tag{3}
\]

where \(T_k\) is Cooper's level-11 sequence. Formula (3) maps a pure mode \(T_k=t^k\) to exactly the mode in (2).

By contrast, the logarithmic MOP system is the ordinary simultaneous Hermite-Pade system for

\[
\operatorname{Li}_2(1/z),\qquad \operatorname{Li}_3(1/z).
\]

It is a mixed-Tate/polylogarithmic approximation system on the interval \([0,1]\). It contains no level-11 modular datum. The fact that its step-line dominant ratio is near \(1/3\), while the Problem 2.7 ratio tends to approximately \(0.8588077\), is therefore not a missing scalar normalization. It is evidence that these are different difference modules.

A finite contiguous relation can alter amplitudes and polynomial powers, but—apart from one common scalar twist—it cannot replace a root \(r\) by the nonlinear expression \((r-2)^2/256\). The nonlinear root map is implemented by the binomial/even trace (3), not by an ordinary MOP gauge.

The most plausible exact homogeneous relation is therefore

\[
\boxed{
q_n=h_n\bigl(u_0(n)W_n+u_1(n)W_{n+1}+u_2(n)W_{n+2}\bigr),
}
\tag{4}
\]

with \(h_{n+1}/h_n\in\mathbf Q(n)\) and \(u_j(n)\in\mathbf Q(n)\). This is an order-at-most-two Ore change of cyclic vector after a rank-one twist. It has not yet been certified.

If one insists on a multiple-orthogonality proof, the measures must be replaced by level-11 modular/Eichler objects, most naturally contour or signed measures obtained from the Cooper modular form and the quadratic phase in (3). The plain logarithmic measures can still describe the mixed \(\zeta(2),\zeta(3)\) extension, but they do not describe the homogeneous level-11 denominator module.

# 1. Correct normalization of the Problem 2.7 spectral polynomial

The displayed recurrence is

\[
A(n)u_{n+1}=B(n)u_n-C(n-1)u_{n-1}+D(n-2)u_{n-2}.
\]

Keeping the degree-12 leading terms gives

\[
991952896c^3-852459520c^2+484352c-946=0.
\tag{5}
\]

After division by the leading coefficient,

\[
\boxed{
\chi_{27}(c)
=c^3-\frac{55}{64}c^2+rac1{2048}c-rac1{2^{20}}.
}
\tag{6}
\]

Setting \(\mu=64c\) gives

\[
\boxed{P_{27}(\mu)=4\mu^3-220\mu^2+8\mu-1.}
\tag{7}
\]

Thus the real scaled root is about \(54.9636951\), while the corresponding raw recurrence ratio is about

\[
\frac{54.9636951}{64}=0.8588077\ldots.
\]

The other two roots form a nonreal conjugate pair. Their modulus in the raw normalization is approximately \(0.001053785\).

This normalization matters when comparing a MOP ratio with the challenge ratio. Comparing \(1/3\) with the scaled root \(54.96\), or comparing a ratio per total polynomial degree with a ratio per diagonal lattice step, produces meaningless constants.

## 1.1 The exact cubic norm identity

Put

\[
H_{11}(t)=t^3-20t^2+56t-44.
\]

Let \(u=t-2\). Then

\[
H_{11}(2+u)=u^3-14u^2-12u-4,
\]

and

\[
H_{11}(2-u)=-u^3-14u^2+12u-4.
\]

Therefore

\[
\begin{aligned}
-H_{11}(2+u)H_{11}(2-u)
&=(u^3-12u)^2-(14u^2+4)^2\\
&=u^6-220u^4+32u^2-16.
\end{aligned}
\]

On the other hand,

\[
16P_{27}(u^2/4)=u^6-220u^4+32u^2-16.
\]

This proves (1).

The two cubic fields are actually the same. On the common zero set,

\[
\mu=\frac{(t-2)^2}{4},
\]

and eliminating \(t^2\) from \(H_{11}(t)=0\) gives

\[
\boxed{t=\frac{16\mu-5}{\mu-3}.}
\tag{8}
\]

This is a strong arithmetic relation between the spectra. It is still only a spectral relation: it does not identify the lower-order coefficients of the two recurrence operators.

## 1.2 Why the quadratic \(t^2-11t+1\) is not the relevant object

Even before any calculation, degree gives

\[
\deg_t P_{27}((t-2)^2/4)=6,
\]

whereas

\[
\deg_t\bigl((t^2-11t+1)((4-t)^2-11(4-t)+1)\bigr)=4.
\]

So the identity stated with that quadratic is impossible. A quadratic with coefficient \(11\) occurs in other Apéry-like and modular recurrence conventions, but it is not the characteristic polynomial of Cooper's level-11 **four-term** sequence used here. The relevant level-11 object is rank three and has cubic \(H_{11}\).

# 2. The explicit level-11 pullback

Cooper's level-11 sequence satisfies

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
(n+1)^3T_{n+1}={}&2(2n+1)(5n^2+5n+2)T_n\\
&-8n(7n^2+1)T_{n-1}\\
&+22n(2n-1)(n-1)T_{n-2},
\end{aligned}}
\tag{9}
\]

with

\[
T_0,T_1,T_2,T_3,\ldots=1,4,28,268,\ldots.
\]

Its Poincare polynomial is \(H_{11}(t)\).

## 2.1 Centered binomial transform

Define

\[
b_m=\sum_{k=0}^{m}\binom{m}{k}(-2)^{m-k}T_k.
\tag{10}
\]

For a pure exponential \(T_k=t^k\), the binomial theorem gives

\[
b_m=(t-2)^m.
\]

Taking the even section and applying the challenge scaling gives (3):

\[
W_n=256^{-n}b_{2n}.
\]

Hence

\[
t^k\longmapsto
\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}\right)^n.
\]

This is not a heuristic. It is the exact sequence-theoretic realization of the root map (2).

## 2.2 Generating-function formula

Let

\[
Z_{11}(X)=\sum_{k\ge0}T_kX^k,
\qquad
B(s)=\sum_{m\ge0}b_ms^m.
\]

Summing the binomial kernel gives

\[
\boxed{
B(s)=\frac1{1+2s}
Z_{11}\!\left(\frac{s}{1+2s}\right).
}
\tag{11}
\]

Now put \(r=\sqrt z\). The generating function of \(W_n\) is the even trace

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal W(z)
=\sum_{n\ge0}W_nz^n
=\frac12\Bigg[&
\frac1{1+r/8}
Z_{11}\!\left(\frac{r}{16+2r}\right)\\
&+\frac1{1-r/8}
Z_{11}\!\left(-\frac{r}{16-2r}\right)
\Bigg].
\end{aligned}}
\tag{12}
\]

The two arguments are the two inverse branches of

\[
\boxed{
z=\frac{256X^2}{(1-2X)^2}.}
\tag{13}
\]

Equivalently, (12) is the trace along this quadratic covering of the function

\[
(1-2X)Z_{11}(X).
\]

Thus the exact geometric operation is

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal W(z)
=\frac12\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathbf Q(X)/\mathbf Q(z)}
\bigl((1-2X)Z_{11}(X)\bigr),
\qquad
z=\frac{256X^2}{(1-2X)^2}.
}
\tag{14}
\]

This is the requested explicit pullback, with one essential qualification: it is a pullback **plus trace/even projection**, not a scalar substitution in one solution.

## 2.3 Differential-operator construction

The level-11 Euler operator is

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal L_{11}={}&\theta_X^3
-2X(2\theta_X+1)(5\theta_X^2+5\theta_X+2)\\
&+8X^2(\theta_X+1)\bigl(7(\theta_X+1)^2+1\bigr)\\
&-22X^3(\theta_X+1)(\theta_X+2)(2\theta_X+3),
\end{aligned}}
\tag{15}
\]

where \(\theta_X=X\,d/dX\).

An exact operator for \(\mathcal W\) is obtained by the following finite Ore operations:

1. apply the Möbius pullback \(X=s/(1+2s)\) to \(\mathcal L_{11}\);
2. conjugate by the prefactor \((1+2s)^{-1}\);
3. substitute \(s=\pm\sqrt z/16\);
4. take the least common left multiple of the two branch operators;
5. extract the even/trace submodule and desingularize.

This constructs the homogeneous level-11 descendant operator exactly. It is the operator that should be compared with \(L_{27}\), not the operator of the naive logarithmic MOP.

## 2.4 Contour form

Cauchy's coefficient formula gives the equivalent modular kernel

\[
\boxed{
W_n=rac1{2\pi i}\oint
\frac{Z_{11}(X)}{X}
\left(\frac{1-2X}{16X}\right)^{2n}dX.
}
\tag{16}
\]

The three critical/branch values of the phase in (16) are precisely the three Problem 2.7 Poincare roots. This explains why the level-11 kernel has the correct spectral geometry.

## 2.5 The direct trace is not the challenge denominator

The first values are

\[
W_0=1,\qquad
W_1=\frac1{16},\qquad
W_2=\frac{355}{16384},\qquad
W_3=\frac{44611}{4194304}.
\tag{17}
\]

If \(q_n\) were merely a constant multiple of \(W_n\), matching \(n=0\) would force \(q_n=q_0W_n\). But

\[
q_0W_1=-13\,440\,026\,250
\]

whereas

\[
q_1=-\frac{167282265043404}{905}
\approx-1.8484\times10^{11}.
\]

So a scalar constant normalization is ruled out immediately. The numerical quotient investigated in `scripts/gauge_2_7.py` is only the quotient of two distinguished solutions; it is not automatically a module gauge.

# 3. What the naive MOP actually represents

Define the two Cauchy transforms

\[
F_2(z)=\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{z-t}\,dt,
\qquad
F_3(z)=\int_0^1\frac{\frac12\log^2t}{z-t}\,dt.
\]

For \(|z|>1\), their moment expansions are

\[
\int_0^1t^m(-\log t)\,dt=\frac1{(m+1)^2},
\]

and

\[
\int_0^1t^m\frac12\log^2t\,dt=\frac1{(m+1)^3}.
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{F_2(z)=\operatorname{Li}_2(1/z),
\qquad F_3(z)=\operatorname{Li}_3(1/z).}
\tag{18}
\]

A type-II polynomial \(Q_{n_2,n_3}\) for these two measures is characterized by

\[
\int_0^1t^kQ_{n_2,n_3}(t)(-\log t)\,dt=0,
\quad 0\le k<n_2,
\tag{19}
\]

and

\[
\int_0^1t^kQ_{n_2,n_3}(t)\frac12\log^2t\,dt=0,
\quad 0\le k<n_3.
\tag{20}
\]

This is a perfectly legitimate Hermite-Pade system for \(\operatorname{Li}_2\) and \(\operatorname{Li}_3\). It naturally produces linear forms in \(1,\zeta(2),\zeta(3)\) when evaluated at the endpoint. But it is not selected uniquely by the target number \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\). Many inequivalent recurrences can have the same connection constant.

The two weights are parameter derivatives of one elementary Jacobi weight:

\[
-\log t=-\left.\partial_\alpha t^\alpha\right|_{\alpha=0},
\qquad
\frac12\log^2t=\left.\frac12\partial_\alpha^2t^\alpha\right|_{\alpha=0}.
\]

Thus the naive MOP belongs to a confluent Jacobi/polylogarithmic, mixed-Tate setting. Cooper's system is a level-11 elliptic/modular local system. A direct rational pullback and rational gauge of the standard polylogarithm system cannot manufacture the elliptic monodromy of the level-11 system. At the discrete-operator level this conceptual obstruction must be supplemented by an exact Ore calculation, but it explains why no simple \(z=\phi(t)\) has appeared.

# 4. Why the two dominant ratios do not determine a transformation

Suppose an order-three recurrence has monic Poincare polynomial

\[
\chi(r)=r^3-ar^2+br-d.
\]

Under a scalar exponential gauge with

\[
\frac{h_{n+1}}{h_n}\longrightarrow\kappa,
\]

all roots are multiplied by the same \(\kappa\). Consequently

\[
\frac{b}{a^2},
\qquad
\frac{d}{a^3}
\]

are invariant.

For Problem 2.7, using the scaled polynomial

\[
\mu^3-55\mu^2+2\mu-\frac14,
\]

these invariants are

\[
\boxed{
\frac{b}{a^2}=\frac{2}{3025},
\qquad
\frac{d}{a^3}=\frac1{665500}.
}
\tag{21}
\]

Therefore the correct first screening test for the MOP operator is not

\[
0.8588/(1/3)\approx2.6.
\]

It is:

1. put the MOP recurrence and the challenge recurrence in the same index convention;
2. compute the complete MOP Poincare cubic;
3. compare the two scale invariants in (21);
4. compare the three formal powers at infinity.

If either invariant differs, no scalar hypergeometric gauge exists.

## 4.1 Fixed contiguous relations preserve the exponential bases

Suppose

\[
q_n=h_n\sum_{j=0}^m u_j(n)v_{n+j},
\qquad
u_j(n)\in\mathbf Q(n),
\]

where \(v_n\) is a solution of another regular recurrence and \(h_{n+1}/h_n\to\kappa\). On a pure mode \(v_n\sim r^nn^\sigma\), the finite sum contributes

\[
r^n\left(\sum_{j=0}^m u_j(n)r^j\right).
\]

It can change the amplitude, the power of \(n\), or even kill a particular mode. It does not replace every root \(r\) by a nonlinear expression such as \((r-2)^2/256\). Apart from the common factor \(\kappa\), the root set is preserved.

Hence a fixed-width contiguous relation cannot repair a genuine mismatch between the MOP cubic and \(\chi_{27}\). It is structurally plausible for the Cooper trace \(W_n\), because \(W_n\) already has the correct roots by construction.

## 4.2 Audit the lattice-step normalization

For a two-index MOP family there are at least three possible indices:

- total degree \(N=n_2+n_3\);
- diagonal step \(m\mapsto(n_2+m,n_3+m)\), which raises total degree by two;
- a periodic word in the two nearest-neighbor directions.

If a quoted root \(1/3\) is per unit of total degree while the challenge root is per diagonal step, the appropriate comparison may involve a square. More generally, a period-\(r\) lattice path replaces the one-step transfer by a product of \(r\) transfer matrices. This can change the spectral polynomial nontrivially.

Thus a different multi-index path is not ruled out by the number \(1/3\) alone. But it must be tested through its entire period transfer matrix. Merely selecting a slope until one dominant eigenvalue is close to \(0.8588\) does not identify the module.

# 5. Audit of the four proposed possibilities

## 5.1 Level-11 modular pullback

**Yes, an explicit level-11 pullback exists:** equations (12)--(16).

It connects Cooper's level-11 modular generating function to the trace sequence \(W_n\). It does not directly connect the standard logarithmic MOP to \(q_n\).

The modular data are

\[
Z_{11}(\tau)=\Theta_{11}(\tau)^2,
\qquad
X(\tau)=\frac{\eta(\tau)^2\eta(11\tau)^2}{Z_{11}(\tau)},
\]

with

\[
B^2=1-20X+56X^2-44X^3.
\]

Putting \(t=1/X\) gives the genus-one branch equation

\[
v^2=tH_{11}(t).
\]

The descendant coordinate is exactly (13). This is the geometrically meaningful level-11 transformation.

## 5.2 A different path in the MOP lattice

This remains logically possible, but no path is presently identified.

For a rational ray

\[
(n_2,n_3)\sim(\theta N,(1-\theta)N),
\]

one must derive the limiting nearest-neighbor transfer matrices and then the transfer over the periodic path representing that ray. A candidate path is acceptable only if, after one common twist and the correct index rescaling, it reproduces:

- all three roots of \(\chi_{27}\), including the nonreal conjugate pair;
- the scale invariants (21);
- the formal powers;
- and eventually the full finite-\(n\) Ore operator.

The path changes the approximation problem. It does not by itself insert the eta product \(\eta(\tau)^2\eta(11\tau)^2\), so there is no current reason to expect level \(11\) to emerge from the unmodified log weights.

## 5.3 A contiguous relation

This is the most plausible remaining homogeneous mechanism **after the Cooper trace**. The correct ansatz is (4), not \(q_n=CW_n\).

Numerical work in the repository found

\[
\frac{q_n/W_n}{q_{n-1}/W_{n-1}}
=1+\frac{3}{2n}+O(n^{-2}),
\]

which only implies

\[
q_n/W_n\sim Cn^{3/2}.
\]

It does not identify an exact gamma-product twist. For example, \((5/2)_n/n!\) has the correct leading power but is only one of infinitely many possible twists.

A fixed-width contiguous relation with the naive MOP is possible only if the full MOP root set first passes the invariant tests above.

## 5.4 Modified measures

This is the most plausible way to retain a Hermite-Pade interpretation while respecting level \(11\).

The homogeneous denominator should be built from the modular contour kernel

\[
\frac{Z_{11}(X)}{2\pi iX}\Psi(X)^n\,dX,
\qquad
\Psi(X)=\left(\frac{1-2X}{16X}\right)^2,
\tag{22}
\]

possibly multiplied by the twist and cyclic-vector polynomial appearing in (4):

\[
\boxed{
K_n^{(q)}(X)=
\frac{h_nZ_{11}(X)}{2\pi iX}\Psi(X)^n
\bigl(u_0(n)+u_1(n)\Psi(X)+u_2(n)\Psi(X)^2\bigr).
}
\tag{23}
\]

These are contour or signed modular moments, not the two positive logarithmic measures on \([0,1]\).

The mixed constant can then enter through a parameter derivative or Eichler/normal-function extension. A universal elementary parameter carrying the desired two zeta values is

\[
\Phi(s)=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{(xy)^s}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy
=\zeta(2,s+1),
\]

so

\[
\boxed{
\Phi(0)-\frac12\Phi'(0)=\zeta(2)+\zeta(3).
}
\tag{24}
\]

But multiplying the homogeneous modular kernel by \(\Phi(s)\) is not sufficient: the rational numerator sequence \(p_n\) requires a nontrivial extension certificate. This is exactly where an Eisenstein class, Eichler integral, or parameter-deformed creative telescoper should appear.

# 6. The finite exact test

Let

\[
L_{27}=\sum_{j=0}^3a_j(n)S^j
\]

be the primitive challenge operator and let

\[
L_M=\sum_{j=0}^3b_j(n)S^j
\]

be the exact scalar operator for either a chosen MOP path or the level-11 trace sequence.

## 6.1 Scalar-twist test

Suppose \(u_n=h_nv_n\), with

\[
\rho(n)=\frac{h_{n+1}}{h_n}\in\mathbf Q(n).
\]

Put

\[
H_0=1,
\qquad
H_j(n)=\prod_{r=0}^{j-1}\rho(n+r).
\]

The twisted operator has coefficients \(b_j/H_j\). Equality up to multiplication by a unit forces

\[
\boxed{
\rho(n)=\frac{a_0(n)b_1(n)}{b_0(n)a_1(n)}.
}
\tag{25}
\]

It is a valid scalar gauge precisely if

\[
\boxed{
\rho(n)\rho(n+1)
=\frac{a_0(n)b_2(n)}{b_0(n)a_2(n)},
}
\tag{26}
\]

and

\[
\boxed{
\rho(n)\rho(n+1)\rho(n+2)
=\frac{a_0(n)b_3(n)}{b_0(n)a_3(n)}.
}
\tag{27}
\]

These three identities give a complete yes/no answer for a scalar hypergeometric gauge.

## 6.2 Order-two Ore intertwiner

If the scalar test fails, search

\[
U=u_0(n)+u_1(n)S+u_2(n)S^2,
\]

and

\[
V=v_0(n)+v_1(n)S+v_2(n)S^2
\]

such that

\[
\boxed{
L_{27}U=V L_M^{[\rho]}.
}
\tag{28}
\]

Both sides have order at most five. Equating the six coefficients of \(S^0,\ldots,S^5\) gives a finite rational-function linear system once \(\rho\) and denominator supports are fixed.

For the MOP route, failure of (28) proves that the naive MOP does not underlie the challenge recurrence. For the Cooper route, a nonzero invertible cyclic-vector map in (28), followed by three initial-value checks, would prove the homogeneous denominator identification.

## 6.3 What must be checked after a homogeneous match

Even a successful homogeneous intertwiner does not yet prove the limit \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\). One must also identify the numerator/error extension. A complete proof needs:

1. the exact homogeneous map for \(q_n\);
2. a parameter-deformed or Eichler integral whose three initial values equal
   \(p_n-(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))q_n\);
3. a creative-telescoping identity proving the same recurrence;
4. a bound showing that the error lies in the conjugate recessive two-dimensional space.

# 7. Recommended next computation

The shortest decisive workflow is:

1. **Compute the exact MOP operator.** Construct \(Q_{n_2,n_3}\) from the exact moments \((m+1)^{-2}\) and \((m+1)^{-3}\), choose the precise path, and derive the primitive scalar recurrence for the evaluated denominators.
2. **Normalize the index.** Record whether one recurrence step raises total degree by one or two.
3. **Compare all spectral invariants.** Check (21), the nonreal pair, and the formal powers. Stop if they fail.
4. **Run (25)--(27).** This decides every scalar gamma/Pochhammer gauge.
5. **Run (28).** Search an order-two cyclic-vector map.
6. Independently construct the exact trace operator from (15) by the pullback/trace (12), and run the same tests against \(L_{27}\).
7. Only after the homogeneous module is identified, add the parameter derivative that carries (24).

This separates three issues that were previously mixed together:

- the spectral quadratic map;
- the finite homogeneous module equivalence;
- the mixed extension whose connection coefficient is \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\).

# Final conclusion

The ratio mismatch is not repaired by multiplying the standard MOP denominator by an unexplained constant near \(2.6\). The naive logarithmic MOP and Problem 2.7 are different approximation systems.

The exact level-11 transformation that is presently available is

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal W(z)=\frac12\left[
\frac{Z_{11}(\sqrt z/(16+2\sqrt z))}{1+\sqrt z/8}
+
\frac{Z_{11}(-\sqrt z/(16-2\sqrt z))}{1-\sqrt z/8}
\right],
}
\]

or equivalently the centered-binomial transform (3). It realizes the exact nonlinear root map

\[
t\mapsto\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}.
\]

There is currently no certified direct pullback from the step-line MOP for \((-\log t)dt\) and \(\tfrac12\log^2t\,dt\) to the Problem 2.7 operator. The honest remaining finite certificate is an order-at-most-two Ore intertwiner, most plausibly from the level-11 trace module, together with a modular/Eichler parameter extension producing the mixed zeta value.
