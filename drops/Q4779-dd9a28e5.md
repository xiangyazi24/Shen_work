# Problem 2.7: the centered-binomial image of Cooper level 11

## Executive conclusion

There are two different transformations in the question, and they must not be conflated.

1. The centered binomial transform
   \[
   (KT)_n=256^{-n}\sum_{k=0}^{2n}\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}T_k
   \]
   is a **nonlocal summation transform**. It genuinely changes a Poincaré base
   \(t\) into
   \[
   \lambda=\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}.
   \]

2. A finite Ore operator
   \[
   T=t_0(n)+t_1(n)S+\cdots+t_r(n)S^r
   \]
   in the same discrete variable cannot make that nonlinear change of
   exponential base. It preserves every surviving base, apart from one common
   scalar-gauge multiplier.

Consequently, there is **no direct finite Ore equivalence**
\[
L_{27}T=R L_C
\]
between Cooper's operator and the challenge operator. The nonlocal transform
\(K\) must be performed first.

The correct finite problem is
\[
L_K K
 =U L_C+(S_k-1)V,
\]
where \(L_K\) is a creative-telescoping operator for the kernel, followed—if
\(L_K\) has the expected order-three factor—by
\[
L_{27}T=R L_K^{(3)}.
\]

This note gives:

- the exact conjugation rules for the full two-parity transform;
- an explicit scalar recurrence for the ordinary centered binomial transform;
- an explicit coupled system for its even and odd sections;
- the exact order-six spectral factor
  \[
  (\lambda-1/64)^3\chi_{27}(\lambda);
  \]
- a proof that the direct Ore intertwiner requested in part (b) cannot exist;
- the precise meaning of the observed \(g_n\asymp n^{3/2}\), and why it does
  not identify a Pochhammer gauge.

# 1. The two operators and their spectra

Cooper's level-11 recurrence is
\[
\begin{aligned}
(n+1)^3T_{n+1}={}&2(2n+1)(5n^2+5n+2)T_n\\
&-8n(7n^2+1)T_{n-1}\\
&+22n(2n-1)(n-1)T_{n-2}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{1.1}
\]
Put
\[
\begin{aligned}
P(x)&=2(2x+1)(5x^2+5x+2),\\
Q(x)&=-8x(7x^2+1),\\
R(x)&=22x(2x-1)(x-1).
\end{aligned}
\tag{1.2}
\]
Its Poincaré polynomial is
\[
C(t)=t^3-20t^2+56t-44.
\tag{1.3}
\]

For Problem 2.7, the rationally normalized recurrence is
\[
u_{n+1}=\frac{B(n)}{A(n)}u_n
-\frac{C(n-1)}{A(n-1)}u_{n-1}
+\frac{D(n-2)}{A(n-2)}u_{n-2},
\tag{1.4}
\]
where
\[
\begin{aligned}
A(n)={}&1024(2n+5)^4(2n+7)^3(2n+9)^3
       (946n^2+6407n+10860),\\
B(n)={}&128(2n+7)^3(2n+9)^3\\
&\quad\times(104060n^6+1745370n^5+12145238n^4
+44886481n^3\\
&\qquad\qquad+92943995n^2+102256019n+46709052),\\
C(n)={}&16(n+3)^4(2n+9)^3\\
&\quad\times(3784n^5+57792n^4+351019n^3
+1059230n^2+1587211n+944620),\\
D(n)={}&(n+3)^4(n+4)^6(946n^2+4515n+5399).
\end{aligned}
\tag{1.5}
\]
Its characteristic polynomial is
\[
\boxed{
\chi_{27}(\lambda)
=\lambda^3-\frac{55}{64}\lambda^2
 +\frac1{2048}\lambda-\frac1{2^{20}}.
}
\tag{1.6}
\]
If \(t_i\) is a root of \(C\), then
\[
\boxed{
\lambda_i=\frac{(t_i-2)^2}{256}
}
\tag{1.7}
\]
is a root of \(\chi_{27}\). Equivalently, for
\(F(\mu)=4\mu^3-220\mu^2+8\mu-1\),
\[
16F\!\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{4}\right)
=-C(t)C(4-t).
\tag{1.8}
\]

Equation (1.7) is exact, but it is a **quadratic spectral map**, not an

# 2. Exact algebra of the centered binomial transform

First retain every parity. Define the ordinary shifted binomial transform
\[
b_m=\sum_{k=0}^m\binom{m}{k}(-2)^{m-k}T_k,
\tag{2.1}
\]
and scale it by
\[
c_m=16^{-m}b_m.
\tag{2.2}
\]
Now split into even and odd sections:
\[
U_n=c_{2n}=256^{-n}b_{2n},
\qquad
V_n=c_{2n+1}=\frac{b_{2n+1}}{16\,256^n}.
\tag{2.3}
\]
The transform in the question is exactly \(K T=U\).

Let \(E\) denote the shift in Cooper's index \(k\), and let \(S\) denote
shift in the new index \(n\). Let \(N\) denote multiplication by \(n\) in
the target variable.

## 2.1 Shift conjugation

For the ordinary binomial transform,
\[
\mathcal B E=(E_m+2)\mathcal B.
\tag{2.4}
\]
After scaling and splitting parity, this becomes
\[
\boxed{
\mathcal K_2 E=\mathsf E\,\mathcal K_2,
\qquad
\mathsf E=
\begin{pmatrix}
2&16\\
16S&2
\end{pmatrix},
}
\tag{2.5}
\]
where \(\mathcal K_2T=(U,V)^T\).

Thus
\[
(KE T)_n=2U_n+16V_n.
\tag{2.6}
\]
In particular, the even section is not closed under one source shift.

Writing
\[
J=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\S&0\end{pmatrix},
\qquad J^2=S I,
\]
we have \(\mathsf E=2I+16J\), and hence
\[
\boxed{
K(E-2)^{2j}=256^jS^jK.
}
\tag{2.7}
\]
For odd powers one necessarily needs the odd section:
\[
K(E-2)^{2j+1}=16\,256^jS^jK_{\rm odd}.
\tag{2.8}
\]
This is the exact version of the “shift the summation index” idea. Only
polynomials in \((E-2)^2\) close on \(U\) alone.

For reference,
\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathsf E^2&=
\begin{pmatrix}
4+256S&64\\64S&4+256S
\end{pmatrix},\\
\mathsf E^3&=
\begin{pmatrix}
8+1536S&192+4096S\\
(192+4096S)S&8+1536S
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{2.9}
\]

## 2.2 Index conjugation

The ordinary binomial transform also satisfies
\[
\mathcal B\,k
=m(1+2E_m^{-1})\mathcal B.
\tag{2.10}
\]
After the scale (2.2) and parity split,
\[
\boxed{
\mathcal K_2\,k=\mathsf N\,\mathcal K_2,
\qquad
\mathsf N=
\begin{pmatrix}
2N&\dfrac N4S^{-1}\\[2mm]
\dfrac{2N+1}{8}&2N+1
\end{pmatrix}.
}
\tag{2.11}
\]
The matrices satisfy the correct Ore relation
\[
\mathsf E\mathsf N=(\mathsf N+I)\mathsf E.
\tag{2.12}
\]
Therefore (2.5) and (2.11) define an exact representation of Cooper's
shift algebra in a \(2\times2\) Laurent-Ore algebra over \(\mathbf Q(n)\).

# 3. The exact image operator before scalar elimination

Shift (1.1) forward by two indices and write it with nonnegative powers of
\(E\):
\[
L_C^+=(k+3)^3E^3-P(k+2)E^2-Q(k+2)E-R(k+2).
\tag{3.1}
\]
Using (2.5) and (2.11), the exact two-parity image is
\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf L_K={}&(\mathsf N+3I)^3\mathsf E^3
-P(\mathsf N+2I)\mathsf E^2\\
&-Q(\mathsf N+2I)\mathsf E
-R(\mathsf N+2I).
\end{aligned}
}
\tag{3.2}
\]
The order of every factor in (3.2) is significant; multiplication is in the
Ore ring with \(Sf(n)=f(n+1)S\).

For every bilateral solution of Cooper's recurrence,
\[
\boxed{
\mathbf L_K\binom{U}{V}=0.
}
\tag{3.3}
\]
For the unilateral self-starting sequence, the same identity holds after the
finite initial boundary range is passed, or with the corresponding explicit
boundary terms retained.

Equation (3.2) is the correct explicit meaning of “\(K\cdot L_C\)”. It is a
matrix operator. The even-only map \(K\) is not invertible because it discards
\(V\), so the expression \(K E K^{-1}\) does not exist as a scalar Ore
operator.

# 4. A completely expanded scalar recurrence for the ordinary transform

There is also a convenient scalar form before taking the even section.
Let
\[
Z(x)=\sum_{n\ge0}T_nx^n,
\qquad
B(r)=\sum_{m\ge0}b_mr^m.
\]
The binomial transform gives
\[
B(r)=\frac1{1+2r}Z\!\left(\frac{r}{1+2r}\right).
\tag{4.1}
\]
Direct substitution in Cooper's third-order differential equation gives
\[
\boxed{
\sum_{j=0}^6 r^jF_j(\delta)B(r)=0,
\qquad \delta=r\frac d{dr},
}
\tag{4.2}
\]
where
\[
\begin{aligned}
F_0(x)&=x^3,\\
F_1(x)&=-8x^3-18x^2-10x-2,\\
F_2(x)&=-84x^3-192x^2-164x-52,\\
F_3(x)&=-236x^3-678x^2-718x-268,\\
F_4(x)&=-280x^3-1032x^2-1312x-560,\\
F_5(x)&=-144x^3-696x^2-1080x-528,\\
F_6(x)&=-32(x+1)(x+2)(x+3).
\end{aligned}
\tag{4.3}
\]
Equivalently, the coefficients \(b_m\) satisfy the explicit six-lag
recurrence
\[
\boxed{
 m^3b_m+\sum_{j=1}^6F_j(m-j)b_{m-j}=0.
}
\tag{4.4}
\]
The first values are
\[
 b_m=1,2,16,140,1420,15512,178444,\ldots
\tag{4.5}
\]
and (4.4) checks term by term. For example, at \(m=2\),
\[
8\cdot16-38\cdot2-52\cdot1=0.
\]

The leading characteristic polynomial of (4.4) is
\[
\begin{aligned}
P_B(s)={}&s^6-8s^5-84s^4-236s^3-280s^2-144s-32\\
={}&(s+2)^3C(s+2).
\end{aligned}
\tag{4.6}
\]
The three roots \(s=t_i-2\) are the transformed Cooper modes. The additional
triple root \(s=-2\) is the extraneous branch introduced when the scalar
coefficient recurrence is written without retaining the full transformed
module and its boundary conditions.

# 5. Explicit even/odd coupled recurrence

Divide (4.4) by \(16^m\), and use \(c_m=16^{-m}b_m\). Then
\[
 m^3c_m+\sum_{j=1}^6 16^{-j}F_j(m-j)c_{m-j}=0.
\tag{5.1}
\]
Putting \(c_{2n}=U_n\) and \(c_{2n+1}=V_n\) gives two explicit equations.

For even indices:
\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
0={}&(2n)^3U_n
+\sum_{\ell=1}^3 16^{-2\ell}
 F_{2\ell}(2n-2\ell)U_{n-\ell}\\
&+\sum_{\ell=0}^2 16^{-(2\ell+1)}
 F_{2\ell+1}(2n-2\ell-1)V_{n-\ell-1}.
\end{aligned}
}
\tag{5.2}
\]
For odd indices:
\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
0={}&(2n+1)^3V_n
+\sum_{\ell=1}^3 16^{-2\ell}
 F_{2\ell}(2n+1-2\ell)V_{n-\ell}\\
&+\sum_{\ell=0}^2 16^{-(2\ell+1)}
 F_{2\ell+1}(2n-2\ell)U_{n-\ell}.
\end{aligned}
}
\tag{5.3}
\]
Equations (5.2)–(5.3) are an entirely explicit, rational, finite image system.
Eliminating \(V\) gives a scalar annihilator of \(U\) of order at most six.

At infinity its characteristic polynomial is
\[
\boxed{
(\lambda-1/64)^3\chi_{27}(\lambda).
}
\tag{5.4}
\]
Indeed, two-sectioning and the factor \(256^{-n}\) send a base \(s\) to
\(s^2/256\). The triple root \(-2\) in (4.6) gives \(1/64\), while the
three roots \(t_i-2\) give (1.7).

This is the exact structural reason the challenge cubic appears. To prove
that the scalar order-six elimination has a rational order-three factor equal,
up to gauge, to the challenge operator, one must actually factor that
elimination operator. Spectral factorization alone does not prove finite-
\(n\) factorization.

# 6. Why the direct finite Ore intertwiner cannot exist

## 6.1 Base-preservation lemma

Let
\[
u_n\sim \lambda^n n^\alpha
\]
and let
\[
T=\sum_{j=0}^r t_j(n)S^j,
\qquad t_j(n)\in\mathbf C(n).
\]
If \(T u\) is not identically zero on this branch, then
\[
(Tu)_n\sim \lambda^n n^{\alpha+d}\tau(\lambda)
\]
for some integer \(d\) and some nonzero leading symbol \(\tau\). Cancellations
can lower the power of \(n\), or annihilate the branch, but they do not replace
\(\lambda\) by a nonlinear function of \(\lambda\).

A scalar hypergeometric gauge with
\[
\frac{g_{n+1}}{g_n}\longrightarrow\gamma
\]
multiplies every base by the same number \(\gamma\). Therefore a rank-three
finite Ore equivalence can only identify the two multisets of roots up to one
common scale and permutation.

## 6.2 A scale-invariant contradiction

For Cooper's roots,
\[
\sum t_i=20,
\qquad
\prod t_i=44.
\]
The scale-invariant quantity
\[
J=\frac{(\sum t_i)^3}{\prod t_i}
\]
is therefore
\[
J_C=\frac{20^3}{44}=\frac{2000}{11}.
\tag{6.1}
\]
For the challenge roots,
\[
\sum\lambda_i=\frac{55}{64},
\qquad
\prod\lambda_i=2^{-20},
\]
so
\[
J_{27}
=\left(\frac{55}{64}\right)^3 2^{20}
=4\cdot55^3
=665500.
\tag{6.2}
\]
These are unequal. Hence the two spectra are not related by a common scalar
multiplier.

Therefore
\[
\boxed{
\text{there is no rank-three finite Ore intertwiner }
L_{27}T=R L_C
\text{ in the original index.}
}
\tag{6.3}
\]
The centered binomial transform is essential precisely because it is nonlocal
and is capable of the nonlinear map (1.7).

This also invalidates the proposed scalar expression involving
\(K E^jK^{-1}\): the even transform \(K\) has no inverse. The invertible object
is the full two-parity transform \(\mathcal K_2\), and its conjugated shift is
the matrix (2.5).

# 7. Why the observed gauge behaves like \(n^{3/2}\)

The observed asymptotic is real, but it is only a formal-exponent comparison.

## 7.1 Cooper exponent

Insert
\[
T_n\sim t^n n^\beta
\]
into (1.1). The leading equation is \(C(t)=0\). The coefficient of \(1/n\)
gives
\[
\beta\,[t^3-56t+88]
+[3t^3-30t^2+66]=0.
\]
Using \(C(t)=0\),
\[
\begin{aligned}
t^3-56t+88&=4(5t^2-28t+33),\\
3t^3-30t^2+66&=6(5t^2-28t+33),
\end{aligned}
\]
and therefore
\[
\boxed{\beta=-\frac32}
\tag{7.1}
\]
for all three Cooper branches.

The binomial transform preserves this power exponent generically:
\[
\mathcal B[t^k k^\beta]_m
=(t-2)^m\left(\frac{mt}{t-2}\right)^\beta
(1+O(m^{-1})).
\tag{7.2}
\]
Thus \(U_n\) also has power exponent \(-3/2\).

## 7.2 Challenge exponent

The next-to-leading coefficient divided by the leading coefficient in each of
the four degree-12 challenge polynomials is the same number:
\[
\begin{aligned}
A:&\quad 34+\frac{149}{22}=\frac{897}{22},\\
B:&\quad 24+\frac{369}{22}=\frac{897}{22},\\
C:&\quad \frac{51}{2}+\frac{168}{11}=\frac{897}{22},\\
D:&\quad 36+\frac{105}{22}=\frac{897}{22}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{7.3}
\]
Consequently the three rational recurrence coefficients in (1.4) have no
\(1/n\) correction. Substitution of
\(u_n\sim\lambda^n n^\alpha\) then gives \(\alpha=0\) at every simple
Poincaré root.

Therefore any scalar comparison between the transformed Cooper branch and a
challenge branch must satisfy
\[
\boxed{
\frac{g_{n+1}}{g_n}
=1+\frac{3}{2n}+O(n^{-2}),
\qquad
g_n\asymp n^{3/2}.
}
\tag{7.4}
\]
This explains the numerical observation without proving an exact gauge.

## 7.3 Why this does not determine a gamma quotient

If
\[
g_n=C\frac{\Gamma(n+a)}{\Gamma(n+b)},
\]
then
\[
\frac{g_{n+1}}{g_n}=\frac{n+a}{n+b}
=1+\frac{a-b}{n}+O(n^{-2}).
\]
Equation (7.4) gives only
\[
a-b=\frac32.
\tag{7.5}
\]
There are infinitely many such pairs. More generally,
\[
\prod_i\frac{(a_i)_n}{(b_i)_n}
\]
has the same asymptotic whenever
\(\sum_i(a_i-b_i)=3/2\).

For the canonical Cooper solution, the first transformed values are
\[
\widehat T_0=1,
\quad
\widehat T_1=\frac1{16},
\quad
\widehat T_2=\frac{355}{16384},
\quad
\widehat T_3=\frac{44611}{4194304}.
\tag{7.6}
\]
Using the repository normalization of the challenge denominator \(q_n\), the
first two exact ratios of the tautological sequence
\(g_n=q_n/\widehat T_n\) are
\[
\rho_0=\frac{g_1}{g_0}
=16\frac{167282265043404}{905\cdot215040420000}
\approx13.7531191069,
\tag{7.7}
\]
\[
\rho_1=\frac{g_2}{g_1}
=\frac{1024}{355}
\frac{964185327658080\cdot905}
{6071\cdot167282265043404}
\approx2.47839524964.
\tag{7.8}
\]
These do not match the obvious one-factor choices such as
\((n+5/2)/(n+1)\) or \((n+3)/(n+3/2)\). They do not rule out a more
complicated rational coboundary, a different Cooper companion solution, or an
order-one/order-two Darboux map after the image factor is extracted.

The exact gauge can only be recovered after an explicit order-three image
factor \(L_K^{(3)}\) is known.

# 8. The corrected Ore-intertwiner computation

## 8.1 Creative telescoping first

The summation kernel is
\[
H(n,k)=256^{-n}\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}.
\tag{8.1}
\]
Its two basic rational shift quotients are
\[
\boxed{
\frac{H(n,k+1)}{H(n,k)}
=-\frac{2n-k}{2(k+1)},
}
\tag{8.2}
\]
\[
\boxed{
\frac{H(n+1,k)}{H(n,k)}
=\frac{(2n+2)(2n+1)}
{64(2n+2-k)(2n+1-k)}.
}
\tag{8.3}
\]
A correct certificate has the mixed-Ore form
\[
\boxed{
L_K^{(n)}H(n,k)
=U(n,k)(L_C^{(k)})^*H(n,k)
+(S_k-1)\bigl(V(n,k)H(n,k)\bigr).
}
\tag{8.4}
\]
Summing over \(k\), checking the finite boundaries, and using \(L_CT=0\)
then proves \(L_K(KT)=0\).

The two-parity construction gives the rigorous order bound
\[
\boxed{\operatorname{ord}L_K\le6.}
\tag{8.5}
\]
An order-three right factor with characteristic polynomial \(\chi_{27}\) is
possible and is strongly suggested by (5.4), but it must be found by exact Ore
factorization or by saturating the transformed module; it does not follow from
(5.4) alone.

## 8.2 Intertwiner after the factor is known

Suppose an order-three factor
\[
L_K^{(3)}=a_0(n)+a_1(n)S+a_2(n)S^2+a_3(n)S^3
\]
has been extracted. Write
\[
L_{27}=b_0(n)+b_1(n)S+b_2(n)S^2+b_3(n)S^3.
\]
Any module homomorphism may be represented modulo \(L_K^{(3)}\) by
\[
\boxed{T=t_0(n)+t_1(n)S+t_2(n)S^2.}
\tag{8.6}
\]
Thus the exact order bound is
\[
\boxed{\operatorname{ord}T\le2.}
\tag{8.7}
\]
Likewise one may take \(R=r_0+r_1S+r_2S^2\). Equating coefficients in
\[
L_{27}T=R L_K^{(3)}
\]
gives the six linear difference equations
\[
\boxed{
\sum_{i+j=m}b_i(n)t_j(n+i)
=
\sum_{i+j=m}r_i(n)a_j(n+i),
\qquad 0\le m\le5.
}
\tag{8.8}
\]
Once denominators are fixed, this is ordinary linear algebra over \(\mathbf Q\).

There is no universal numerator-degree bound determined solely by “order 3,
degree 12”. The rigorous procedure is:

1. compute an Abramov universal denominator from the finite singular shift
   orbits of the two companion systems;
2. write \(t_j=p_j/D\) and \(r_j=q_j/D'\);
3. use the indicial system at infinity to bound \(\deg p_j,\deg q_j\);
4. solve (8.8).

A blind degree cutoff is heuristic. The order cutoff \(2\), in contrast, is
exact.

## 8.3 Recovering a scalar gauge

If the comparison is purely scalar, \(T=t_0(n)\), and if the operators are
normalized with nonzero leading coefficients, the ratio
\(\rho_n=g_{n+1}/g_n\) is obtained directly from the \(S^2/S^3\)
coefficients. With
\[
b_j(n)=\mu(n)a_j(n)\frac{g_n}{g_{n+j}},
\]
one has
\[
\boxed{
\rho_{n+2}
=\frac{b_2(n)a_3(n)}{b_3(n)a_2(n)}.
}
\tag{8.9}
\]
The remaining coefficients provide exact consistency checks. Formula (8.9)
cannot be used until \(L_K^{(3)}\) is explicitly known.

# 9. Exact verification code

The following Sage/Python code uses exact integers and rationals. It verifies
Cooper's sequence, the transformed recurrence (4.4), and the initial even
section. It does not assume the challenge factorization.

```python
from sage.all import *

QQx.<x> = PolynomialRing(QQ)

F = [
    x^3,
    -8*x^3 - 18*x^2 - 10*x - 2,
    -84*x^3 - 192*x^2 - 164*x - 52,
    -236*x^3 - 678*x^2 - 718*x - 268,
    -280*x^3 - 1032*x^2 - 1312*x - 560,
    -144*x^3 - 696*x^2 - 1080*x - 528,
    -32*(x+1)*(x+2)*(x+3),
]

def cooper_terms(N):
    T = [QQ(1), QQ(4), QQ(28)]
    for n in range(2, N):
        v = (
            2*(2*n+1)*(5*n^2+5*n+2)*T[n]
            - 8*n*(7*n^2+1)*T[n-1]
            + 22*n*(2*n-1)*(n-1)*T[n-2]
        ) / (n+1)^3
        T.append(QQ(v))
    return T

def centered_binomial(T, m):
    return sum(binomial(m,k)*(-2)^(m-k)*T[k] for k in range(m+1))

T = cooper_terms(30)
b = [centered_binomial(T, m) for m in range(20)]
assert b[:7] == [1,2,16,140,1420,15512,178444]

for m in range(6, len(b)):
    assert m^3*b[m] + sum(F[j](m-j)*b[m-j] for j in range(1,7)) == 0

U = [b[2*n] / QQ(256)^n for n in range(len(b)//2)]
assert U[:4] == [QQ(1), QQ(1)/16, QQ(355)/16384, QQ(44611)/4194304]

s = polygen(QQ, 's')
C = s^3 - 20*s^2 + 56*s - 44
PB = s^6 - 8*s^5 - 84*s^4 - 236*s^3 - 280*s^2 - 144*s - 32
assert PB == (s+2)^3 * C(s+2)
```

For the final computation, one should build (5.2)–(5.3), eliminate \(V\) in
\(\mathbf Q(n)\langle S\rangle\), factor the scalar order-six result, and only
then solve (8.8) against the primitive challenge operator.

# Final answers to (a), (b), and (c)

## (a) Image operator

The exact image is the two-parity matrix Ore operator (3.2), equivalently the
fully expanded scalar recurrence (4.4) together with the even/odd system
(5.2)–(5.3). The even-only scalar elimination has order at most six and
spectral polynomial
\[
(\lambda-1/64)^3\chi_{27}(\lambda).
\]
It is not legitimate to replace this by a scalar expression
\(K E K^{-1}\), because the even transform has no inverse and is not closed
under one source shift.

## (b) Ore intertwiner

There is no finite rational Ore intertwiner directly from \(L_C\) to
\(L_{27}\): such an operator cannot implement
\(t\mapsto(t-2)^2/256\), and the scale-invariant spectral quantities
(6.1)–(6.2) disagree.

The correct target is an intertwiner from an extracted order-three factor
\(L_K^{(3)}\) of the transformed/eliminated operator to \(L_{27}\). If that
factor exists over \(\mathbf Q(n)\), the intertwiner may be taken of order at
most two and is found from (8.8).

## (c) Gauge

The asymptotic law
\[
g_{n+1}/g_n=1+3/(2n)+O(n^{-2})
\]
is forced by the difference between the Cooper exponent \(-3/2\) and the
challenge exponent \(0\). It does not identify a unique gamma product. A
single quotient \(\Gamma(n+a)/\Gamma(n+b)\) would require only
\(a-b=3/2\), and the first exact ratios for the canonical transformed Cooper
solution do not match the obvious half-integral choices.

An exact Pochhammer or rational gauge can be determined only after the
order-three image factor has been computed; then (8.9) gives its ratio
immediately.
