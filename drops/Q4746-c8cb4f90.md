# Problem 2.1: exact elliptic-period identities for \(u^2=x(1+11x-x^2)\)

## Executive verdict

There are two important corrections to the proposed strategy.

1. The curve has **no nonzero \(\mathbf Q\)-rational point of order 3**. It does have a Galois-stable cyclic subgroup of order 3, hence a rational 3-isogeny. Its two nonzero geometric points are defined over \(\mathbf Q(\sqrt3)\).
2. The numbers \(\phi^5\) and \(-\phi^{-5}\) are simple branch points of the fixed double cover. They are not nodal fibers of a family. An Abelian integral with a moving endpoint has square-root behavior there, not logarithmic behavior. A logarithm appears only after introducing a genuine deformation in which two branch points collide.

The useful positive result is very concrete. Put

\[
a=\phi^5=\frac{11+5\sqrt5}{2},\qquad
b=\phi^{-5}=\frac{5\sqrt5-11}{2},\qquad
S=a+b=5\sqrt5.
\]

Then \(ab=1\), \(a-b=11\), and

\[
u^2=x(a-x)(x+b).
\]

A tangent at a 3-torsion flex gives the exact single-integral identities

\[
J_+=\frac65 I_+,
\qquad
J_-=\frac65 I_-+\frac{6\pi}{5\sqrt3},
\]

where

\[
\begin{aligned}
I_+&=\int_0^a\frac{dx}{\sqrt{x(a-x)(x+b)}},\\
J_+&=\int_0^a\frac{dx}{(x+\frac13)\sqrt{x(a-x)(x+b)}},\\
I_-&=\int_{-b}^0\frac{dx}{\sqrt{-x(a-x)(x+b)}},\\
J_-&=\int_{-b}^0\frac{dx}{(x+\frac13)\sqrt{-x(a-x)(x+b)}}.
\end{aligned}
\]

Thus

\[
\boxed{J_- - \frac65 I_- = \frac{6\pi}{5\sqrt3}.}
\]

This is the structural identity that should replace a blind PSLQ search. In particular, for every rational \(c\),

\[
\boxed{
\frac{6c}{5\sqrt3}\left(J_- - \frac65 I_-\right)^{-1}=\frac{c}{\pi}.
}
\]

Equivalently, in complete elliptic-integral notation one obtains an explicit \(1/\pi\) identity displayed in Section 4 below.

What cannot be determined from the information in the question is the rational number \(c\). The matrix field, its initial vector, the exact double integral, the region, the branches, and the decomposition defining \(I_0,I_1\) were not supplied. Multiplying the CMF output by a rational scalar changes \(c\) without changing the elliptic curve. The minimal remaining certificate is therefore an exact derivation of the PCF limit as a rational multiple of the reciprocal of the boxed period difference.

## 1. The curve, its branch points, and its invariants

The branch points are

\[
-b=-\phi^{-5}<0< a=\phi^5,
\]

together with the point at infinity. The identities

\[
a-b=11,\qquad ab=1
\]

give

\[
x(1+11x-x^2)=x(a-x)(x+b).
\]

For arithmetic purposes set

\[
X=-x,\qquad Y=u.
\]

Then

\[
E:\quad Y^2=X^3+11X^2-X.
\]

This is the integral Weierstrass model with coefficients

```text
[a1,a2,a3,a4,a6] = [0,11,0,-1,0].
```

Its basic invariants are

\[
\begin{aligned}
b_2&=44,& b_4&=-2,& b_6&=0,& b_8&=-1,\\
c_4&=1984=2^6\cdot31,\\
c_6&=-88352,\\
\Delta&=2000=2^4\cdot5^3,\\
j(E)&=\frac{c_4^3}{\Delta}
     =\frac{2^{14}31^3}{5^3}
     =\frac{488095744}{125}.
\end{aligned}
\]

The nonintegral rational value of \(j\) proves that this is **not a CM elliptic curve**: a CM \(j\)-invariant is an algebraic integer, and a rational algebraic integer is an integer. Here “CMF” must therefore mean Conservative Matrix Field, not complex multiplication.

## 2. Answer to (a): a period basis in complete elliptic integrals

Use the convention

\[
K(k)=\int_0^{\pi/2}\frac{d\theta}{\sqrt{1-k^2\sin^2\theta}}.
\]

Define complementary moduli by

\[
\begin{aligned}
k^2&=\frac{a}{a+b}
 =\frac12+\frac{11}{10\sqrt5},\\
{k'}^2&=\frac{b}{a+b}
 =\frac12-\frac{11}{10\sqrt5},\\
k^2+{k'}^2&=1.
\end{aligned}
\]

Numerically,

```text
a  = 11.09016994374947...
b  =  0.090169943749474...
k  =  0.995959313953112...
k' =  0.0898055953159172...
```

### 2.1 The real oval

Choose \(u>0\) on \((0,a)\). With \(x=a\sin^2 t\), followed by \(t\mapsto\pi/2-t\),

\[
\begin{aligned}
I_+
&=\int_0^a\frac{dx}{\sqrt{x(a-x)(x+b)}}\\
&=\frac{2}{\sqrt{a+b}}K(k)
 =\frac{2}{\sqrt{5\sqrt5}}K(k).
\end{aligned}
\]

A closed cycle \(\alpha\) surrounding the cut \([0,a]\) has period

\[
\boxed{
\Omega_\alpha=\oint_\alpha\frac{dx}{u}
 =2I_+
 =\frac{4}{\sqrt{5\sqrt5}}K(k).
}
\]

### 2.2 The complementary cycle

On the upper side of the cut \([-b,0]\), take

\[
u=i\sqrt{-x(a-x)(x+b)}.
\]

Then

\[
I_-=\int_{-b}^0\frac{dx}{\sqrt{-x(a-x)(x+b)}}
   =\frac{2}{\sqrt{5\sqrt5}}K(k').
\]

With a compatible orientation, an independent cycle \(\beta\) has period

\[
\boxed{
\Omega_\beta=\oint_\beta\frac{dx}{u}
 =\frac{4i}{\sqrt{5\sqrt5}}K(k').
}
\]

Reversing \(\beta\) changes the sign. Thus a standard half-period convention is

\[
\boxed{
\omega_1=\frac{2}{\sqrt{5\sqrt5}}K(k),\qquad
\omega_2=\frac{2i}{\sqrt{5\sqrt5}}K(k').
}
\]

The lattice periods are \(2\omega_1,2\omega_2\), and

\[
\tau=\frac{\omega_2}{\omega_1}
=i\frac{K(k')}{K(k)}.
\]

For numerical checking,

```text
omega1 = 2.27416519904108...
omega2 = 0.941459190912278... i
2 omega1 = 4.54833039808216...
2 omega2 = 1.88291838182456... i
```

The factor of two is a convention issue: an interval from one branch point to another is a half-period, while the loop around the cut traverses both sheets.

## 3. Answer to (b): 3-torsion, the flex function, and the exact period relation

### 3.1 There is no nonzero rational 3-torsion

For

\[
E:\quad Y^2=X^3+11X^2-X,
\]

the 3-division polynomial factors as

\[
\boxed{
\psi_3(X)=3X^4+44X^3-6X^2-1
=(3X-1)(X^3+15X^2+3X+1).
}
\]

The cubic factor has no rational root: by the rational-root theorem only \(\pm1\) are possible, and neither is a root. The remaining possible rational \(X\)-coordinate is \(X=1/3\), but

\[
Y^2=\frac{25}{27},
\]

which is not a square in \(\mathbf Q\). Therefore

\[
\boxed{E(\mathbf Q)[3]=0.}
\]

Over \(\mathbf Q(\sqrt3)\), however, the two nonzero points

\[
P_\pm=\left(\frac13,\ \pm\frac{5\sqrt3}{9}\right)
\]

have order 3. Galois conjugation interchanges \(P_+\) and \(P_-=-P_+\), so

\[
\{O,P_+,P_-\}
\]

is a rational cyclic subgroup scheme. This is exactly why a rational 3-isogeny exists even though there is no rational point of order 3.

In the original coordinates these points are

\[
T_\pm=\left(-\frac13,\ \pm\frac{5\sqrt3}{9}\right).
\]

### 3.2 The tangent at the flex

At \(P_+\), the tangent line is

\[
\ell(X)=\sqrt3\left(2X-\frac19\right).
\]

A direct expansion gives the unusually strong identity

\[
\boxed{
Y^2-\ell(X)^2=\left(X-\frac13\right)^3.
}
\]

Thus the tangent meets the cubic with multiplicity three at \(P_+\), as it must for a 3-torsion flex. Define

\[
F(X,Y)=\frac{Y-\ell(X)}{Y+\ell(X)}.
\]

Then

\[
\operatorname{div}(F)=3(P_+)-3(P_-).
\]

Differentiating on the curve yields the exact third-kind differential

\[
\boxed{
 d\log F
 =\sqrt3\,\frac{2X+1}{(X-\frac13)Y}\,dX.
}
\]

In the original variables, put

\[
\mathcal F(x,u)=
\frac{u+\sqrt3(2x+\frac19)}
     {u-\sqrt3(2x+\frac19)}.
\]

Then

\[
\operatorname{div}(\mathcal F)=3(T_+)-3(T_-)
\]

and

\[
\boxed{
 d\log\mathcal F
 =\sqrt3\left(-2+\frac{5}{3(x+\frac13)}\right)\frac{dx}{u}.
}
\]

This formula is the structural certificate. Its periods are integral multiples of \(2\pi i\), because it is the logarithmic differential of a meromorphic function.

### 3.3 The interval \([0,a]\): no winding

On \([0,a]\), choose \(u>0\). The identity

\[
u^2-3\left(2x+\frac19\right)^2
=-\left(x+\frac13\right)^3
\]

shows that \(\mathcal F(x,u)<0\) throughout the open interval, and \(\mathcal F=-1\) at both endpoints. Hence a continuous logarithm has no net change. Integrating \(d\log\mathcal F\) gives

\[
-2I_++\frac53J_+=0,
\]

so

\[
\boxed{J_+=\frac65 I_+.}
\]

No numerical recognition is involved.

### 3.4 The interval \([-b,0]\): one full winding

Let

\[
v(x)=\sqrt{-x(a-x)(x+b)}>0,
\qquad u=iv.
\]

Write

\[
q(x)=2x+\frac19,
\qquad t(x)=\frac{\sqrt3\,q(x)}{v(x)}.
\]

Then on the upper side of the cut

\[
\mathcal F(x,iv)
 =\frac{i+t(x)}{i-t(x)}
 =e^{-2i\arctan t(x)}.
\]

As \(x\) runs from \(-b\) to \(0\),

\[
t(x): -\infty\longrightarrow 0\longrightarrow +\infty.
\]

The continuous argument of \(\mathcal F\) therefore decreases from \(\pi\) to \(-\pi\). Consequently

\[
\int_{-b}^0 d\log\mathcal F=-2\pi i.
\]

Since \(dx/u=-i\,dx/v\), the logarithmic-differential identity gives

\[
\frac53J_- -2I_-=\frac{2\pi}{\sqrt3}.
\]

Equivalently,

\[
\boxed{
J_-=\frac65I_-+\frac{6\pi}{5\sqrt3}.
}
\]

This is the promised tangent-line proof of a period identity. The \(\pi\)-term is topological: it is the winding number of the flex function, not a PSLQ coincidence.

## 4. The same identities in \(K\) and \(\Pi\) notation

Use

\[
\Pi(n,k)=\int_0^{\pi/2}
\frac{d\theta}
{(1-n\sin^2\theta)\sqrt{1-k^2\sin^2\theta}}.
\]

### 4.1 The positive cut

Set

\[
n_+=\frac{3a}{3a+1}.
\]

The same substitution as in Section 2 gives

\[
J_+=\frac{6}{\sqrt S(3a+1)}\Pi(n_+,k).
\]

Using \(J_+=6I_+/5\), and

\[
\frac{2(3a+1)}5=7+3\sqrt5,
\]

one obtains

\[
\boxed{
\Pi(n_+,k)=(7+3\sqrt5)K(k).
}
\]

### 4.2 The negative cut

Set

\[
n_-=-\frac{3b}{1-3b}.
\]

Here \(b<1/3\), and

\[
J_-=\frac{6}{\sqrt S(1-3b)}\Pi(n_-,k').
\]

The identity of Section 3 becomes

\[
\boxed{
\Pi(n_-,k')
=(7-3\sqrt5)K(k')
+\frac{7-3\sqrt5}{2}
 \sqrt{\frac{5\sqrt5}{3}}\,\pi.
}
\]

Therefore the curve itself supplies the explicit \(1/\pi\) formula

\[
\boxed{
\frac{
 \frac{7-3\sqrt5}{2}\sqrt{\frac{5\sqrt5}{3}}
}
{
 \Pi(n_-,k')-(7-3\sqrt5)K(k')
}
=\frac1\pi.
}
\]

This is likely the normal form against which the CMF limit should be compared. To prove that the PCF limit is \(c/\pi\), it is enough to prove the exact algebraic-period reduction

\[
L_{\rm PCF}
=c\,
\frac{
 \frac{7-3\sqrt5}{2}\sqrt{\frac{5\sqrt5}{3}}
}
{
 \Pi(n_-,k')-(7-3\sqrt5)K(k')
}
\]

for a rational \(c\). The period identity itself is then finished.

## 5. Answer to (c): what the nodal-deformation argument can and cannot prove

### 5.1 The branch points of the fixed curve are not nodal fibers

Let

\[
p(z)=z(1+11z-z^2)
\]

and let

\[
A(z)=\int^z\frac{dx}{\sqrt{p(x)}}
\]

be an incomplete Abelian integral. Since \(A'(z)=p(z)^{-1/2}\), it satisfies

\[
\boxed{
2p(z)A''(z)+p'(z)A'(z)=0,
}
\]

or explicitly

\[
2z(1+11z-z^2)A''
 +(1+22z-3z^2)A'=0.
\]

At every simple branch point the indicial exponents are

\[
0,\quad \frac12.
\]

Thus the local behavior is constant plus a square root. There is no logarithmic solution. Calling \(a\) and \(-b\) “nodal fibers” conflates an endpoint singularity with a degeneration of the curve.

### 5.2 A genuine nodal family

A useful deformation is

\[
\mathcal E_s:\quad
u^2=x(a-x)(x+s b).
\]

At \(s=1\) this is the original curve. Its discriminant is

\[
\Delta(s)=16s^2(a+s b)^2,
\]

because \(ab=1\). The fibers at

\[
s=0,\qquad s=-\frac ab=-a^2=-\phi^{10}
\]

are singular. At \(s=0\), the roots \(0\) and \(-sb\) collide and

\[
u^2=x^2(a-x)
\]

has a node at \((0,0)\).

The real-cycle period is

\[
\Omega(s)=
2\int_0^a\frac{dx}{\sqrt{x(a-x)(x+s b)}}
=\frac{4}{\sqrt{a+s b}}K(k_s),
\]

where

\[
k_s^2=\frac{a}{a+s b},
\qquad {k_s'}^2=\frac{s b}{a+s b}.
\]

As \(s\to0^+\), the standard asymptotic

\[
K(k_s)=\log\frac4{k_s'}+O({k_s'}^2\log k_s')
\]

gives

\[
\boxed{
\Omega(s)=
\frac{2}{\sqrt a}
\log\frac{16a}{s b}
+O(s\log s).
}
\]

Since

\[
\frac ab=a^2=\phi^{10},
\]

the regularized boundary constant is

\[
\boxed{
\lim_{s\to0^+}
\left(\Omega(s)+\frac{2}{\sqrt a}\log s\right)
=
\frac{2}{\sqrt a}
\left(\log16+10\log\phi\right).
}
\]

This is a precise way in which \(\log\phi\) enters a nodal degeneration. It does **not** by itself evaluate the period at \(s=1\); one must still compute the connection from the nodal Frobenius basis to the target fiber.

### 5.3 Hypergeometric Picard-Fuchs equation and the logarithmic solution

Let

\[
m=\frac{a}{a+s b},
\qquad
\mathbf K(m)=K(\sqrt m).
\]

Then \(\mathbf K(m)\) satisfies

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal L_m f:=
 m(1-m)f''+(1-2m)f'-\frac14f=0.
}
\]

The node \(s=0\) corresponds to \(m=1\). The exponents there coincide, so Frobenius theory gives a logarithmic solution. More explicitly,

\[
{}_2F_1\left(\frac12,\frac12;1;m\right)
=\frac2\pi\mathbf K(m)
=\frac1\pi\log\frac{16}{1-m}
+O((1-m)\log(1-m)).
\]

This logarithm comes from resonant local monodromy. Referring to it as a “\(\zeta(1)\)-term” is at best mnemonic; a rigorous proof should specify the differential equation, the local parameter, and the chosen Frobenius normalization.

### 5.4 Why an inhomogeneous equation naturally produces \(1/\pi\)

Suppose the CMF integral gives a normal function or iterated integral \(N(m)\) satisfying

\[
\mathcal L_m N=R(m),
\]

with an explicit rational or algebraic function \(R\). Take the homogeneous solutions

\[
y_1(m)=\mathbf K(m),
\qquad
y_2(m)=\mathbf K(1-m).
\]

Legendre's relation

\[
\mathbf K(m)\mathbf E(1-m)
+\mathbf E(m)\mathbf K(1-m)
-\mathbf K(m)\mathbf K(1-m)
=\frac\pi2
\]

implies the exact Wronskian

\[
\boxed{
W(y_1,y_2)=-\frac{\pi}{4m(1-m)}.
}
\]

Variation of parameters therefore gives a particular solution in the form

\[
\boxed{
N_p(m)=\frac4\pi
\left[
 y_1(m)\int^m y_2(t)R(t)\,dt
-y_2(m)\int^m y_1(t)R(t)\,dt
\right].
}
\]

This formula identifies the universal source of the reciprocal \(\pi\): it is the exactly normalized Wronskian of the two elliptic periods. If the two connection integrals on the right reduce to algebraic numbers and logarithms of algebraic units, then a \(c/\pi\) evaluation can follow.

But the rational number \(c\) depends on all of the following:

- the exact source \(R(m)\);
- the base point of the variation-of-parameters integrals;
- the branch of the logarithmic solution;
- the boundary value of the normal function;
- the normalization of the CMF solution and its initial vector.

None of those data are present in the question, so there is no honest way to state \(c\) yet.

## 6. Answer to (d): explicit isogeny to conductor 80

The curve is in the rational isogeny class of conductor \(80\). This can be shown by a completely explicit chain of a 3-isogeny, a 2-isogeny, and a rational change of variables.

### 6.1 The rational 3-isogeny

Starting from

\[
E:\quad Y^2=X^3+11X^2-X,
\]

put

\[
z=X+\frac{11}{3}.
\]

Then

\[
E_{\rm sh}:\quad
Y^2=z^3-\frac{124}{3}z+\frac{2761}{27}.
\]

The nonzero kernel points have

\[
z=4,\qquad Y=\pm\frac{5\sqrt3}{9}.
\]

Vélu's formulas give the rational map

\[
\begin{aligned}
Z&=z+\frac{40}{3(z-4)}
      +\frac{100}{27(z-4)^2},\\
V&=Y\left(
1-\frac{40}{3(z-4)^2}
 -\frac{200}{27(z-4)^3}
\right).
\end{aligned}
\]

A direct denominator-cleared calculation proves

\[
\boxed{V^2=Z^3-108Z-297.}
\]

These formulas are defined away from the kernel and extend uniquely to the degree-3 isogeny.

### 6.2 A 2-isogeny to the optimal conductor-80 model

Set

\[
T=Z+3.
\]

Then

\[
V^2=T^3-9T^2-81T,
\]

and \((T,V)=(0,0)\) is rational 2-torsion. The standard 2-isogeny is

\[
\begin{aligned}
S&=\frac{V^2}{T^2},\\
W&=-\frac{V(T^2+81)}{T^2}.
\end{aligned}
\]

It lands on

\[
\boxed{W^2=S^3+18S^2+405S.}
\]

Finally put

\[
S=9(\xi-1),\qquad W=27\eta.
\]

The result is

\[
\boxed{
E_{80}:\quad
\eta^2=\xi^3-\xi^2+4\xi-4.
}
\]

This is the optimal model traditionally labelled Cremona \(80b1\), in isogeny class \(80B\); the corresponding LMFDB isogeny-class notation is \(80.b\). Therefore

\[
\boxed{N(E)=80.}
\]

Every arrow above is over \(\mathbf Q\), and every landing identity is a `field_simp; ring` calculation. Thus the database identification is supported by an explicit degree-6 isogeny chain rather than by matching decimal invariants.

As an independent check, the original model has

\[
\Delta=2^4 5^3,
\]

and a Tate-algorithm computation gives conductor exponents \(4\) at \(2\) and \(1\) at \(5\), again yielding \(2^4\cdot5=80\).

The conductor-80 class has rank zero. The rational torsion of the original curve is exactly \(\mathbf Z/2\mathbf Z\): \((0,0)\) is 2-torsion; reduction modulo \(3\) has two points and controls all prime-to-3 torsion; and Section 3 proves that there is no rational 3-torsion.

Consequently

\[
L(E,s)=L(80.b,s),
\]

and modular symbols identify \(L(E,1)\) with a rational multiple of a Néron period. This is a useful cross-check and a possible alternative proof endpoint, but a database statement about \(L(E,1)\) still does not identify the normalization of the CMF limit.

## 7. A rigorous route from the CMF to \(c/\pi\)

The most efficient proof architecture is now:

### Step 1: prove the PCF-to-integral identity

Starting from the actual matrix field and its initial vector, prove an exact formula for the limit. Do not leave this as a numerical observation. The output should specify the two-form, the region, orientations, and square-root branches.

### Step 2: reduce every one-dimensional factor to a fixed basis

Use the basis

\[
I_+,\ I_-,\ J_+,\ J_-
\]

or equivalently

\[
K(k),\ K(k'),\ \Pi(n_+,k),\ \Pi(n_-,k').
\]

All substitutions are elementary and are given above.

### Step 3: eliminate the third-kind integrals algebraically

Use

\[
J_+=\frac65I_+,
\qquad
J_-=\frac65I_-+\frac{6\pi}{5\sqrt3}.
\]

These identities follow from the single rational function \(\mathcal F\) and its winding number. They should be proved before running any PSLQ.

### Step 4: eliminate remaining period/quasi-period determinants

If the double integral produces products of periods and second-kind periods, use Legendre's relation. Its determinant is exactly \(\pi/2\), and its reciprocal Wronskian is exactly proportional to \(1/\pi\).

### Step 5: isolate the one genuinely problem-specific rational coefficient

A particularly clean target lemma is

\[
\boxed{
L_{\rm PCF}
=\frac{6c}{5\sqrt3}
\left(J_- - \frac65I_-\right)^{-1}
}
\]

for a stated \(c\in\mathbf Q\). Once this is established, the conclusion \(L_{\rm PCF}=c/\pi\) is one line.

Equivalently use the \(K,\Pi\) formula from Section 4.

### Step 6: use PSLQ only as discovery

If numerical recognition is still needed to guess the reduction, use a number-field-aware basis. The algebraic coefficient field contains at least

\[
\mathbf Q\left(\sqrt5,\sqrt3,\sqrt{5\sqrt5}\right).
\]

A raw integer PSLQ on unnormalized decimal integrals can hide algebraic prefactors and can return a misleading relation. After guessing, prove the relation with the flex identity, Legendre relation, and exact algebra.

## 8. Minimal missing data needed to determine the rational constant

The following objects are still required before an explicit numerical value of \(c\) can be certified:

1. The exact 3-by-3 matrix field \(M\) and the precise scalar extracted from its PCF.
2. The initial vector and normalization of the recurrence solution.
3. The exact double-integral representation, including the rational integrand and the region.
4. The exact decomposition called \(I_0+I_1\).
5. The orientation and branch conventions for every elliptic or logarithmic integral.
6. An exact derivation reducing that expression to the period basis above.

Without these, the curve determines the period relations but not the rational scalar multiplying their reciprocal.

## 9. Exact checks suitable for a formal or CAS certificate

The following identities are small and should be checked symbolically.

```text
1.  3*X^4 + 44*X^3 - 6*X^2 - 1
      = (3*X - 1)*(X^3 + 15*X^2 + 3*X + 1).

2.  On Y^2 = X^3 + 11*X^2 - X:

    Y^2 - 3*(2*X - 1/9)^2 = (X - 1/3)^3.

3.  For F=(Y-sqrt(3)*(2*X-1/9))/(Y+sqrt(3)*(2*X-1/9)):

    dlog(F) = sqrt(3)*(2*X+1)/((X-1/3)*Y) dX.

4.  Substitute the displayed Velu formulas and clear denominators:

    V^2 - (Z^3 - 108*Z - 297) = 0.

5.  Substitute the displayed 2-isogeny formulas and clear denominators:

    W^2 - (S^3 + 18*S^2 + 405*S) = 0.

6.  Substitute S=9*(xi-1), W=27*eta:

    eta^2 - (xi^3 - xi^2 + 4*xi - 4) = 0.
```

The only non-polynomial ingredient in the key period proof is the winding-number statement on \([-b,0]\). It can be formalized using

\[
\mathcal F(x,iv)=e^{-2i\arctan(\sqrt3 q(x)/v(x))}
\]

and the endpoint limits \(-\infty\) and \(+\infty\).

## Final assessment
