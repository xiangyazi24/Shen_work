# Problem 3.1: certifying the beta endpoint as the maximal \((2,3,17)\) Fuchsian character

Let

\[
\begin{aligned}
f_\beta(s)={}&s^{21}-2s^{20}-3s^{19}+2s^{18}+2s^{17}
 +8s^{16}+6s^{15}+s^{14}+5s^{13}-4s^{12}\\
&-4s^9+5s^8+s^7+6s^6+8s^5+2s^4+2s^3-3s^2-2s+1.
\end{aligned}
\tag{0.1}
\]

This is the reciprocal polynomial obtained from \(A_{7_2}(s,s)\) after removing the monomial factor \(s^3\).

## Executive answer

1. **The most accessible certificate is the rotation/trace certificate, not global root uniqueness.** Once the exact face-pairing holonomy and the words for the Seifert generators are available, one only has to verify

   \[
   \operatorname{tr}X=0,
   \qquad
   \operatorname{tr}Y=-1,
   \qquad
   \operatorname{tr}(XY)=-2\cos\frac{\pi}{17},
   \tag{0.2}
   \]

   together with non-elementarity and the central lift. By the Fricke--Vogt trace-coordinate theorem, an irreducible pair in \(\mathrm{SL}_2\) is determined up to conjugacy by these three traces. Thus no explicit conjugating matrix is needed.

2. **The proposed uniqueness certificate does not hold at the level of \(A(s,s)\).** The polynomial \(f_\beta\) has exactly

   \[
   \boxed{2}
   \]

   real roots in \((0,1)\), not one. One is the challenge endpoint \(s_\beta\approx0.4068\); the other lies between \(1/2\) and \((6-\sqrt{11})/5\). Therefore counting roots of \(A(s,s)\) cannot by itself identify the maximal character.

3. The four real shapes at beta determine a projective holonomy only after one also specifies the ordered face pairings, a developing-map normalization, and the words representing the Seifert generators. The numerical tuple

   \[
   (T,U,V,W)\approx(-0.258288,4.34296,26.2420,3.55551)
   \]

   alone does **not** determine matrices called \(\rho(x)\) and \(\rho(y)\).

4. Conditional on the trace certificate, the filled character is conjugate to the standard uniformizing triangle-group representation. In the presentation written in the question, its \(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb R)\) shadow may be taken explicitly as

   \[
   \rho(h)=-I,
   \tag{0.3}
   \]

   \[
   \rho(x)=
   \begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix},
   \tag{0.4}
   \]

   and

   \[
   \rho(y)=
   \begin{pmatrix}
   -\frac12&-c+\frac d2\\[1mm]
   c+\frac d2&-\frac12
   \end{pmatrix},
   \qquad
   c=\cos\frac{\pi}{17},
   \quad
   d=\sqrt{4c^2-3}.
   \tag{0.5}
   \]

   These satisfy

   \[
   \rho(x)^2=\rho(h),
   \qquad
   \rho(y)^3=I=\rho(h)^2,
   \qquad
   \bigl(\rho(x)\rho(y)\bigr)^{17}=I=\rho(h)^{14}.
   \tag{0.6}
   \]

   Their traces are exactly

   \[
   \boxed{
   \operatorname{tr}\rho(x)=0,
   \quad
   \operatorname{tr}\rho(y)=-1,
   \quad
   \operatorname{tr}\rho(xy)=-2\cos\frac{\pi}{17}.}
   \tag{0.7}
   \]

   The absolute values are the ones stated in the question. The negative signs are the lift choices compatible with the displayed Seifert presentation after \(h\mapsto-I\).

5. **The exponent \(14\) in the central relation cannot be certified from ordinary \(2\times2\) matrices alone.** In \(\mathrm{SL}_2\), \(\rho(h)^{14}=I\), so all even central exponents have the same image. The integer \(14\) must be checked in the universal central extension, equivalently by translation/rotation numbers or by the continuous logarithm/flattening lift along the real deformation path.

6. Once the beta character is identified with this lifted uniformizing representation,

   \[
   \boxed{GV(\rho_\beta)=-\frac{242\pi^2}{51}}
   \]

   in the oriented Brooks--Goldman/Khoi convention used in Q4825. In Khoi's normalization the challenge integral gives

   \[
   GV(\rho_\beta)-GV(\rho_\alpha)=-\frac{16\pi^2}{85},
   \]

   while the renormalized invariant \(\mathcal G=-GV/4\) has difference \(4\pi^2/85\).

# 1. Exact count of the roots of \(f_\beta\) in \((0,1)\)

The polynomial is reciprocal of odd degree:

\[
f_\beta(s)=s^{21}f_\beta(1/s).
\]

Consequently \(s=-1\) is a root. Dividing by \(s+1\), and setting

\[
x=s+s^{-1},
\]

one obtains the exact identity

\[
\boxed{
f_\beta(s)=(s+1)s^{10}g(s+s^{-1}),}
\tag{1.1}
\]

where

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
g(x)={}&x^{10}-3x^9-10x^8+29x^7+35x^6-87x^5\\
&-52x^4+81x^3+35x^2-16x-4.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{1.2}
\]

For \(0<s<1\), the map \(s\mapsto s+s^{-1}\) is strictly decreasing from \(+\infty\) to \(2\). Thus roots of \(f_\beta\) in \((0,1)\) are in bijection with roots of \(g\) in \((2,\infty)\).

Translate \(x=y+2\). Direct expansion gives

\[
\begin{aligned}
g(y+2)={}&y^{10}+17y^9+116y^8+397y^7+665y^6+305y^5\\
&-558y^4-663y^3-23y^2+152y+16.
\end{aligned}
\tag{1.3}
\]

The coefficient sequence has exactly two sign changes. By Descartes' rule, \(g\) has at most two roots in \((2,\infty)\). The following exact evaluations exhibit two distinct roots:

\[
\boxed{g\!\left(\frac{12}{5}\right)
=\frac{224426444}{9765625}>0,}
\tag{1.4}
\]

\[
\boxed{g\!\left(\frac52\right)
=-\frac{8181}{1024}<0,}
\tag{1.5}
\]

\[
\boxed{g\!\left(\frac{14}{5}\right)
=-\frac{642158784}{9765625}<0,}
\tag{1.6}
\]

and

\[
\boxed{g(3)=425>0.}
\tag{1.7}
\]

Hence there is one root in \((12/5,5/2)\) and one in \((14/5,3)\), and Descartes proves that these are all the roots greater than \(2\).

Transforming back to \(s\), the two positive roots are isolated exactly by

\[
\boxed{
\frac12<s_{\beta,1}<\frac{6-\sqrt{11}}5,}
\tag{1.8}
\]

and

\[
\boxed{
\frac{3-\sqrt5}{2}<s_{\beta,2}<\frac{7-2\sqrt6}{5}.}
\tag{1.9}
\]

The challenge endpoint \(s_\beta\approx0.4068\) is \(s_{\beta,2}\).

## 1.1 Why both roots survive the elementary real-shape test

For either root \(s\in(0,1)\), the beta deformation formulas from Q4820 are

\[
U(s)=\frac{1+s^5}{s^2(1+s)},
\qquad
R(s)=-\frac{1+\sqrt{1+4U(s)^2}}{2U(s)},
\tag{1.10}
\]

\[
T=\frac{R}{U},
\qquad
V=\frac{U}{s^2},
\qquad
W=\frac{1+s}{s(1-s^4)}.
\tag{1.11}
\]

Moreover

\[
1+s^5-s^2-s^3=(1-s^2)(1-s^3)>0,
\]

so \(U>1\). It follows that, for **both** positive roots,

\[
T<0,
\qquad
U>1,
\qquad
V>1,
\qquad
W>1.
\tag{1.12}
\]

Thus the sign pattern of the real shapes does not distinguish the challenge beta root from the other positive intersection.

A reducible knot-group representation has longitude eigenvalue \(1\). Since both roots have \(L=s\ne1\), the nonabelian \(A\)-polynomial intersection does not reduce to the abelian character. Therefore the statement "there is exactly one irreducible root in \((0,1)\)" cannot be obtained from \(f_\beta\).

The selected root is distinguished by continuation on the prescribed lifted real gluing component, not by the scalar equation \(A(s,s)=0\) alone.

# 2. The canonical uniformizing trace certificate

Let

\[
\theta=\frac{\pi}{17},
\qquad c=\cos\theta,
\qquad d=\sqrt{4c^2-3}>0.
\]

Define \(X_0,Y_0,H_0\) by (0.3)--(0.5) and put

\[
Z_0=X_0Y_0.
\]

## 2.1 Direct verification of the relations

First,

\[
\det X_0=\det Y_0=1.
\]

The matrix \(X_0\) satisfies

\[
X_0^2=-I=H_0.
\tag{2.1}
\]

Since \(\operatorname{tr}Y_0=-1\), Cayley--Hamilton gives

\[
Y_0^2+Y_0+I=0,
\]

and therefore

\[
Y_0^3=I=H_0^2.
\tag{2.2}
\]

A multiplication gives

\[
\operatorname{tr}Z_0=-2c.
\]

Thus

\[
Z_0^2+2cZ_0+I=0.
\tag{2.3}
\]

Its eigenvalues are

\[
-c\pm i\sqrt{1-c^2}
=e^{\pm16\pi i/17}.
\]

Consequently

\[
Z_0^{17}=I=H_0^{14},
\tag{2.4}
\]

and its projective order is exactly \(17\).

Projectively, replacing a matrix by its negative does not change the isometry. Hence \(Y_0\) and \(Z_0\) are the usual order-\(3\) and order-\(17\) rotations of the uniformizing \((2,3,17)\) triangle group, with signs chosen to match the stated \(\mathrm{SL}_2\) lift.

## 2.2 Non-elementarity

For a pair \(X,Y\in\mathrm{SL}_2\), the Fricke identity is

\[
\operatorname{tr}[X,Y]
=(\operatorname{tr}X)^2+(\operatorname{tr}Y)^2
+(\operatorname{tr}XY)^2
-\operatorname{tr}X\operatorname{tr}Y\operatorname{tr}XY-2.
\]

For \(X_0,Y_0\),

\[
\boxed{
\operatorname{tr}[X_0,Y_0]=4\cos^2\frac{\pi}{17}-1>2.}
\tag{2.5}
\]

Thus the commutator is hyperbolic and the image is non-elementary.

## 2.3 Why three traces are enough

For an irreducible representation of the free group on two generators, the conjugacy class is determined by

\[
\bigl(\operatorname{tr}X,\operatorname{tr}Y,\operatorname{tr}XY\bigr).
\]

Therefore, if the beta holonomy produces words \(X_\beta,Y_\beta\) satisfying

\[
\boxed{
\operatorname{tr}X_\beta=0,
\qquad
\operatorname{tr}Y_\beta=-1,
\qquad
\operatorname{tr}(X_\beta Y_\beta)=-2\cos\frac{\pi}{17},}
\tag{2.6}
\]

and the pair is non-elementary, then it is conjugate to \((X_0,Y_0)\) over \(\mathbb C\). Since the matrices are real, this gives the required Fuchsian conjugacy, up to the orientation-reversing real conjugate.

This is computationally shorter than constructing the conjugating matrix explicitly.

# 3. What the deformed shapes determine

At beta, after peripheral conjugation one may take

\[
\rho_\beta(m)=\rho_\beta(\ell)
=\begin{pmatrix}s_\beta&0\\0&s_\beta^{-1}\end{pmatrix},
\tag{3.1}
\]

up to simultaneous inversion and the lift sign. This records the \(-1\) filling relation.

The four tetrahedron shapes determine the remaining projective holonomy only after the following marking data are supplied:

1. the ordered vertices of every tetrahedron;
2. the eight face-pairing permutations;
3. a developing-map normalization;
4. loops representing the chosen knot-group generators;
5. a Tietze/Kirby word map from those knot generators to the Seifert generators \(x,y,h\).

Without these data, the same four numbers describe only an unmarked conjugacy class of gluing solutions. There is no canonical way to label two nonperipheral matrices as \(\rho(x)\) and \(\rho(y)\).

## 3.1 Exact face-pairing construction

For an ordered triple \((a,b,c)\) of distinct points of \(\mathbb P^1\), set

\[
T_{a,b,c}(z)
=\frac{(z-b)(c-a)}{(z-a)(c-b)}.
\tag{3.2}
\]

This sends \((a,b,c)\) to \((\infty,0,1)\). If a face pairing sends an ordered face \((a,b,c)\) to \((a',b',c')\), its Möbius matrix is the matrix of

\[
T_{a',b',c'}^{-1}\circ T_{a,b,c}.
\tag{3.3}
\]

Starting with one tetrahedron normalized to vertices \((\infty,0,1,T)\), equations (3.2)--(3.3) construct every face-pairing matrix as a rational function of the four shapes. Products along the specified loops give exact holonomy matrices in the beta number field.

The missing information is therefore combinatorial marking data, not numerical precision.

# 4. The central lift is a separate certificate

The matrices in Section 2 verify the relations after the central fiber is mapped to \(-I\). They do **not** distinguish the integer \(14\) from another even exponent in the universal center, because

\[
(-I)^{14}=I.
\]

For the Brooks--Goldman invariant one needs the lifted representation in the universal cover of \(\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb R)\). The final check is one of the following equivalent computations:

- compute the translation numbers of lifted \(x,y,xy\);
- compute the orbifold Euler class and show that its absolute value is
  \[
  -\chi_{\rm orb}=\frac{11}{102};
  \]
- transport the logarithm/flattening lift continuously from the known real component and verify the central exponents in the Seifert presentation.

The compatible endpoint flattenings found in Q4820 are exactly the sort of data needed for this last step. Ordinary traces alone certify the projective triangle group; the flattenings certify the central lift and therefore the sign of \(GV\).

# 5. Recommended finite computation

The shortest machine-checkable route is:

1. **Root isolation.** Isolate the chosen beta root in
   \[
   \left(\frac{3-\sqrt5}{2},\frac{7-2\sqrt6}{5}\right)
   \]
   and track it from the prescribed real gluing branch by interval Newton continuation.

2. **Exact shapes.** Work in the algebraic extension generated by \(s_\beta\) and
   \[
   R_\beta=-\frac{1+\sqrt{1+4U_\beta^2}}{2U_\beta}.
   \]

3. **Marked holonomy.** Export the ordered face pairings and form the exact Möbius matrices by (3.3).

4. **Seifert word map.** Supply explicit words \(w_x,w_y,w_h\) in the face-pairing or Wirtinger generators, obtained from a Tietze transformation of the \(-1\)-filled knot group to the Seifert presentation.

5. **Trace reduction.** Reduce in the beta number field and verify (2.6). Verify
   \[
   \operatorname{tr}[X_\beta,Y_\beta]>2
   \]
   by an isolating interval.

6. **Lift check.** Use the continuous flattening/logarithm branches to verify the central relations in the universal cover and the maximal Euler class.

Steps 3--5 prove the projective Fuchsian character. Step 6 upgrades it to the particular maximal lifted character needed by Brooks--Goldman.

This route is preferable to a global uniqueness proof because the scalar beta polynomial already has two positive roots.

# 7. Exact audit code for the model triangle representation

zeta = QQbar.zeta(34)
c = (zeta + zeta^-1) / 2       # cos(pi/17)
d = (4*c^2 - 3).sqrt()

X = matrix(QQbar, [[0, -1], [1, 0]])

assert X^2 == H
assert Y^3 == identity_matrix(QQbar, 2)

assert X.trace() == 0
assert Y.trace() == -1
assert Z.trace() == -2*c

comm = X*Y*X^-1*Y^-1

# 8. Consequence for the endpoint invariant

After the projective and central-lift certificates are verified, the beta representation is the maximal uniformizing representation of

\[
S^2(2,3,17),
\]

and hence, for

\[
M(0;-2;(2,1),(3,2),(17,14)),
\]

\[
e=-\frac1{102},
\qquad
\chi_{\rm orb}=-\frac{11}{102}.
\]

The signed Brooks--Goldman value is then

\[
\boxed{
GV(\rho_\beta)
=\frac{4\pi^2\chi_{\rm orb}^2}{e}
=-\frac{242\pi^2}{51}.}
\tag{8.1}
\]

Until the trace words and the universal-cover lift are supplied, (8.1) remains conditional on the representation-level identification, not on the topology of the filling.
