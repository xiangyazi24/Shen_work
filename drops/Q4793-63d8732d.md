# Problem 2.7: what level 11 proves, where \(\zeta(3)\) enters, and the exact missing certificate

## Executive verdict

Several objects have been conflated in the question. Separating them changes the answer materially.

1. The five numbers
   \[
   (a,b,c,d,e)=(10,4,-56,-8,22)
   \]
   are the parameters of **Cooper’s cubic four-term recurrence**. They are not, by themselves, entries or parameters of the printed \(3\times3\) challenge CMF.

2. Cooper’s actual level-11 recurrence is
   \[
   \boxed{
   \begin{aligned}
   (n+1)^3T_{n+1}={}&2(2n+1)(5n^2+5n+2)T_n\\
   &-8n(7n^2+1)T_{n-1}\\
   &+22n(2n-1)(n-1)T_{n-2},
   \end{aligned}}
   \tag{0.1}
   \]
   with \(T_0=1\), and
   \[
   T_n=1,4,28,268,3004,36784,476476,\ldots.
   \]

3. The recurrence quoted in the question,
   \[
   n^3u_n=(2n-1)(11n^2-11n+3)u_{n-1}+(n-1)^3u_{n-2},
   \tag{0.2}
   \]
   is **not** Cooper’s level-11 recurrence. After shifting the index it is
   \[
   (n+1)^3u_{n+1}=(2n+1)(11n^2+11n+3)u_n+n^3u_{n-1}.
   \tag{0.3}
   \]
   With \(u_0=1,u_1=3\), it gives \(u_2=113/4\), so it is not the integer sequence \(1,4,28,268,\ldots\). The coefficient \(11n^2+11n+3\) is associated with the classical **level-5** Zagier/Apéry \(\zeta(2)\) family, but (0.3) is not even the standard quadratic level-5 recurrence.

4. In the spectral identity, the notation should be kept separate:
   \[
   P_{27}(\mu)=4\mu^3-220\mu^2+8\mu-1
   \]
   is the scaled Problem 2.7 Poincaré polynomial, while
   \[
   C_{11}(t)=t^3-20t^2+56t-44
   \]
   is Cooper’s level-11 branch/spectral cubic. The exact identity is
   \[
   \boxed{
   16P_{27}\!\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{4}\right)
   =-C_{11}(t)C_{11}(4-t).}
   \tag{0.4}
   \]
   The variable \(t=1/X\) is a coordinate on the genus-zero Fricke quotient \(X_0(11)^+\); the polynomial \(C_{11}\) is not itself a Hauptmodul. Also, \(X_0(11)\) has genus one, so it has no Hauptmodul in the ordinary genus-zero sense.

5. For the official Problem 2.7 normalization, the verified target is
   \[
   \boxed{\zeta(2)+\zeta(3),}
   \]
   not a number in \(\mathbf Q+\mathbf Q\zeta(3)\) alone. If a different CMF normalization is intended, its initial vectors and scalar recurrence must be supplied before its connection constant can be identified.

6. There is an exact transform realizing the nonlinear spectral map, but it is **not a same-index finite Ore operator**. It is the shifted-binomial/even-section transform
   \[
   \boxed{
   (\mathcal KT)_n=
   \frac1{256^n}\sum_{k=0}^{2n}
   \binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k}T_k.}
   \tag{0.5}
   \]
   It maps a pure mode \(t^k\) to
   \[
   \left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}\right)^n.
   \]
   This is the exact sequence-level realization of (0.4).

7. No explicit finite certificate identifying the transformed Cooper operator with the printed degree-18 Problem 2.7 operator is currently available in Cooper’s paper or in the challenge repository. The missing statement is a creative-telescoping identity followed, possibly, by a local Ore intertwiner.

8. The cleanest source of \(\zeta(3)\) is a **logarithmic extension**. It can be written either as a parameter derivative of a double Euler integral or as a critical value of a weight-4 Eisenstein series at level 11. These are two realizations of the same type of extension; spectral data alone do not produce the period.

# 2. Why spectral data do not identify a period

Suppose an order-three recurrence has a basis of asymptotic solutions
\[
u_n^{(0)},\quad u_n^{(+)},\quad u_n^{(-)},
\]
with \(u^{(0)}\) dominant. Two solutions have connection expansions
\[
p_n=a_0u_n^{(0)}+a_+u_n^{(+)}+a_-u_n^{(-)},
\]
\[
q_n=b_0u_n^{(0)}+b_+u_n^{(+)}+b_-u_n^{(-)}.
\]
If \(b_0\neq0\), then
\[
\frac{p_n}{q_n}\longrightarrow\frac{a_0}{b_0}.
\tag{2.1}
\]
The number \(a_0/b_0\) is a **connection coefficient**. It is not one of the Poincaré modes.

Thus the phrase “the CMF approaches \(1\) and \(\zeta(3)\) along different modes” is not literally correct. The algebraic modes govern growth rates. Constants such as \(\zeta(3)\) occur in the matrix that connects a local basis at one singular point to a basis at another singular point. Equivalently, if
\[
L=\frac{a_0}{b_0},
\]
then
\[
p_n-Lq_n
\]
has its dominant mode cancelled and lies in the recessive subspace.

Consequently, the Poincaré polynomial can prove a convergence **rate** once the correct connection coefficient is known, but it cannot determine whether that coefficient is \(\zeta(3)\), \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\), an elliptic period, or an unrelated real number.

# 3. The exact transform and the true Ore-certificate problem

Define
\[
K(n,k)=256^{-n}\binom{2n}{k}(-2)^{2n-k},
\qquad 0\le k\le2n.
\tag{3.1}
\]
Then
\[
(\mathcal KT)_n=\sum_{k=0}^{2n}K(n,k)T_k.
\]
The kernel is hypergeometric in both variables:
\[
\boxed{
\frac{K(n,k+1)}{K(n,k)}
=-\frac{2n-k}{2(k+1)},}
\tag{3.2}
\]
\[
\boxed{
\frac{K(n+1,k)}{K(n,k)}
=\frac{(2n+2)(2n+1)}
{64(2n+2-k)(2n+1-k)}.}
\tag{3.3}
\]

For \(T_k=t^k\), the binomial theorem gives
\[
(\mathcal KT)_n
=\left(\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}\right)^n.
\tag{3.4}
\]

## 3.1 Why no direct local Ore intertwiner can realize this map

A finite same-index Ore operator
\[
R(n,S_n)=r_0(n)+r_1(n)S_n+\cdots+r_m(n)S_n^m
\]
acts on a pure exponential as
\[
R(n,S_n)t^n
=\left(\sum_{j=0}^mr_j(n)t^j\right)t^n.
\tag{3.5}
\]
It changes the amplitude by a rational-polynomial factor in \(t\), but it does **not** change the exponential base from \(t\) to
\[
\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}.
\]
Therefore there cannot be a direct finite local Ore equivalence between the raw Cooper recurrence and the challenge recurrence that implements (0.4). The nonlocal binomial transform and even section are essential.

## 3.2 Generating-function form

Let
\[
B(r)=\sum_{m\ge0}
\left(\sum_{k=0}^m\binom{m}{k}(-2)^{m-k}T_k\right)r^m.
\]
Then
\[
\boxed{
B(r)=\frac1{1+2r}
Z\!\left(\frac{r}{1+2r}\right).}
\tag{3.6}
\]
The scaled even section is
\[
\boxed{
\mathscr S(z)=\frac12\left[
B\!\left(\frac{\sqrt z}{16}\right)
+B\!\left(-\frac{\sqrt z}{16}\right)
\right].}
\tag{3.7}
\]
The resulting algebraic coordinate relation is
\[
\boxed{
z=\frac{256X^2}{(1-2X)^2}.}
\tag{3.8}
\]

## 3.3 The missing mixed-Ore certificate

Let \(L_{27}^{(n)}\) denote the primitive degree-18 Problem 2.7 operator. The exact certificate to seek is
\[
\boxed{
L_{27}^{(n)}\mathcal K
=\mathcal U L_{11}^{(k)}+(S_k-1)\mathcal V,}
\tag{3.9}
\]
where \(\mathcal U\) and \(\mathcal V\) have rational coefficients in \(n,k\). Summing (3.9) over the moving range \(0\le k\le2n\), checking all boundary terms, and using \(L_{11}T=0\) would prove
\[
L_{27}(\mathcal KT)=0.
\]

A practical two-stage computation is:

1. Creative-telescope \(K(n,k)T_k\) using \(L_{11}^{(k)}\) to obtain the minimal transformed operator \(L_{\mathrm{tr}}^{(n)}\).
2. Solve a local Ore-Hom problem
   \[
   \boxed{L_{27}R=Q L_{\mathrm{tr}}}
   \tag{3.10}
   \]
   with an initial ansatz \(\operatorname{ord}R\le2\).
3. Check the resulting map on three independent initial values.

No explicit \(\mathcal U,\mathcal V\), \(L_{\mathrm{tr}}\), or \(R,Q\) is printed in Cooper’s paper or in the challenge repository. Hence the exact finite intertwiner asked for in part (a) is presently a **missing computation**, not an established formula.

For reference, the direct transformed Cooper seed begins

1,\quad\frac1{16},\quad\frac{355}{16384},\quad
\frac{44611}{4194304},\ldots,
\]
whereas the official challenge numerator and denominator have very large rational initial data. This proves that even after the binomial transform, a nontrivial change of basis is required.

# 4. How \(\zeta(3)\) enters: the logarithmic Euler extension

The cleanest analytic mechanism is a parameter derivative. Define
\[
J(\varepsilon)
=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{(xy)^\varepsilon}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
\tag{4.1}
\]
Expanding \((1-xy)^{-1}\) gives
\[
J(\varepsilon)
=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\frac1{(m+1+\varepsilon)^2}.
\tag{4.2}
\]
Therefore
\[
\boxed{J(0)=\zeta(2),}
\tag{4.3}
\]
\[
\boxed{-\frac12J'(0)=\zeta(3).}
\tag{4.4}
\]
Equivalently,
\[
\boxed{
\zeta(3)
=-\frac12\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.}
\tag{4.5}
\]
Thus
\[
\boxed{
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
=\left(1-\frac12\frac{d}{d\varepsilon}\right)_{\varepsilon=0}
J(\varepsilon).}
\tag{4.6}
\]

This is the correct sense in which a parameter derivative creates the weight-three component. It is not differentiation with respect to the discrete level \(11\). It is differentiation in an exponent, spectral, or Eisenstein-family parameter, producing a logarithmic extension.

## 4.1 Exact polynomial moments

Let
\[
Q(t)=\sum_{k=0}^Nc_kt^k.
\]
Then termwise integration gives
\[
\boxed{
-\frac12\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
Q(xy)\frac{\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy
=\zeta(3)Q(1)-\sum_{k=0}^Nc_kH_k^{(3)},}
\tag{4.7}
\]
where
\[
H_k^{(3)}=\sum_{j=1}^k\frac1{j^3}.
\]
Likewise,
\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
Q(xy)\frac{1-\tfrac12\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy\\
&\qquad=(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3))Q(1)
-\sum_{k=0}^Nc_k\bigl(H_k^{(2)}+H_k^{(3)}\bigr).
\end{aligned}}
\tag{4.8}
\]

Formula (4.7) is an exact Beukers-type bridge for a target in \(\mathbf Q+\mathbf Q\zeta(3)\). Formula (4.8) is the corresponding bridge for the official Problem 2.7 target.

If polynomials \(Q_n\) can be constructed so that
\[
q_n=Q_n(1),
\]
\[
p_n=\sum_kc_{n,k}H_k^{(3)},
\]
then
\[
p_n-\zeta(3)q_n
=\frac12\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
Q_n(xy)\frac{\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy.
\]
The analogous statement using (4.8) gives linear forms in \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\).

## 4.2 A direct \(\mathbf Q+\mathbf Q\zeta(3)\) identity

Hadjicostas’s formula states
\[
\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{1-x}{1-xy}(-\log(xy))^s\,dx\,dy
=\Gamma(s+2)
\left(\zeta(s+2)-\frac1{s+1}\right).
\tag{4.9}
\]
At \(s=1\),
\[
\boxed{
\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{1-x}{1-xy}(-\log(xy))\,dx\,dy
=2\zeta(3)-1.}
\tag{4.10}
\]
This is the simplest exact integral producing a rational linear combination of \(1\) and \(\zeta(3)\).

# 5. The level-11 Eisenstein interpretation

There is also a clean modular source for both zeta values. Let
\[
\mathcal G_{11}(\tau)=E_4(\tau)-E_4(11\tau),
\tag{5.1}
\]
where
\[
E_4(\tau)=1+240\sum_{n\ge1}\sigma_3(n)q^n.
\]
The Dirichlet series of its nonconstant Fourier coefficients is
\[
\boxed{
L(\mathcal G_{11},s)
=240(1-11^{-s})\zeta(s)\zeta(s-3).}
\tag{5.2}
\]
Analytic continuation gives
\[
\boxed{
L(\mathcal G_{11},2)
=-\frac{2400}{121}\zeta(2),}
\tag{5.3}
\]
\[
\boxed{
L(\mathcal G_{11},3)
=-\frac{159600}{1331}\zeta(3).}
\tag{5.4}
\]
Hence the critical periods of one weight-4 level-11 Eisenstein class contain precisely the two constants needed for Problem 2.7.

The associated Eichler integral may be written schematically as
\[
\mathscr E_{11}(\tau)
=\sum_{n\ge1}\frac{b_n}{n^3}q^n,
\qquad
(q\tfrac d{dq})^3\mathscr E_{11}
=\mathcal G_{11},
\tag{5.5}
\]
up to the usual \((2\pi i)^3\) normalization. Its period polynomial has coefficients involving the critical values in (5.3)–(5.4).

This explains the modular origin of the mixed period. However, it does not automatically identify the challenge recurrence. One still must embed this Eisenstein extension into the Cooper level-11 function field and then transport it through the quadratic trace.

A concrete finite task is to express
\[
\frac{\mathcal G_{11}}{Z^2}
\]
as an element of the level-11 function field
\[
\mathbf Q(X,B),
\qquad
B^2=1-20X+56X^2-44X^3.
\tag{5.6}
\]
Thus one seeks rational functions \(R_0,R_1\) such that
\[
\boxed{
\mathcal G_{11}=Z^2\bigl(R_0(X)+B R_1(X)\bigr).}
\tag{5.7}
\]
Divisor bounds and sufficiently many \(q\)-coefficients make (5.7) a finite linear-algebra computation. Integrating the resulting expression gives the required Eichler extension in the same modular function field as Cooper’s recurrence.

## What does not produce \(\zeta(3)\) directly

The unique weight-2 newform
\[
g_{11}(\tau)=\eta(\tau)^2\eta(11\tau)^2
\]
has elliptic periods governed by \(L(g_{11},1)\), not \(\zeta(3)\). Likewise, the identity
\[
L(g_{11}\otimes g_{11},s)
=\zeta(s-1)L(\operatorname{Sym}^2g_{11},s)
\]
contains a \(\zeta(2)\) factor at \(s=3\), not \(\zeta(3)\). Thus the weight-4 Eisenstein/Eichler extension is the natural modular source of \(\zeta(3)\), rather than the symmetric square of the weight-2 newform.

# 6. Is there a known Cooper-level-11 Beukers integral?

The standard Beukers integral for Apéry’s \(\zeta(3)\) recurrence uses shifted Legendre polynomials and is attached to the classical Apéry level-6 system. It is not a level-11 formula.

Cooper’s level-11 paper gives the modular parameterization and the homogeneous recurrence (0.1), but it does not give:

- a companion numerator sequence;
- an Apéry limit equal to \(\zeta(3)\);
- a double or triple integral whose creative telescoper is (0.1);
- or a proof that a quotient of two level-11 solutions equals a zeta value.

I did not find a published Beukers-type integral specifically for the Cooper sequence \(1,4,28,268,\ldots\) that produces \(\zeta(3)\). Therefore the honest answer to part (c) is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{A generic Beukers kernel is known, but a Cooper-level-11 realization is not currently certified.}}
\]

The most promising integral search is a parameterized double Euler family
\[
\mathcal J_n(\varepsilon)
=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\Phi_n(x,y)(xy)^\varepsilon\frac{dx\,dy}{1-xy},
\tag{6.1}
\]
with \(\Phi_n\) algebraic-rational and chosen so that creative telescoping yields the transformed level-11 operator. Then
\[
\left(1-\frac12\partial_\varepsilon\right)_{\varepsilon=0}
\mathcal J_n(\varepsilon)
\tag{6.2}
\]
produces the mixed \(\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)\) extension, while
\[
-\frac12\partial_\varepsilon\mathcal J_n(\varepsilon)|_{\varepsilon=0}
\]
produces the pure \(\zeta(3)\) extension.

# 7. A complete proof architecture

A rigorous level-11 proof of the challenge would consist of the following finite certificates.

## Certificate A: homogeneous transform

Compute \(L_{\mathrm{tr}}\) from the kernel (3.1) and prove the mixed Ore identity (3.9), including moving-boundary terms.

## Certificate B: local equivalence to the printed recurrence

Find \(R,Q\in\mathbf Q(n)\langle S_n\rangle\), initially with \(\operatorname{ord}R\le2\), such that
\[
L_{27}R=Q L_{\mathrm{tr}}.
\]
Verify the map on a three-dimensional basis of initial values.

## Certificate C: logarithmic/Eisenstein extension

Construct either:

- a parameterized Euler integral (6.1), or
- the level-11 Eichler integral coming from \(\mathcal G_{11}\),

and prove that its transformed coefficients satisfy the same extended recurrence/module as the CMF numerator solution.

## Certificate D: connection coefficient

Evaluate the endpoint period using either
\[
-\frac12J'(0)=\zeta(3),
\]
Hadjicostas’s formula, or the Eisenstein values (5.3)–(5.4). Check the required initial values exactly.

## Certificate E: decay

Show that
\[
E_n=p_n-Lq_n
\]
lies in the recessive subspace and that \(q_n\) has a nonzero dominant coefficient. Then
\[
\frac{p_n}{q_n}\to L.
\]

Only after Certificates A–E are supplied is the period identification proved. The spectral norm identity (0.4) is the leading-at-infinity shadow of Certificate A; it is not a substitute for the certificate.

# 8. Direct answers

## (a) Exact intertwiner

There is no direct same-index finite Ore intertwiner implementing the nonlinear map \(t\mapsto(t-2)^2/256\). The exact known map is the nonlocal binomial/even-section kernel (0.5). After creative telescoping it may be followed by a local order-at-most-two Ore intertwiner between the transformed operator and the challenge operator, but that operator has not yet been computed or published.

## (b) How \(\zeta(3)\) enters

The most concrete mechanism is the parameter derivative
\[
-\frac12\partial_\varepsilon
\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
\frac{(xy)^\varepsilon}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy
\bigg|_{\varepsilon=0}
=\zeta(3).
\]
On the modular side, the same weight-three period is supplied by the critical value \(L(\mathcal G_{11},3)\) of the weight-4 Eisenstein series \(E_4-E_4(11\tau)\). It is a logarithmic/Eichler extension, not an additional Poincaré mode and not a derivative with respect to the discrete level.

## (c) Beukers-type integral

A universal Beukers/Hadjicostas kernel producing \(\zeta(3)\) is known, and (4.7) gives its exact polynomial moments. No Cooper-specific level-11 polynomial family whose telescoper is (0.1) and whose companion limit is \(\zeta(3)\) is currently certified. Constructing that family—or equivalently the weight-4 Eisenstein extension in \(\mathbf Q(X,B)\)—is the remaining period calculation.
