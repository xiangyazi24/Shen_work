# Problem 2.7: audit of the nested-binomial series

Define, literally as in the question,

\[
d_n:=\sum_{j=0}^{n}\binom nj^2\binom{n+j}{j}
\sum_{m=0}^{n-j}\binom{n-j}{m}\binom{j+m}{m},
\tag{0.1}
\]

and

\[
S_N:=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\frac{(-1)^k}{(k+1)^2}d_k.
\tag{0.2}
\]

## Executive verdict

The proposed theorem is **false as written**, for two independent and exact reasons.

1. The displayed binomial formula does **not** produce the stated sequence
   \(1,5,43,491,6693,\ldots\). It produces

   \[
   \boxed{1,4,46,676,11506,212584,\ldots.}
   \tag{0.3}
   \]

   Already

   \[
   d_1=
   \underbrace{\left(\binom10+\binom11\right)}_{j=0}
   +\underbrace{\binom11^2\binom21}_{j=1}
   =2+2=4,
   \]

   not \(5\).

2. The terms of (0.2) do not even tend to zero. The single summand \(j=n,m=0\) in (0.1) is

   \[
   \binom nn^2\binom{2n}{n}\binom00\binom n0
   =\binom{2n}{n}.
   \]

   Since every summand in (0.1) is nonnegative,

   \[
   \boxed{d_n\ge \binom{2n}{n}.}
   \tag{0.4}
   \]

   Consequently,

   \[
   \left|\frac{(-1)^nd_n}{(n+1)^2}\right|
   \ge \frac{\binom{2n}{n}}{(n+1)^2}
   \sim \frac{4^n}{\sqrt\pi\,n^{5/2}}
   \longrightarrow\infty.
   \tag{0.5}
   \]

   Thus the necessary term test for convergence fails dramatically:

   \[
   S_{N+1}-S_N=\frac{(-1)^Nd_N}{(N+1)^2}\not\longrightarrow0.
   \]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{S_N\text{ does not converge, and in particular cannot converge to }
\zeta(2)+\zeta(3).}
\tag{0.6}
\]

This is not a subtle issue of creative telescoping, modularity, or a missing connection constant. It is an elementary incompatibility in the statement. The recurrence, factorization, and level-11 questions must consequently be separated into two different problems:

- the sequence defined by the displayed double sum, beginning \(1,4,46,\ldots\);
- the unspecified sequence beginning \(1,5,43,491,6693,\ldots\).

Five initial values do not determine a unique P-recursive operator, so no exact minimal recurrence for the second sequence can be supplied until its defining summand is corrected.

# 1. Exact initial-value audit

For convenience put

\[
I(a,j):=\sum_{m=0}^{a}\binom am\binom{j+m}{m}.
\tag{1.1}
\]

The first values from the literal definition are as follows.

| \(n\) | contributions indexed by \(j\) | \(d_n\) |
|---:|---|---:|
| 0 | \(1\) | \(1\) |
| 1 | \(2+2\) | \(4\) |
| 2 | \(4+36+6\) | \(46\) |
| 3 | \(8+288+360+20\) | \(676\) |
| 4 | \(16+1600+7020+2800+70\) | \(11506\) |
| 5 | \(32+7200+79800+106400+18900+252\) | \(212584\) |

The corresponding first partial sums are already incompatible with convergence:

\[
\begin{aligned}
S_1&=1,\\
S_2&=1-\frac44=0,\\
S_3&=\frac{46}{9},\\
S_4&=\frac{46}{9}-\frac{676}{16}
=-\frac{1337}{36},\\
S_5&=-\frac{1337}{36}+\frac{11506}{25}
=\frac{380791}{900}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{1.2}
\]

The growth obstruction (0.4), rather than these sample values, is the rigorous proof of divergence.

# 2. The weight \((-1)^k/(k+1)^2\) does not create \(\zeta(3)\)

A second premise in the question needs correction. The factor

\[
\frac{(-1)^k}{(k+1)^2}
\]

by itself is a weight-two factor. For example,

\[
\sum_{k\ge0}\frac{(-1)^k}{(k+1)^2}
=\eta(2)=\frac12\zeta(2)=\frac{\pi^2}{12}.
\tag{2.1}
\]

It contains no automatic \(\zeta(3)\) contribution. A weight-three term normally enters through one of the following mechanisms:

- a factor \((k+1)^{-3}\);
- a harmonic number such as \(H_k/(k+1)^2\);
- a logarithmic parameter derivative;
- an explicit mixed period such as
  \[
  \int_0^1\!\int_0^1
  \frac{1-\tfrac12\log(xy)}{1-xy}\,dx\,dy
  =\zeta(2)+\zeta(3).
  \]

“Implicit factorial structure” in a positive integer sequence does not change the transcendental weight of the outer denominator.

In an Apéry construction, the exponentially growing integer sequence is usually the **denominator sequence**. One sums or integrates a recessive companion linear form; one does not ordinarily sum the denominator itself with only a polynomially decaying weight.

# 3. Exact simplification of the inner sum

Although the proposed limit is false, the literal sequence has a useful exact hypergeometric structure.

Using Pfaff's transformation,

\[
\begin{aligned}
I(a,j)
&={}_2F_1(-a,j+1;1;-1)\\
&=2^a{}_2F_1\!\left(-a,-j;1;\frac12\right)\\
&=\boxed{\sum_{m=0}^{\min(a,j)}
2^{a-m}\binom am\binom jm.}
\end{aligned}
\tag{3.1}
\]

Equivalently,

\[
\boxed{
I(a,j)=[y^a](1+y)^j(1+2y)^a.}
\tag{3.2}
\]

The complete summand is proper hypergeometric:

\[
\boxed{
F(n,j,m)=
\frac{n!(n+j)!(j+m)!}
{j!^4(n-j)!\,m!^2(n-j-m)!}.}
\tag{3.3}
\]

Its elementary shift quotients are

\[
\frac{F(n+1,j,m)}{F(n,j,m)}
=\frac{(n+1)(n+j+1)}{(n+1-j)(n+1-j-m)},
\tag{3.4}
\]

\[
\frac{F(n,j+1,m)}{F(n,j,m)}
=\frac{(n+j+1)(j+m+1)(n-j)(n-j-m)}{(j+1)^4},
\tag{3.5}
\]

and

\[
\frac{F(n,j,m+1)}{F(n,j,m)}
=\frac{(j+m+1)(n-j-m)}{(m+1)^2}.
\tag{3.6}
\]

Thus multivariate Zeilberger/Chyzak creative telescoping is applicable to the literal sequence. It will produce some polynomial-coefficient recurrence, but that recurrence cannot rescue the divergent outer series.

# 4. An exact Laurent-polynomial period for the literal sequence

Define the weighted Apéry polynomial

\[
A_n(q):=\sum_{j=0}^n
\binom nj^2\binom{n+j}{j}q^j.
\tag{4.1}
\]

A direct constant-term extraction gives

\[
\boxed{
A_n(q)=\operatorname{CT}_{u,v}
\left[(1+u)(1+v)
\left(1+\frac{q(1+v)}{uv}\right)\right]^n.}
\tag{4.2}
\]

Indeed, choosing the \(j\)-th term from the final factor, then taking the \(u\)- and \(v\)-constant terms, gives respectively

\[
\binom nj,\qquad \binom nj,\qquad \binom{n+j}{j}.
\]

By (3.2),

\[
d_n=\operatorname{CT}_{y}
\left(\frac{1+2y}{y}\right)^n
A_n\!\left(\frac{y(1+y)}{1+2y}\right).
\tag{4.3}
\]

Substituting (4.2) into (4.3) yields the explicit three-variable Laurent polynomial

\[
\boxed{
\Phi(u,v,y)=
(1+u)(1+v)
\left[
\frac{1+2y}{y}
+\frac{(1+y)(1+v)}{uv}
\right],}
\tag{4.4}
\]

for which

\[
\boxed{d_n=\operatorname{CT}_{u,v,y}\Phi(u,v,y)^n.}
\tag{4.5}
\]

Equivalently, on suitable small product circles,

\[
\boxed{
d_n=\frac1{(2\pi i)^3}
\oint\!\oint\!\oint
\Phi(u,v,y)^n
\frac{du}{u}\frac{dv}{v}\frac{dy}{y}.}
\tag{4.6}
\]

This answers the integral-kernel question for the **literal** sequence. The measure is the Haar constant-term measure on a three-torus, not a positive two-dimensional measure on \([0,1]^2\).

Its ordinary generating function is the diagonal/constant term

\[
\boxed{
D(z):=\sum_{n\ge0}d_nz^n
=\operatorname{CT}_{u,v,y}\frac1{1-z\Phi(u,v,y)}.}
\tag{4.7}
\]

Consequently it is D-finite, and \((d_n)\) is P-recursive. This is a standard diagonal theorem; see Lipshitz, *D-finite power series*, J. Algebra 122 (1989).

For \(|z|\) inside the disk of convergence, the denominator-square transform has the polylogarithmic period

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{n\ge0}\frac{d_nz^n}{(n+1)^2}
=\operatorname{CT}_{u,v,y}
\frac{\operatorname{Li}_2(z\Phi(u,v,y))}{z\Phi(u,v,y)}.}
\tag{4.8}
\]

The lower bound (0.4) implies

\[
\operatorname{rad}D\le\frac14.
\tag{4.9}
\]

Hence \(z=-1\), which is required by (0.2), is far outside the ordinary convergence disk. Formula (4.8) could be analytically continued along a specified path, but that would define a **regularized/continued value**, not the limit of the partial sums \(S_N\).

The exact finite-sum integral

\[
S_N=\int_0^1(-\log x)
\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}d_k(-x)^k\,dx
\tag{4.10}
\]

is valid, because

\[
\frac1{(k+1)^2}=\int_0^1x^k(-\log x)\,dx.
\]

It does not permit passage to \(N=\infty\), since the polynomial partial sums do not converge pointwise on most of \([0,1]\).

# 5. Recurrence and Ore-factorization status

There is no single recurrence that simultaneously satisfies all data in the question:

- the literal sum gives \(d_1=4,d_2=46\);
- the stated sequence gives \(d_1=5,d_2=43\).

Accordingly, reporting exact polynomial coefficients for “the” minimal operator would be fabrication.

For the literal sequence, (3.3) and (4.5) prove that a P-recursive operator exists. The following complete Sage script:

1. computes the literal terms exactly;
2. confirms the initial-value mismatch;
3. searches for a recurrence
   \[
   \sum_{r=0}^{R}p_r(n)d_{n+r}=0;
   \]
4. keeps a holdout set of terms;
5. converts a surviving candidate to `ore_algebra`;
6. attempts factorization.

A guessed operator becomes a proof only after a creative-telescoping certificate or another exact derivation is supplied.

```python
from sage.all import *

# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
# 1. Exact terms from the formula in the question
# ----------------------------------------------------------------------

def d_literal(n):
    n = ZZ(n)
    total = ZZ(0)
    for j in range(n + 1):
        inner = sum(
            binomial(n - j, m) * binomial(j + m, m)
            for m in range(n - j + 1)
        )
        total += binomial(n, j)^2 * binomial(n + j, j) * inner
    return ZZ(total)


def terms_literal(N):
    return [d_literal(n) for n in range(N)]


small = terms_literal(6)
print("literal terms:", small)
assert small == [1, 4, 46, 676, 11506, 212584]
assert small != [1, 5, 43, 491, 6693, 0]

for n, a in enumerate(small):
    assert a >= binomial(2*n, n)

# Use enough terms for discovery plus an independent holdout range.
# N=180 is usually still inexpensive for this finite double sum.
seq = terms_literal(180)

# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
# 2. Exact recurrence guessing with holdout constraints
# ----------------------------------------------------------------------

R.<n> = PolynomialRing(QQ)


def design_matrix(seq, order, degree, start, stop):
    """Rows encode sum_j p_j(n) seq[n+j] = 0."""
    return matrix(QQ, [
        [
            QQ(seq[k + j]) * QQ(k)^e
            for j in range(order + 1)
            for e in range(degree + 1)
        ]
        for k in range(start, stop)
    ])


def vec_to_polys(v, order, degree):
    return [
        R(sum(v[j*(degree + 1) + e] * n^e
              for e in range(degree + 1)))
        for j in range(order + 1)
    ]


def holds_everywhere(seq, polys):
    order = len(polys) - 1
    for k in range(len(seq) - order):
        value = sum(polys[j](k) * seq[k + j]
                    for j in range(order + 1))
        if value != 0:
            return False
    return True


def primitive_normalize(polys):
    coeffs = [c for p in polys for c in p.list()]
    den = lcm([QQ(c).denominator() for c in coeffs] or [1])
    Zpolys = [ZZ['n'](den * p) for p in polys]
    nonzero_coeffs = [abs(ZZ(c)) for p in Zpolys for c in p.list() if c]
    g = gcd(nonzero_coeffs) if nonzero_coeffs else ZZ(1)
    Zpolys = [p // g for p in Zpolys]

    # Make the highest-shift nonzero coefficient have positive leading term.
    for p in reversed(Zpolys):
        if p != 0:
            if p.leading_coefficient() < 0:
                Zpolys = [-q for q in Zpolys]
            break
    return Zpolys


def guess_p_recursive(seq, max_order=12, max_degree=30,
                      extra_train_rows=12, min_holdout=25):
    N = len(seq)

    for order in range(1, max_order + 1):
        for degree in range(0, max_degree + 1):
            cols = (order + 1) * (degree + 1)
            available = N - order
            train_rows = cols + extra_train_rows
            if available < train_rows + min_holdout:
                continue

            Mtrain = design_matrix(seq, order, degree, 0, train_rows)
            K = Mtrain.right_kernel()
            if K.dimension() == 0:
                continue

            # Impose all remaining rows on linear combinations of the
            # training-kernel basis.
            B = matrix(QQ, K.basis()).transpose()  # cols x nullity
            Mhold = design_matrix(seq, order, degree,
                                  train_rows, available)
            C = Mhold * B
            KH = C.right_kernel()

            for alpha in KH.basis():
                v = B * alpha
                if v == 0:
                    continue
                polys = vec_to_polys(v, order, degree)
                if holds_everywhere(seq, polys):
                    return primitive_normalize(polys)

    return None


polys = guess_p_recursive(seq)
if polys is None:
    print("No recurrence found within the current order/degree bounds.")
else:
    print("Verified recurrence:")
    for j, p in enumerate(polys):
        print(f"p_{j}(n) =", p)

    # ------------------------------------------------------------------
    # 3. Ore operator and factorization
    # ------------------------------------------------------------------
    try:
        from ore_algebra import OreAlgebra
        A.<Sn> = OreAlgebra(R)
        L = A.zero()
        for j, p in enumerate(polys):
            L += A(R(p)) * Sn^j

        print("Ore operator L =", L)
        print("order =", L.order())
        print("factorization =", L.factor())
    except Exception as exc:
        print("ore_algebra factorization unavailable:", exc)
```

The search order is increasing in shift order and then polynomial degree. “Minimal” here means minimal **within the searched ansatz and supported by the holdout data**. Exact minimality requires a certified annihilating ideal or a proof that no lower-order telescoper exists.

# 6. The exact creative-telescoping proof object

For a genuine proof of a recurrence for the literal sum, one should produce polynomials \(p_r(n)\) and rational certificates \(R_j(n,j,m),R_m(n,j,m)\) satisfying

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{r=0}^{R}p_r(n)F(n+r,j,m)
=(S_j-1)\bigl(R_jF\bigr)
 +(S_m-1)\bigl(R_mF\bigr).}
\tag{6.1}
\]

Summing over the triangular region

\[
0\le j\le n,\qquad0\le m\le n-j
\]

and checking the finitely many boundary contributions proves the recurrence.

The following exact Sage checker verifies any proposed certificate after division by \(F(n,j,m)\). Insert the candidate recurrence polynomials and rational certificates returned by a multivariate telescoping package.

```python
from sage.all import *

PR.<n,j,m> = PolynomialRing(QQ)
K = PR.fraction_field()

# Shift quotients of F(n,j,m).
rn = K((n+1)*(n+j+1) / ((n+1-j)*(n+1-j-m)))
rj = K((n+j+1)*(j+m+1)*(n-j)*(n-j-m) / (j+1)^4)
rm = K((j+m+1)*(n-j-m) / (m+1)^2)


def shift(expr, var, amount=1):
    return K(expr.subs({var: var + amount}))


def n_shift_ratio(r):
    out = K(1)
    for a in range(r):
        out *= shift(rn, n, a)
    return K(out)


def verify_certificate(p_list, Rj, Rm):
    """
    p_list[r] is p_r(n), coercible into K.
    Rj and Rm are rational functions in K.
    """
    lhs = K(0)
    for r, p in enumerate(p_list):
        lhs += K(p) * n_shift_ratio(r)

    rhs = (
        rj * shift(Rj, j, 1) - Rj
        + rm * shift(Rm, m, 1) - Rm
    )

    residual = K(lhs - rhs)
    num = residual.numerator()
    assert num == 0, num.factor()
    return True

# Example usage after a CAS has supplied the data:
# p_list = [ ... ]
# Rj = K( ... )
# Rm = K( ... )
# print(verify_certificate(p_list, Rj, Rm))
```

One must additionally verify that the boundary values of \(R_jF\) and \(R_mF\) vanish or combine to the claimed inhomogeneous term. The reciprocal-gamma convention is useful for this triangular support.

# 7. Comparison with Cooper's level-11 operator

Cooper's level-11 four-term sequence is

\[
T_0,T_1,T_2,T_3,\ldots=1,4,28,268,\ldots
\]

and satisfies

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
(n+1)^3T_{n+1}={}&2(2n+1)(5n^2+5n+2)T_n\\
&-8n(7n^2+1)T_{n-1}\\
&+22n(2n-1)(n-1)T_{n-2}.
\end{aligned}}
\tag{7.1}
\]

This is the self-starting level-11 recurrence in Shaun Cooper, [*Apéry-like sequences defined by four-term recurrence relations*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.00757).

Neither sequence in the question matches it:

- the literal double sum has \(d_0=1,d_1=4,d_2=46\), whereas (7.1) forces \(T_2=28\);
- the stated list has second term \(5\), whereas (7.1) forces \(T_1=4\) already at \(n=0\).

Thus

\[
\boxed{
\text{the displayed }d_n\text{ is not Cooper's level-11 sequence.}}
\tag{7.2}
\]

The prior Problem 2.7 analysis found a relation only at the spectral level: a centered-binomial/even-section operation maps Cooper's Poincaré roots by

\[
t\longmapsto\frac{(t-2)^2}{256}.
\]

That does not identify an arbitrary nested binomial sum with the printed Problem 2.7 recurrence at finite \(n\). In fact, the previously tested centered-binomial transform itself fails the printed recurrence at finite index.

Also, “Zagier's level-11 family” is not the right classification phrase. The original fifteen sporadic three-term Apéry-like sequences are discussed, with constant-term representations, in Ofir Gorodetsky, [*New representations for all sporadic Apéry-like sequences*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2102.11839). Cooper's level-11 object belongs to the broader four-term classification, not to Zagier's original six quadratic cases.

# 8. What a corrected theorem would need to say

At least one datum is missing or mistyped. Plausible kinds of correction include:

1. **A missing exponential normalization.** For example,
   \[
   \sum_{k\ge0}\frac{(-1)^kd_k}{\Lambda^k(k+1)^2}.
   \]
   The lower bound (0.4) shows that ordinary convergence requires at least \(|\Lambda|>4\) as a necessary condition; the true growth may require a larger \(\Lambda\).

2. **A reciprocal or recessive sequence** in place of the growing positive \(d_k\).

3. **A harmonic/logarithmic factor** that supplies the \(\zeta(3)\) component.

4. **A different nested summand** whose first terms really are
   \(1,5,43,491,6693,\ldots\).

5. **An Abel/Borel/analytic-continuation prescription** instead of ordinary convergence of partial sums.

Until the exact corrected summand or normalization is specified, the requested statement

\[
\lim_{N\to\infty}S_N=\zeta(2)+\zeta(3)
\]

has no valid proof, because its left-hand side does not exist in the ordinary sense.
