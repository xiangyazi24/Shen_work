# Problem 2.5: the \((1+t^2)\) factor makes the verified AZ period genuinely two-dimensional

Let

\[
\phi(u)=\frac{(1+u)(u+2)}u=3+u+\frac2u,
\qquad
\Phi(u,v)=\phi(u)\phi(v),
\]

and use the carrier

\[
h_N(u,v,t)=\frac{\Phi(u,v)^N}{uv(1+t^2)}.
\]

The verified numerator row is

\[
\mathbf P_N(u,v,t)
=\frac14uv(1+t^2)
\bigl((N+2)^2,\,2(N+2),\,1\bigr).
\tag{1}
\]

The factorization through \(1+t^2\) has a very definite meaning:

\[
\boxed{
\text{at }\varepsilon=0\text{, the }t\text{-direction is a contractible certificate direction, not a Catalan period direction.}
}
\tag{2}
\]

After the carrier is included, the factor \(1+t^2\) cancels exactly. The resulting period is the tensor square of the central-Delannoy period. It is not an Euler beta integral, and it contains no occurrence of Catalan's constant.

There is an important scope qualification. The resulting row is an exact auxiliary row solution of the normalized \(3\times3\) system, but its initial value is

\[
(1,1,1/4),
\]

not either printed CMF row

\[
(30921,-32972,8240),
\qquad
(33750,-36000,9000).
\]

Thus the reduction proves a homogeneous Delannoy-square period in the CMF module. A full Catalan proof still needs the missing extension/framing that identifies the actual numerator and denominator rows.

---

# (a) What the \(t\)-integral is doing

The expression that enters the period is not \((1+t^2)\) by itself, but the product of (1) with the carrier:

\[
\begin{aligned}
h_N\mathbf P_N
&=
\frac{\Phi(u,v)^N}{uv(1+t^2)}
\cdot
\frac14uv(1+t^2)
\bigl((N+2)^2,2(N+2),1\bigr)\\
&=
\frac14\Phi(u,v)^N
\bigl((N+2)^2,2(N+2),1\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\tag{3}
\]

It is independent of \(t\). Therefore, in the constant-term realization,

\[
\operatorname{CT}_t(1)=1.
\tag{4}
\]

If the same algebraic class is represented by an ordinary integral over \([0,1]\), the remaining integral is merely

\[
\int_0^1 1\,dt=1.
\tag{5}
\]

It is **not**

\[
B\!\left(\frac12,\frac12\right)=\pi.
\]

The beta value appears in integrals such as

\[
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{dt}{1+t^2}=\pi,
\]

or, on \([0,1]\),

\[
\int_0^1\frac{dt}{1+t^2}=\frac\pi4.
\]

Here the denominator \(1+t^2\) has already been cancelled by the numerator. The identity

\[
\operatorname{CT}_t((1+t^2)f)
=\operatorname{CT}_t(f)+\operatorname{CT}_t(t^2f)
\]

is harmless but obscures the main point: in the verified period,

\[
f(t)=\frac1{1+t^2},
\]

so \((1+t^2)f(t)=1\) exactly.

## The \(\varepsilon\)-parameter

If the carrier is enlarged to

\[
h_{N,\varepsilon}
=\frac{\Phi(u,v)^N t^\varepsilon}{uv(1+t^2)},
\]

then (1) gives a remaining factor \(t^\varepsilon\). At \(\varepsilon=0\), it still contributes only \(1\). Differentiating this already-cancelled class gives

\[
-\left.\frac{d}{d\varepsilon}\right|_{0}
\int_0^1 t^\varepsilon\,dt=1,
\]

not Catalan's constant. To recover

\[
G=-\int_0^1\frac{\log t}{1+t^2}\,dt,
\]

one needs an \(O(\varepsilon)\) numerator component that is **not** divisible by \(1+t^2\), or an independently retained Catalan functional. The zeroth-order factorized certificate has projected away that extension class.

# (b) What the \(t\)-connection and the \(C\)-block are doing

The covariant derivative is

\[
\mathcal D_t
=\partial_t-\frac{2t}{1+t^2}.
\tag{6}
\]

It is gauge-trivial:

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal D_t
=(1+t^2)\,\partial_t\,(1+t^2)^{-1}.
}
\tag{7}
\]

Equivalently, for every \(C(t)\),

\[
\frac1{1+t^2}\mathcal D_t C
=\partial_t\!\left(\frac{C}{1+t^2}\right).
\tag{8}
\]

Now write the verified block as

\[
\mathbf C_N(u,v,t)
=t(1+t^2)\,\widetilde{\mathbf C}_N(u,v),
\tag{9}
\]

where the reduced block is independent of \(t\). Then

\[
\frac{\mathbf C_N}{1+t^2}
=t\widetilde{\mathbf C}_N,
\]

and

\[
\mathcal D_t\mathbf C_N
=(1+t^2)\widetilde{\mathbf C}_N.
\tag{10}
\]

Thus \(\mathbf C_N\) is an explicit primitive in the gauge-trivial \(t\)-complex. Its role is to make the **pointwise** AZ identity exact before the period functional is applied.

In the constant-term version, include the carrier first:

\[
h_N\mathbf C_N
=rac{t\Phi(u,v)^N}{uv}\,\widetilde{\mathbf C}_N(u,v).
\tag{11}
\]

This lies in the odd \(t\)-sector. Hence

\[
\boxed{
\operatorname{CT}_t(h_N\mathbf C_N)=0.
}
\tag{12}
\]

This is also why the boundary operator described as

\[
(\varepsilon S_t-1)
\]

causes no surviving period at \(\varepsilon=0\): it leaves a term proportional to \(t\), whose constant term is zero. The decomposition

\[
\operatorname{CT}_t(tg)+\operatorname{CT}_t(t^3g)
\]

should not be applied before the carrier is included. The factor \((1+t^2)^{-1}\) cancels the entire \((1+t^2)\), leaving only the odd factor \(t\).

## Closed-cycle versus interval warning

There are two equivalent certificate languages, but their boundary conventions must not be mixed.

- In the differential/closed-contour language, (8) is a total derivative and integrates to zero on the chosen closed cycle.
- In the coefficient-extraction language, the discrete boundary operator and oddness in (11) make the constant term vanish.
- On a literal real interval \([0,1]\), the primitive \(t\widetilde{\mathbf C}_N\) has a generally nonzero endpoint at \(t=1\). In that realization the endpoint term must be retained explicitly.

So the \(t\)-connection is a homotopy variable for the homogeneous certificate. It is not secretly evaluating \(G\) or \(\pi\).

# (c) The exact effective two-dimensional constant-term period

Define

\[
\widehat{\mathbf R}_N(u,v)
=\frac14
\bigl((N+2)^2,\,2(N+2),\,1\bigr).
\tag{13}
\]

Then the complete three-variable period furnished by the verified numerator reduces to

\[
\boxed{
\widehat{\mathbf Q}^{\mathrm{AZ}}_N
=\operatorname{CT}_{u,v}
\left[\Phi(u,v)^N\widehat{\mathbf R}_N(u,v)\right].
}
\tag{14}
\]

Since \(\widehat{\mathbf R}_N\) is independent of \(u,v\), the variables separate. Put

\[
D_N:=\operatorname{CT}_u\phi(u)^N.
\tag{15}
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{
\widehat{\mathbf Q}^{\mathrm{AZ}}_N
=\frac{D_N^2}{4}
\bigl((N+2)^2,\,2(N+2),\,1\bigr).
}
\tag{16}
\]

Equivalently,

\[
\boxed{
\widehat{\mathbf Q}^{\mathrm{AZ}}_N
=\frac1{4(2\pi i)^2}
\oint\!\oint
\Phi(u,v)^N
\bigl((N+2)^2,2(N+2),1\bigr)
\frac{du}{u}\frac{dv}{v}.
}
\tag{17}
\]

The three scalar components are

\[
\frac{(N+2)^2D_N^2}{4},
\qquad
\frac{(N+2)D_N^2}{2},
\qquad
\frac{D_N^2}{4}.
\tag{18}
\]

At \(N=0\), this gives

\[
\widehat{\mathbf Q}^{\mathrm{AZ}}_0=(1,1,1/4).
\]

Therefore (14)--(18) describe the auxiliary normalized period row selected by the AZ numerator. They are not, without an additional basis transformation, the printed CMF numerator or denominator row.

# (d) Delannoy interpretation and the general shifted-moment formula

Expand

\[
\phi(u)^N=(1+u)^N\left(1+\frac2u\right)^N.
\]

Taking the constant term gives

\[
\boxed{
D_N
=\sum_{k=0}^N\binom Nk^2 2^k
=P_N^{\mathrm{Leg}}(3),
}
\tag{19}
\]

the central Delannoy number. Hence

\[
\boxed{
D_N^2
=\sum_{j=0}^N\sum_{k=0}^N
\binom Nj^2\binom Nk^2 2^{j+k}.
}
\tag{20}
\]

So the reduced period is not merely “Delannoy-like”: it is exactly the tensor square of the Delannoy period, multiplied by the elementary vector in (16). There is no coupling between the two lattice directions in this particular row.

For a more general reduced Laurent-polynomial numerator

\[
R_N(u,v)=\sum_{a,b}r_{a,b}(N)u^av^b,
\]

define the shifted Delannoy moments

\[
M_N(a):=\operatorname{CT}_u\left[u^a\phi(u)^N\right].
\]

An exact finite-sum formula is

\[
\boxed{
M_N(a)
=\sum_{i=\max(0,-a)}^{\min(N,N-a)}
\binom Ni\binom N{i+a}2^{i+a}.
}
\tag{21}
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{
\operatorname{CT}_{u,v}\left[\Phi(u,v)^NR_N(u,v)\right]
=\sum_{a,b}r_{a,b}(N)M_N(a)M_N(b).
}
\tag{22}
\]

Thus every two-dimensional period arising from a Laurent-polynomial reduced numerator is a finite bilinear combination of shifted Delannoy moments. Formula (16) is the simplest case \(M_N(0)^2=D_N^2\).

# (e) What the two-dimensional period proves arithmetically

For the auxiliary row, the dyadic denominator statement is immediate.

- \(\Phi(u,v)^N\) is a Laurent polynomial with integer coefficients.
- Every shifted moment \(M_N(a)\) in (21) is an integer.
- The only denominator in (13) is \(4\).

Therefore

\[
\boxed{
4\widehat{\mathbf Q}^{\mathrm{AZ}}_N\in\mathbb Z^3.
}
\tag{23}
\]

More generally, if

\[
R_N(u,v)\in\mathbb Z[1/2][u^{\pm1},v^{\pm1}]^3,
\]

then its constant-term periods lie in \(\mathbb Z[1/2]^3\). This is exactly the usual Apéry/Zeilberger arithmetic mechanism: an integral Laurent-polynomial phase plus a controlled rational numerator gives a controlled denominator lattice.

However, three distinctions are essential.

## 1. It proves the arithmetic of the auxiliary row, not automatically the printed rows

The single row (16) does not match either printed initial row. To deduce that the actual normalized numerator and denominator rows have only powers of \(2\) in their denominators, one still needs one of:

1. a full rank-three period basis with entries in \(\mathbb Z[1/2]\), together with a fixed basis-change matrix in \(\operatorname{GL}_3(\mathbb Z[1/2])\);
2. an invariant \(2\)-local lattice preserved by the normalized step matrices;
3. a direct odd-prime valuation/divisibility theorem for the matrix products.

Without such a basis or lattice certificate, the observed cancellation of odd factors in the actual rows remains a separate arithmetic statement.

## 2. Prime support is not denominator-size control

Even after proving that a denominator is \(2^{s_N}\), an irrationality proof needs a bound on \(s_N\). The support “only the prime \(2\)” does not imply that the integer-cleared linear form tends to zero.

## 3. The collapsed period contains no Catalan extension

The two-dimensional integral proves:

- the homogeneous recurrence for the Delannoy-square auxiliary row;
- its exact finite-sum formula;
- and its dyadic integrality.

It does **not** prove:

- that the actual CMF connection coefficient is \(G\);
- that the Catalan error row is represented by this period;
- or that the corresponding integer linear forms tend to zero.

A genuine Catalan certificate must retain a nontrivial \(t\)-cohomology class, for example through an \(\varepsilon\)-derivative whose first-order numerator is not divisible by \(1+t^2\), and then match that differentiated period to

\[
G\widehat{\mathbf Q}_N-\widehat{\mathbf P}_N
\]

at one index.

# Final interpretation

The complete factorization has a clean conceptual meaning:

\[
\boxed{
\text{verified AZ class}
=
\text{Delannoy rank-two period}\;\boxtimes\;
\text{Delannoy rank-two period}\;\boxtimes\;
\text{trivial }t\text{-class}.
}
\]

The \(t\)-variable is present at the chain/certificate level so that the local divergence identity closes, but it disappears from the period after projection. The surviving period is exactly

\[
\frac{D_N^2}{4}igl((N+2)^2,2(N+2),1\bigr).
\]

That explains the symmetric-square Poincaré roots and the dyadic arithmetic of this auxiliary solution. It does not yet explain the Catalan connection coefficient; the latter lives in the missing nontrivial extension/framing, not in the collapsed zeroth-order \(t\)-sector.
