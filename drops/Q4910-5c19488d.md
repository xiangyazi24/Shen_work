# Problem 2.5: why the present moment identity is not yet a Nikishin certificate

## Executive conclusion

The proposed Nikishin/Gonchar–Rakhmanov argument **cannot be completed from the stated moment formula**. The obstruction is exact and elementary: the polynomial

\[
R_{N,j}(X)=A_{N,j}+B_{N,j}X
\]

has degree one for every \(N\), and the formulas

\[
Q_{N,j}=A_{N,j}-B_{N,j},\qquad P_{N,j}=-B_{N,j}
\]

are simply

\[
\boxed{Q_{N,j}=R_{N,j}(-1),\qquad P_{N,j}=-R'_{N,j}.}
\]

Moreover,

\[
\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}\,dt=G,
\qquad
\int_0^1\frac{-t^2\log t}{1+t^2}\,dt=1-G.
\]

Therefore, for **every** linear polynomial \(R=A+BX\),

\[
\begin{aligned}
\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}R(t^2)\,dt
&=AG+B(1-G)\\
&=G(A-B)+B\\
&=GQ-P.
\end{aligned}
\tag{1}
\]

Thus the integral identity is a two-dimensional linear-algebra identity valid for arbitrary \(A,B\). It imposes no orthogonality, no Padé interpolation condition, no zero distribution, and no asymptotic cancellation. It is consequently compatible with **any** limiting value selected by the recurrence.

The exact additional condition required is one of the following equivalent types.

1. A direct, independent bound
   \[
   \boxed{
   \frac{1}{Q_{N,j}}
   \int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}R_{N,j}(t^2)\,dt
   \longrightarrow0.}
   \tag{2}
   \]
   Together with \(P_{N,j}/Q_{N,j}\to L\), this proves \(L=G\).
2. A genuine Padé/Hermite–Padé realization by **degree-growing** denominator polynomials, with exact orthogonality conditions and an evaluation identity recovering the CMF rows.
3. A matrix-valued orthogonal-polynomial realization with a spectral parameter, a positive matrix measure, and a block-Jacobi/Favard recurrence whose evaluation at one point is the printed CMF trajectory.

None of these conditions follows from the present linear moment formula or from the factorization of \(\det M(n)\).

# 1. The moment formula is tautological on \(\mathbb Q[X]_{\le1}\)

Write

\[
\mathcal C[R]
:=\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}R(t^2)\,dt.
\]

On the basis \(1,X\),

\[
\mathcal C[1]=G,
\qquad
\mathcal C[X]=1-G.
\tag{3}
\]

Hence

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal C[A+BX]=G(A-B)+B.}
\tag{4}
\]

The two auxiliary moment representations in the question are likewise identities on the two-dimensional space of linear polynomials:

\[
\int_0^1(10-18x)(A+Bx)\,dx=A-B=R(-1),
\tag{5}
\]

and

\[
\int_0^1(6-12x)(A+Bx)\,dx=-B=-R'.
\tag{6}
\]

The densities \(10-18x\) and \(6-12x\) are not newly discovered spectral measures. They are merely two signed integral representations of the functionals

\[
R\mapsto R(-1),
\qquad
R\mapsto-R'.
\]

There are infinitely many signed measures representing either functional on \(\mathbb Q[X]_{\le1}\).

## A universal counterexample

Let \(r_N\) be any rational sequence converging to any prescribed real number \(\ell\), and let \(Q_N\ne0\) be arbitrary. Define

\[
P_N=r_NQ_N,\qquad
B_N=-P_N,\qquad
A_N=Q_N-P_N.
\]

Then

\[
Q_N=A_N-B_N,\qquad P_N=-B_N,
\]

and identity (1) holds exactly, while

\[
\frac{P_N}{Q_N}=r_N\longrightarrow\ell.
\]

Thus the moment identity itself cannot distinguish \(G\) from any other possible connection coefficient.

# 2. Why the Birkhoff limit does not repair the gap

Put

\[
r_N:=\frac{P_{N,j}}{Q_{N,j}}.
\]

Since \(A=Q-P\) and \(B=-P\), one has the exact normalized polynomial

\[
\boxed{
\frac{R_{N,j}(X)}{Q_{N,j}}
=1-r_N(1+X).}
\tag{7}
\]

Dividing the moment identity by \(Q_{N,j}\) gives

\[
G-r_N
=
\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}
\bigl[1-r_N(1+t^2)\bigr]dt.
\tag{8}
\]

If Birkhoff theory gives \(r_N\to L\), then (7) converges uniformly in \(X\) to

\[
1-L(1+X),
\]

and dominated convergence in (8) gives

\[
G-L=G-L.
\]

This is a tautology, not a determination of \(L\). The sharper estimate

\[
\left|r_N-L\right|=O(N^{-3}\rho^N)
\]

only says that the normalized linear polynomial approaches its limiting linear polynomial at that rate. It does not imply that the integral in (2) tends to zero.

The minimal missing analytic statement is precisely (2), or any stronger estimate implying it.

# 3. The displayed signed functionals are not a Nikishin system

A Nikishin system is generated by finite measures of constant sign on real intervals, with consecutive generating intervals disjoint or meeting only at an endpoint. Its later measures are obtained by multiplying a root measure by Cauchy transforms of measures on the next intervals. Type-II denominator polynomials then have degree \(|\mathbf n|\) and satisfy \(|\mathbf n|\) orthogonality conditions. Perfectness means that these degrees never drop.

These requirements are explicit in Fidalgo Prieto and López Lagomasino, [*Nikishin systems are perfect. Case of unbounded and touching supports*](https://arxiv.org/abs/1009.1922). Their Stieltjes-type convergence theorem additionally assumes an admissible sequence of growing multi-indices and support/moment hypotheses.

The two densities proposed here fail as a direct Nikishin pair:

\[
10-18x=0\quad\text{at }x=\frac59,
\qquad
6-12x=0\quad\text{at }x=\frac12.
\]

Both change sign on \([0,1]\). Moreover, their ratio

\[
\frac{6-12x}{10-18x}
\]

has a pole at \(x=5/9\). It therefore cannot be the restriction to \([0,1]\) of the Cauchy transform of a finite measure supported on an interval disjoint from \([0,1]\), since such a transform is analytic on a neighborhood of \([0,1]\).

A linear recombination of the two functionals may have a positive representing density, but that does not solve the main issue: there is still no degree-growing type-II polynomial and no increasing family of orthogonality conditions.

## Degree one cannot support the required orthogonality

Suppose a fixed positive measure \(\mu\) on \([0,1]\) made every \(R_N=A_N+B_NX\) an orthogonal polynomial of degree one. Orthogonality to constants would require

\[
A_Nm_0+B_Nm_1=0,
\qquad
m_k=\int x^k\,d\mu(x).
\]

Hence

\[
\frac{A_N}{B_N}=-\frac{m_1}{m_0}
\]

would be independent of \(N\), and therefore

\[
\frac{P_N}{Q_N}
=-\frac{B_N}{A_N-B_N}
\]

would also be constant. The actual convergents are not constant. Thus the displayed \(R_N\) cannot be a fixed-measure orthogonal-polynomial sequence.

Allowing the measure to vary with \(N\) leaves the classical Nikishin and Markov convergence theorems; one would then need a separate varying-weight equilibrium analysis.

# 4. A complete one-measure Markov proof that would suffice

A two-measure Nikishin system is not necessary to represent Catalan's constant. After \(x=t^2\),

\[
G
=\frac14\int_0^1
\frac{-\log x}{\sqrt{x}(1+x)}\,dx.
\tag{9}
\]

Define the positive measure

\[
\boxed{
d\sigma(x)=\frac{-\log x}{4\sqrt{x}}\,dx,
\qquad 0<x<1,}
\tag{10}
\]

and its Markov function

\[
F(z)=\int_0^1\frac{d\sigma(x)}{z-x}.
\tag{11}
\]

Then

\[
\boxed{F(-1)=-G.}
\tag{12}
\]

Let \(\mathcal Q_N\) be the monic degree-\(N\) orthogonal polynomial for \(\sigma\):

\[
\int_0^1x^k\mathcal Q_N(x)d\sigma(x)=0,
\qquad k=0,\ldots,N-1.
\tag{13}
\]

Define the associated Padé numerator

\[
\mathcal P_N(z)
=\int_0^1
\frac{\mathcal Q_N(z)-\mathcal Q_N(x)}{z-x}
\,d\sigma(x).
\tag{14}
\]

Then the exact remainder formula is

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal Q_N(z)F(z)-\mathcal P_N(z)
=\int_0^1\frac{\mathcal Q_N(x)}{z-x}\,d\sigma(x).}
\tag{15}
\]

At \(z=-1\), this becomes

\[
\boxed{
G\mathcal Q_N(-1)+\mathcal P_N(-1)
=\int_0^1\frac{\mathcal Q_N(x)}{1+x}\,d\sigma(x).}
\tag{16}
\]

The classical Markov theorem gives

\[
\frac{\mathcal P_N(z)}{\mathcal Q_N(z)}\longrightarrow F(z)
\]

locally uniformly off \([0,1]\). Therefore

\[
-\frac{\mathcal P_N(-1)}{\mathcal Q_N(-1)}\longrightarrow G.
\tag{17}
\]

Consequently, a complete identification of the CMF would follow from exact nonzero factors \(c_{N,j}\) satisfying

\[
\boxed{
Q_{N,j}=c_{N,j}\mathcal Q_N(-1),
\qquad
P_{N,j}=-c_{N,j}\mathcal P_N(-1).}
\tag{18}
\]

Equations (13) and (18), not the linear identity (1), are the missing certificate.

This one-measure route is stronger and simpler than invoking the full Nikishin apparatus. A genuine two-measure construction may still be natural if the rank-three CMF encodes a simultaneous or derivative approximation, but it must produce analogous growing-degree multiple orthogonality.

# 5. Exact checklist for a Nikishin/Hermite–Padé proof

To invoke Nikishin perfectness and a Gonchar–Rakhmanov/Stieltjes convergence theorem, one must exhibit the following data.

## 5.1 Fixed generating measures

Construct constant-sign measures \(\sigma_0,\sigma_1\) on intervals \(\Delta_0,\Delta_1\), disjoint or touching at one endpoint, and form

\[
(s_0,s_1)=\mathcal N(\sigma_0,\sigma_1),
\qquad
s_0=\sigma_0,
\qquad
ds_1(x)=\widehat\sigma_1(x)d\sigma_0(x).
\]

The measures must be independent of \(N\).

## 5.2 Growing type-II denominators

For a step-line multi-index \(\mathbf n(N)=(n_0(N),n_1(N))\) with \(|\mathbf n(N)|\to\infty\), construct a polynomial \(\mathcal Q_{\mathbf n}\) of degree \(|\mathbf n|\) satisfying

\[
\int x^k\mathcal Q_{\mathbf n}(x)ds_j(x)=0,
\qquad
k=0,\ldots,n_j-1,\quad j=0,1.
\tag{19}
\]

Perfectness then supplies full degree and uniqueness. It does not manufacture (19); those equations must first be proved for the CMF polynomial.

## 5.3 Evaluation map to the printed rows

For a point \(z_0\notin\Delta_0\), prove exact identities of the form

\[
Q_{N,j}=c_{N,j}\mathcal Q_{\mathbf n(N)}(z_0),
\]

and

\[
P_{N,j}=c_{N,j}\mathcal P_{\mathbf n(N)}(z_0),
\]

where \(\mathcal P_{\mathbf n}\) is the appropriate type-II numerator or a fixed linear combination of the associated numerators. Three consecutive indices plus a common recurrence can prove this equality once the polynomial recurrence is known.

## 5.4 Admissible multi-index growth and support hypotheses

The components of \(\mathbf n(N)\) must satisfy the bounded-imbalance or related hypotheses of the selected convergence theorem. Perfectness alone gives normality; convergence also uses support, moment, and multi-index assumptions. See the Stieltjes-type theorem in [Fidalgo Prieto–López Lagomasino](https://arxiv.org/abs/1009.1922) and the mixed/multi-level convergence results of González Ricardo, López Lagomasino, and Medina Peralta, [*On the convergence of multi-level Hermite–Padé approximants*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.11370).

## 5.5 Identification of the target value

Finally, the limiting Markov or Nikishin function must be evaluated at \(z_0\) and shown to equal \(G\). Formula (12) provides a canonical choice at \(z_0=-1\).

Until items 5.1–5.3 are constructed, the words “perfect” and “AT system” cannot be applied to the printed CMF denominators.

# 6. Why the matrix recurrence alone does not give matrix orthogonality

A matrix-valued Favard theorem starts from a sequence of matrix polynomials \(\mathbf Q_N(x)\), with \(\deg\mathbf Q_N=N\), satisfying a block three-term recurrence such as

\[
x\mathbf Q_N(x)
=A_{N+1}\mathbf Q_{N+1}(x)
+B_N\mathbf Q_N(x)
+A_N^*\mathbf Q_{N-1}(x),
\tag{20}
\]

where the off-diagonal blocks are invertible and the recurrence can be symmetrized with \(B_N=B_N^*\). Positivity then gives a positive matrix measure. Higher odd-order scalar recurrences can sometimes be repackaged into such a matrix system; see Durán and Van Assche, [*Orthogonal matrix polynomials and higher order recurrence relations*](https://arxiv.org/abs/math/9310220).

The printed \(M(n)\) is instead a first-order cocycle

\[
Y_{n+1}=Y_nM(n)
\]

at one fixed problem. It contains no spectral variable \(x\), no polynomial-degree condition, no block-Jacobi self-adjointness, and no positive matrix moment functional. The factorization of \(\det M(n)\) controls invertibility and arithmetic singularities; it does not imply positivity or Favard structure.

A matrix-orthogonality proof therefore requires an additional construction:

\[
\boxed{
\text{a polynomial family }\mathbf Q_N(x),
\text{ a positive matrix measure }\Sigma,
\text{ and an evaluation }x=x_0
\text{ recovering }M(n).}
\]

Without this spectral lift, matrix-valued orthogonality is only an analogy.

# 7. The shortest remaining route to \(L=G\)

The Birkhoff result already proves that the ratio converges. The shortest possible completion is not necessarily full Nikishin theory. It is enough to prove the independent estimate

\[
\boxed{
\left|
\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}R_{N,j}(t^2)dt
\right|
=o\bigl(|Q_{N,j}|\bigr).}
\tag{21}
\]

Indeed,

\[
G-\frac{P_{N,j}}{Q_{N,j}}
=rac{1}{Q_{N,j}}
\int_0^1\frac{-\log t}{1+t^2}R_{N,j}(t^2)dt,
\]

so (21) immediately yields \(P_{N,j}/Q_{N,j}\to G\), and uniqueness of the Birkhoff limit gives \(L=G\).

A genuine Markov/Nikishin representation proves (21) through orthogonality and zero distribution. An explicit positive double integral or a first-order \(\varepsilon\)-AZ extension could also prove it. The existing degree-one moment identity does not.

## Final verdict

\[
\boxed{
\text{The remaining gap is not “prove perfectness.”
It is “construct the degree-growing Padé/Nikishin denominator
and identify its evaluation with the CMF rows.”}}
\]

Once that certificate is supplied, standard Markov or Nikishin convergence theory can identify the limit with Catalan's constant. Until then, the moment formula and the Birkhoff limit coexist without determining the connection coefficient.
