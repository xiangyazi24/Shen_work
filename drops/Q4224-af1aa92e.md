# Spectral source regularity and time-`C¹` for the Neumann chemotaxis equation

## Executive verdict

There are two important corrections to the proposed “standard route.”

1. **Two spatial integrations by parts give only quadratic decay**
   \[
   |c_k(t)|=O(k^{-2}).
   \]
   This is enough for \(\sum_k |c_k(t)|<\infty\), but it is **not** enough for
   \(\sum_k\lambda_k|c_k(t)|<\infty\), because
   \(\lambda_k|c_k|=O(1)\). It is also not, by itself, enough to prove
   \(\sum_k\lambda_k^2|c_k|^2<\infty\) by termwise comparison.

2. There are two clean ways to obtain the requested weighted conclusion:

   - For the weighted Hilbert-space statement
     \[
     \sum_k\lambda_k^2|c_k(t)|^2<\infty,
     \]
     **spatial \(H^2\) plus the Neumann trace condition
     \(S_x(t,0)=S_x(t,1)=0\) is sufficient**. The proof uses the exact weak
     integration-by-parts identity followed by Bessel/Parseval for
     \(S_{xx}\), not a scalar \(O(k^{-2})\) comparison.

   - For the absolute weighted statement
     \[
     \sum_k\lambda_k|c_k(t)|<\infty,
     \]
     one needs decay \(|c_k|=O(k^{-p})\) with **\(p>3\)**. The clean
     integer-order route is quartic decay \(O(k^{-4})\), obtained from
     \(S\in W^{4,1}\) together with the load-bearing boundary conditions
     \(S_x(0)=S_x(1)=0\). A repository-friendly, slightly stronger version is
     the depth-two weak-Neumann tower
     `IntervalWeakH2Neumann S` plus
     `IntervalWeakH2Neumann S.secondDeriv`.

For the actual mild-to-classical bootstrap, there is a third route that is often
strictly cheaper and is already implemented in the repository:

> Use only an unweighted \(\ell^1\) source envelope, obtained from the
> quadratic \(C^2_N\) decay, together with time-`C¹` source coefficients. A
> time integration by parts then proves eigenvalue-weighted summability of the
> **Duhamel coefficients**, which is the quantity needed to differentiate the
> mild term twice in space.

That route avoids requiring the full chemotaxis source itself to be spatially
\(C^4\).

# 1. Exact cosine integration-by-parts identities

Fix a positive mode \(k\ge 1\), and write

\[
\omega_k=k\pi,
\qquad
\lambda_k=\omega_k^2,
\qquad
c_k=\int_0^1 S(x)\cos(\omega_kx)\,dx.
\]

The zeroth mode is harmless for all eigenvalue-weighted statements because
\(\lambda_0=0\).

The coefficients in the question are the **raw** cosine coefficients. The
repository's `cosineCoeffs` convention multiplies every positive mode by `2`.
That changes constants but none of the decay or summability conclusions below.

## 1.1 Two integrations by parts

Assume first that \(S'\) is absolutely continuous and \(S''\in L^1(0,1)\).
The first integration by parts gives

\[
\begin{aligned}
c_k
&=\left[\frac{S(x)\sin(\omega_kx)}{\omega_k}\right]_{0}^{1}
  -\frac1{\omega_k}\int_0^1S'(x)\sin(\omega_kx)\,dx\\
&=-\frac1{\omega_k}\int_0^1S'(x)\sin(\omega_kx)\,dx,
\end{aligned}
\]

because \(\sin(0)=\sin(k\pi)=0\). Thus **no boundary condition on the values
\(S(0),S(1)\) is used**.

Integrating the remaining integral by parts gives

\[
\boxed{
 c_k
 =\frac{(-1)^kS'(1)-S'(0)}{\omega_k^2}
  -\frac1{\omega_k^2}\int_0^1S''(x)\cos(\omega_kx)\,dx.
}
\]

This formula identifies the load-bearing compatibility exactly:

\[
\boxed{S'(0)=S'(1)=0.}
\]

It is the derivative of the source, not the source itself, that must satisfy
homogeneous Neumann data. If either endpoint derivative is nonzero, an explicit
\(k^{-2}\) boundary term remains even when \(S\) is infinitely smooth.

Under the Neumann condition,

\[
\boxed{
 c_k=-\lambda_k^{-1}
       \int_0^1S''(x)\cos(k\pi x)\,dx,
}
\]

and therefore

\[
|c_k|\le \frac{\|S''\|_{L^1}}{(k\pi)^2}.
\]

This is the exact quadratic-decay statement represented in the repository by
`IntervalWeakH2Neumann`.

## 1.2 Four integrations by parts

Assume now \(S\in W^{4,1}(0,1)\) and still
\(S'(0)=S'(1)=0\). Applying the preceding two-integration formula to
\(S''\) yields

\[
\int_0^1 S''(x)\cos(\omega_kx)\,dx
 =\frac{(-1)^kS'''(1)-S'''(0)}{\omega_k^2}
  -\frac1{\omega_k^2}\int_0^1S^{(4)}(x)\cos(\omega_kx)\,dx.
\]

Hence

\[
\boxed{
 c_k
 =\frac{S'''(0)-(-1)^kS'''(1)
       +\int_0^1S^{(4)}(x)\cos(k\pi x)\,dx}
      {(k\pi)^4}.
}
\]

Consequently,

\[
\boxed{
 |c_k|
 \le
 \frac{|S'''(0)|+|S'''(1)|+\|S^{(4)}\|_{L^1}}
      {(k\pi)^4}.
}
\]

A useful subtlety is that the **direct** four-integration proof does not require
\(S'''(0)=S'''(1)=0\). Those two traces occur only in the numerator of a
\(k^{-4}\) term. The indispensable endpoint condition remains
\(S'(0)=S'(1)=0\).

The repository's depth-two weak-`H²_N` theorem is deliberately stronger: asking
that `S''` itself carry `IntervalWeakH2Neumann` corresponds, in the classical
setting, to also imposing \(S'''(0)=S'''(1)=0\). This stronger hypothesis is
very convenient in Lean because the same weak-Laplacian interface can be
applied twice.

# 2. Which decay exponent is actually needed?

Suppose

\[
|c_k|\le C k^{-p}.
\]

Since \(\lambda_k\asymp k^2\),

\[
\lambda_k|c_k|\lesssim k^{2-p}.
\]

Thus

\[
\boxed{
\sum_k\lambda_k|c_k|<\infty
\quad\Longleftarrow\quad
p>3.
}
\]

The exponent \(p=3\) is the harmonic-series borderline and is not sufficient.
The clean integer exponent is therefore

\[
\boxed{p=4.}
\]

This gives the following hierarchy.

| Desired conclusion | Clean sufficient spatial input | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| \(\sum |c_k|<\infty\) | \(S\in W^{2,1}\), \(S'(0)=S'(1)=0\) | \(O(k^{-2})\) |
| \(\sum \lambda_k^2|c_k|^2<\infty\) | \(S\in H^2\), \(S'(0)=S'(1)=0\) | weak IBP + Bessel/Parseval |
| \(\sum \lambda_k|c_k|<\infty\) | \(S\in W^{4,1}\), \(S'(0)=S'(1)=0\) | \(O(k^{-4})\) |
| same weighted \(\ell^1\), Sobolev form | \(S\in H^s_N\), \(s>5/2\) | Cauchy--Schwarz in mode space |

For the last row, if
\(\sum\lambda_k^s|c_k|^2<\infty\), then

\[
\sum_{k\ge1}\lambda_k|c_k|
\le
\left(\sum_{k\ge1}\lambda_k^{2-s}\right)^{1/2}
\left(\sum_{k\ge1}\lambda_k^s|c_k|^2\right)^{1/2}.
\]

Because \(\lambda_k\asymp k^2\), the first series converges precisely when
\(2(2-s)<-1\), i.e. \(s>5/2\). A clean integer Sobolev assumption is
\(H^3_N\).

# 3. Why `H²_N` is enough for the weighted `ℓ²` alternative

Assume

\[
S\in H^2(0,1),
\qquad
S'(0)=S'(1)=0
\]

in the trace sense. In one dimension, an \(H^2\) function has a well-defined
\(H^1\) first derivative and hence endpoint traces.

For \(k\ge1\), the weak Neumann integration-by-parts identity gives

\[
\lambda_k c_k
=-\int_0^1S''(x)\cos(k\pi x)\,dx.
\]

Bessel's inequality for the unnormalized positive-frequency cosine family gives

\[
\sum_{k\ge1}
\left|\int_0^1S''(x)\cos(k\pi x)\,dx\right|^2
\le \frac12\|S''\|_{L^2(0,1)}^2.
\]

Therefore, for the raw coefficients used in the question,

\[
\boxed{
\sum_{k\ge1}\lambda_k^2|c_k|^2
\le \frac12\|S''\|_2^2<\infty.
}
\]

For the repository-normalized positive modes `cosineCoeffs S k = 2*c_k`,
the corresponding nonsharp bound is

\[
\sum_{k\ge1}\lambda_k^2|\operatorname{cosineCoeffs}(S,k)|^2
\le 2\|S''\|_2^2.
\]

This proof explains why the scalar estimate \(|c_k|\le C/k^2\) is the wrong
way to justify the weighted \(\ell^2\) conclusion: the convergence comes from
orthogonality of the coefficients of \(S''\), not from bounding every one of
them by a common constant.

The Neumann trace is again essential. Without it, \(\lambda_kc_k\) contains the
nondecaying sequence
\((-1)^kS'(1)-S'(0)\), which is not square-summable unless both traces vanish.

# 5. The full chemotaxis source loses one spatial derivative

Write, on a positive-time slab where the positive part can be handled,

\[
L(u)=u(a-bu^\alpha),
\qquad
Q(u,R)=\frac{uR_x}{(1+R)^\beta},
\qquad
S=L(u)-\chi_0\partial_xQ(u,R).
\]

The chemotaxis term is already **one spatial derivative** of a product
containing \(R_x\). Consequently the assumption “\(u\) is spatially
\(H^2/C^2\)” does not by itself imply that the full source is
\(H^2/C^2\).

For example, in a Sobolev scale in one space dimension:

- if \(u\in H^2\) and the elliptic resolver gives \(R\in H^4\), then
  \(Q\in H^2\), hence \(Q_x\in H^1\);
- therefore the chemotaxis-divergence source is generally only \(H^1\), not
  \(H^2\).

In a classical scale, a transparent sufficient budget is:

- to obtain \(S\in C^2\): require \(u\in C^3\) and \(R\in C^4\);
- to obtain \(S\in W^{4,1}\) or \(C^4\): require, for example,
  \(u\in C^5\) and \(R\in C^6\).

These are sufficient, not claimed minimal. Since the elliptic resolver gains two
spatial derivatives, a positive smooth \(u\) of class \(C^3\) or \(C^5\)
normally supplies even more regularity for \(R\).

# 6. Why the full source satisfies the Neumann compatibility

Assume enough smoothness and the Neumann conditions

\[
u_x(0)=u_x(1)=0,
\qquad
R_x(0)=R_x(1)=0.
\]

## 6.1 Logistic part

For \(L(u)=u(a-bu^\alpha)\) on the positive region,

\[
\partial_xL(u)
=\bigl(a-b(1+\alpha)u^\alpha\bigr)u_x.
\]

Thus \(L_x=0\) at both endpoints.

## 6.2 Chemotaxis part

Let

\[
Q=\frac{uR_x}{(1+R)^\beta}.
\]

The derivative of the chemotaxis source is \(\partial_x(Q_x)=Q_{xx}\).
At an endpoint, every term in \(Q_{xx}\) contains either \(u_x\), \(R_x\),
or \(R_{xxx}\). If the resolver equation is

\[
-R_{xx}+\mu R=\nu u^\gamma,
\]

then differentiation gives

\[
-R_{xxx}+\mu R_x=\nu\gamma u^{\gamma-1}u_x.
\]

Hence \(R_{xxx}=0\) at each endpoint, and therefore \(Q_{xx}=0\) there.
It follows that

\[
S_x(0)=S_x(1)=0.
\]

For formalization, the parity argument is usually cleaner. Extend the Neumann
cosine representatives evenly about both endpoints. Then:

- `u` and `R` are even;
- `R_x` is odd;
- `Q = u R_x (1+R)^{-β}` is odd;
- `Q_x` is even;
- the logistic source is even;
- hence the full source `S` is even and `S_x` is odd, so `S_x` vanishes at the
  reflection points.

This is the strategy used by `chemDivSource_weakH2_of_cosineRep`.

Again, **no condition `S(0)=S(1)=0` is needed**.

# 7. A cheaper route for the actual Duhamel bootstrap

Suppose

\[
b_k(t)=\int_{\ell}^{t}e^{-\lambda_k(t-s)}c_k(s)\,ds.
\]

If \(c_k\) is time-`C¹`, integration by parts in time gives

\[
\boxed{
\lambda_k b_k(t)
=c_k(t)-e^{-\lambda_k(t-\ell)}c_k(\ell)
 -\int_\ell^t e^{-\lambda_k(t-s)}c_k'(s)\,ds.
}
\]

Assume on a compact window:

1. \(|c_k(s)|\le E_k\), with \(\sum_kE_k<\infty\);
2. \(|c_k'(s)|\le M_{\dot c}\), uniformly in both \(s\) and \(k\).

Then

\[
\lambda_k|b_k(t)|
\le 2E_k+M_{\dot c}\int_\ell^t e^{-\lambda_k(t-s)}\,ds.
\]

For positive modes,

\[
\int_\ell^t e^{-\lambda_k(t-s)}\,ds
\le \lambda_k^{-1},
\]

and in one dimension

\[
\sum_{k\ge1}\lambda_k^{-1}
=\pi^{-2}\sum_{k\ge1}k^{-2}<\infty.
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{
\sum_k\lambda_k|b_k(t)|<\infty.
}
\]

This needs only quadratic spatial source decay, because that already produces a
summable unweighted envelope \(E_k=O(k^{-2})\). It does **not** require
instantaneous source weighted summability \(\sum\lambda_k|c_k(t)|\).

# 8. Time-`C¹` of the physical source

Fix a compact positive-time interval

\[
J=[\varepsilon,T_0]\Subset(0,T].
\]

Working on such a window avoids the initial heat singularity and is the correct
interface for Lean. At the terminal horizon, one-sided `HasDerivWithinAt` data
is sufficient.

## 8.1 Positive-time `C¹` of the mild solution is not automatic from continuity alone

Let \(A=\nu\Delta_N\). A restarted mild identity reads

\[
u(t)=e^{(t-a)A}u(a)+\int_a^t e^{(t-s)A}F(u(s))\,ds,
\qquad 0<a<t.
\]

The standard analytic-semigroup conclusion

\[
u\in C^1(J;X),
\qquad
u_t=Au+F(u)
\]

is valid when the restarted datum lies in the generator domain and the forcing
has enough local time regularity, for example local Hölder or Dini continuity
in the base Banach space. It should not be justified by the phrase “the forcing
is merely continuous” without a theorem covering that exact hypothesis.

There are two noncircular formalization routes:

1. prove a dedicated analytic-semigroup/maximal-regularity theorem with the
   required Banach scale; or
2. use the repository's local restart cosine representation and differentiate
   the series mode by mode.

The second route is already represented by

```lean
restartCosineSeries_hasDerivAt_time
ShenWork.Paper2.PicardLimitK1.slopeSlice
```

and the assembly in

```text
ShenWork/Paper2/IntervalPicardLimitK1.lean
```

It proves genuine two-sided derivatives at interior times by restarting before
the evaluation time and using a clamped source on an open neighborhood. This
avoids using the desired classical PDE itself to manufacture `u_t`, which would
be circular.

## 8.2 The elliptic resolver is a smooth map once the power source is smooth

Write the resolver as

\[
R[w]=(\mu-\partial_{xx,N})^{-1}(\nu w^\gamma)=:\mathcal R(\nu w^\gamma),
\qquad \mu>0.
\]

The load-bearing resolver fact is:

\[
\mathcal R:X^r\longrightarrow X^{r+2}_N
\]

is bounded linear, for the selected Sobolev or Hölder scale. Because it is
linear, it commutes with time differentiation. Thus, whenever
\(t\mapsto \nu w(t)^\gamma\) is `C¹` into \(X^r\),

\[
\boxed{
R_t(t)=\mathcal R\bigl(\nu\gamma w(t)^{\gamma-1}w_t(t)\bigr).
}
\]

A convenient sufficient classical-space package for source time-`C¹` is

\[
u\in C^1(J;C^1([0,1])),
\qquad
R\in C^1(J;C^2([0,1])).
\]

Then the flux is `C¹` in time with values in `C¹` in space, and its spatial
derivative is `C¹` in time with values in `C⁰`.

The maximum principle for the positive resolver source gives \(R\ge0\), hence

\[
1+R\ge1.
\]

Therefore the denominator and all powers \((1+R)^{-\beta-j}\) are uniformly
smooth; there is no denominator singularity.

## 8.3 Explicit derivative of the source

On a region where \(u_+=u\), put

\[
h=(1+R)^{-\beta},
\qquad
Q=uR_xh.
\]

The logistic time derivative is

\[
\boxed{
\partial_t\bigl(u(a-bu^\alpha)\bigr)
=\bigl(a-b(1+\alpha)u^\alpha\bigr)u_t.
}
\]

The flux derivative is

\[
\boxed{
Q_t
=u_tR_xh
 +u(R_t)_xh
 -\beta uR_xR_t(1+R)^{-\beta-1}.
}
\]

Hence

\[
\boxed{
S_t
=\bigl(a-b(1+\alpha)u^\alpha\bigr)u_t
 -\chi_0\partial_x\!\left[
   u_tR_x(1+R)^{-\beta}
   +u(R_t)_x(1+R)^{-\beta}
   -\beta uR_xR_t(1+R)^{-\beta-1}
 \right].
}
\]

This is the field that should be proved jointly continuous on
\(J\times[0,1]\), or integrably dominated on local slabs, before differentiating
the cosine coefficient.

# 9. The positive-part subtlety

This issue must be split into three different nonlinearities.

## 9.1 The flux factor `u₊`

If the solution is known to satisfy

\[
u(t,x)\ge0
\]

for every nearby positive time and every spatial point, then along the actual
trajectory

\[
u_+(t,x)=u(t,x)
\]

identically. This remains true at points where \(u=0\). Thus the kink of the
ambient scalar map `positivePart` does not obstruct differentiation **along
that nonnegative trajectory**.

In Lean, the robust proof pattern is to establish an eventual equality on a
time neighborhood and use a congruence lemma such as
`HasDerivAt.congr_of_eventuallyEq`; do not ask the global positive-part map to be
Fréchet differentiable at zero.

## 9.2 The logistic map `r ↦ r(a-b(r₊)^α)`

For every \(\alpha>0\), this scalar map is globally `C¹`:

- for \(r<0\), its derivative is \(a\);
- for \(r>0\), its derivative is
  \(a-b(1+\alpha)r^\alpha\);
- the right derivative tends to \(a\) at zero.

Thus the logistic **time-`C¹`** step does not require strict positivity. Higher
spatial differentiability at the zero set can require stronger exponent
conditions or a positive floor.

## 9.3 The resolver power `(u₊)^γ`

Here the exponent matters.

- If \(\gamma>1\), \(r\mapsto(r_+)^\gamma\) is globally `C¹`, with derivative
  zero at \(r=0\).
- If \(\gamma=1\), the ambient positive-part map is not differentiable at zero,
  but along a nonnegative trajectory it is simply `u`.
- If \(0<\gamma<1\), nonnegativity alone is not sufficient at a zero. Even when
  a nonnegative curve is differentiable and has derivative zero at its minimum,
  its fractional power need not be differentiable. For example,
  \(u(t)=(t-t_0)^2\) gives \(u(t)^\gamma=|t-t_0|^{2\gamma}\), which is not
  differentiable at \(t_0\) when \(2\gamma<1\).

Therefore the clean hypothesis valid for every \(\gamma>0\) is a positive-time
floor

\[
\boxed{
\inf_{(t,x)\in J\times[0,1]}u(t,x)>0.
}
\]

For a nontrivial nonnegative uniformly parabolic solution, a strong maximum
principle normally gives strict positivity for every \(t>0\), and compactness of
\(J\times[0,1]\) then gives the floor. The identically zero solution is a
separate trivial case.

This is exactly why the repository's power-source time-chain theorem

```lean
ShenWork.EWA.hasDerivAt_powerLiftSlice
```

uses `HasDerivAt.rpow_const` through the strictly positive branch. See

```text
ShenWork/Wiener/EWA/SourcePowerCoeffDeriv.lean
```

for the coefficient-level implementation.

# 10. Passing time differentiation through the cosine coefficient

Define

\[
\dot c_k(t)=\int_0^1S_t(t,x)\cos(k\pi x)\,dx.
\]

A formalizable local hypothesis package at an interior time \(t_0\) is:

1. for all nearby `t`, the slice `S t` is interval-integrable;

# 12. Differentiating the Duhamel coefficient

For a fixed mode, define

\[
b_k(t)=\int_0^t e^{-\lambda_k(t-s)}c_k(s)\,ds.
\]

Only continuity of \(c_k\) is needed for the basic derivative formula. Factor

\[
b_k(t)=e^{-\lambda_kt}\int_0^t e^{\lambda_ks}c_k(s)\,ds
\]

and apply the product rule and the fundamental theorem of calculus. Then

\[
\boxed{
 b_k'(t)=c_k(t)-\lambda_kb_k(t).
}
\]

The exact committed theorem is



criterion.

# 13. Recommended proof architecture for the classical PDE upgrade

For each compact positive-time window \(J=[\varepsilon,T_0]\), prove the
following in this order.

## A. Source value package

1. Establish sufficient spatial regularity of `u` and `R` to make every full
   source slice `S t` spatially `C²` or weak `H²`.
2. Prove `S_x(t,0)=S_x(t,1)=0`, preferably by cosine parity.
3. Produce
   `IntervalWeakH2Neumann (S t)`.
4. Obtain the uniform quadratic coefficient envelope
   \(|c_k(t)|\le C/(k\pi)^2\), plus the zeroth-mode bound.

This gives a summable unweighted source envelope.

## B. Source time package

1. Produce `u_t` noncircularly from a positive-time restart/analytic-semigroup
   theorem or the existing restart spectral `K1` route.
2. Prove the resolver mapping and derivative identity for `R_t`.
3. Prove the explicit pointwise source chain rule for `S_t`.
4. Prove joint continuity or a local dominated slab for `S_t`.
5. Set `adot t k = cosineCoeffs (S_t t) k` and apply
   `cosineCoeffs_hasDerivAt_of_smooth_param`.
6. Obtain one uniform `Mdot` from a compact-slab spatial bound on `S_t`.

This constructs `DuhamelSourceTimeC1On`.

## C. Duhamel spectral bootstrap

Apply

```lean

```

to get

\[
\sum_k\lambda_k|b_k(t)|<\infty.
\]

Then use

```lean

```

and the summable derivative majorant to differentiate the reconstructed cosine
series and recover

\[

on the positive-time window, together with the Neumann boundary conditions.

## D. Only if instantaneous source weighted `ℓ¹` is explicitly required

Upgrade the source spatial package to the depth-two weak-Neumann tower and call


This is a stronger and more expensive route. It should not be imposed merely to
prove regularity of the Duhamel term, because the time-IBP route already supplies
that regularity from `C²_N + time-C¹`.

# Final answer to the two requested facts

## Fact 1: eigenvalue-weighted source summability

- `S ∈ C²/H²` with `S'(0)=S'(1)=0` gives quadratic coefficient decay.
- Quadratic decay alone does **not** imply
  `Summable (fun k => λ_k * |c_k|)`.
- The exact pointwise exponent needed is any `p > 3`; quartic decay is the clean
  choice.
- A sufficient direct hypothesis is `S ∈ W^{4,1}` and
  `S'(0)=S'(1)=0`; no condition on `S(0),S(1)` is needed, and direct fourfold IBP
  does not require `S'''` to vanish.
- The repository-ready stronger hypothesis is
  `IntervalWeakH2Neumann S` plus
  `IntervalWeakH2Neumann S.secondDeriv`, consumed by
  `intervalWeakH4Neumann_eigenvalue_L1_summable`.
- Alternatively, `S ∈ H²` with Neumann traces gives
  `∑ λ_k² |c_k|² < ∞` by weak IBP plus Parseval.

## Fact 2: time-`C¹` of the source

- Work locally on every compact positive-time window.
- Obtain `u_t` from a noncircular restart/analytic-semigroup theorem, not from
  the desired classical PDE conclusion itself.
- Use the bounded linear elliptic inverse to obtain `R_t`.
- On a positive slab, or under exponent-specific zero-set hypotheses, apply the
  explicit chain rule displayed above to obtain `S_t`.
- Strict positivity is the clean universal treatment of `(u₊)^γ` when only
  `γ>0` is assumed; nonnegativity alone can fail at zeros for `0<γ<1`.
- Joint continuity/dominated local bounds justify
  `c_k'(t)=∫S_t(t,x)cos(kπx)dx` through
  `cosineCoeffs_hasDerivAt_of_smooth_param`.
- Continuity of `c_k` already gives
  `b_k'=c_k-λ_kb_k`; source `C¹` additionally gives the time-IBP identity that
  produces eigenvalue-weighted Duhamel summability.

These two packages remove the *spectral-regularity* hypotheses from the
mild-to-classical upgrade. The final global-existence theorem still also needs
the independent global a priori bounds and continuation criterion, but no
extra global smallness condition is introduced by this spectral step.
