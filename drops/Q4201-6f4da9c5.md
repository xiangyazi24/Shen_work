# Uniform gradient bounds from the half-order Volterra resolvent

## Verdict

**Yes.** Retaining the time-ordered Volterra operator gives a uniform-in-`n` gradient envelope on every finite horizon for **arbitrary** coupling coefficient. No condition such as

\[
2C_g|\chi_0|B\sqrt T<1
\]

is needed for the a priori bound.

The smallness condition appears only if one replaces the Volterra operator by its one-step sup-norm bound

\[
\|K\|_{C([0,T])\to C([0,T])}=2\sqrt T,
\qquad
(Kf)(t):=\int_0^t(t-s)^{-1/2}f(s)\,ds,
\]

and then treats every iteration as multiplication by the scalar `2 sqrt(T)`. That loses the time-ordering. The exact powers of `K` contain Gamma-function denominators, so the Neumann/resolvent series converges for every coefficient on every finite `T`.

There is one necessary qualification: with only `u_0 in L^infty`, the homogeneous heat term is of size `t^{-1/2}`. Thus the natural statement is

\[
\sup_n\sup_{0<t\le T}\sqrt t\,\|\partial_xu_n(t)\|_\infty<\infty,
\]

or, equivalently, a flat uniform bound on every buffered interval `[tau,T]`, `tau>0`. A flat bound including `t=0` requires stronger initial data such as `u_0 in W^{1,infty}`.

## 1. The scalar half-order Volterra lemma

Let

\[
(Kf)(t)=\int_0^t(t-s)^{-1/2}f(s)\,ds,
\qquad 0<t\le T.
\]

Suppose nonnegative functions `g_n` satisfy

\[
g_{n+1}(t)\le \phi(t)+\kappa(Kg_n)(t),
\qquad \kappa\ge0,
\]

and assume, after enlarging the constants if necessary to cover the base iterate,

\[
g_0(t)\le\phi(t),
\qquad
\phi(t)=H t^{-1/2}+B t^{1/2},
\qquad H,B\ge0.
\]

Then positivity of `K` and induction give

\[
g_n(t)
\le \sum_{m=0}^{n}\kappa^mK^m\phi(t)
\le \sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\kappa^mK^m\phi(t).
\]

For every `beta>0`, the Beta-function identity gives the exact formula

\[
\boxed{
K^m(t^{\beta-1})
=\frac{\pi^{m/2}\Gamma(\beta)}
       {\Gamma(\beta+m/2)}
 t^{\beta+m/2-1}.
}
\]

Define the two-parameter Mittag-Leffler function by

\[
E_{\alpha,\beta}(z)
:=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\frac{z^m}{\Gamma(\alpha m+\beta)}.
\]

With

\[
z(t)=\kappa\sqrt{\pi t},
\]

the resulting exact supersolution is

\[
\boxed{
G(t)
=H\sqrt\pi\,t^{-1/2}E_{1/2,1/2}(z(t))
 +B\Gamma(3/2)t^{1/2}E_{1/2,3/2}(z(t)).
}
\]

Since `Gamma(3/2)=sqrt(pi)/2`, this is also

\[
G(t)
=H\sqrt\pi\,t^{-1/2}E_{1/2,1/2}(z(t))
 +\frac{B\sqrt\pi}{2}t^{1/2}E_{1/2,3/2}(z(t)).
\]

The series defining both Mittag-Leffler functions is entire. Therefore, for every finite `T` and every finite `kappa`,

\[
\boxed{
\sup_n\sup_{0<t\le T}\sqrt t\,g_n(t)
\le \sup_{0<t\le T}\sqrt t\,G(t)<\infty.
}
\]

Consequently, for every `tau>0`,

\[
\boxed{
\sup_n\sup_{\tau\le t\le T}g_n(t)
\le \tau^{-1/2}
   \sup_{0<t\le T}\sqrt t\,G(t)<\infty.
}
\]

No smallness condition on `kappa` or `T` occurs.

A compact reusable statement is:

```text
Half-order Volterra envelope.
Let T,kappa,H,B >= 0. If
  g_0(t) <= H/sqrt(t) + B*sqrt(t),
  g_{n+1}(t) <= H/sqrt(t) + B*sqrt(t)
                 + kappa * integral_0^t (t-s)^(-1/2) g_n(s) ds,
then g_n(t) <= G(t) for every n and 0<t<=T, where
  G(t) = H*sqrt(pi)/sqrt(t) * E_{1/2,1/2}(kappa*sqrt(pi*t))
       + B*Gamma(3/2)*sqrt(t) * E_{1/2,3/2}(kappa*sqrt(pi*t)).
In particular, sup_n sup_{0<t<=T} sqrt(t) g_n(t) is finite.
```

## 2. Application to the truncated Neumann Picard iterate

Set

\[
g_n(t):=\|\partial_xu_n(t)\|_\infty.
\]

Assume the Neumann heat semigroup satisfies, on the bounded interval,

\[
\|\partial_xS(t)f\|_\infty
\le C_g t^{-1/2}\|f\|_\infty,
\qquad 0<t\le T,
\]

and that truncation gives the uniform source estimate

\[
\|\operatorname{source}(u_n)(s)\|_\infty
\le A+|\chi_0|B_F g_n(s).
\]

Then

\[
\begin{aligned}
g_{n+1}(t)
&\le C_g t^{-1/2}\|u_0\|_\infty
 +C_g\int_0^t(t-s)^{-1/2}
   \bigl(A+|\chi_0|B_Fg_n(s)\bigr)\,ds\\
&=H t^{-1/2}+2C_gA\sqrt t+\kappa(Kg_n)(t),
\end{aligned}
\]

where

\[
H=C_g\|u_0\|_\infty,
\qquad
\kappa=C_g|\chi_0|B_F.
\]

Thus the preceding lemma applies with

\[
B=2C_gA.
\]

An explicit uniform envelope is therefore

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
G(t)
={}&C_g\|u_0\|_\infty\sqrt\pi\,t^{-1/2}
 E_{1/2,1/2}\!\left(C_g|\chi_0|B_F\sqrt{\pi t}\right)\\
&+C_gA\sqrt\pi\,t^{1/2}
 E_{1/2,3/2}\!\left(C_g|\chi_0|B_F\sqrt{\pi t}\right).
\end{aligned}
}
\]

After ensuring the base Picard iterate is below the same envelope,

\[
\boxed{
\|\partial_xu_n(t)\|_\infty\le G(t)
\quad\text{for all }n\text{ and }0<t\le T.
}
\]

This is the desired uniform-in-`n`, finite positive-time gradient estimate for arbitrary `C_g|chi_0|B_F`.

The bound can grow rapidly with the horizon. Indeed,

\[
E_{1/2,1}(z)=e^{z^2}\operatorname{erfc}(-z)\le2e^{z^2}
\qquad(z\ge0),
\]

so the natural growth scale is roughly

\[
\exp(\pi\kappa^2t).
\]

Thus the result is finite on every finite horizon but is not claimed to be uniform as `T tends to infinity`.

## 3. Why the resolvent exists for every coupling coefficient

For bounded `f`, the exact operator powers satisfy

\[
\boxed{
\|K^m\|_{C([0,T])\to C([0,T])}
=\frac{\pi^{m/2}T^{m/2}}{\Gamma(m/2+1)}.
}
\]

Hence

\[
\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}
\kappa^m\|K^m\|
=E_{1/2,1}\!\left(\kappa\sqrt{\pi T}\right)
<\infty
\]

for every finite `kappa,T`. In particular,

\[
\boxed{
\|(I-\kappa K)^{-1}\|_{C\to C}
\le E_{1/2,1}\!\left(\kappa\sqrt{\pi T}\right)
\le2e^{\pi\kappa^2T}.
}
\]

Also,

\[
\lim_{m\to\infty}\|K^m\|^{1/m}=0.
\]

Thus `K` is quasinilpotent: its spectral radius is zero. This is the operator-theoretic reason that `I-kappa K` is invertible for every scalar `kappa`.

The first convolution of the initial singularity is scale invariant,

\[
K(t^{-1/2})=\pi,
\]

but repeated convolutions do **not** produce a geometric sequence of constant factors. Instead,

\[
K^2(t^{-1/2})=2\pi\sqrt t,
\qquad
K^3(t^{-1/2})=\pi^2t,
\qquad\ldots,
\]

and the general Gamma denominator forces convergence.

## 4. Equivalent Bielecki-norm contraction

The same fact can be phrased as a contraction after changing to an equivalent exponentially weighted norm.

For bounded functions on `[0,T]`, define

\[
\|f\|_\lambda
:=\sup_{0\le t\le T}e^{-\lambda t}|f(t)|.
\]

Then

\[
\begin{aligned}
e^{-\lambda t}|Kf(t)|
&\le \|f\|_\lambda
 \int_0^t(t-s)^{-1/2}e^{-\lambda(t-s)}\,ds\\
&\le \sqrt{\frac\pi\lambda}\,\|f\|_\lambda.
\end{aligned}
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{
\|\kappa K\|_\lambda
\le q_\lambda
:=\kappa\sqrt{\frac\pi\lambda}.
}
\]

Choosing

\[
\lambda>\pi\kappa^2
\]

makes `q_lambda<1`, regardless of the size of `kappa` or `T`. If `phi` and `g_0` are bounded, the recursion gives

\[
\sup_n\|g_n\|_\lambda
\le
\max\left\{
\|g_0\|_\lambda,
\frac{\|\phi\|_\lambda}{1-q_\lambda}
\right\}.
\]

Since

\[
\|f\|_\infty\le e^{\lambda T}\|f\|_\lambda,
\]

this yields a finite ordinary sup bound on `[0,T]`.

### The `t^{-1/2}` initial singularity

When only `u_0 in L^infty`, `g_n` need not be bounded at `t=0`, so the preceding flat Bielecki norm is not the correct space. One may either:

1. apply it after a buffer `t>=tau>0`, treating the earlier interval as known forcing; or
2. use the adapted norm

\[
\|f\|_{\lambda,1/2}
:=\sup_{0<t\le T}e^{-\lambda t}\sqrt t\,|f(t)|.
\]

For this adapted norm,

\[
\|Kf\|_{\lambda,1/2}
\le \frac{C_*}{\sqrt\lambda}\|f\|_{\lambda,1/2}
\]

with a universal finite constant `C_*`. A direct split of the defining integral at one half gives, for example, `C_*<=4`. Hence

\[
\boxed{
\|\kappa K\|_{\lambda,1/2}<1
\quad\text{whenever}\quad
\lambda>16\kappa^2.
}
\]

This gives a completely elementary geometric-series proof in the natural `sqrt(t) g(t)` space. The Mittag-Leffler formula above is sharper and records the exact resolvent.

## 5. Why the flat-window affine recursion asks for smallness

If one takes the supremum too early, then

\[
\|Kf\|_{L^\infty(0,T)}
\le2\sqrt T\,\|f\|_{L^\infty(0,T)}.
\]

Replacing the operator by this scalar norm turns the recursion into

\[
D_{n+1}\le b+(2\kappa\sqrt T)D_n.
\]

A fixed affine envelope from this scalar inequality requires

\[
2\kappa\sqrt T<1.
\]

But this is only a sufficient condition produced by the coarse estimate

\[
\|K^m\|\le\|K\|^m=(2\sqrt T)^m.
\]

The true bound is

\[
\|K^m\|
=\frac{\pi^{m/2}T^{m/2}}{\Gamma(m/2+1)},
\]

which is much smaller for large `m`. The Gamma denominator is precisely what the scalar affine closure discards.

Therefore:

- the one-step flat affine profile needs a coefficient below one;
- the exact time-dependent Volterra resolvent does not;
- a Bielecki norm can make the same operator contractive for arbitrary coupling by choosing a sufficiently large exponential weight.

There is no contradiction between these statements.

---

## 6. What this does and does not remove

This argument removes the smallness/window hypothesis for the **uniform positive-time gradient a priori bound**.

It does not automatically prove every other part of a nonlinear Picard construction. In particular:

- the constants `A` and `B_F` must genuinely be uniform in `n`; truncation is what normally supplies this;
- a raw flat bound at `t=0` is impossible from `u_0 in L^infty` alone because of the homogeneous `t^{-1/2}` term;
- uniform boundedness of iterates is not by itself convergence, although a difference recursion of the same Volterra form can also be made contractive in a Bielecki norm;
- if other components of the system introduce non-Volterra terms or nonuniform Lipschitz constants, those must be controlled separately.

For the semigroup step itself, direct analytic-semigroup smoothing plus the scalar fractional-Volterra lemma is the clean route. Full `L^infty` maximal regularity is not needed and is in fact a less convenient framework at the endpoint `p=infinity`.

# Final formula

With

\[
H=C_g\|u_0\|_\infty,
\qquad
\kappa=C_g|\chi_0|B_F,
\]

and

\[
\|\operatorname{source}(u_n)(s)\|_\infty
\le A+|\chi_0|B_F\|\partial_xu_n(s)\|_\infty,
\]

one has, uniformly in `n`,

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\|\partial_xu_n(t)\|_\infty
\le{}&H\sqrt\pi\,t^{-1/2}
 E_{1/2,1/2}\!\left(\kappa\sqrt{\pi t}\right)\\
&+C_gA\sqrt\pi\,t^{1/2}
 E_{1/2,3/2}\!\left(\kappa\sqrt{\pi t}\right),
\qquad 0<t\le T.
\end{aligned}
}
\]

Hence

\[
\boxed{
\sup_n\sup_{0<t\le T}
\sqrt t\,\|\partial_xu_n(t)\|_\infty<\infty
}
\]

for every finite `T` and every finite coupling coefficient, with no smallness assumption.
