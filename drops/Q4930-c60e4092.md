# Apéry gcd problem: a rigorous reduction and the exact missing theorem

## Verdict

The proposed two-part strategy can be made precise, but two corrections are essential:

1. The Beukers supercongruence is a congruence for a **difference of Apéry numbers**, not a lower bound for a difference of their valuations.
2. For every prime \(p\le n\), the factor \(p\mid d_n\) already implies \(p\mid d_n b_n\). Thus a common factor does **not** require simultaneous congruences \(p\mid a_n\) and \(p\mid b_n\).

After correcting these points, Part 2 is indeed the right issue. In fact, I prove below that it is equivalent to the original conjecture, and that for every fixed \(\varepsilon>0\), all sufficiently large primes \(p\in(\varepsilon n,n]\) satisfy the exact equivalence

$$
p\mid\gcd(d_na_n,d_nb_n)
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
p\mid b_n.
$$

The remaining statement is therefore a pointwise, cross-prime distribution theorem for zeros of the Apéry numbers:

$$
\#\{p\in(\varepsilon n,n]:p\mid b_n\}
=o\!\left(\frac{n}{\log n}\right)
\qquad(\text{every fixed }\varepsilon>0).
$$

I did not find a published theorem proving this estimate. The July 2026 Ramanujan Challenge paper explicitly presents Problem 3.2 among the unproved conjectures. What follows is a complete proof of the reduction, including a local congruence for the rational companion \(a_n\), and a precise identification of the only missing input. Thus this is a rigorous proof reduction, not a claim that the final open diagonal estimate has already been proved.

```text
PROVED BELOW:
  • an unconditional prime-tower valuation bound from the Wronskian;
  • an exact large-prime transfer p^3 a_{qp+r} ≡ a_q b_r (mod p);
  • p | gcd(d_n a_n,d_n b_n) ⇔ p | b_n for p > εn, apart from
    finitely many fixed exceptions;
  • equivalence of the original conjecture, the proposed prime-count
    statement, and a truncated-radical statement for b_n.

ONLY MISSING INPUT:
  a pointwise diagonal Apéry sieve controlling primes p ≍ n for which
  b_n ≡ 0 (mod p), equivalently b_{n-⌊n/p⌋p} ≡ 0 (mod p).
```

## 1. Notation

Put

$$
P(n)=34n^3+51n^2+27n+5,
$$

so both sequences satisfy

$$
(n+1)^3u_{n+1}=P(n)u_n-n^3u_{n-1}.
$$

Let

$$
L_n=\operatorname{lcm}(1,\ldots,n),\qquad D_n=L_n^3,
$$

so \(D_n=d_n\) in the question, and define

$$
X_n=D_na_n,\qquad Y_n=D_nb_n,\qquad
G_n=\gcd(X_n,Y_n).
$$

The integral Apéry sequence is

$$
b_n=\sum_{k=0}^n\binom nk^2\binom{n+k}{k}^2
=1,5,73,1445,33001,819005,\ldots .
$$

The goal is \(\log G_n=o(n)\).

## 2. Three necessary corrections

### 2.1 The supercongruence was misread

For \(p\ge5\), the standard Apéry supercongruence is

$$
b_{mp^r}\equiv b_{mp^{r-1}}\pmod{p^{3r}},
$$

or equivalently

$$
v_p\!\left(b_{mp^r}-b_{mp^{r-1}}\right)\ge3r.
$$

It is **not**

$$
v_p(b_{mp^r})-v_p(b_{mp^{r-1}})\ge3r.
$$

For example, \(b_1=5\) and \(b_5=819005\), so

$$
v_5(b_1)=v_5(b_5)=1,
$$

whereas \(b_5-b_1\) is divisible by \(5^3\). Thus the supercongruence by itself does not give the asserted tower bound for the common gcd.

A suitable tower bound does exist, but it comes directly from the Wronskian; see Section 3.

### 2.2 For \(p\le n\), the second scaled term is automatically divisible by \(p\)

Since \(p\mid L_n\), one has \(p^3\mid D_n\), hence

$$
p\mid Y_n=D_nb_n
$$

for every prime \(p\le n\), independently of \(b_n\bmod p\). Consequently

$$
p\mid G_n\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad p\mid X_n=D_na_n
$$

for \(p\le n\). The phrase \(p\mid a_n\) is also ambiguous because \(a_n\) is rational.

There is an exact denominator interpretation. Write \(a_n=A_n/C_n\) in lowest terms. Since \(C_n\mid D_n\),

$$
\begin{aligned}
G_n
&=\gcd\!\left(\frac{D_n}{C_n}A_n,D_nb_n\right)\\
&=\frac{D_n}{C_n}\gcd(A_n,C_nb_n)\\
&=\frac{D_n}{C_n}\gcd(A_n,b_n).
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus a common prime can arise simply because the denominator of \(a_n\) fails to contain the full prime power supplied by \(D_n\). For large primes this denominator defect is exactly controlled by a zero of an Apéry number; Section 5 makes this precise.

### 2.3 All common prime factors are at most \(n\)

This follows from the Wronskian determinant in the next section: \(G_n\) divides an integer composed only of primes at most \(n\) (apart from the harmless factors \(2,3\), which are also at most \(n\) for large \(n\)). Hence counting primes only in \([1,n]\) is legitimate.

## 3. Wronskian rigidity: the correct Part 1

Define the Casoratian

$$
W_n=a_nb_{n-1}-a_{n-1}b_n.
$$

The recurrence gives

$$
W_{n+1}=\left(\frac{n}{n+1}\right)^3W_n.
$$

Since \(W_1=6\), induction yields the exact identity

$$
\boxed{W_n=\frac6{n^3}}. \tag{3.1}
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\frac{a_n}{b_n}-\frac{a_{n-1}}{b_{n-1}}
=\frac6{n^3b_nb_{n-1}}. \tag{3.2}
$$

After scaling,

$$
X_nY_{n-1}-X_{n-1}Y_n
=\frac{6D_nD_{n-1}}{n^3}. \tag{3.3}
$$

Because \(G_n\mid X_n\) and \(G_n\mid Y_n\), it divides the left side of (3.3). Therefore, for every prime \(p\),

$$
\boxed{
 v_p(G_n)
 \le v_p(6)+3\lfloor\log_p n\rfloor
       +3\lfloor\log_p(n-1)\rfloor-3v_p(n).
} \tag{3.4}
$$

In particular,

$$
v_p(G_n)\le v_p(6)+6\lfloor\log_p n\rfloor. \tag{3.5}
$$

This is the required individual-prime tower bound. It implies

$$
v_p(G_n)\log p\le 6\log n+O(1) \tag{3.6}
$$

for every prime \(p\mid G_n\). Hence

$$
\log G_n\le 6\,\omega(G_n)\log n+O(1), \tag{3.7}
$$

where \(\omega\) counts distinct prime factors.

For later use, fixed small primes have a uniformly controllable total contribution. For every fixed \(\varepsilon>0\), (3.5) gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
\sum_{p\le\varepsilon n}v_p(G_n)\log p
&\le 6\sum_{p\le\varepsilon n}
       \lfloor\log_p n\rfloor\log p+O(1)\\
&=6\,\vartheta(\varepsilon n)+O(\sqrt n\log n)\\
&=(6\varepsilon+o(1))n, \tag{3.8}
\end{aligned}
$$

by the prime number theorem. Thus small primes cost at most \(6\varepsilon n+o(n)\), and \(\varepsilon\) can be sent to zero after the large-prime analysis.

## 4. Lucas congruences for \(b_n\)

Gessel proved that the Apéry numbers satisfy Lucas congruences. The one-digit form is

$$
\boxed{b_{qp+r}\equiv b_qb_r\pmod p}
\qquad(0\le r<p). \tag{4.1}
$$

For completeness, this also follows directly from Lucas's theorem applied to

$$
T(n,k)=\binom nk^2\binom{n+k}{k}^2.
$$

Writing \(n=qp+r\) and \(k=sp+t\), the summand vanishes modulo \(p\) when a base-\(p\) carry occurs, and otherwise factors as

$$
T(qp+r,sp+t)\equiv T(q,s)T(r,t)\pmod p.
$$

Summing over \(s,t\) gives (4.1).

We shall also use the elementary congruence

$$
\boxed{b_{p-1}\equiv1\pmod p}. \tag{4.2}
$$

Indeed, in the defining sum for \(b_{p-1}\), every term with \(k\ge1\) contains

$$
\binom{p-1+k}{k},
$$

which is divisible by \(p\); only the \(k=0\) term survives.

## 5. A companion congruence for \(a_n\)

Congruences between rational numbers below are interpreted in the local ring \(\mathbb Z_{(p)}\): \(x\equiv y\pmod p\) means \(x-y\in p\mathbb Z_{(p)}\).

### Lemma 5.1 (leading companion/Frobenius congruence)

Let \(p\ge5\) be prime, let \(0\le q<p\), and suppose

$$
p\nmid b_0b_1\cdots b_q.
$$

Then for every \(0\le r<p\),

$$
\boxed{p^3a_{qp+r}\equiv a_qb_r\pmod p}. \tag{5.1}
$$

#### Proof

Because \(D_m a_m\in\mathbb Z\), and because \(m<p^2\), the element \(p^3a_m\) belongs to \(\mathbb Z_{(p)}\). Define

$$
C_{q,r}=p^3a_{qp+r}\pmod p.
$$

For \(q=0\), every \(a_r\) with \(r<p\) is \(p\)-integral, so \(C_{0,r}=0=a_0b_r\).

Now assume \(q\ge1\), and put \(K_q=C_{q,0}\). Reduce the recurrence modulo \(p\) inside the block \(qp,qp+1,\ldots,qp+p-1\). At the first step, where the recurrence index is \(qp\),

$$
C_{q,1}=5C_{q,0}.
$$

For \(1\le r\le p-2\),

$$
(r+1)^3C_{q,r+1}
=P(r)C_{q,r}-r^3C_{q,r-1}.
$$

This is exactly the recurrence and initial relation satisfied by \(b_r\). Therefore

$$
C_{q,r}=K_qb_r\qquad(0\le r<p). \tag{5.2}
$$

It remains to identify \(K_q\). Set \(S_m=a_m/b_m\). Formula (3.2), at \(m=qp\), gives

$$
p^3(S_{qp}-S_{qp-1})
=\frac6{q^3b_{qp}b_{qp-1}}. \tag{5.3}
$$

By (4.1), (4.2), and (5.2), reduction of (5.3) modulo \(p\) gives

$$
\frac{K_q}{b_q}-\frac{K_{q-1}}{b_{q-1}}
\equiv\frac6{q^3b_qb_{q-1}}\pmod p. \tag{5.4}
$$

On the other hand, (3.2) at the small index \(q\) says exactly

$$
\frac{a_q}{b_q}-\frac{a_{q-1}}{b_{q-1}}
=\frac6{q^3b_qb_{q-1}}. \tag{5.5}
$$

Since \(K_0=a_0=0\), comparison of (5.4) and (5.5), followed by induction on \(q\), yields

$$
\frac{K_q}{b_q}\equiv\frac{a_q}{b_q}\pmod p,
$$

and hence \(K_q\equiv a_q\pmod p\). Combining this with (5.2) proves (5.1). ∎

### Corollary 5.2 (exact large-prime support transfer)

For every fixed \(\varepsilon>0\), there is \(N_\varepsilon\) such that, whenever \(n\ge N_\varepsilon\) and \(p\) is prime with

$$
\varepsilon n<p\le n,
$$

one has

$$
\boxed{p\mid G_n\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad p\mid b_n}. \tag{5.6}
$$

#### Proof

Write \(n=qp+r\), \(0\le r<p\). Since \(p>\varepsilon n\), the integer \(q\) belongs to a finite set depending only on \(\varepsilon\). Once \(n\) is large, \(p\) avoids all prime divisors of the finitely many fixed nonzero numbers \(b_q\) and the numerators of \(a_q\), for these possible \(q\). It also satisfies \(n<p^2\).

Thus \(v_p(D_n)=3\), and \(D_n/p^3\) is a \(p\)-adic unit. Since \(p\mid Y_n\) automatically,

$$
\begin{aligned}
p\mid G_n
&\Longleftrightarrow p\mid X_n\\
&\Longleftrightarrow p^3a_n\equiv0\pmod p\\
&\Longleftrightarrow a_qb_r\equiv0\pmod p\\
&\Longleftrightarrow b_r\equiv0\pmod p.
\end{aligned}
$$

Meanwhile Lucas gives \(b_n\equiv b_qb_r\pmod p\), and \(b_q\) is a unit. Hence \(p\mid b_r\) is equivalent to \(p\mid b_n\). ∎

This corollary is the main simplification: in the linear prime range, the rational companion \(a_n\) is not an additional independent congruence. Its denominator defect is governed by exactly the same Apéry zero as \(b_n\bmod p\).

## 6. Exact equivalence with the proposed prime-count statement

Define the truncated radical

$$
R_n=\operatorname{rad}_{\le n}(b_n)
=\prod_{\substack{p\le n\\p\mid b_n}}p.
$$

### Theorem 6.1

The following four statements are equivalent:

1. \(G_n=e^{o(n)}\), i.e. \(\log G_n=o(n)\).
2. \(\omega(G_n)=o(n/\log n)\).
3. \(R_n=e^{o(n)}\), i.e. \(\log R_n=o(n)\).
4. For every fixed \(\varepsilon>0\),
   
   $$
   N_\varepsilon(n):=
   \#\{p\in(\varepsilon n,n]:p\mid b_n\}
   =o\!\left(\frac n{\log n}\right). \tag{6.1}
   $$

#### Proof

**(2) implies (1).** This is immediate from (3.7).

**(1) implies (2).** Fix \(\varepsilon>0\). Since every prime divisor of \(G_n\) is at most \(n\),

$$
\omega(G_n)
\le\pi(\varepsilon n)+\frac{\log G_n}{\log(\varepsilon n)}.
$$

Divide by \(n/\log n\), use the prime number theorem and \(\log G_n=o(n)\), and then let \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\).

**Equivalence of (1) and (3).** For fixed \(\varepsilon>0\), Corollary 5.2 identifies the supports of \(G_n\) and \(R_n\) on \((\varepsilon n,n]\). In this range (3.5) gives \(v_p(G_n)\le6\) for large \(n\). Therefore

$$
\sum_{\substack{\varepsilon n<p\le n\\p\mid b_n}}\log p
\le
\sum_{p>\varepsilon n}v_p(G_n)\log p
\le
6\sum_{\substack{\varepsilon n<p\le n\\p\mid b_n}}\log p. \tag{6.2}
$$

The small-prime contribution to \(\log G_n\) is at most \((6\varepsilon+o(1))n\) by (3.8), while the small-prime contribution to \(\log R_n\) is at most

$$
\vartheta(\varepsilon n)=(\varepsilon+o(1))n.
$$

Consequently one of \(\log G_n,\log R_n\) is \(o(n)\) if and only if the other is; let \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\).

**Equivalence of (3) and (4).** If \(\log R_n=o(n)\), then

$$
N_\varepsilon(n)\log(\varepsilon n)\le\log R_n,
$$

which gives (6.1). Conversely, assuming (6.1),

$$
\log R_n
\le\vartheta(\varepsilon n)+N_\varepsilon(n)\log n
\le(\varepsilon+o(1))n.
$$

Let \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\). ∎

This answers Question 1 precisely:

- Your Part 2 count is the right framing **after** replacing the incorrect tower argument by (3.4).
- A cleaner equivalent target is \(\operatorname{rad}_{\le n}(b_n)=e^{o(n)}\).
- On every fixed linear prime range, no separate analysis of \(a_n\) is needed.

## 7. Exactly how many summands survive when \(p>n/2\)?

Let \(p<n<2p\), and write

$$
n=p+r,\qquad0\le r<p,
$$

and

$$
m=\min(r,p-1-r).
$$

Every \(k\in[0,n]\) has the form \(k=s\) or \(k=p+s\). Lucas's theorem gives:

- for \(k=s\), the summand is nonzero modulo \(p\) exactly when \(0\le s\le m\), and then
  
  $$
  T(p+r,s)\equiv T(r,s)\pmod p;
  $$

- for \(k=p+s\), it is nonzero modulo \(p\) exactly when \(0\le s\le m\), and then
  
  $$
  T(p+r,p+s)\equiv4T(r,s)\pmod p.
  $$

Therefore the exact number of nonzero summands modulo \(p\) is

$$
\boxed{2(m+1)=2\bigl(\min(r,p-1-r)+1\bigr)}. \tag{7.1}
$$

Moreover,

$$
\begin{aligned}
b_{p+r}
&\equiv5\sum_{s=0}^{m}\binom rs^2\binom{r+s}{s}^2\pmod p\\
&\equiv5b_r\pmod p, \tag{7.2}
\end{aligned}
$$

which is the \(q=1\) case of Lucas.

The count (7.1) is not uniformly small. It is \(O(1)\) only in a thin regime such as \(p=n/2+O(1)\); it reaches \(p+1\) when \(r=(p-1)/2\), corresponding to \(p\approx2n/3\). Thus there is no general “few surviving terms” sieve throughout \(p\in(n/2,n]\).

Lemma 5.1 gives, for \(p\ge7\),

$$
p^3a_{p+r}\equiv6b_r\pmod p. \tag{7.3}
$$

Consequently, apart from the fixed small primes,

$$
\boxed{
p\mid G_{p+r}
\Longleftrightarrow p\mid b_r
\Longleftrightarrow p\mid b_{p+r}.
} \tag{7.4}
$$

For a fixed \(n\), the top-half part of the missing sieve can therefore be written exactly as

$$
\#\left\{
0\le r<\frac n2:
n-r\text{ is prime and }n-r\mid b_r
\right\}. \tag{7.5}
$$

This is a shifted large-prime-divisor problem for the moving sequence \(b_r\), not a standard sieve for a polynomial in \(r\).

Malik and Straub also prove the palindromy

$$
b_{p-1-r}\equiv b_r\pmod p. \tag{7.6}
$$

Near \(p=n/2\), this replaces \(r=n-p\) by \(p-1-r=2p-n-1\), explaining the very short sum in that narrow region. It does not control a positive proportion of the whole interval strongly enough to yield (6.1).

## 8. The Poisson heuristic and why it is not yet a proof

There are two related but distinct random models.

### 8.1 Fixed \(p\), varying the index

Because of (7.6), zeros among \(b_0,\ldots,b_{p-1}\pmod p\) occur in reflected pairs. Malik and Straub model the roughly \((p-1)/2\) independent representatives as uniform random residues. The number of zero pairs then tends heuristically to a Poisson random variable of mean \(1/2\), and the probability that \(p\) divides no Apéry number tends to

$$
e^{-1/2}.
$$

They formulate the resulting density statement as a conjecture. In particular, even the infinitude and density of primes dividing no Apéry number are not presently supplied by the Lucas theorem alone.

### 8.2 Fixed \(n\), varying \(p\asymp n\)

For a prime \(p\in(\varepsilon n,n]\), the exact reduction in Corollary 5.2 suggests the random estimate

$$
\Pr(p\mid G_n)=\Pr(p\mid b_n)\approx\frac1p.
$$

Hence

$$
\mathbb E N_\varepsilon(n)
\approx\sum_{\varepsilon n<p\le n}\frac1p
\sim\frac{\log(1/\varepsilon)}{\log n}. \tag{8.1}
$$

For \(p\in(n/2,n]\), this is approximately \(\log2/\log n\). Thus the natural heuristic is actually sharper than merely \(O(1)\): the expected number tends to zero.

However, (8.1) is a heuristic along a **diagonal** in which both the modulus and the residue index vary. Known Lucas and Dwork congruences describe the sequence for a fixed prime; they do not establish cross-prime equidistribution of the residues \(b_{n\bmod p}\).

The relevant state of the art does not presently close this gap:

- Gessel's theorem gives the Lucas factorization modulo \(p\).
- Malik--Straub give the palindromy and the \(e^{-1/2}\) heuristic, but state the density conclusion conjecturally.
- Rowland--Yassawi--Krattenthaler prove certain Lucas congruences modulo \(p^2\) for prescribed base-\(p\) digit sets; this is higher \(p\)-adic precision, not a zero-equidistribution theorem.
- Caruso--Fürnsinn--Vargas-Montoya--Zudilin compute Galois groups of Apéry-like generating series modulo primes; their result does not furnish the coefficient-zero statistics required by (6.1).

Thus the Poisson model is compelling, but no known large-sieve, Chebotarev, or trace-function estimate currently turns it into the required pointwise bound for every \(n\).

## 9. What happens to the harmonic sums in \(a_n\)?

A standard explicit representation is

$$
a_n=
\sum_{k=0}^n
\binom nk^2\binom{n+k}{k}^2
\left(
H_n^{(3)}+
\sum_{j=1}^k
\frac{(-1)^{j-1}}
     {2j^3\binom nj\binom{n+j}{j}}
\right), \tag{9.1}
$$

where

$$
H_n^{(3)}=\sum_{j=1}^n\frac1{j^3}.
$$

Wolstenholme--Ljunggren type congruences are highly effective for complete \(p\)-blocks. For \(p\ge5\), for example,

$$
H_{p-1}^{(1)}\equiv0\pmod{p^2},\qquad
H_{p-1}^{(2)}\equiv0\pmod p,
$$

and \(H_{p-1}^{(3)}\equiv0\pmod p\). Such identities underlie many Apéry supercongruences and their polynomial analogues.

But for this gcd problem the direct harmonic-sum route is unnecessary at the level of prime support. The Wronskian and Lemma 5.1 already give

$$
p^3a_{qp+r}\equiv a_qb_r\pmod p,
$$

so, for \(p\asymp n\), the \(a_n\)-condition collapses to the same zero \(b_r\equiv0\pmod p\). Wolstenholme-type estimates may help with higher powers \(p^j\mid G_n\), but (3.4) has already bounded those powers well enough. What remains is the distribution of first-order zeros as \(p\) and \(r=n-qp\) move together.

In other words, \(a_n\) is not the harder half after the Wronskian reduction; the hard object is the diagonal zero set of \(b_n\bmod p\).

## 10. The complete conditional proof outline

For each fixed \(\varepsilon>0\), let

$$
\mathrm{DAS}_\varepsilon(n):
\qquad
\#\{p\in(\varepsilon n,n]:p\mid b_n\}
=o\!\left(\frac n{\log n}\right).
$$

Assume \(\mathrm{DAS}_\varepsilon(n)\) for every fixed \(\varepsilon>0\). Split

$$
\log G_n
=
\sum_{p\le\varepsilon n}v_p(G_n)\log p
+
\sum_{p>\varepsilon n}v_p(G_n)\log p.
$$

The first sum is at most \((6\varepsilon+o(1))n\) by (3.8). In the second sum, Corollary 5.2 identifies the supporting primes with the primes counted by \(N_\varepsilon(n)\), and (3.5) gives \(v_p(G_n)\le6\). Hence

$$
\sum_{p>\varepsilon n}v_p(G_n)\log p
\le6N_\varepsilon(n)\log n=o(n).
$$

Therefore

$$
\limsup_{n\to\infty}\frac{\log G_n}{n}\le6\varepsilon.
$$

Letting \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\) proves \(G_n=e^{o(n)}\).

Conversely, Theorem 6.1 shows that the original gcd assertion implies every \(\mathrm{DAS}_\varepsilon\). Thus this is not merely a sufficient technical hypothesis: it is an equivalent formulation of the unsolved part.

A weighted version is often cleaner:

$$
\sum_{\substack{\varepsilon n<p\le n\\p\mid b_n}}\log p=o(n). \tag{10.1}
$$

Since \(\log p\asymp\log n\) in this range, (10.1) is equivalent to \(\mathrm{DAS}_\varepsilon\).

Writing \(q=\lfloor n/p\rfloor\) gives the block form of the desired diagonal sieve. For each fixed \(Q\), one needs

$$
\sum_{q=1}^{Q}
\#\left\{
\frac n{q+1}<p\le\frac nq:
 p\text{ prime and }p\mid b_{n-qp}
\right\}
=o\!\left(\frac n{\log n}\right), \tag{10.2}
$$

up to the finitely many fixed exceptional primes attached to \(a_qb_q\). This is the precise theorem a successful Part 2 must prove.

## 11. Direct answers to the five questions

### 1. Is Part 2 the right framing?

Yes, after correcting Part 1 and the interpretation of common divisibility. The exact equivalence is Theorem 6.1. The best framing is either

$$
\omega(G_n)=o(n/\log n),
$$

or, more intrinsically,

$$
\operatorname{rad}_{\le n}(b_n)=e^{o(n)}.
$$

The Wronskian, not the supercongruence, supplies the needed valuation bound.

### 2. How many terms survive for \(p\sim n/2\), and is there a sieve?

For \(n=p+r<2p\), exactly

$$
2\bigl(\min(r,p-1-r)+1\bigr)
$$

summands survive modulo \(p\), and their sum is \(5b_r\pmod p\). The number is \(O(1)\) only when \(p\) lies within \(O(1)\) of \(n/2\) or \(n\); it can be \(\Theta(n)\) in the interval. The condition is exactly \(p\mid b_{n-p}\), not simultaneous divisibility of \(a_n,b_n\). No standard sieve currently controls (7.5) pointwise.

### 3. Is there a Poisson heuristic, and can it be made rigorous?

Yes. The published fixed-\(p\) model predicts Poisson zero-pairs of mean \(1/2\). Along the fixed-\(n\) diagonal it predicts

$$
\mathbb E N_\varepsilon(n)\sim\frac{\log(1/\varepsilon)}{\log n}.
$$

No available result I found proves the necessary diagonal equidistribution. Even the related \(e^{-1/2}\) density statement for primes dividing no Apéry number remains conjectural in Malik--Straub.

### 4. What is the state of the art for \(a_n\) and Wolstenholme-type control?

Complete-block harmonic congruences and binomial supercongruences are very strong and give high \(p\)-adic precision. They do not presently give cross-prime zero statistics for the moving incomplete index. For the gcd support, Lemma 5.1 bypasses the harmonic sums entirely and reduces \(a_n\) to \(b_r\). Thus the unresolved distribution problem belongs to \(b_n\bmod p\), not to an independent \(a_n\)-sieve.

### 5. Is there a complete proof outline?

Yes: Sections 3--6 and 10 give a complete proof conditional on the diagonal Apéry sieve (6.1)/(10.1), and Theorem 6.1 proves that this missing input is equivalent to the original assertion. All other steps are unconditional. A genuinely complete solution now requires a new theorem establishing (10.1) or (10.2); existing Lucas, supercongruence, and Wolstenholme machinery does not by itself provide it.

## Bottom line

The useful advance is not a new heuristic but the exact transfer

$$
p^3a_{qp+r}\equiv a_qb_r\pmod p
$$

and its consequence

$$
p\mid\gcd(d_na_n,d_nb_n)
\Longleftrightarrow p\mid b_n
\qquad(p>\varepsilon n,\ n\text{ large}).
$$

This turns the challenge into one sharply stated problem: prove that the truncated radical of \(b_n\) below \(n\) is subexponential, equivalently prove the diagonal prime-zero bound (6.1). That estimate is precisely where a new idea beyond currently known Apéry congruences is required.
